# Trading Book Series: 09 "The Art and Science of Technical Analysis"

The core of trading lies in identifying imbalances in buying and selling pressure by analyzing market structure and Price Action, thereby establishing a verifiable trading edge.

Do not trade in a market where Price Action is completely random. Instead, focus on finding moments when buying and selling pressure is clearly imbalanced (such as trend breakout points) to enter.

The fundamental reason for market price movement is supply and demand imbalance.

> Imbalance of Buying and Selling Pressure

That is, the urgency of one side overwhelms the other, leading to liquidity exhaustion and driving price changes.

Buying and selling pressure is the force behind technical indicators or chart patterns.

The "Holy Grail" of technical analysis lies in identifying market structures caused by supply and demand imbalances. Any trading edge must be based on a verifiable statistical advantage (i.e., the sum of winning trades exceeds the sum of losing trades).

Every trade you make must be based on an imbalance of buying and selling pressure that exists in the market. Do not try to find patterns that don't exist in random price movements.

Market structure is a visible, static record on the chart (such as the arrangement of highs and lows). It is the static footprint left by Price Action.

> It provides market context, helping traders identify whether the market is currently in a trend or a trading range.



Price Action is the dynamic process that creates that structure (such as the strength and weakness of buying and selling forces). It describes how the market moves in a dynamic state.

> That is, how price moves from one level to another (usually randomly).



A common misconception is that it cannot be quantified or is merely naked candlestick trading (when in fact it is a description of the game between buying and selling forces).

By analyzing the characteristics of price movement (such as speed and magnitude), one can infer potential supply and demand imbalances.

Trend structure is the basic pattern of market movement, consisting of an impulse wave, a counter-trend retracement, and another impulse wave.

A trader's edge: a positive expectancy that can be repeatedly realized over a large sample — not the result of a single trade.

It pulls trade evaluation back from "feeling" to measurable standards. Key practical assessment points: every trade can be mapped to an R-multiple.

Trading profits do not come from perfect chart patterns, but from the statistical edge created by buying and selling pressure imbalances. A successful trading system must have a positive mathematical expectancy (win rate × average profit > loss rate × average loss) — this is the foundation of survival.

If your system does not have positive expectancy, no matter how good your discipline, money management, or psychological training, you are only amplifying ineffective effort.

Successful trading relies on repeatable patterns, but the value of a pattern lies in whether it maps to an identifiable imbalance.

The Anti pattern captures the first pullback after a trend change, exploiting the conflict between short-term momentum and long-term momentum.

Observe the first pullback after a breakout:

The breakout level holds, or price briefly pierces through and quickly recovers.

The pullback magnitude is manageable, and counter-momentum weakens.

This is the evidence that "the trend has been accepted by the market."

Truly effective support/resistance often manifests as rapid directional rejection. When price touches a certain level and quickly and violently reverses, it indicates that there is strong opposing supply and demand force at that level.

Observe the follow-through quality after a breakout. Observe the response to key levels during pullbacks. Observe the speed difference between rallies and declines.



Use "first assume I'm wrong" to combat hope and fear. Immediately after entering a position, assume you are wrong — not out of pessimism, but to eliminate the obsession of "having to prove I'm right."

This step can significantly reduce emotional position-adding, delayed stop losses, and revenge trading.

What you need is not stronger willpower, but a clearer definition of invalidation and execution boundaries.

The core sequence of technical analysis should be:

First confirm the edge, then execute the strategy. First determine the structure, then find the timing. First define risk, then talk about reward.

The key contribution of this book is restoring technical analysis from "pattern recognition" to "imbalance recognition."
