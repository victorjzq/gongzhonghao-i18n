# Trading Book Series: 04 "Principles of Professional Speculation"

The first priority of trading is to understand the rules and confirm the odds, not to rush into placing bets.

The definition of speculation is: **only taking risks when the odds are tilted in your favor**;

Gambling is: **blindly taking risks under uncertainty**.

This is not about "high risk, high reward" logic. A truly professional trader only acts when probability is on their side. Once the edge disappears, they exit immediately. When the stop loss is triggered, they execute it — no hesitation, no averaging down on losses. This is the core of the "Alligator Principle."

When an alligator bites your leg, the best choice is not to struggle, but to sever the leg to survive.

In trading, this means: when price hits your stop loss, leave the market immediately.

Draw a trendline — as long as price hasn't broken below it, remain patient; once it breaks, exit.

If price makes a new high and immediately falls back (the 2B rule), this is often a "false breakout," meaning the market is clearing out stop orders, and you should exit quickly.

If you are uncertain, do not trade. Not making that money will not change your survival situation.

The exit rules here are vague — in fact, the clearest exit method comes from price action itself. Price action trading does include advanced techniques like "scaling in to break even," but for beginners, these methods amplify risk. The more robust choice is:

Cut losses quickly, exit proactively, prioritize risk management.

This is the embodiment of the Alligator Principle in live trading.

The magnitude and duration of trends roughly follow a normal distribution. In other words, trends also have a "life cycle."

Just as insurance actuaries assess risk, traders can judge the probability of a trend continuing based on how long it has already run and how far it has moved.

When a trend has exceeded the historical average magnitude or the median duration, it enters its "elderly stage."

At this point, continuing to hold heavy positions with the trend is statistically dangerous. This thinking provides an objective basis for "when to reverse," rather than relying on intuition.

In price action, this is highly consistent with the concept of a "sell climax" or "buy climax."

> A trend bar is simultaneously a breakout and a climax move.
>
> If it occurs early in the trend = breakout; if it occurs after the trend has already run for 20+ bars = potential sell (or buy) climax.

The 2B rule is a classic tool for capturing this momentum failure, and it is one of the clearest and most practical reversal signals in the book.

When price makes a new high (or new low) but fails to continue and quickly falls back within the previous high (or low), the trend is often about to reverse.

This is essentially the "final flag" in price action.

This process of "probe failure" is fundamentally the result of floor traders clearing out stop orders.

Once successfully identified, the stop loss can be very tight, while the potential profit space is large, with risk-reward ratios often better than 1:5.

> Typical signals of a final flag:
>
> Appears after 20+ bars of an uptrend or downtrend.
>
> The pattern is a sideways trading range where the market reaches a brief consensus.
>
> Located near obvious resistance or support: the end of a bull trend often hugs the channel top; the end of a bear trend often hugs the channel bottom or the measured move target.
>
> Accompanied by multiple buy climaxes or sell climaxes, with significantly exhausted momentum.
>
> After breaking out of the range, price is quickly pulled back, indicating the market has lost interest in the original direction.

On the macro level, traders need not fight against policy.

Governments set the rules, and the Federal Reserve can create booms or even bubbles through credit expansion.

The trader's job is not to judge right or wrong, but to follow the trend and leave the market before the bubble bursts. This aligns with Soros's reflexivity theory.

So-called bubbles, in price action terms, often manifest as consecutive buy or sell climaxes.

Only trade a few high-quality setups — this is better than frequent trading.

1-2-3: Used to confirm trend reversals, not to guess tops or bottoms.

1-2-3: Trendline break, test failure, neckline confirmation.

1-2-3 failure: Exit when point 2 is breached.

Every small loss must be bearable, so the systematic edge can compound over time.
