# Trading Book Series: 03 "Technical Analysis of Stock Trends"

Chart patterns are essentially geometric projections of human psychology onto the price axis.

Head and shoulders, triangles, double tops and double bottoms — these technical patterns are not accidental lines piled up from noise. They are the visualized results of behavioral reactions such as fear, greed, hesitation, wishful thinking, and panic, acting repeatedly upon one another. The market gives you only one variable: price. But behind price is a group of people making similar choices under the same pressure.

If Freud attempted to map human psychology, then Edwards and Magee mapped the emotions of the market. Both point to the same fact: price patterns have memory, and group behavior follows paths.

This also directly challenges two extreme views: "fundamentals determine everything" and "markets are just a random walk." Fundamentals change, narratives shift, and sectors rotate, but human reactions at critical moments have never changed. That is why you see the 1929 crash and the 2000 internet bubble — despite completely different backgrounds — exhibiting strikingly similar chart structures. Because what changes is the story; what stays the same is human nature.

> **The Essence of Patterns: The Number of Failures**
>
> A pattern is not a graphic — the graphic is merely the manifestation of a contest.
>
> The essence of a pattern is the number of times the opposing side has failed.
>
> The more times one side fails, the higher the win rate for the other side. The graphic is simply a tool that allows you to quickly identify this "accumulation of failures."

If you are a trend follower, do not base your trades on subjective predictions like "I think the top is near." A more stable approach is: let the market decide when you exit. What you should do is hold with the trend, let profits run, and use a mechanical trailing stop to isolate your emotions. You don't need to guess the top — you just need to follow until the trend fails and the rules take you out.

But that is still not enough. Active exits based on price action are usually superior to passive stop losses. In other words, you are not merely waiting for your stop to be hit. Instead, you continuously read price action: once a trendline is broken, a structural reversal appears, or momentum fades, you prioritize active position reduction or exit. The stop loss is the bottom line, not the only exit.

Regarding breakouts, volume remains a key filter in many scenarios. Especially when a bottom pattern breaks upward (such as a head and shoulders bottom), if there is no "significant volume increase," the probability of a false breakout rises noticeably. Conversely, the initial decline from a top often does not require equivalent volume — price can fall on its own once buying dries up. This is why "watching price only" works in some phases but distorts in others: what you see is the result, not necessarily the force driving the result.

The problem is that traditional case studies relied on centralized exchange volume (such as NYSE). Now, a large portion of trading volume flows to dark pools and off-exchange venues, and on-screen exchange volume no longer fully represents real supply and demand. If you mechanically apply old-era volume-price rules, you risk misreading "data gaps" as "market signals." Al Brooks' view is that you do not need to watch volume.

Mechanical systems follow the same logic. Rules-based systems like the Turtle system can be very effective in specific environments. But markets evolve, participants adapt, and edges get compressed. The value of human analysis lies not in being faster than machines, but in identifying market states: is the market in a trend or a trading range? First determine the state, then select the tool — otherwise your win rate will be dragged down by a single system.

On exits, pattern-based measured targets (such as the equal-distance projection from head to neckline in a head and shoulders top) can be retained, but they are better used as reference coordinates rather than hard endpoints. What truly determines exit quality is usually structural change itself: trendline violation, reversal signal appearance, price action no longer supporting the original thesis. In many cases, price action-based measured moves (MM) are more aligned with actual market rhythm than static target levels.

Additionally, the head and shoulders top is called a "reliable major reversal pattern," but this claim needs to be discounted in live trading. Many so-called head and shoulders patterns are essentially just segments of fluctuation within a larger trading range, not the end of a trend. If you treat every head and shoulders as a reversal, you will often be worn down by repeated whipsaws in a range.

Finally, you need to place this book in its proper context: it is more like a foundational text for the language of technical analysis, not a plug-and-play trade execution manual. It provides you with a naming system and an observation framework, but it still has obvious shortcomings in statistical validation: heavy reliance on visual identification, potentially cherry-picked samples, lack of large-scale backtesting, and unavoidable survivorship bias.

It raised important concepts, but the next step you need to take is to turn these concepts into rules that can be tested, reviewed, and executed.

**Markets Have Trends**

This book was born during the establishment of the technical analysis language system (1930s–1950s).

The core question it sought to answer was: can markets be understood through charts?

It does not directly answer whether you should go long on today's bar.

You need to place it back in its original context. The Edwards and Magee framework came from the 1929–1950 era: hand-drawn charts, no quantitative methods, no electronic order matching, no high-frequency trading, and no mature intraday futures systems. It is more like an "observation and naming system" rather than a modern execution engine.

For most people, a reasonable learning path is actually a layered progression:

First experience the skepticism of "random walk," then confirm "whether technical analysis has validity," then build the language of patterns and trend concepts, and finally enter price action and execution systems. If you skip the earlier layers, the later stages are usually very painful.

Therefore, the true value of this book is not to give you direct trading profits, but to give you a technical analysis dictionary. Many terms that seem like common sense today were systematically disseminated from here: Head and Shoulders, Triangle, Rectangle, Support/Resistance, Breakout. It defined the "vocabulary" you use to describe the market.

It also established several foundational philosophies of trend trading: do not predict the top, follow the trend, exit with stop losses, and verify with volume. Later, Turtle trading, CTA strategies, and quantitative trend strategies all essentially inherited this set of ideas — they just algorithmized and institutionalized them.

But if you are already studying Brooks' price action, you will naturally find it "shallow." The reason is not that it is wrong, but that the level of observation is different. Price action studies the "grammar" and "syntax" of the market: why the same structure works or fails in different contexts. Edwards & Magee primarily teaches you to identify "words."

In terms of the trading learning hierarchy, you can understand it as follows:

Level 1 - Does the Market Have Structure

Dow Theory / Edwards & Magee / Wyckoff

Level 2 - Pattern and Trend Trading

Livermore / Turtle / CTA

Level 3 - Price Action

Al Brooks

Therefore, this book is most suitable for three types of people:

Those who are new to technical analysis, those who do not understand trends, and those who do not believe in charts.

If your goal is to develop executable trading ability as quickly as possible, you will ultimately need to move toward price action and execution systems.
