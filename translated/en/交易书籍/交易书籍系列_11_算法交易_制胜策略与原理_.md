# Trading Book Series: 11 "Algorithmic Trading: Winning Strategies and Their Rationale"

Backtesting is the process of applying a trading strategy to historical data to simulate its performance, with the purpose of evaluating the strategy's expected returns and risks.

The mathematical essence of mean reversion is: the further price deviates from the mean, the stronger the force pulling it back toward the mean.

Directly trading mean reversion on a single asset is usually not feasible — you need to construct spreads or portfolios.

Even if individual assets do not exhibit mean reversion characteristics, cointegration techniques can be used to combine multiple assets and artificially create mean reversion opportunities.

A strategy that performs well in backtesting may not be profitable in the future, because it overlooks the risks of overfitting and regime changes.

For example, mistakenly using future information that was not available at the time during backtesting (such as using the day's closing price to make decisions before the close).

Just because something is mean-reverting doesn't mean it's tradable. If the reversion takes too long, trading opportunities are scarce and capital is tied up for extended periods, making it potentially unprofitable.

For mean reversion strategies, stop losses usually reduce backtesting performance, but they can prevent the catastrophic damage caused by black swan events or market regime shifts.

Survivorship bias is most harmful to long-only mean reversion strategies, because the strategy may buy stocks that ultimately go bankrupt and drop to zero.

Momentum strategies profit from price continuation and focus on trend persistence and decay.

Momentum strategies have the characteristic of limited losses and unlimited profits, while mean reversion strategies may face enormous risks during extreme events.

The causes of momentum include persistence of futures roll returns, delayed information dissemination, passive fund trading, and market microstructure effects.

Opening gaps: For futures and forex, opening gaps are often triggered by stop loss orders, which then generate further momentum that can be traded in the direction of the move.

For the post-earnings announcement drift strategy, there is no need to over-interpret earnings report content. Simply go long at the open after the announcement if price gaps significantly higher, or go short if it gaps significantly lower, and exit at the close to capture intraday momentum while avoiding overnight risk.

Mean reversion and momentum are two sets of tools for different market conditions — the key lies in identifying when to switch.

The Kelly formula provides the theoretical leverage to maximize growth rate, but in practice, half-Kelly should be used to account for parameter estimation errors and non-normal distribution risks.

Simple linear models are more reliable than complex nonlinear models because they have fewer parameters and are less prone to overfitting.

Algorithmic trading achieves repeatable trading through rule-based signals, programmatic execution, and risk control — it is not just automated order placement.

Algorithmic trading should follow the scientific research process: formulate hypotheses based on market inefficiencies, then build models and conduct out-of-sample validation, rather than blindly mining data.

The most dangerous illusion in algorithmic trading is believing that the smarter the model, the more stable live trading will be. Long-term results are more determined by system discipline than by signal novelty.

During the validation phase, priority should be given to addressing look-ahead bias, data-snooping bias, sample contamination, and specification drift. During the execution phase, distinguish between mean reversion and momentum strategies. During the risk control phase, set position sizing, stop losses, drawdown thresholds, and shutdown rules as hard standards.

Backtest curves can be artificially inflated by sample selection, parameter degrees of freedom, feature screening, and cost assumptions. Without out-of-sample validation, they are unlikely to withstand real trading friction.

A more robust approach is to first complete data governance and bias screening, then proceed to strategy construction and execution stress testing, and finally unify constraints through risk management.

Trading psychology problems often stem from process problems. Behavioral constraints, anomaly responses, and review mechanisms should be institutionalized.
