# Trading Book Series: 02 "Reminiscences of a Stock Operator"

"Reminiscences of a Stock Operator" — this is a bestselling book, and some of its views and concepts are consistent with Price Action. Price Action explains clearly how to execute specific trades.

Exiting a bull (long) position too early for profit is a mistake, because what you lose is your position, and the position is your only tool for capturing large swing trade profits. Price Action solves this problem through actively exiting and then re-entering.

One should not predict direction when the market is in a trading range, but rather wait for price to break through a key point (such as 120 or 130), and only trade with trend after the path of least resistance has been established. So what constitutes a successful breakout?

> For any breakout of a resistance level, the minimum requirement is one bull (long) bar, with a body at least average in size, closing near its high, and with a closing price above the high of the previous bar.

"Buy high, sell higher" — the idea is that when a stock reaches a new high (such as breaking through the round numbers of 100 or 200), it is often the safest time to buy, not to sell. Without a definition of a sell climax scenario, one risks buying at the very top.

> Many traders fall into a misconception when the market makes a new high — they believe that since there is no resistance above, the price can "skyrocket." This thinking is wrong. In fact, every new high inevitably comes with a retest by the market. That is the market testing the determination of the bulls (longs), to see whether they can continue to buy and continue to push the price higher.

The speculator's main enemy is internal, not external — the inversion of hope and fear. The average person, when losing, is full of hope, hoping to break even; when profiting, is full of fear, fearing the profit will be given back. A successful speculator must reverse this instinct: when losing, one should feel fear, because losses can expand indefinitely; when profiting, one should feel hope, because profits can continue to grow.

Livermore repeatedly emphasized never listening to tips, calling it the shortcut to a trader's destruction. Yet in the book, he suffered severe losses multiple times from listening to others — for example, listening to Ed Harding at Saratoga and selling Union Pacific, which cost him $40,000 in missed profits, and listening to "Cotton King" Percy Thomas to go long cotton, which led to losses of several million dollars. This contradiction reveals that even the most elite traders find it difficult to completely overcome human weaknesses, such as friendship, gratitude, or blind admiration for others. The most powerful evidence lies in the fact that every time he violated his own principles, he suffered losses.

The early Livermore was a pure tape reader, only watching price fluctuations; but later he emphasized that one must study general conditions to make big money. He illustrated this with his case of shorting the market in 1906 — he was right about the fundamentals, but went bankrupt because his entry timing was wrong. The conclusion is that fundamentals determine direction, while technicals determine timing.

Professional speculation can be divided into multiple stages. First is the trend identification stage, which requires determining whether the overall market is in a bull or bear trend. In Price Action, determining the Always In direction first is more precise than this.

Second is the probing and position-building stage, which requires testing through probe trades with small positions. Only if the first trade is profitable should one proceed with the second trade, using a pyramid scaling in strategy — only adding to position when the price rises, never averaging down on losses. At the same time, one should watch for breakouts of key round numbers as important buy points. Placing orders at breakout points and gaps (gap), and using retests to add to positions is even better.

During the holding and management stage, once position building is complete and the market moves as expected, one should ignore minor fluctuations and hold firmly until the overall trend changes. If a trade shows a loss at the outset, one should immediately implement a stop loss — for example, not letting losses exceed 10% of capital or setting a clear point limit. This is too rough and completely unexecutable; Price Action explains it very clearly and is actionable.

During the exit and distribution stage, do not try to sell at the very top, but begin selling in batches while the market still has strong buying power. When the market stops rising on heavy volume, or when the leading stocks behave abnormally, one should consider exiting. This is a vague description; there are standard actions for exiting.

One problem with this book is the attribution error caused by overconfidence — when making money, he attributes it to "studying general conditions"; when losing money (such as with cotton), he attributes it to "listening to others" or "violating his own rules," rather than flaws in the system itself.
