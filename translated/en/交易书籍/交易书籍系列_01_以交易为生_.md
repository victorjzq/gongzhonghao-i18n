# Trading Book Series: 01 "Trading for a Living"

We have already written a great deal of content on Price Action — so much that it is already sufficient for actual trading. Going forward, the focus is on refining and deepening the existing framework. What is truly needed at this stage is not more content, but better questions.

Therefore, I am starting a new series: **Trading Book Series**

The core idea of this series is to go through all the important trading-related books on the market and see if there are any points worth learning from.

The biggest difference of this blog lies in its writing standard:

**It is not written for the readers, but written because it is useful to myself.**

I exist as an explorer, walking about 6–18 months ahead of my audience, continuously and publicly demonstrating how I understand trading and form my judgments.

As long as the content is valuable to my own trading understanding and decision-making, even if the articles are long, not easy to read, and more like research notes or reading journals, they are still worth writing.

At the same time, as trading experience and writing experience grow, standards keep rising, and the amount of content worth writing keeps decreasing.

"Trading for a Living" — this book leans more toward trading psychology. For my current stage, it no longer offers substantial help, but here are some interesting viewpoints.

Losing is a form of psychological addiction, not just bad luck. Conventional wisdom holds that trading failure is due to lack of knowledge, but many traders subconsciously not only want to win — they are addicted to the emotional swings (dopamine release) during the trading process.

Admitting powerlessness over the market (similar to the principle of AA — Alcoholics Anonymous): only by letting go of the subconscious need to "get a thrill from trading" can one trade calmly, as if running a business.

The Triple Screen system: using the longer timeframe to define the trend, and the intermediate timeframe to capture pullbacks.

> The pattern structure on the larger timeframe determines the support and resistance levels, as well as the corresponding breakout probabilities. We should follow the side with higher probability and wait for entry conditions on the smaller timeframe. This is the essence of "follow the larger timeframe, enter against the smaller timeframe."

The opening price can be seen as the consensus of amateur players, and the closing price can be seen as the consensus of professionals. The moving average (MA) can be seen as the "average consensus" of value.

Humans instinctively pursue certainty and a sense of control, hoping to predict the future, but the market is inherently uncertain and uncontrollable. Therefore, one must abandon Forecasting and shift to Managing. Forecasters often go bankrupt from a single major mistake, while managers survive through money management (e.g., the 2% rule) by leveraging long-term probabilistic edges.

> The vast majority of the time, trading is ambiguous and unclear. The win rate of any trade falls between 40% and 60%.

Admitting powerlessness — tell yourself every morning: "I am a loser (meaning someone with a tendency to lose), but I will strictly follow discipline."
