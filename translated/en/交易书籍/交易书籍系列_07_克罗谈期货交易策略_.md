# Trading Book Series: 07 "Kroll on Futures Trading Strategy"

From "Understanding the Trend" to "Getting the Execution Right"

Your biggest opponent in the futures market is not the price action — it's yourself.

What truly separates you from the majority is not how well you can predict, but whether you can execute an effective strategy consistently, steadily, and without deviation over the long term.

Only act when the market has already shown a strong trend or is building one; exit when the direction is wrong, and stay on the sidelines when the structure is unclear.

In complex markets, those who ultimately survive are often not the smartest, but the most disciplined, the most pragmatic, and those who can keep their system simple.

Predicting trends is difficult — even experts are often wrong.

So the focus should not be on "guessing the next move" but on "following the major trend that has already developed."

Long-term weekly and monthly charts are the core tools for major trend traders. Watch the weekly chart to see the true path of least resistance.

Your opportunity is not in every fluctuation, but at key nodes within the major trend: pullback stabilization, bounce confirmation, or a valid breakout of support/resistance levels.

Don't trade based on news.

Price often moves first, and the news comes later. When you see bullish news, capital has usually already been deployed; when you see media consensus, it's often no longer a low-risk entry point.

Price is the voting result; news is often just the after-the-fact explanation.

When the fundamental narrative conflicts with the technical picture, prioritize respecting the price structure.

Everyone can find a system that "looks good," but a system is just a tool.

Its profit ceiling depends on the user's patience and discipline. No system works perfectly in all market phases.

What you should do is not constantly switch systems, but choose one that suits your personality, has been tested, and has clear rules — then execute every signal 100%.

> You need to find the style that suits you. Some people prefer short-term trading, others prefer medium- to long-term; some like breakouts, others like reversals. You must keep experimenting to find the approach that best fits your personality and schedule. Only then can you stick with it.

Gap anxiety — you understand the trend in the morning, and by the afternoon when it starts choppping, you can't sit still.

"Not trading" gets misread as "missing an opportunity," so you actively create trades to fill the anxiety.

When a profitable position pulls back, if you haven't written down in advance "where to scale out and where to exit completely," you'll end up in a psychological battle every single time.

This kind of high-frequency subjective decision-making rapidly depletes discipline, eventually becoming: exit on small fluctuations, hold through real reversals.

These two layers of traps amplify each other: frequent random trades make you more hesitant during the major trend; premature profit-taking makes you overly aggressive the next time.

Extended choppy periods erode patience, which in turn alters the original strategy.

Seasonal patterns should only be used as confirmation, not as the primary basis. For example, after the "February pause," a March close that breaks above the January-February high can be considered strong confirmation, but it cannot be the sole reason for blindly opening a position.

Key support/resistance bounces after a pullback in the major trend. A closing price that effectively breaks out of a trading range (not just an intraday spike). However, a close alone isn't enough — Price Action emphasizes good follow-through.

Kroll's trend confirmation is interesting — at least three consecutive weeks in the same direction. In Price Action, this corresponds to three consecutive trend bars, indicating strong directionality. Rose uses the metaphor of "3 White Walkers." In Game of Thrones, when the White Walkers arrive, you need to avoid their edge and must not fight against them.
