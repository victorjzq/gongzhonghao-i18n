Today, let's step outside trading and explore the boundless world of literature, history, and philosophical reflection.

Does reading history really make you wiser?

Many people read history books and, over time, develop a strange side effect.

Rather than becoming more clear-headed, they become more anxious and more agitated. They accumulate a lot of historical knowledge, yet very little of it can be applied to solve real-life problems.

We think we are learning lessons from history, but in reality, we are absorbing a kind of narrative toxicity.

Why?

Because the basic sentence structure of most history books goes like this: In a certain year, **someone did something.**

**This type of narrative is direct and information-dense, but it has a hidden side effect. It cuts out the most essential part of doing anything in the real world: "preparation."**

As a result, readers of history easily develop an illusion — that history is composed of moments when someone flipped a switch. Wars seem to start when someone slams a table in anger. Policies seem to emerge from a flash of inspiration. Promotions and demotions of officials seem to hinge on a single remark. History books seem to suggest that as long as you have an identity, a position, and an idea, you can push the world in whatever direction you want.

But reality is the exact opposite. What we see in history books is usually just the one centimeter of the iceberg above the waterline. What truly determines success or failure is the nine hundred and ninety-nine centimeters below the surface — including preparation, networking, probing, alliance-building, resource allocation, risk hedging, rhetorical groundwork, public opinion cultivation, and finding cracks in institutional frameworks. And the part that is hardest to write into any history book: the long-term patience and endurance of one person or a group of people.

History books don't deliberately omit these things — they simply cannot contain them by nature. They must describe what happened within limited space, rather than documenting in detail how things came into being. This in itself is not a problem. The problem is that we, as readers, often mistake "what happened" for "how it was generated."

This leads to three very real types of harm.

The first type of harm is **misjudging time.**

History books leap forward in units of years or months, compressing long-cycle undertakings into single nodes. This subconsciously instills a dangerous belief in readers: big things should be done quickly, major trends can be reversed in the short term, and results should be immediately visible. But if you've ever done anything even slightly complex, you know that from concept to mobilization, from mobilization to pilot testing, from pilot to replication, from replication to steady state — each step takes time. This time is not procrastination; it is the essential process of building a system. The more you read these leaping history books, the more you come to resent this building process, and the more likely you are to mistake preparation for hesitation. So you start feeling anxious: Why is everyone else jumping straight into action while I'm still preparing? You start blaming yourself: Am I not decisive enough? You start accelerating: Forget the preparation, just charge ahead!

This is the first type of harm that history books invisibly inflict — **making you resent the rhythm of the real world.**

The second type of harm is **misjudging cause and effect.**

History books like to string events together through characters, which compresses structural forces into individual will. Fiscal constraints, demographic structures, technological boundaries, institutional friction, geopolitical security, organizational capacity — these truly fundamental variables are often rendered invisible within the sentence structure of "someone did something." As a result, readers of history easily learn a flawed model of action: when faced with a problem, they first imitate postures — being decisive, being ruthless, being patient, being cunning. Instead of first modeling based on multidimensional conditions: What resources do I have? What resources does my counterpart have? What are the incentives on both sides? What are my path dependencies? Where is my tolerable risk exposure? When you substitute posture for modeling, you develop a typical "history-reader's approach to action": enthusiastic about making judgments, skilled at picking sides, fond of talking about the big picture — but hollow when it comes to execution. You feel you "understand the grand trend," yet you don't know who to contact next, what to prepare, what to pilot, or how to break down uncertainty into manageable sub-problems.

This is the second type of harm that history books inflict — **making you addicted to explaining, rather than skilled at building.**

The third type of harm is **misjudging the path to getting things done.**

History books are inherently a "survivor's catalog." What gets written in tends to be what succeeded, what had great impact, what ultimately won. Meanwhile, hundreds or thousands of failed attempts, aborted alliances, deals that never closed, and plans that were crushed before they even launched — none of these get recorded in detail. This leads readers of history to believe that the path to success is inherently clear, clean, and replicable. But in reality, success looks more like a pile of dirty work. You have to make countless probes, invest in many seemingly useless favors. You have to tolerate phases that "appear to show no progress." You have to accumulate credibility, resources, and mobilization capacity where others cannot see. You have to design backup plans and exit strategies for when things fail. You have to push forward with imperfect information, rather than waiting until "everything is in place." These are the truly transferable skills, but they don't fit neatly into history books because they're too messy. They're not dramatic enough, not climactic enough to make a good story.

This is the third type of harm that history books inflict — **making you fall in love with romantic narratives and despise the mechanical process.**

It's not that reading history is useless. The problem is that most people read history as if it were a life instruction manual.

History books should be treated as an event index. They tell you "something happened there," but they are not responsible for telling you "how that thing was produced."

In other words: history books record the surface of results; what reality requires is the engineering of process.

So how should you read history?

You need to treat "in such-and-such year, so-and-so did such-and-such" as a clue, not as a conclusion. Every time you encounter a historical event, force yourself to reconstruct the underwater portion that was edited out. You can ask five questions:

1) Before this event happened, what resources did he need? Where did those resources come from?

2) What allies did he need? What were the allies' incentives? How did he pay the price for their cooperation?

3) Where was the resistance from opponents? How did he break down that resistance and advance in stages?

4) If it failed, what was his stop loss? Were there alternative paths?

5) What was the true timeline of this event? Which phases were the critical nodes?

You'll find that once you read this way, history books are no longer a collection of events that suddenly happened. They become engineering blueprints of long-term preparation. What you read is no longer who was wise and who was foolish, but rather organization, resources, incentives, constraints, path dependencies, and how people carved out feasible space within constraints.

This is the true meaning of "using history as a mirror." It's not about imitating the actions of historical figures, but about learning how, under the conditions of their time, they turned the uncontrollable into the controllable.

**History books record what happened; life depends on how things are generated.**

**Those who mistake "what happened" for "how it was generated" will misjudge time, misjudge costs, and misjudge causation — leading to rushed action, shallow reflection, and intense frustration.**

**But those who treat "what happened" merely as clues can read history as engineering, creating compound returns in their own lives.**

The real value of reading history is not turning you into someone who is good at lecturing, but turning you into someone who respects the structure of reality, who excels at preparation, and who understands that "slow is fast" — and therefore actually gets things done in the real world.

Because the world has never been changed by a single sentence. The world is changed by countless inconspicuous acts of preparation.
