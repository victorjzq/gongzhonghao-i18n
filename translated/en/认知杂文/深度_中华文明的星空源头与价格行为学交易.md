Reading *On Civilization* by Professor Feng Shi, the founder of Chinese astroarchaeology, gave me precisely this kind of experience.

**Placing the stargazing of Chinese ancestors thousands of years ago alongside Price Action trading in technical analysis seems like a fantastical dialogue spanning time and space. Yet, upon closer examination of Professor Feng Shi's archaeological interpretation of the origins of Chinese civilization and the system established by Al Brooks, we are astonished to find that the two share extremely profound inner connections in epistemology, concepts of time and space, the construction of order, and faith in underlying patterns. Whether gazing up at the vast starry sky or staring at a flickering candlestick chart, the foundational philosophical wisdom that humanity displays when confronting the immense, unknown, and powerful forces of nature and markets is fundamentally the same.**

**The starting point of all wisdom originates from pure, reverential observation of the objective world — what the ancient Chinese summarized as "investigating things to extend knowledge" (格物致知).**

Professor Feng Shi pointed out that Chinese civilization established a highly mature knowledge system as early as eight thousand years ago. This system was not born from the imagination of the ancients but was derived from years upon years of actual observation of natural celestial phenomena (such as the sun and the Dragon constellation). This empirical spirit based on "what you see is what you get" is identical in essence to what Al Brooks repeatedly emphasizes: **"Price is truth."**

**Brooks believes that traders must ignore the expert commentary on television, news events, and all kinds of fundamental noise, because those often lead to misdirection. He advocates that traders should observe charts bar by bar, just as the ancients observed celestial phenomena.** Because every price movement on a chart is an indelible imprint left by the real buying and selling actions of institutional capital, honestly reflecting the objective patterns of human behavior — patterns of price behavior determined by human genes that have remained unchanged for decades or even longer. The ancients guided their farming through stargazing to ensure survival, while modern traders follow the footprints of institutional capital through chart reading to seek profit. Both are pursuing the "true knowledge" of survival through "investigating things to extend knowledge" (格物致知).

**After establishing the method of observation, the question of how to define where and when things occur constitutes the high degree of alignment between the two in their concepts of time and space — namely, "space determines time."**

Professor Feng Shi profoundly pointed out that the traditional Chinese concept of time and space holds that space determines time. Through erecting gnomons and measuring shadows, the ancients had to first "distinguish directions and establish positions" — determining the five fundamental spatial orientations of east, west, south, north, and center (such as establishing the meridian). Only when the shadow of the sun was cast onto a specific spatial position could they then determine what time it was (such as noon or the summer solstice). Without established spatial coordinates, time lost its meaning. This ancient philosophy finds its perfect validation in Price Action.

**In trading, time (meaning when to enter) is often determined by space (the specific price level reached). Brooks emphasizes that the market is attracted to "magnets" such as support, resistance, trendlines, channel lines, and measured move targets.** Only when price (like the shadow) touches these key spatial coordinates (like the gnomon for shadow measurement) and produces specific bar patterns (such as reversal patterns) does the timing for a trade truly mature. If price has not reached a meaningful spatial position, the trader exists in a state of uncertain void, and acting blindly at such a time carries enormous risk.

**When the ancients and traders discovered patterns within a defined framework of time and space, what followed was a steadfast faith in those patterns.**

Professor Feng Shi's research demonstrates that the core meaning of "De" (virtue) in Chinese civilization is "Xin" (trustworthiness/faithfulness), and this moral concept originates directly from astronomy. The ancients discovered that no matter what happened in the human world, the alternation of the four seasons and the rotation of the stars always arrived on schedule — time was always faithful to its promise. Because "the Way of Heaven is trustworthy," human society must also establish trust in these irreversible objective patterns.

**In the trading market, this "faithfulness" manifests as faith in market inertia and mathematical probability. Brooks points out that the market possesses powerful inertia — once a trend forms, it has an extremely strong tendency to perpetuate itself, and approximately 80% of attempts to reverse the trend will fail. Likewise, within a trading range, 80% of breakout attempts also fail.** Successful traders must, like the ancients who revered the Way of Heaven, build faith in market probabilities (i.e., the Trader's Equation). When the market is in a strong trend, traders must believe that trend-following trades have an extremely high success rate and must not fight against the pattern. They understand that while each individual trade may be random at the micro level, under the macro probability distribution, as long as they follow the objective patterns on the chart, they can achieve positive results over the long term — just as the cycle of the four seasons repeats.

**In order to survive for the long term in a human society filled with infinite desires and an ever-changing trading market, both paths converge on the ultimate road of constructing order and restraining desire.**

Professor Feng Shi proposed that the original meaning of "Kunlun" does not refer to a geographical mountain range, but rather to the sun moving in an orderly, sequential manner ("the sun travels in orderly sequence"). To prevent humanity's infinite desires from causing destructive competition over finite resources, Chinese civilization evolved the system of "Li" (ritual propriety), using order to restrain desire and thereby achieve the "longevity" of life and civilization.

**The trading philosophy of Price Action is likewise a modern interpretation of this very idea. The market is extremely cruel. Individual greed and fear are limitless, but the capital in one's account is finite.** Brooks emphasizes that the market constantly alternates between trends (unilateral imbalances) and trading ranges (searching for new value equilibrium) — this in itself is the market's process of establishing order amid chaos. Facing the market, traders must use strict trading rules (the "Li" — ritual propriety — of trading) to restrain their ever-present desire to counter-trend pick tops and bottoms, or the impulse to gamble with heavy positions. Only by eliminating subjective desires from the mind, synchronizing one's own operational rhythm with the objective rhythm of the market — "the unity of heaven and humanity" (天人合一) — and always placing the safety of capital and long-term survival as the top priority, can one survive in this cruel market.

**Whether they are the Chinese ancestors who spent eight thousand years observing stars and measuring shadows, or the modern traders sitting before multiple screens analyzing candlestick charts today, the core proposition they face is the same.** The traditional cosmology of Chinese civilization and modern Price Action both teach people: let go of subjective attachments, recognize the objective structure of how things operate through meticulous "investigating things to extend knowledge" (格物致知), wait for the right moment to act within clearly defined spatial coordinates, maintain steadfast faith in high-probability patterns, and use strict discipline to restrain greed. This wisdom of following the Way of Heaven and knowing when to stop so as to avoid danger is not only the hidden code behind Chinese civilization's unbroken continuity of thousands of years, but also the ultimate mental framework for every trader seeking sustained profitability in the market.
