# Price Action: How to Understand Pattern Analysis

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 239th article, and you should also learn Price Action.

Every bar is the current market node, and each new node is always a reaction to the preceding price action. Traders use this correlation to evaluate the validity of prior price action. Is the previous event still valid? And does the new event withstand verification — will it produce a continuation in the same direction, or will it produce a reversal?

Market behavior is a reaction to prior price action and may either confirm or negate the preceding trend. This is the fundamental principle behind almost all analysis.

Every pattern failure triggers a market reaction, and every success is the exact opposite — the market uses new bars to confirm the success.

Market reactions boil down to two types: acceptance or rejection.

Acceptance means the price will continue to develop in its original direction, maintaining the existing trend. Rejection, on the other hand, often signals a trend reversal, and this counter-move is especially effective during trend reversals.

For example, when a bullish wedge pattern appears and is fully formed — even before a signal bar has appeared, all the legs are complete, the pattern is well-shaped, symmetrical, structurally complete, and an ideal setup for Price Action trading — the market is already in breakout mode.

If the market accepts the influence of this event, the price should continue to develop in the direction indicated by the event, meaning the price movement should align with the probability expectation — the probability inherent in the event itself.

A bull flag has a 25% probability of breaking out downward and a 75% probability of breaking out upward.

When a pattern shows reversal probability but the chart does not produce a reversal signal, it indicates the market has rejected the leading pattern. Although the pattern suggests a reversal should occur, the price instead continues its upward move — this type of scenario belongs to low-probability events in statistics. The most interesting situation in event response is when a double failure occurs — this is the so-called "failure of a failure."
