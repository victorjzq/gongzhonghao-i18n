# Price Action: Counting Legs

Leg counting is a core concept in Price Action,
and it is one of the key skills that every trader should understand and develop.

A leg is a minor trend that breaks a trendline.
We only use this term when at least two legs appear on the chart.
If there is only one leg on the chart, it is usually considered a swing or a trend.

A leg can be a sub-trend within a larger trend,
such as a pullback, or a swing within a trading range.
It can also be a continuation move in the direction of the trend,
that is, the thrust that occurs between two pullbacks.

A trend is Price Action composed of a series of mostly upward or downward bars.
In other words, if most bars are rising,
then we say it is a bull trend;
if most bars are falling,
then it is a bear trend.

A trend has three smaller sub-structures:
swings, legs, and pullbacks.
This is the order from large to small.
A swing is part of a trend, a leg is part of a swing, and a pullback is part of a leg.

The market is fractal,
meaning every price move is a trend on some timeframe.
A small leg on the monthly chart
may consist of thousands of bars on a five-minute chart.
This shows that leg structures across different timeframes are nested within each other.

Because everyone uses different charts,
the leg count will vary from person to person.
Sometimes, one person sees three legs on a 5-minute chart,
another person may see five on a 1-minute chart,
and only one on a 30-minute chart.

So the methods of counting legs are diverse,
but as long as you practice more, identifying them becomes increasingly easier.

As experience grows,
you will learn to judge the number and nature of legs
based on market structure across different timeframes.

The larger the leg,
the greater its impact on the market.

When traders across different timeframes reach consensus on a particular leg,
for example, everyone agrees this is the second upward push within a trading range,
and this judgment is visible across multiple timeframe charts,
then this leg becomes more significant.

The broader the consensus,
the stronger the market's reaction.
The action resulting from this consensus
often produces a significant move,
and this is one of the core purposes of leg counting.

The purpose of leg counting
is to help traders identify minor trends.
And these minor trends
are where we look for trading opportunities.

Pullbacks in the market are essentially minor trends,
that is, legs in the opposite direction.
We often look for trend-following entry opportunities
after one, two, or three counter-trend legs.

For example, a High 2 signal
is a rally that occurs after two counter-trend downward legs,
and it is typically a trend-following buy signal for going long.

A High 3 is a reversal signal after three downward legs,
and it is usually called a wedge bottom,
also a buy signal in a bull trend.

A parabolic wedge is also a common three-leg structure,
where the third leg breaks out beyond the channel line formed by the first two legs.
This structure foreshadows a minor reversal,
after which the trend may continue.

Because the bears in this structure are exhausted,
the market has produced consecutive sell climaxes, and the behavior becomes unsustainable.
But at the same time, because the entire channel is tight,
on higher timeframe charts, it is viewed as a breakout.
Even if the subsequent pullback is deep,
it can still be treated as a bear flag.

In leg counting, the most critical thing is to identify the first breakout leg,
which is the reset point of the market cycle.

The breakout resets the market cycle

Traders expect a second leg up due to the presence of trapped traders

Once you can confirm this is the first breakout leg,
you can look for entry opportunities in the second leg.

The probability of a second leg following the first breakout exceeds 80%, making it the highest win rate structure in trading.

You should also learn to identify late-stage legs,
that is, the third leg or later.
These late-stage legs are more likely to fail, reverse, or enter a trading range.
You should not enter during these late-stage legs,
unless you specialize in counter-trend trading.

The second leg is the most context-dependent leg.
In trending markets, the second leg typically continues with the trend;
but in trading range markets, the second leg may be a trap,
worth fading.

The most important skill you need to develop
is recognizing the first leg and participating actively,
while recognizing the third leg and avoiding participation.
When the market reaches the third leg,
many traders already realize this is the end,
and begin preparing for counter-trend trades.

The more traders in the market agree on the leg count,
the more effective the pattern becomes.

When you see a strong first leg,
do not be tempted by minor reversals to trade in the opposite direction,
but wait for the second leg after the first leg completes,
and look for trend-following entry opportunities.

When the market is in breakout mode,
it is a 50/50 market,
which is a trading range.

One typical trading range pattern is a converging triangle,
also called a symmetrical triangle.

In breakout mode, an upward breakout may occur,
and you can buy at the close of the breakout bar,
holding until the measured move target,
or you can wait until the first leg equals the second leg in magnitude before exiting.

The market sometimes pulls back to the breakout point,
and you can place a limit order at the breakout point,
or you can wait for the market to successfully retest and reverse again before buying,
for example, entering when several consecutive bull bars break through the moving average (MA).

Another common structure is an expanding triangle,
which is both a breakout mode
and can be viewed as a wedge bottom.

There is no shortcut to practicing leg counting.
You can only develop this ability to identify structures
by constantly observing the market and analyzing charts.

Once you master this skill,
your understanding of the market will deepen significantly,
and your trading will become much more precise and efficient.
