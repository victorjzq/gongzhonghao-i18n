# Price Action: An Obvious Signal

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 152nd article, and you should study Price Action too.

An obvious signal is a breakout or a failed breakout. Whether it is a breakout of a trendline, a breakout of a trading range, or a moving average (MA), especially yesterday's high and low, observe the market's reaction at these key support and resistance levels.

If the breakout succeeds, it usually produces a trend move; if the breakout is met with resistance, it may reverse and form a trend in the opposite direction.

You also need to determine whether the current trend is continuing or is failing. If it has failed, is it the beginning of a new trend, or just a pullback? Will there be buying pressure entering at this level?

Swing trades usually begin with a strong signal bar.
For example, a bull swing usually starts with a strong bull bar, with a large body, closing near the high; a bear swing usually starts with a strong bear bar, with a large body, closing near the low.

Some traders, especially swing traders, do not look at the chart after they enter.
They know the trend is in progress, so they hold with confidence, even if there are minor pullbacks.
They do not want to be distracted by every small fluctuation of the bars.

The market produces a breakout with three consecutive bull bars, each closing at or near the high—this is a very strong signal. You can buy at the close of each bar, and you will make money.
But later the market enters a trading range, so if you do not take profit at the high in time, you may give back your profits.
Therefore, if you want to swing trade, you should also learn to observe whether the trend is weakening.
For example, if large upper wicks, small bars, doji bars, and so on begin to appear, that may mean the trend has ended, and you can choose to exit partially or entirely.

As long as the trend remains strong, traders will continue to sell short at the close of any bear bar, or sell short below the low of the prior bar, especially when the bar closes below its midpoint or near the low.
They will continue to do this as long as the trend maintains reasonable strength.
Usually, if a series of strong bear bars appears after the open, it will eventually lead to a measured move.
A common measured move is based on the first group of bars, followed by a pause bar, such as a bull bar.
The expected range from the open of the first bar to the close or low of the last bar in this group, projected downward, is a measured move target—a good profit target.
When the market falls to near this target, there is usually some profit-taking.

Traders should use stop orders to enter in most situations.
To increase the probability of profit, you should wait for strong signals.

An I-I structure, on a smaller time frame, is a triangle.

When you see a bull flag after a reversal, that forms a cup and handle pattern—a cup, then a handle.
Traders will also buy during the rally.
You can also call it a "higher low Major Trend Reversal (MTR)"—that is, after a bear trend, a strong minor reversal appears, followed by a pullback that tests the prior low but does not break below it—this is a higher low.
You can call it a "higher low double bottom," or a "higher low Major Trend Reversal (MTR)."
Traders will buy above this bull bar, or buy above any bull bar that closes at its high.
