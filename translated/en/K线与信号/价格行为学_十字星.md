# Price Action: Doji

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 3 years now. Today is my 265th article, and you should be learning Price Action too.

For any trader, one of the most important questions is being able to determine with relatively high reliability where buyers and sellers are roughly positioned. For example, if you are planning to follow a trend but you judge that the trend will transition into a consolidation within the next few bars, then there is insufficient reason to continue following the trend at that point.

When the market enters a consolidation and forms a trading range, scalpers typically do not stay for long. Once the market begins to move sideways, they usually exit. Therefore, being able to identify when a trading range is beginning to form is crucial for anyone trading on intraday charts, because intraday price action spends most of its time in various forms of trading ranges.

A doji provides a very important piece of information: opposing forces are entering the market. For example, in a bull trend, bears begin to enter the market. Suppose you see a bull leg followed by a doji — the meaning of this bar depends on how much it overlaps with the previous bar; but in common scenarios, it represents a breakout pullback within the same bar. At the same time, it also indicates that there is selling pressure in the market — it is the selling pressure that pushed out the wick, and the price level corresponding to the wick is where the selling pressure appeared. This means that there are likely sellers above this bar.

The appearance of selling pressure means that bears are entering the market, so you have reason to expect that bears will become more active. Typically this bar either gets triggered or it does not, and this is often one of the earliest signals that "a trading range is forming."

Within a trading range, understanding "when a failure will occur" is a critical skill. For example, in a bear trend, a doji appears near a support level and triggers a reversal; location is always key, and analysis must be combined with market context. The typical characteristic of a trading range is: there are buyers below and sellers above.

When wicks and dojis begin to appear frequently, trends are usually constrained and the room for extension gets compressed.

You must identify the type of trend the market is in. If it is a small pullback trend, then the significance of these reversal signals diminishes.

If the day is in a trend or a small pullback trend, these bars with wicks often mean: you may soon have another opportunity to go long. This is because pullbacks in a small pullback trend are usually short in time and shallow in depth. Therefore, being able to anticipate when a pullback will form is a valuable skill — it allows you to mentally prepare yourself in advance to "get ready to buy."

In this type of trend, a better way to enter is to trade when a counter-trend bear bar appears: when a bear bar appears, buy below it; sometimes you do not even need to wait for it to be broken — you can buy at the close. This is because in a small pullback trend, the first one or two bear bars that appear typically cannot push the market down significantly, and price usually begins to resume its upward movement from that bear bar quite quickly.

Furthermore, if you study a large number of small pullback trend examples, you will find that many bear bars ultimately do not get triggered — the market does not break below their lows — which shows that bulls are actively absorbing all the selling pressure.
