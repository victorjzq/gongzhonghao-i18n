# Price Action: The Fractal Nature of the Market

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today marks my 245th article. You should learn Price Action too.

The market exhibits fractal characteristics. All price movements on the current chart actually correspond to movements on higher timeframes, where they display more obvious technical patterns.
In reality, there is nothing overly complex about many concepts, especially in trading. Many profitable setups are quite simple. The more variables you introduce, the more detrimental it actually becomes to your trading. Whether your "knowledge" and "execution" can be highly unified determines whether you can ultimately achieve consistent profitability.

When you trade, you need to constantly switch between macro and micro perspectives in your mind. The micro perspective involves analyzing price movements on smaller timeframes, while the macro perspective involves grasping the overall market structure. This kind of mental training helps you develop critical market intuition.

When you can see through charts across fast and slow timeframes at a glance, you can also anticipate how a pattern will evolve on higher timeframes such as the daily and weekly charts. This way, you develop cross-dimensional, cross-timeframe market intuition, and at that point your analysis and judgment become second nature.

Trading is inherently counterintuitive, requiring you to break conventional thinking patterns and establish unique market expectations—the kind of thinking that often finds little support in everyday life.

But the hardest part of trading lies in its unique feedback mechanism. This feedback mechanism is uncommon and differs from the way humans are accustomed to receiving feedback. It is not directly visible. You have to observe pattern changes on bar / candlestick charts, then analyze the meaning of these signals in your mind to obtain market feedback.
Unlike an electric shock that produces a strong physical reaction, the market does not give you direct physiological stimulation—no detectable energy fluctuations. This makes accumulating practical experience as a trader more challenging than in virtually any other profession.
For this very reason, a trader's growth journey requires years of patient development, stemming from the special nature of market feedback.
