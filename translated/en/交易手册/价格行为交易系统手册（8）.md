# Price Action Trading System Manual (8)

> As for buying at the top of a TR. I actually have some viewers who send me their trade records every day, and I can see them chasing rallies and selling breakdowns in a trading range day after day, as if they've never watched any of my videos.

> Market cycle refers to: is there a trend right now? If there is a trend, is it strong or weak? If it's the strongest spike, then to participate you can only do so with a small position by chasing. If it's a channel, even a relatively tight channel, it's best to buy on pullbacks.

> If there is no trend, or the trend is too weak and it's nearly sideways, then you need to use different methods to trade. If the range is too narrow, you shouldn't trade at all.

These two passages from the instructor are actually quite profound, and contain several critical issues that are also the most common sources of confusion for beginners:

First, how do you determine whether the market has truly entered a trend, and when does the trend begin?

Second, if a trend already exists, how do you assess its strength?

Third, under what circumstances does a pullback stop being a buy point and instead become a signal that the trend logic has failed?

Fourth, how do you identify the top and bottom of a trading range to avoid misreading a range leg as a trend?

These foundational questions are far more important than any seemingly advanced technique, and they form a complete decision chain: **determine whether a trend exists → assess trend strength → identify pullback buy points → determine trend failure / range boundary**.

You can answer these questions directly to test your own learning progress.

**A trend is price moving primarily in one direction — either a spike or a channel.**

### For a trend to begin, there must be trend bars with good follow-through, meaning consecutive bars (Consecutive Bars): 2 medium-sized strong trend bars, or 3 to 5 consecutive small trend bars.

Consecutiveness is more important than the size of any single bar.

#### Or one very large trend bar (surprise bar).

Once the market is determined to be in an Always In state, traders can enter with-trend "for any reason" (market order, buy a pullback, buy a breakout, etc.).

Steps for determining the start of a trend:

**Step 1: Look for a spike**

Is there one massive surprise bar? (Close far beyond the highs/lows of the past 20–30 bars)

Are there 2–3 medium-strength trend bars appearing consecutively?

Are there 4–5 small trend bars appearing consecutively?

**Step 2: Check for follow-through**

After the spike bar, is there a 2nd and 3rd strong trend bar in the same direction?

Are the closing prices of the follow-through bars near their extremes?

Has "bad follow-through" been avoided (opposing bar, long wick, doji)?

**Step 3: Confirm Always In**

If you had to hold a position right now, would you go long or short?

Is the answer clear? (If uncertain, it may be a range.)

**Step 4: Exclude range illusions**

Has the "big rally followed by big selloff" confusion pattern been avoided?

Has a major structural point (Major High/Low) been broken, not just a minor one?

After the breakout, has momentum been maintained without immediately returning to the range?

**If all 4 steps pass, the trend is confirmed.**

Trend strength is assessed by the structure of pullbacks.

---

Once a spike occurs and the first meaningful pullback begins, the channel phase starts. Observing the force of the pullback allows you to assess trend strength.

### Strong trend: pullbacks are small and disorganized (opposition is unorganized)

### Weak trend: pullbacks are large and organized (opposition forms a microchannel / consecutive trend bars)

#### Strong trend pullback characteristics (Tight Channel)

| Characteristic | Specific Behavior |
|---|---|
| **Depth** | Very shallow, usually only 1–3 bars |
| **Gap** | Pullback bar lows remain above the spike point, forming a gap |
| **Body gap** | Even if wicks overlap, there is no overlap between bar bodies |
| **Consecutiveness** | Lacks consecutive large bear bars (in a bull pullback) |
| **Wick** | Pullback bars have lower wicks, showing buying at the lows |
| **Urgency** | Buyers are unwilling to wait for a deep pullback, aggressively buying after just 1 bar |

#### Weak trend pullback characteristics (Broad Channel)

| Characteristic | Specific Behavior |
|---|---|
| **Depth** | Deep (often retracing 50% or more of the prior leg) |
| **Duration** | Long (5–10 bars or more) |
| **Overlap** | Pullback price frequently falls below prior spike point, closing the gap |
| **Two-sided trading** | Pullback structure contains a clear opposing trend (organized) |
| **Microchannel** | The pullback itself forms a microchannel for the opposing side (consecutive advances) |

### A microchannel is a trend without pullbacks, meaning opposing force is extremely strong. The first counter-move reversal usually fails and can evolve into an "endless pullback," ultimately leading to a trend reversal.

### When a counter-trend microchannel appears, a trading range may have formed — do not chase longs at the top.

### Strong trend (tight channel) trading strategy

    ✓ Trade with-trend only
    ✓ Aggressive entries (market order, Buy the Close)
    ✓ Place limit orders to buy on any small pullback
    ✓ Do not wait for a deep pullback (it may never come)
    ✓ Swing trade (hold partial position)
    ✓ High win probability (60%+), suitable for wider stops

#### Weak trend (broad channel) trading strategy

    ✓ Two-sided trading (allow counter-trend scalps)
    ✓ Buy low, sell high (limit order strategy)
      - Bull broad channel: avoid chasing rallies, buy on pullbacks to prior lows or support
      - Bear broad channel: avoid chasing selloffs, sell high on bounces
    ✓ Quick scalps (do not expect extended swing moves)
    ✓ Watch for evolution into a trading range

**Steps for assessing trend strength:**

**Step 1: Observe pullback depth**

Is the pullback shallow (less than half the average bar height)? → Strong trend

Is the pullback deep (retracing 50% or more)? → Weak trend

**Step 2: Check for gaps**

Do pullback bars remain above the spike point (gap present)? → Strong trend

Does the pullback fall below the spike point (gap closed, heavy overlap)? → Weak trend

**Step 3: Assess pullback organization**

Is the pullback disorganized (no consecutive advances)? → Strong trend

Does the pullback form a microchannel or a clear opposing trend? → Weak trend (dangerous)

**Step 4: Count pullback duration**

Is the pullback short (1–3 bars)? → Strong trend

Is the pullback long (5–10 bars or more)? → Weak trend

Has the pullback lasted more than 20 bars? → Has evolved into a range

**Step 5: Observe buyer/seller urgency**

Do buyers aggressively re-enter after just 1 pullback bar? → Strong trend

Do buyers need to wait for a deep pullback before entering? → Weak trend

**Choose strategy based on assessment:**

**3 or more "strong trend" characteristics → aggressive with-trend**

**3 or more "weak trend" characteristics → conservative buy-low sell-high**

**Recommendation for beginners: only trade strong trends, stop trading when the trend weakens, and you don't want to see opposing consecutive trend bars.**

---

When a pullback evolves into behavior that breaks major market structure, the with-trend logic (such as buying pullbacks) is declared invalid.

| Situation | Bull trend invalidation | Bear trend invalidation |
|---|---|---|
| **Key point** | Breaks below major higher low (Major Higher Low) | Breaks above major lower high (Major Lower High) |
| **Definition** | Major higher low = the swing origin that triggered a strong spike and made a new high | Major lower high = the swing origin that triggered a strong decline and made a new low |
| **Result** | Market enters a trading range or turns bearish | Market enters a trading range or turns bullish |
| **Minor point** | Breaking a minor low → trend only weakens (tight → broad), but the bull trend remains valid | Breaking a minor high → trend only weakens, but the bear trend remains valid |

**The pullback turns into an opposing-side dominant trend, changing the Always In state**

Pullback exhibits extreme momentum (e.g., consecutive large bear bars)

The original "pullback" has actually become a new trend

If a pullback lasts **20 bars or more**, the market is no longer in a pullback — it has evolved into a **trading range**.

The market is always either in a trend or in a trading range. When you look at a chart and feel confused about direction, that is a trading range.

#### Very often a trend is just one leg within a range

**A range leg occurs within a range that has been consolidating for a long time (20+ bars), has not broken the major swing high/low, and the powerful move is still contained within the range.**

A strong spike that closes **above the major lower high (Major Lower High)** — if it only tests the prior high but cannot break it effectively, it is still a range.

In a trading range, a very strong second push up often occurs: it looks like the start of a trend, but is actually a trap, followed by a reversal back down.

| Type | Characteristics | Subsequent Move |
|---|---|---|
| **Range leg** | Large bull bar followed by poor bars (doji, bear bar), lacking consecutiveness | Just a bull leg within the range, not the start of a new trend, followed by reversal |
| **True trend** | Consecutive strong trend bars, good follow-through, breaks major structural points | Trend continues, forms new high/new low |

### Avoid chasing rallies and selling breakdowns

**Step 1: Identify whether in a trading range**

Confusion test
- Do you feel confused looking at the chart, unclear on direction?
- Is there a "big rally big selloff" pattern?

Disappointment test
- Is the price action after a strong bar disappointing?
- Does a seemingly strong breakout quickly fail?

20-bar test
- Has the market been consolidating sideways for 20 bars or more?

Two-sided trading test
- Can both bulls and bears make money?
- Is there no clear "Always In" state?

**Step 2: Identify range boundaries**

Top zone
- Connect recent swing highs
- Observe bars with upper wicks (selling pressure)
- Is price repeatedly reversing down from this area?

Bottom zone
- Connect recent swing lows
- Observe bars with lower wicks (buying pressure)
- Is price repeatedly reversing up from this area?

Confirm boundary validity
- Did prior "breakouts" quickly fail and return inside the range?

**Summary: Complete trading decision process**

### Systematic decision flowchart

    Begin trading decision
    ↓
    [Question 1] Is there a trend?
    ├─ Yes → Continue
    └─ No → Possibly in a range → Jump to [Question 4]
    ↓
    [Question 2] How strong is the trend?
    ├─ Strong trend (tight channel) → Aggressive with-trend strategy
    └─ Weak trend (broad channel) → Conservative buy-low sell-high strategy
    ↓
    [Question 3] Is the pullback still tradeable?
    ├─ Yes → Look for buy/sell pullback opportunities
    └─ No → Trend invalid → Jump to [Question 4]
    ↓
    [Question 4] Are we in a range? Where in the range?
    ├─ Range top → Only short, no chasing rallies
    ├─ Range bottom → Only long, no chasing selloffs
    └─ Range middle → Avoid trading or extremely short-term scalps only

Look for a spike (1 surprise bar, or 2–3 medium bars, or 4–5 small bars), check follow-through (2nd and 3rd bars in the same direction), confirm Always In state, exclude range illusions.
