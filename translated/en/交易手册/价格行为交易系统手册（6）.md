# Bar Signal System: Setup Bar → Signal Bar → Entry Bar → Follow-through Bar

These four types of bars each serve a distinct function. Understand this sequence and you master the rhythm of trading.

When to wait (setup phase)

When to prepare (signal appears)

When to enter (breakout confirmed)

When to exit (follow-through fails)

## Foundational Bars

### Trend Bar

The close is far from the open, showing one-sided strength.

The distance between the open and close is large, the upper and lower wicks are short relative to the body, and the close is near the extreme.

A trend bar indicates that one side held absolute dominance during that bar's period. Price moved continuously in one direction from open to close, without hesitation.

- One side dominated within the current time period
- If it appears at a key level, it may signal trend continuation or breakout confirmation
- Consecutive trend bars (especially in the same direction) form a strong trend

### Doji

Represents two-sided trading and balance of power.

The open and close are very close (or equal), forming an extremely small or nonexistent body.

During that bar's period, bulls and bears were evenly matched. Price may have swung widely (long wicks), but ultimately returned to the starting point — the market is in a state of indecision.

A doji is a weak signal. "If the signal bar is a doji, the win rate is lower."

- A doji as an entry signal is less reliable than a trend bar
- If you must enter after a doji, reduce position size or wait for further confirmation
- Dojis are more useful for reading market state (indecision, balance) than as direct entry triggers

### Reversal Bar

Shows that price was immediately rejected. Price moved in one direction during the bar's period, was then rejected, and ultimately closed in the opposite direction.

When trading with the trend, the signal bar can be lower quality; but when trading against the trend, you must wait for a near-perfect signal bar.

- Watch the behavior of subsequent bars (follow-through bars)
- Reversal bars frequently fail and evolve into flags (trend continuation)
- The key is the location and context in which the reversal bar appears

### Wick/Tail

Indicates that counter-pressure exists at that price level.

- **Upper wick:** The portion between the high and the top of the body
- **Lower wick:** The portion between the low and the bottom of the body

If a signal bar has a long counter-directional wick (tail), it is considered a weak signal — enter with caution or exit early.

## Mastering the Four-Bar Sequence

### Setup Bar

The setup bar is the bar that forms the context for a trading opportunity. Together, multiple setup bars create a recognizable pattern or structure.

- Does not directly trigger entry on its own
- They create the "setup" — the preconditions for a trading opportunity

### Signal Bar

The signal bar is the specific bar pattern that prompts entry. It is the concrete bar that triggers the entry logic.

- Confirms the validity of the setup
- Provides a specific reference price for entry
- Its pattern quality directly affects the win rate of the trade

### Entry Bar

The entry bar is the bar on which the trade is actually filled.

When entering with a stop order, the entry bar is the bar that triggers the order.

- The bar following the signal bar is typically the entry bar
- Entry usually occurs the moment the signal bar's high/low is broken
- The behavior of the entry bar affects confidence in the trade

**Ideal:** The entry bar continues moving in the expected direction and closes near its extreme.

**Warning signal:** The entry bar has a long counter-directional wick or a very small body.

**Danger signal:** The entry bar moves against the expected direction.

### Follow-through Bar

The follow-through bar is the confirmation bar after entry. It is used to judge whether there are consecutive bars confirming direction after the breakout.

**Exit on lack of follow-through:** If the entry bar or follow-through bar performs poorly, immediately stop out or exit at breakeven.

- The follow-through bar is an early indicator of whether the trade will succeed
- If the follow-through bar does not support the expected direction, consider exiting
- Do not wait passively for your stop loss to be hit

A **high-quality follow-through bar** continues moving in the expected direction, is aligned with the entry bar/signal bar, and shows momentum continuation.

## Additional Bar Types

### Surprise Bar

A surprise bar has an unusually large body — it is a powerful, unexpected large bar with extreme momentum, signaling that a second leg of movement is likely.

"After a surprise bar pays off all risk, take profits."

- **As a profit-taking signal:** When the surprise bar rapidly pushes price to the target
- **As a precursor to a second leg:** A surprise bar is often followed by further continuation
- **Sell the Close:** Aggressive strategy — enter immediately at the close of the surprise bar
- **Sell the Pullback:** Conservative strategy — place a limit order at the 50% level or upper third of the bar on a pullback

### Disappointing Bar

A disappointing bar moves against the expected direction and often causes early exits.

When you expect the market to move in a certain direction, and the bar's performance disappoints.

- Expected continuation higher, but a bear bar appears
- Expected a strong breakout, but only a small body forms
- Expected rapid movement, but price stalls

Reassess the trading premise. If the premise has been undermined, consider exiting early. Do not stubbornly hold a failing trade just because "the stop hasn't been hit yet."

### Climax Bar

A climax bar is an extreme bar that appears at the end of a trend.

**Buying/Selling Climax:** An extreme acceleration move at the end of a trend. The climax bar/exhaustion bar marks the signal that one side of traders is closing out positions or that momentum has overextended.

A climax bar represents the market's "excessive behavior" — price moves rapidly in a short time, pushing the trend to an extreme.

A climax typically leads to a pullback of at least 10 bars with two legs. The trend will likely pause or reverse; at minimum there will be a meaningful pullback. Do not chase entries immediately after a climax bar.

The extreme of a climax bar becomes an important price reference:

- The high/low of the climax bar can serve as a profit target, or as support/resistance for subsequent trades.
- In a strong bear trend, rather than chasing the move directly, wait for price to bounce slightly back up to the top of the climax bar before going short.

## Mastering Composite Bar Structures

### Inside Bar

An inside bar is a bar that is completely contained within the previous bar.

- The inside bar's high ≤ the previous bar's high
- The inside bar's low ≥ the previous bar's low
- The entire bar's range falls completely within the previous bar

Indicates deepening market contraction, a continuously narrowing range, a temporary truce between bulls and bears, and the market building energy.

Inside bars late in a trend often serve as a final flag.

- After a trend has been running for some time, an inside bar may signal the trend is about to end
- The inside bar may be the last pullback (final flag), and the trend may exhaust after the inside bar is broken

Price contraction may be followed by a breakout; the direction of the breakout requires confirmation from subsequent bars.

### Outside Bar

An outside bar is a bar that completely contains the previous bar.

- The outside bar's high > the previous bar's high
- The outside bar's low < the previous bar's low
- The outside bar's range completely covers the previous bar

During this bar's period, both bulls and bears held the advantage at some point — price broke out in one direction and was then pulled back the other way, with volatility suddenly expanding.

- An outside bar can itself be viewed as a mini trading range; its high and low become short-term support and resistance, and subsequent bars may oscillate within the outside bar's range.
- Generally indicates directional uncertainty. The position of the close may provide some directional bias, but the overall signal is ambiguous and requires subsequent confirmation.

### Two-Bar Reversal

A two-bar reversal is a reversal pattern composed of two bars of opposite polarity.

**Bullish reversal (bottom reversal):**

- First bar: Bear trend bar (bear bar, close near the low)
- Second bar: Bull trend bar (bull bar, close near the high)
- Together the two bars form a "U" shape, showing price bottoming and reversing

The force of the original direction peaks at the first bar; the second bar shows the opposing force rapidly taking over. Together the two bars form a complete reversal signal.

If the two bars of a two-bar reversal are merged into a single bar, the bullish version resembles a bull reversal bar with a long lower wick — demonstrating that this is essentially a reversal bar unfolding over time.

### Microchannel

A microchannel is an extreme trend structure: consecutive bars without breaking the previous bar's high/low.

**Bull microchannel:** Each bar's low is higher than the previous bar's low.

Trend strength is extremely powerful with almost no meaningful pullbacks; the opposing side is completely unable to fight back. Attempting counter-trend trades within a microchannel is typically a trap.

A microchannel is a signal of a healthy trend with sufficient momentum. Do not attempt to pick a bottom or top within a microchannel.

Trade with the trend, or wait for the microchannel to be broken before considering reversal.

A microchannel can be viewed as an extreme form of a **tight channel**:

- **Tight channel:** Pullbacks are very small
- **Microchannel:** Almost no pullback (does not break the previous bar's extreme)

## Common Mistakes (Avoid These Traps)

**Mistake 1: Treating a doji as a high-quality signal**

A doji represents indecision; its win rate is lower. Entering immediately after a doji gives you only a 40% win rate.

Wait for a confirmation bar after the doji, or skip the opportunity entirely.

**Mistake 2: Ignoring the follow-through bar**

Entering without watching the follow-through bar, then passively waiting to be stopped out. Poor follow-through bar performance means the trading premise has been undermined — you should exit immediately.

Exit on lack of follow-through. If the follow-through bar does not support your direction, close the position immediately or stop out.

**Mistake 3: Chasing entries after a climax bar**

A climax bar typically leads to a pullback of at least 10 bars with two legs. Chasing an entry after a climax bar will get you stopped out in the pullback.

Recognize the climax bar and wait for the pullback. Enter with the trend after the pullback ends, or take profits when the climax bar appears.

**Mistake 4: Trading counter-trend within a microchannel**

A microchannel indicates an extremely strong trend. Going short in a bull microchannel, or going long in a bear microchannel, will get you crushed by the trend.

Only trade in the direction of the microchannel. Wait for the microchannel to be broken before considering reversal.

**Mistake 5: Entering without evaluating signal bar quality**

A signal bar with a long counter-directional wick, a doji, or a very small body — these are all weak signals. Entering without evaluating quality will drop your win rate below 50%.

- **With-trend trades:** Lower quality signal bars are acceptable
- **Counter-trend trades:** You must wait for a near-perfect signal bar
