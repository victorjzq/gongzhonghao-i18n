## Why Do You Always Get Trapped at the End of a Trend?

Most traders have had this experience: you watch price make consecutive new highs, finally summon the courage to chase in, and the market immediately reverses. Or the opposite — you clearly see a reversal signal, enter, and then get stopped out by the continuing trend.

The problem is not that you lack diligence. It is that you have never truly understood the language of market exhaustion — three pushes.

Master wedge trading, and you will be on the right side when 80% of traders are trapped at tops or bottoms.

## The False Signals the Market Gives You

You were taught to "trade with the trend," so you chase every breakout. But the truth of the market is: when most people finally agree on the trend direction, the trend is often nearing its end.

In a tight channel, you see price making new highs again and again, think the trend will reverse, yet 80% of reversal attempts fail inside a tight channel — your "wedge top" is just part of a bull flag.

You see a tight stop and think it is a good trade. But low risk usually comes with low probability, and frequent small losses eventually drain the account.

You finally catch one reversal, but exit too early because of a scalp mentality. A wedge reversal is a swing trade — exiting too early means the reward is insufficient to offset previous failed attempts.

These are all traps of a "buy high, sell low" mindset. The real question is: how do you identify when a trend is truly exhausted?

## What Is a Wedge?

A wedge is essentially a tilted triangle — the most important exhaustion pattern in price action trading.

As long as there are three pushes, it can generally be called a wedge. The momentum of each push gradually weakens, signaling that the trend may be about to reverse. A complete wedge contains five legs: three pushes in the same direction plus two countertrend pullbacks. The key to identifying a wedge is finding these three pushes.

Wedges are rarely perfect. Even if the shape is not textbook, as long as it contains three pushes, the market will usually treat it as a wedge. A double top or double bottom reversal that fails to break out functions equivalently to a wedge.

**Momentum decay is the core signal of a wedge.** In the first push, bars are large, the advance is wide, trend bar ratio is high, and tails are few. In the second push, all these metrics become moderate. By the third push, bars become smaller, the advance shrinks, the trend bar ratio decreases, and tails noticeably increase. When the third push is clearly weaker than the first two, it is time to watch for a reversal.

All channels are essentially some form of wedge, as long as two lines can be drawn and three pushes are contained. Converging lines form a wedge; parallel lines form a channel. Within channels, there is a 75% probability rule: a bull channel has a 75% probability of breaking down, and a bear channel has a 75% probability of breaking up.

There is an exception: if the wedge appears in a very tight channel (strong trend), the reversal is usually minor and the trend resumes. This is why context determines everything.

## Five Wedge Variants

**Standard Wedge:** The three pushes converge progressively.

**Nested Wedge:** The third push leg of a larger wedge itself forms a smaller wedge. This reflects the fractal nature of price action. When wedges appear simultaneously on both the larger and smaller timeframe, the probability of reversal is higher. The entry timing is when the smaller wedge reversal signal forms inside the third push of the larger wedge.

**Parabolic Wedge:** Appears in extremely strong trends, characterized by three consecutive climaxes with the channel line becoming progressively steeper. The channel is very tight with almost only minor pullbacks, representing extreme emotion (FOMO or panic) and indicating the trend is in an exhaustion phase. A parabolic wedge does not cause an immediate reversal into a strong opposing trend — it is more likely to lead into a trading range or a deep pullback. The target is TBTL (at least 10 bars and a two-legged sideways move/pullback), with roughly 50% probability of reversal and 50% probability of a failed breakout and trend continuation. If the higher timeframe is below the moving average, the probability of a direct reversal is greater.

**Truncated Wedge:** The third push fails to exceed the extreme of the second push — for example, the third low of a wedge bottom is higher than the second low. The reason is that too many traders jump in early, preventing price from making a new extreme. Despite the imperfect shape, it should still be treated as a reversal signal.

**Overshoot Wedge:** The third push strongly breaks through the channel line, typically a manifestation of climax behavior, and may lead to a reversal.

**Wedge-Double Top/Bottom Equivalence:** A double top plus an upside fake breakout (third high) equals a wedge top; a double bottom plus a downside fake breakout (third low) equals a wedge bottom.

**Wedge Flag:** A pullback within the main trend that contains three pushes. In a bull trend, a wedge bull flag equals a High 3; in a bear trend, a wedge bear flag equals a Low 3. The expectation is trend resumption.

## Strategies for Three Market Environments

**In a strong trend (tight channel):** Wedge reversals typically fail and evolve into wedge flags. Strategy: do not trade countertrend; wait for a pullback to enter with the trend.

**In a weak trend (wide channel):** A wide channel is often essentially one large wedge, and two-way trading is profitable. Strategy: look for reversal trades from the channel boundaries.

**In a trading range:** Wedges are a common reversal pattern at range boundaries. Buy wedge bottoms at the bottom of the range; sell wedge tops at the top of the range.

A horizontal wedge equals a triangle, with roughly 50/50 breakout direction probability. A failed triangle breakout often evolves into a wedge bottom or wedge top, which is a powerful reversal signal.

## The Magnetic Pull of the Moving Average

The first target of a wedge reversal is often the moving average (20 EMA). When the market moves far from the moving average to form a wedge, the moving average acts as a magnet pulling price back.

**Moving average gap bars** are bars that lie entirely on one side of the moving average, representing an extremely strong trend. The first pullback after a wedge reversal in this context will typically be bought or sold, and the trend resumes.

If the wedge appears late in the trend and price has been crossing back and forth above and below the moving average, it may evolve into a final flag, followed by a reversal.

## What to Do When a Breakout Fails

After a wedge breakout, approximately 50% of the time price continues in the new direction (forming a measured move), and approximately 50% of the time the breakout fails and reverses again.

A **failed failure** refers to a wedge bottom breaking down, that breakdown failing, and price then reversing upward. This is an extremely high-probability entry point. If a strong breakout has follow-through, abandon the reversal idea and trade with the new direction.

## Your Trading Checklist

**Step 1:** Identify three distinct consecutive highs (or lows).

**Step 2:** Observe whether the momentum of the third push is weakening.

**Step 3:** Look for a reversal signal bar (strong countertrend bar, doji, long tail/wick).

**Step 4:** Place a stop order at the extreme of the signal bar. For a wedge bottom, place a buy stop one tick above the high of the bullish signal bar; for a wedge top, place a sell stop one tick below the low of the bearish signal bar.

**Step 5:** Place the stop loss beyond the wedge extreme. For a wedge bottom, the stop loss goes below the lowest point of the wedge; for a wedge top, the stop loss goes above the highest point of the wedge.

**Step 6:** The first target is the start of Leg 3.

**Step 7:** The advanced target is the start of the wedge (start of Leg 1). More complete targets include the TBTL principle (10 bars plus a two-legged countertrend move), the start of the channel, a measured move target, and the 50% retracement of the prior trend.

Because a wedge reversal is a countertrend trade, the first attempt often fails. The safer approach is to wait for a second entry signal. For a wedge bottom, wait for a High 2 buy; for a wedge top, wait for a Low 2 sell. The second signal is generally more reliable.

Once the market breaks out strongly in your favor, move the stop loss to the most recent major swing high or low. In a bull trend, that is a major higher low; in a bear trend, it is a major lower high.

After a wedge top appears, you will typically see a two-legged move in the opposite direction. If the wedge fails, a measured move extends after the breakout.

## Six Core Principles

1. **Three pushes are the soul.** Regardless of whether the shape is perfect, three failed attempts constitute a wedge.

2. **Context determines everything.** Do not trade countertrend in a strong trend; look for reversals in a weak trend or trading range.

3. **Wait for confirmation.** Do not blindly enter just because you see three pushes — you need a signal bar.

4. **Let profits run.** A wedge reversal is a swing trade, not a scalp.

5. **Fractal thinking.** Nested wedges (multi-timeframe confluence) carry higher probability.

6. **The 75% rule.** Channels break in the opposite direction 75% of the time.
