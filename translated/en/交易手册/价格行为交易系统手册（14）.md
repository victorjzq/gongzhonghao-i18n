# Price Action Trading System Manual (14)

Principle of Inertia / 80% Rule: the market has a tendency to maintain its current state.

    Trends tend to continue

Trend-following principle — in a strong trend:

    Do not try to capture the 20% reversal opportunity (picking tops and bottoms)

Enter with the trend when reversal signals fail; short the pullback highs in a bear trend

Counting rule — the probability of a successful reversal increases incrementally:

When you see a strong breakout bar in a trading range

    Do not chase (80% will fail)

Cause-Effect / Event-Response: treating price action as a continuous cycle of "events" and "responses."

Every structure on the chart is an event:

    Strong spike (Spike)

Response (Response / Effect) — the market's reaction to the event:

    Acceptance: Follow Through or trend continuation

Calibrated Expectation (CE): anticipate the standard response before entry:

    Event: strong spike

Continuous Estimation Process (CEP): real-time comparison during the trade:

    Each new bar → compare actual vs. expected

Failure of a Failure (FOF) is one of the highest-probability trading patterns; it typically leads to a strong measured move in the direction of the failure.

    When a pattern that should have caused a reversal itself fails

    Identify the first failure:

**Two-Test Principle: before establishing a new direction, the market typically needs two validations.**

    The market usually attempts to do something at least twice

Most Major Trend Reversals (MTR) involve a second test:

    1st rally → typically fails

First Attempt Principle: in a strong trend, the first attempt to change the existing environment typically fails.

    Do not trade the first reversal signal

Breakout test: after breaking a key level, the market typically pullbacks (tests) that breakout point.

    Successful test (support holds) → trend continues

Symmetry & Proportion: the market tends to seek balance in both price (space) and time.

AB = CD pattern: after a strong push (Leg 1), it is typically followed by a second push (Leg 2) of similar magnitude.

    Leg 1 (first push) → pullback → Leg 2 (second push)

The duration and magnitude of a correction (pullback) is proportional to the preceding trend leg.

    Large trend → requires a long-duration, deep pullback

Measured Move (MM) — the most commonly used profit-taking tool.

    Step 1: Measure the length of Leg 1

Sometimes the second push is not obvious in price but is symmetric in time:

    Leg 1: lasted 10 bars

When the market should produce a symmetric second push but fails:

    Expected: should have Leg 2

Principle of Cyclical Volatility: the market perpetually cycles between low volatility and high volatility.

Volatility cycle:

    Low volatility (contraction) → High volatility (expansion) → Low volatility → …



    Do not assume the market is dead during extremely low volatility

**The market's objective is to maximize trading volume. The market tends to maintain: a state where both bulls and bears feel uncertain = trading range. Clear, simple trading opportunities (strong trends) are brief.**

### `Most of the time the market is in an ambiguous state` `Accept that most of the time the market is ambiguous` `Only trade the small number of clear opportunities` `Do not force trades`



    The strongest trends (e.g., microchannel) move at constant velocity (zero acceleration) and are sustainable

Vacuum Effect: when price moves rapidly toward an obvious support/resistance level:

    Lack of trading interest in between (vacuum) — price is "pulled" toward it
