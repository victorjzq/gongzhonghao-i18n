# Price Action Trading System Manual (16)

**Tight Stop — used for high-probability scalp trades or momentum trades; the stop loss is placed just outside the extreme of the signal bar or entry bar.**
---------------------------------------------------------

Applicable scenarios:

    With-trend trades in a strong trend

Wide Stop — primarily used for swing trades or trading range strategies; the stop loss is set far enough away to avoid being stopped out by market noise.

    Based on measured move: calculate the measured move target from the entry point, and place the stop loss beyond that target

This type of stop is a "worst case" insurance. Traders typically exit actively before this stop is hit.

Price Action Stop (Logical Stop) — based on the point where the market structure is invalidated; not a fixed price, but a logical boundary.

    Example 1: Microchannel

    Example 2: "Brick wall" concept

    Example 3: Gap stop

#### **Breakeven Stop — many traders habitually move to breakeven as soon as there is a small profit**

    Problem: The market tends to retest breakout points, making it easy to be stopped out and missing the subsequent big move

Acceptable scenarios for use:

    Already realized more than 50% of the target

"Show me proof" principle — unless the market provides clear opposing evidence, do not move your stop or exit out of fear.

Position management and money management — "I Don't Care Size"

Trading Psychology and Execution Discipline — "Widget Factory" Mindset

    Treat trading like running a factory:

Your job: ensure the production line repeatedly produces products that meet standards

    No need to care about: the fate of any individual product

Accept defects — the factory always has defects (losing trades); this is part of the cost of production

    Correct mindset:

Quality control

    If the defect rate is too high (win rate declining):

Stay specialized — before achieving consistent profitability on one production line (strategy), do not start a new one

    Divided attention

Before a trade, ask yourself:

    Does this trade meet my "production standards"?

During a trade, ask yourself:

    Is the production process for this "widget" proceeding normally?

After a trade, ask yourself:

    What was the yield rate for this batch?

Calibrated Expectation (CE) — before entry, establish specific standards for the expected outcome of the trade based on market context and pattern.

    Expected win rate

    Step 1: Study historical data

    Step 2: Record the standards

    Step 3: Calibrate before the trade

Continuous Estimation Process (CEP) — after entry, continuously compare "actual price action" against "calibrated expectation" with each new bar or tick

Ask yourself at the close of each bar:

    Is the market behaving as expected?
