The exit is another key component of trading — when and how to take profit directly impacts the final result of a trade.

Scalping is a trading style that seeks small, rapid profits. The scalp target is typically fixed and relatively small; profit targets are small and fixed, holding time is short, it pursues a high win rate but low per-trade profit, and accumulates profits through frequent trading.

Scaling out is a strategy of taking profits in stages — partially closing at an initial target while holding the remainder for a swing move. Rather than closing the entire position at a single price, profits are taken in phases to balance locking in gains against capturing a larger move, while reducing psychological tension during the hold. The psychological advantage is that pressure noticeably decreases once some profit is locked in; the remaining position, having already "paid for itself," is more like a "free ride," allowing more composure for the swing hold.

Structural target profit-taking is based on price structure and technical levels to set exit points, making the exit decision more aligned with market logic. The measured move is a method for predicting targets based on the height of a formation. A measured move exit means exiting at the specific target predicted by the formation; market movement often displays symmetry, and the magnitude of the prior move can be used to predict the target. For example, the target can be calculated as "breakout point + the height of the impulse before the flag formation," and the equal-leg target can be estimated as "pullback low + the height of the first leg."

Climax bar extremes are also important profit-taking references. Taking profit at the high of a climax bar can be used as a target, and the origin of a climax bar can also be used as a pattern target. The core logic is that a climax bar represents extreme trend movement; its extreme point often becomes a significant resistance or support level in subsequent movement.

Magnet targets are another common structural profit-taking approach — the edge of a prior trading range, a previous closing price, prior highs and lows, the opening price, and other technical levels can all serve as closing targets. Profit targets should be set at structural target levels (such as climax point highs) rather than arbitrarily chosen, meaning the take-profit level requires a technical basis and is not an arbitrary number — structural targets are more consistent with market logic.

When multiple magnets exist simultaneously, their priority must be judged. The general principle is that the nearest magnet is most likely to be reached first, the most recently formed magnet has stronger market memory, and levels that have been tested multiple times may have already lost effectiveness.

The "gift" exit is a practice of exiting directly when a strong follow-through bar closes, treating this rapid profit as a "gift" from the market. The core logic is that when an unexpectedly strong bar appears, profit should be locked in immediately rather than reaching for more. Profit-maximization exits emphasize a similar idea: when a large surprise trend bar (surprise bar) appears after entry and the movement of that bar has already covered the full risk, profit should be taken immediately to lock in this "gift."

Active exit is when a trader proactively decides to exit based on changes in market behavior rather than passively waiting for a stop loss to be triggered. It can reduce losses and protect profits, and is an important skill of experienced traders.

The behavior-change exit approach is to exit when a previously strong trend begins to show overlap or counter-signals. Typical signals include: bars that were previously separate beginning to overlap, a trend bar appearing in the opposite direction of the position, weakening momentum shown by smaller bar bodies and longer wicks, a signal bar appearing in the opposite direction, and price stalling without continuing to move in the favorable direction.

The counter-signal exit emphasizes exiting when a second counter-signal or a strong counter-trend bar appears. For example, after entering long and the price rises, an L2 (bear second entry) signal then appears — since this is itself a short entry signal, the long position should be closed immediately.

Exiting based on bar quality requires: if the entry bar has a long wick or failed to close near its extreme, exit at break-even or a small loss; if consecutive counter-trend bars appear (e.g., consecutive bear bars when long), exit immediately — that is, exit 1 tick below a strong counter-reversal bar.

Time exit emphasizes exiting when the price moves sideways for multiple bars (such as 7 to 8) with no direction. The core logic is that if price fails to move in the expected direction for an extended time after entry, the judgment may be wrong or the timing off, and one should exit.

Protect capital — don't let small losses become large losses; if there is no follow-through, exit and don't expect miracles; if behavior changes, be alert — when the market changes, react.

Position size is the core of risk management, determining how much capital is committed to each trade.

The "don't care" position is the core position management concept — reducing the position size to a level where a loss would not produce an emotional reaction.

The psychological basis is that an oversized position causes emotional tension and distorted judgment; fear of loss leads to premature profit-taking or failure to stop out; watching the screen anxiously prevents calm market observation; decision distortion causes violation of the trading plan. Therefore the position should be reduced to a "don't care" level to eliminate emotional interference.

Minimum position practice applies to traders who are still learning, traders experiencing consecutive losses, traders whose emotions are easily affected, and traders who need to rebuild confidence. The small-scale practice method is to use extremely small positions long-term; the purpose is to shift attention from money to technique, to focus on execution rather than profit and loss, and to hone skills in a low-risk environment. The self-test method for determining a "don't care" position is to imagine the trade losing and check the emotional response — if still feeling anxious or uneasy, the position is too large and must be further reduced until "don't care." In practice, start from the minimum position and gradually increase until feeling uncomfortable, then retreat to a comfortable position size.

When trying a new strategy, use the minimum position because the strategy is unverified, learning costs can be reduced, and trading confidence can be protected.

When feeling rusty, returning from a break, or uncertain about signals, reduce the position size. Being rusty includes returning from a break, not having traded for an extended period, poor emotional state, and feeling unfamiliar with the market.

The advancement rule is a 50-trade phase — the strategy must be proven effective before adding position size, using gradual growth to avoid sudden large increases, and giving the mind time to adapt to larger monetary swings.

Demotion is triggered by consecutive losses, deteriorating strategy performance, emotional impact, or changing market conditions — reduce the position to the prior phase or directly back to the minimum, and re-prove profitability.

Stop loss setting and management is the last line of defense in risk control. Correct stop loss placement protects capital; stop loss management can optimize the risk-reward ratio.

A technical stop loss is set based on price structure rather than a fixed dollar amount. Stop loss levels are typically at the signal bar extreme or the nearest swing high/low. A logical stop is placed outside the signal bar high/low or at the location where the premise for the trade is broken. The stop loss should be placed at "the position where, if price reaches there, the entry logic is invalidated." Common technical stop loss locations include: signal bar extreme (in standard entries, placed outside the signal bar high or low); swing high/low (in trend trades, placed outside the nearest swing extreme); support/resistance level (in key-level trades, stop out when the key level is broken); entry bar extreme (tighter stop placed at the other end of the entry bar).

A wide stop is a stop placement that gives the trade more room to avoid being randomly stopped out by volatility. It can also be combined with a scaling-in strategy to allow room for price to breathe. Its advantages are that it is less easily triggered by random volatility, gives the trade "breathing room," suits scaling in, and reduces the number of times one is "stopped out." The disadvantages are that the per-trade loss is larger, position size must be reduced, psychological pressure may be greater, and the risk-reward ratio may be worse.

A tight stop is tighter, suitable for certain swing trades but easily stopped out due to poor positioning. Its advantages are smaller per-trade losses, larger position sizes can be used, the risk-reward ratio may be better, and psychological pressure is lower. Disadvantages are that it is easily triggered by random volatility, can lead to frequent stop-outs, requires more precise entry, and is more demanding on timing.

The choice between wide and tight stops depends on volatility, entry method, signal quality, trading style, and position preference: high-volatility markets lean toward wide stops, low-volatility markets lean toward tight stops; scaling in suits wide stops, single entry suits tight stops; average signals lean toward wide stops, excellent signals lean toward tight stops; swing trading leans toward wide stops, scalping leans toward tight stops; willingness to reduce size suits wide stops, preference for larger positions suits tight stops.

Being "stopped out" refers to price triggering the stop and then immediately reversing — the stop fires and price moves in the originally expected direction, making the trader feel intentionally swept out by the market. Methods for reducing stop-outs include using a wide stop, placing the stop beyond a stronger support/resistance level, waiting for a better entry, and accepting this as part of trading. Stop loss movement strategy refers to adjusting the stop loss position during the trade; its purpose is to protect existing profit, reduce potential loss, and lock in gains. The logical stop is still equal to the position where the trade premise is invalidated.

Regarding risk control for scaling in: scaling in is building a planned position in multiple increments rather than entering the full position at once. The core logic is that a single-entry in the right direction maximizes profit, whereas scaling in reduces the risk of a single judgment error — but if the direction is correct, the average cost may be worse; the account curve is smoother.

Execution methods for scaling in include planned scaling, where the total planned position and incremental entry schedule are set in advance — for example, total plan of 6 contracts: enter 2 on the first signal, enter 2 more after confirmation or on a pullback, and enter the final 2 as the trend continues;

And conditional scaling, where the decision to add is made based on market feedback — for example, after the first entry observe the entry bar: if it closes strongly, add the second batch; if not, maintain the original position and continue watching; if follow-through then appears, add; if not, don't add and manage the original plan.

Scaling-in timing options include after breakout confirmation with follow-through (lower risk), on pullback stabilization (moderate), when the signal reappears (lower), and immediate addition in extremely strong market conditions (higher risk).

The core principle of risk management for scaling in is that total risk remains unchanged. Situations when not to scale in include: high-certainty signals (when the signal bar is extremely strong and structure is clear, a single entry is more efficient); fast markets (during a spike phase where price is moving quickly, scaling in may miss the move); small accounts (where positions are already small and scaling in has no meaning); and counter-trend trades (where the risk is already high and expanding exposure through adding should not be done). Only scale in when the trend is clear but the entry point is imperfect; if the signal is strong, enter full position directly; if uncertain, prefer a small position rather than scaling in.

Only enter in the Always In direction, or enter against the trend only with strong reversal evidence. Execution standards: before entry, clearly judge the Always In direction; if direction is unclear, wait; counter-trend entry requires a key level plus a reversal bar plus confirmation. Signal standards: prefer second entries (H2/L2) — second entries require a signal bar of moderate quality or above; first entries require the signal bar to be a strong trend bar; counter-trend entries must be at a key level with a strong reversal signal.

Stop loss preset: the stop loss level must be determined before entry — do not enter without a clear stop loss. Stop placement standards: 1 to 2 ticks outside the signal bar extreme or outside a key structural level, with the stop distance being reasonable — not too large, not too small. Position limit: single trade risk not exceeding 2% of account.

The position management rule system requires: after entry, observe the entry bar to assess trade quality — strong close in the right direction means the trade is good, hold and consider adding; neutral close means the trade is acceptable, hold but stay alert; weak close in the wrong direction means the trade is poor, prepare for early exit. Follow-through monitoring requires watching bars after entry: favorable follow-through increases holding confidence; a strong counter bar prompts consideration of exit or immediate stop; consecutive neutral bars lower expectations and prepare for target 1 exit. Not expanding the stop is an iron rule: once set, the stop can only be tightened, never widened.

The exit rule system includes: stop loss execution — exit unconditionally when the stop is triggered, no hesitation, no hoping, no expanding; profit target — at the preset target, exit at least partially; the suggested approach is to exit 50% at target 1 (1R) and exit the remainder at target 2 (2R or structural level) or track with a trailing stop. Active exit conditions include: appearance of a counter-trend bar, obvious change in market structure such as Always In direction flipping, original entry logic no longer valid, or appearance of an extreme counter-signal such as a climax bar — in these cases exit proactively without waiting for stop or target. Rule priority: when rules conflict, first execute risk management rules (stop loss, position limits, circuit breakers), then direction rules (Always In judgment), then entry rules (signal standards, entry timing), and finally exit rules (targets, active exit conditions). The core principle is: protecting capital is more important than being right on direction, which is more important than entry precision, which is more important than maximizing profit.

Not all signals meeting basic standards are worth trading. Filter conditions are used to select higher-quality opportunities. Structural filters require alignment with the larger timeframe direction and distance from key levels — or being at a key level with sufficient space to the next resistance/support; otherwise filter out if opposed to the larger timeframe, too close to a key level, or with insufficient space of less than 1R.

Signal quality filters require the signal bar body to be more than 50% of the bar range, positioned at a key level or within the trend with clear prior structure; otherwise filter out if it is a small doji, in a meaningless area, or with messy structure.

Time filters require being in the main trading session with normal market activity; otherwise filter out if within the first 30 minutes of the open with high volatility, or if the market is extremely quiet or extremely violent. 60% of losses come from "forced" entries — the signal is imperfect but one talks oneself into it. Therefore set hard filter conditions: if imperfect, abandon; missing an opportunity is better than taking a loss.

Absolute prohibitions include counter-trend first entries: L1 when Always In Long, H1 when Always In Short — the reason is that counter-trend first entries have extremely low win rates.

Emotional entries: entering immediately after a loss as revenge trading, entering with overconfidence after a win, or chasing an entry due to FOMO from having missed a move.

Vague entries: being unable to clearly articulate the entry reason, having an unclear stop loss level, or acting on the "feeling" that price will rise or fall — vague reasons are equivalent to random gambling.

Avoid entries 30 minutes before major news releases and during the violent volatility after announcements.

Enforcement of the prohibition rules relies on the reality that the market always offers tempting exceptions, short-term profitability reinforces incorrect behavior, and emotions will override rational judgment. Therefore: write the rules down by printing the prohibitions and posting them in view of the screen; use a checklist to verify each item before entry — abandon the trade if any item triggers; record every violation and its result and regularly compile statistics on the profit and loss of violated trades; and set a penalty mechanism — for example, reduce the number of trades after a violation, and mandatory rest after a serious violation. Writing "counter-trend first entry = loss" on a sticky note on the monitor and looking at it every time one is tempted to violate the rule — most of the time it works.
