# How People Go from Not Knowing to Knowing

Today I came across a video teaching the kip-up move (a martial arts floor technique where you spring from lying down to standing).

It was broken down into 10 steps:

1. Can't do a kip-up yet — start by lying flat
2. Once you can lie flat, practice bridging your lower back
3. Once you can bridge, practice bridging with both hands beside your ears
4. Once you can bridge with hands up, lie down and do a bridge-and-kick
5. Once you can kick, draw a circle with your feet and land them on the ground
6. Once you can land, increase the range and height
7. As you get higher, draw a circle with your feet while cocking your arms for push-off
8. Once you can push off, increase force and push up into a squat
9. As you push-squat higher and higher, go directly into a push-up to standing
10. Once you can stand up directly — the kip-up is complete

Not a single step here is a "leap."

Every step does only one thing:

Break the "impossible" into "something that has already happened."

No talent? Just repeat more.

Repeat until you no longer need to think about it.

---

**Trading failure is, at its core, a failure of skipping steps.**

Now, map this logic fully onto trading.

If you can identify a trend, you won't trade against it.

If you can identify a trading range, you know the upper and lower boundaries.

If you can identify the boundaries, you can tell whether they have been broken out.

If you can identify whether a boundary has broken out, you can stay out before it does.

If you can stay out of a trading range before it breaks, you will place limit orders at the boundaries of the trading range.

If you're sharp, you've already figured out where this is going.

---

## Stop Order Entry

A stop order entry is a condition-triggered entry method. Typically, you place an order in advance at one end of a signal bar, and it only fills when price actually moves in the expected direction and triggers that price level. For this reason, it has the highest win rate among all entry methods.

Its core advantage lies in directional confirmation and market validation — the order only fills when price breaks out in the direction of the trade, avoiding buying dips or selling rallies while price is still moving against you. The market proves the direction through actual price action, and after entry, floating profit often develops relatively quickly, making it psychologically easier to hold the position.

However, stop orders also have clear disadvantages: entry price is less favorable compared to limit orders, and slippage can occur during fast breakouts.

---

## Market Order Entry (Buy/Sell the Close)

In contrast, a market order entry (Buy/Sell the Close) means entering immediately at market when a strong trend bar closes. It is mainly used when a very large trend bar appears, when a breakout is clearly confirmed, or when the trend is accelerating. Its characteristic is no waiting — it emphasizes urgency.

Urgency comes from the momentum shown by a powerful bar, the fear of missing the move, and the expectation of follow-through. But the risk is that it's easy to chase longs at highs or chase shorts at lows, there is no confirmation from the next bar, and emotions are more likely to interfere with judgment.

Therefore, market order entry is better suited for large-body trend bars, clear trend direction, extremely strong signals, and when the trader is calm and rational — not suitable for doji bars, small-body bars, range-bound conditions, or emotionally volatile states.

---

## Limit Order Entry

A limit order entry means entering when price pulls back to a specific level, with the goal of obtaining a better entry price and thus improving the risk-reward ratio. This approach requires more judgment and patience.

The most common is the **50% pullback entry**: after a strong impulsive move, you expect price to pull back, and you place a limit order at the 50% level of that swing. This improves your cost basis while still participating when the trend continues.

One optimized variant of the limit order entry is placing an order 1 tick below a bullish signal bar.

---

## Breakout Point Retest Entry

This is another important limit order entry method. The approach is to place an order at a previous breakout level and wait for price to pull back — buying or selling on the first retest near the breakout point or trend line of a strong trend.

The core logic: after price breaks through an important level, it frequently retests that breakout point. The breakout level transitions from resistance to support, or from support to resistance, so you can enter on the retest.

Breakout points tend to act as magnets, attracting price to retest them. The market "remembers" where the breakout occurred, causing that level to continue functioning on retest.

Breakout point retest entry is appropriate when: the breakout is strong with clear confirmation, trend direction is clear, and the pullback is shallow without damaging the structure. It is not appropriate when: the breakout is weak, the market is in a range or direction is unclear, or the pullback is deep enough to potentially break the structure.

---

## Key Level Test Entry

This entry method involves entering when price reaches a preset important price level. Typical examples include testing the prior day's close, the current day's open, or prior highs and lows, as well as testing the opening price or a prior breakout point.

The core logic: certain price levels carry special significance — such as the prior day's close or today's open — and when price tests these levels, support or resistance reactions may appear, creating entry opportunities.

Common key levels include: yesterday's close, yesterday's high and low, today's open, the origin of a channel, and the high or low of a climax bar.

Key level test entry has two operating modes:

- **Mode 1:** Pre-place a limit order at the key level — for longs, place a buy limit at key support; for shorts, place a sell limit at key resistance.
- **Mode 2:** Wait for price to reach the key level, observe the reaction, then enter — after price tests the key level and a reversal signal bar appears, enter on confirmation using either a stop order or market order.

Directional bias at key levels can be inferred from different scenarios: price testing yesterday's close from above may provide support and suggest a long; testing from below may form resistance and suggest a short; testing yesterday's high may suggest a short or a long on breakout; testing yesterday's low may suggest a long or a short on breakdown.

---

## Second Entry Premium Rule

A high-quality second entry typically comes with a worse price (a premium). "A good fill is often a bad trade" — this means that an entry where you get a "good price" may actually carry higher risk. A deep pullback reaching a key level may signal that the trend has changed; you cannot enter simply because the price looks cheap.

---

## Limit Order vs. Stop Order: Comparison

The core difference:

- **Limit order entry:** Enters on pullback. Better entry price, but weaker directional confirmation, relatively lower win rate, relatively higher risk-reward ratio.
- **Stop order entry:** Enters on breakout. Worse entry price, but stronger directional confirmation, relatively higher win rate, relatively lower risk-reward ratio.

Limit order entry is appropriate when: trend direction is clear, the pullback is shallow, and signs of support appear. It is not appropriate when: direction is unclear, a deep pullback may reverse the trend, or support signs are absent.

Limit order entry carries the risks of missing the move, overshoot, and the "good price trap" — a cheap entry price may mean the trend has changed.

## Advanced Entries

Advanced entries require deeper market understanding and richer experience, but when applied correctly, they can provide higher-quality trading opportunities.

---

### Trap Price Entry

This is an entry strategy that exploits the opposing side being trapped. The core logic: when a group of traders enters at a certain level and gets trapped, their stop orders cluster at a specific location. When those stops are triggered, you can enter and use the force of those stop orders to propel price.

- **Bull trap:** Bulls enter long at a level, then price reverses lower, trapping them. Bull stop orders cluster at a certain point. When price hits that stop level, a surge of sell orders floods the market — bears can enter short using that force.
- **Bear trap:** Bears enter short at a level, then price reverses higher, trapping them. Bear stop orders cluster at a certain point. When price hits that stop level, a surge of buy orders floods the market — bulls can enter long using that force.

Identify where stops are likely to be triggered, look for a counter-direction entry opportunity near that level, and use the force of trapped traders' stops.

---

### Failed Breakout Entry (Reversal Breakout Entry)

This is a counter-trend trade made when a breakout is expected to fail. The core logic: when you identify that a breakout may fail, instead of trading in the breakout direction, you wait for the breakout to fail and enter in the opposite direction.

Criteria for judging a likely failed breakout:
- Weak breakout (small-body breakout bar, long tails)
- Lack of follow-through (no consecutive same-direction bars after breakout)
- Inside a range (breakouts within a trading range often fail)
- Counter-trend breakout (opposite to the dominant trend direction)
- End of a channel (trend-direction breakouts from a channel fail approximately 75% of the time)

Example of the process — shorting after a false upside breakout from a range:
1. Identify the trading range
2. Price attempts to break out upward
3. Judge that the breakout may fail
4. Wait for price to return inside the range
5. Short below the low of the reversal signal bar after price returns inside the range, with stop above the false breakout high

---

### Scaling In

Scaling in is a position management and entry strategy combined. You split the total position into multiple entries. If price temporarily moves against you, you add at a better price. You add to the position at a better price while the logic remains unchanged.

This is for advanced traders only. Scaling in can devolve into "averaging down into a loss" — it requires strict discipline and risk control. Beginners can easily misuse it and suffer large losses.

Do not attempt to scale in against an extremely strong trend without a signal bar. Situations where you cannot scale in include: counter-trend operations, trading against the trend direction in a strong trend, no clear support or resistance basis, or simply because "it's fallen too much" or "it's risen too much."

Small accounts typically scale in only twice; large accounts may scale in three times. This means scaling in requires sufficient capital support.
