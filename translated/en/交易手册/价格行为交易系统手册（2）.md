# Price Action Trading System Manual (2)

The only measure of successful learning = whether you can reproduce the author's judgment sequence

* * *

Have You Really "Learned" It?
----------

Most people still cannot profit after learning a trading method. The problem is not the method — it's the wrong standard of learning.

They think "understanding it" equals learning it. But there is only one true standard: **can you fully reproduce the author's judgment sequence?**

If you cannot, you have not learned it. You have merely created an illusion of "I get it."

* * *

Trading Illusion: The Cost of Premature Guessing
------------

Most losses come from "trading illusions."

What is a trading illusion? You start guessing direction before the signal appears. You see a large bull bar and immediately think: "It's going up!" Then you start looking for evidence to support your idea, ignoring all opposing signals.

This is not trading — it is self-deception.

The root cause of trading illusions is **reversed judgment order**: you reach a conclusion first, then look for reasons. This is the complete opposite of the scientific method.

**The correct judgment sequence must be fixed, mechanical, and unskippable.**

* * *

The Correct Judgment Sequence: Signal First, Then the Three Elements
----------------

To constrain trading illusions and avoid premature guessing, the judgment sequence must be:

### Step One: Observe the Direction of the Signal Bar

Observe without any expectation: which direction does the signal bar point? Bull or bear?

The purpose of this step is to **let the market tell you the direction**, not for you to tell the market the direction.

### Step Two: Check the Three Elements to Evaluate Trade Quality

Only after the signal appears do you use the three elements to decide whether it is worth trading:

Element | Core Question
--- | ---
**Context** | What is the market's overall direction? Which way is Always In?
**Setup/Pattern** | How many times has the opposing side failed? How reliable is the signal?
**Location** | Where on the chart is the entry point? Is there enough room?

All three elements pass — trade. Any one fails — wait.

* * *

The Essence of a Pattern: The Number of Failures
-----------

A pattern is not a shape. A shape is merely the visual representation of a battle.

**The essence of a pattern is the number of times the opposing side has failed.**

The more times one side fails, the higher the win rate for the other side. Shapes are simply tools to help you quickly identify this "accumulated failure."

This is why **the second entry (H2/L2) is a reliable entry setup**.

* * *

Second Entry: H2/L2
----------

Instead of entering on the first signal, wait for the market to "confirm twice" — after the opposing side has failed two attempts — then enter with the trend.

    Don't buy H1... sellers above. Wait for H2
    Double top at the moving average, low 2 sell

### Why Does the Second Entry Work?

H2 means the bears attempted to push price down twice and both attempts failed:

- First failure (H1): bears had strength, but not enough to sustain
- Second failure (H2): bear strength is exhausted, bulls regain control

Three levels of advantage:

1. **Opposing side lacks strength**

   → Two failed attempts prove they cannot dominate

2. **Trapped positions drive price**

   → Opposing traders who entered on both attempts are trapped; their stop losses will push price

3. **With-trend confidence increases**

   → After two failures, with-trend traders gain confidence and join the move

### H2 (High 2) - Second Entry Long

In an uptrend:

1. Confirm Always In Long
2. Wait for a pullback to develop
3. See H1 (first attempt to break upward) → **do not buy**
4. H1 fails, price pulls back again
5. See H2 form (second upward break above the prior bar's high)
6. Place a buy stop 1 tick above the H2 signal bar's high
7. Stop loss below the pullback's lowest point
8. Target: prior high or 2R

### L2 (Low 2) - Second Entry Short

In a downtrend:

1. Confirm Always In Short
2. Wait for a bounce to develop
3. See L1 (first attempt to break downward) → **do not sell**
4. L1 fails, price bounces again
5. See L2 form (second downward break below the prior bar's low)
6. Place a sell stop 1 tick below the L2 signal bar's low
7. Stop loss above the bounce's highest point
8. Target: prior low or 2R

The H2/L2 Trap

A second entry usually means you enter at a **worse price** than the first entry, but this buys you **higher certainty**.

**Key judgment**: if the second entry gives you a better price, it is usually a trap.

Complete Judgment Checklist
------

Before every trade, check in this order:

1. ☐ What is the signal bar's direction? (look at the signal first, do not guess)
2. ☐ Context: is Always In direction aligned?
3. ☐ Setup/Pattern: is it H2/L2? How many times has the opposing side failed?
4. ☐ Location: is there enough room? Are you not entering at a resistance/support level?
5. ☐ All pass → execute. Any one fails → wait.

**The test of successful learning: can you, in real-time price action, make judgments in this sequence — without skipping steps, without premature guessing?**

If yes — you have learned it.
If not — keep practicing to reproduce this sequence.
