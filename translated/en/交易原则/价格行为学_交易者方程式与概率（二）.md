# Price Action: The Trader's Equation and Probability (Part 2)

I. Risk, Reward, and Probability
Risk, reward, and probability are the three core variables that determine every trade. Risk refers to the maximum loss when you are wrong, reward is the expected profit when you are right, and probability is the chance of success. Only by weighing all three before placing an order can a trade possess a mathematical edge.

II. Market Trade-Offs
In the market, there are always buyers and sellers. The reason both sides dare to act is that each weighs the three variables differently: one side is willing to accept a wider stop-loss and smaller reward in exchange for a higher win rate; the other accepts a low win rate in pursuit of a larger risk-reward ratio. Both approaches are valid — the key is whether you can manage your own weaknesses.

III. Beginner's Pitfall
Beginners tend to focus solely on risk. They fear blowing up their accounts, so they seek "safe" entry points with the smallest possible stops, ignoring that extremely low risk often means extremely low win rates and extremely low rewards. Eventually, they are ground down by countless small losses. To be profitable in the long run, probability, risk, and reward must all factor into the decision.

IV. Scaling In and Money Management
Scaling in is a common method for "improving win rate": adding to a losing position to lower the average cost. It does push the success rate higher, but it also significantly amplifies total risk and compresses per-unit returns. Without mature money management, this approach should not be used.

V. System Reliability: Z-Score
System reliability can be assessed using the Z-score: divide the average profit/loss distance by the standard deviation of profits and losses. The higher the value, the smoother the equity curve and the lower the risk of blowing up. Top scalpers can achieve a Z-score of 5; an ordinary system scoring 2–3 is already quite good. A high-Z system allows risking 1–2% of account equity per trade, while a low-Z system must reduce position size — otherwise, a few consecutive losses could destroy the entire year.

VI. Strategy Comparison
For example: a strategy that scalps High 2 or Low 2 flags for a one-times-risk profit target has a high win rate and low volatility — this is a high-Z approach. In contrast, a simple strategy that chases a breakout above or below the high or low of the last ten bars has a low win rate and heavy dependence on a few big winners — this is a low-Z approach.

VII. Practical Steps
In practice, first clearly define the three variables: estimate the win rate, set the stop-loss, and mark the target. Then apply the Trader's Equation — win rate multiplied by expected reward must be greater than the failure probability multiplied by risk; otherwise, pass on the trade. Practice with a position size so small that you "truly don't care about losing it," and first stabilize your Z-score above 2 before considering scaling up.
Always remember this trade-off: a high win rate usually comes with high risk and low reward; high reward usually comes with a low win rate and high volatility.

VIII. Conclusion
The perfect trade does not exist, but as long as you consistently design and execute positive-expectancy trades, a small mathematical edge will compound into substantial profits over time.
