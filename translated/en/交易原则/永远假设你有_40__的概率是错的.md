# Always Assume You Have a 40% Chance of Being Wrong

**You can trade counter-trend, but you need to know how to manage it**

These ideas are all fine.

The question is: **how do you manage the trade?**

---

### 1. Counter-Trend Trading Is OK—What Matters Is "How"

For counter-trend operations, remember three things:

1. **Light position size** — You're not here to bet your life; you're here to test the market's temperature. A light position keeps you calm and prevents emotional breakdowns from small fluctuations.

2. **Wide stop loss** — Placing a stop loss two ticks away doesn't count. You're trading against the wind; the stop loss needs to go in a truly reasonable place—for example, using the overall range of the move to gauge pullback space.

3. **Have an exit plan** — After scaling in, don't fantasize about "making a killing." If the market gives you a chance to get back to your average cost, take it. Not every trade needs to become a "trend trade"—surviving first is what gives you a future.

---

### 2. Trading Logic and Probability: Know What Market You're In

When trading, don't ask "can I take this trade?"

Instead ask — "Is this a trend, or a trading range?"

1. **If it's a trend:** Trade with it. The win rate is high, and profit targets are easier to reach—even a 1:1 risk/reward ratio is worth taking.

2. **If it's a trading range:** The win rate is low, so you must pursue a higher risk/reward ratio. Either use a wide stop loss and scale in to improve your odds, or simply don't trade.

Your trading approach must match the market environment.

---

### 3. Only Trades with Logic Are Worth Taking

Many people obsess over entry timing: too early and they fear getting stopped out; too late and they fear chasing.

But what truly matters is:

**Does this trade have structure and logic?**

If you've set your stop loss and your profit target,

then whether you enter a bit early or a bit late doesn't matter—

it's a correct trade.

Trading has never been about "who gets in first."

It's about whose thinking can go the distance.

---

### 4. Smart People Are Actually More Likely to Lose Money

Try to "make yourself dumber" when trading.

Why?

Because "smart people" love to over-analyze:

"What if it goes up?"

"What if it comes back down?"

"Would it be safer to wait a bit longer?"

The result is—the market moves, and they're still hesitating.

Being smart isn't wrong.

What's wrong is letting "smartness" paralyze your decision-making.

### Final Thought

This insight deserves to be posted next to every trader's screen — "Always assume you have a 40% chance of being wrong."
