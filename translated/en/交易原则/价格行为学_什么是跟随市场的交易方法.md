# Price Action: What Is a Market-Following Trading Approach

The essence of the market is that it is composed of institutions—those large banks, hedge funds, and high-frequency trading firms. They control massive capital and set the trading rhythm.

As retail traders, our task is not to fight against them, but to learn to "ride their coattails"—to do what they are already doing.

You cannot control the market, but you can wait for their moves, then use the price action they create to follow in at the right moment and capture your small share of the profit.

You need an "event" before there can be a "reaction." This is the embodiment of cause and effect.

You first need an A-to-B move—a clear rally or decline. Then you wait for a reasonable pullback, and at that point you can capture the C-to-D continuation move.

You cannot rush into the market without seeing anything first. That would be like sitting on a seesaw that swings unpredictably—price going up and down—and you have no idea which side you're on, making it impossible to profit consistently.

In other words, it's like standing in the middle of a trading range—your win rate is only 50%, with no edge whatsoever.

In a bullish scenario, what you need to do is identify an A-to-B rally—a breakout or an impulse move—then wait for a pullback, and enter at a reasonable level to capture the C-to-D upward continuation.

In a bearish scenario, you look for an A-to-B decline—a clear selling impulse—then after a bounce pullback, look for the C-to-D continued decline.

What you should do is not predict the market, but identify impulse moves that have already occurred, then wait for a reasonable pullback, and enter after "others have already shown their hand," rather than relying on your own guesses.

Then there is money management.

It's simple, but also critical.

When you believe your win rate is high, you can increase your position size, allowing each high-quality opportunity to generate more profit.

When you find the market uncertain or your win rate declining, you should reduce your position size to lower risk.

Once the market starts moving against you, or you feel disappointed, hesitant, nervous, or afraid, you need to learn to exit quickly.

Don't hold on stubbornly, don't hope the market will come back to save you, and don't fall into the illusion of "just wait a little longer."

Everyone hopes to see that "perfect pattern"—a clear A-B rally, then a nice pullback, followed by a symmetrical C-D leg, with price rising steadily and profits coming easily.

But most of the time, reality isn't that perfect.

You might buy at the close of the B move, thinking the market is very strong, not wanting to miss the opportunity, so you chase in after the last big bull bar.

Then price immediately starts pulling back, and you find yourself having bought at the top, feeling very disappointed.

So you scale in below, trying to lower your average cost, hoping price can bounce back to your original entry point so you can exit at breakeven.

The psychology behind this scaling in is actually "hope." You hope the market will help you just this once, letting you escape.

But trading doesn't survive on hope. Trading depends on logic, discipline, and structure.

When you feel disappointed, what you should do is not add more, not fantasize, but exit quickly, then wait for the next clear structure to appear, and start again.

You're not in the market to "prove yourself right." You're here to make money.

Your task is: identify the A-B move where a trend has already begun, wait for a reasonable pullback, and capture the C-D continuation.

You are not the protagonist of the market. You are just a passenger following behind the large institutions—getting on with the wind at your back, and getting off in time.

This is the true essence of trading.
