# Price Action: Mechanical Trading

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 173rd article. You should also learn Price Action.

The less trading skill and experience you have, the more you need to follow mechanical trading.
Starting with mechanical trading is actually the fastest path to becoming a discretionary trader, yielding results in the shortest time.

Mechanical trading is the foundation of quantitative trading. Rather than agonizing over details, operate according to systematic signals.
Set simple rules—only exit early in the case of extreme volatility.
In all other situations, wait for the bar to close, then exit according to system signals.

For beginners, the higher the degree of systematization and the less subjective input, the better the performance.
You can trade live and remain steady, accumulate experience and develop intuition—these are all valuable growth experiences.
After all, with rich experience, you naturally know which variables to focus on, building confidence. Operations become increasingly refined, almost without deliberate thinking, as the entire process becomes muscle memory.
At that point, they can easily handle discretionary trading.
But for beginners, you must strictly follow the trading system.

If you have already developed the habit of strictly executing trading discipline, you will discover more opportunities within your trading system.
That is to say, your trading system generates eight trading signals per day. When you have solid system signal rules, you'll find that although these eight opportunities don't completely overlap, they still share certain commonalities.
Subjectively and speculatively engaging in intraday analysis will still yield marginal win rate returns, indicating that your chart reading ability is improving.
Going one step beyond what your system provides means you can gradually apply trading rules with more flexibility.

It mainly comes down to the current energy on the chart.
Roughly 20-30% of chart information is meant for intraday traders, but it simply cannot be captured on static charts, since you can't witness bars forming in real time.
Daily charts display bars after the fact, but when you're actually trading, you face rapidly changing live price action.
You can also feel the market's vitality and directly observe the formation process of bars.
This information is crucial for traders and directly influences all types of trading decisions.
That's why you need to watch the screen.
