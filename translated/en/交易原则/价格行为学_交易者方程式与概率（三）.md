# Price Action: The Trader's Equation and Probability (Part 3)

I. Why Focus on the 50% Pullback
In a strong bull channel, when price pulls back from a new high to roughly half the entire leg up, there is about a 60% chance it will retest the prior high before dropping to the channel bottom. Bulls know this and often place buy orders at the 50% level. The same logic applies in reverse for shorting in a strong bear channel — a retracement to the halfway point is a good entry.

II. "False Signals" Within Pullbacks
During a half-measure pullback, a seemingly powerful bounce often appears. As long as the bounce does not clearly exceed the 50% level, it most likely remains a pullback rather than a genuine reversal to the upside. Impulsive buyers easily get trapped here; experienced bears often wait for a second probe down before entering.

III. Calculate the Risk-Reward Clearly
- If the target is less than twice the risk, the payoff is poor.
- A 1:1 scalp is only worthwhile if your win rate can reach 60% or above.
- Set the target at twice the risk, and as long as your win rate is 40%, you will be net profitable over time.

IV. The Difficulty of Short-Term Win Rates
To make money scalping, you need a win rate above 70% to offset the small wins and small losses. Few people achieve this. Most traders are better suited to swing trading, using a 2:1 or higher reward to offset a 40–50% success rate.

V. The 40–60 Rule
Approximately 90% of the time, the market could move one unit higher or one unit lower first, with the probability range being only 40–60%. Truly strong "breakouts" with sustained follow-through account for only about 10%. When a breakout occurs, trade with it; the rest of the time, rely on strict stop-losses and a 2:1 target to capture swings.

VI. The Pros and Cons of Scaling In
Scaling in can raise the win rate, but it also expands total risk and lowers the reward ratio. Beginners unfamiliar with the rhythm should avoid it for now and consider it only after developing a feel for volatility.

VII. Zero-Sum or Positive-Sum
On an intraday basis, the market is close to zero-sum: your gain is someone else's loss. Over a span of years, the economy is expanding overall, so the market is not truly zero-sum. But to survive, you must be better than average. If you cannot identify who is most likely to lose money, that person might be you.

VIII. The Keys to Achieving "Positive Expectancy"
- Enter at positions that are statistically favorable, such as the 50% pullback.
- Set targets at least twice the risk.
- Accept that even your most favored direction has a 40% chance of going the other way.

As long as all three points are satisfied simultaneously, your trading system has a mathematical edge, and the longer you trade, the more that edge accumulates.
