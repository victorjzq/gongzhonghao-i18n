# Price Action: Stand on the Side with Higher Probability

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 205th article. You should also learn Price Action.

After a channel has been running for some time, you'll notice that bar bodies start getting smaller and tails get longer and longer. This indicates that selling pressure is weakening. Eventually, price breaks below the lower boundary of the channel, but instead of continuing to accelerate downward, it enters sideways consolidation. At this point you must be careful—the trend may be ending. What often appears is a so-called "final flag." The last leg of a trend is typically accompanied by a small consolidation, after which the market enters a trading range or even reverses.

If you were previously short, when you see the market entering a final flag state, you should gradually take profit and exit. Don't be greedy; don't fantasize that the trend will extend indefinitely. Trends always end, and the ending signal is often the channel beginning to weaken, tails getting longer, and a consolidation zone forming.

Trading is not predicting. You can never be certain whether this bounce will become a reversal. But you know that in a bear trend, the higher probability scenario is continued decline, not reversal. As long as you trade based on probability and always protect yourself with stop losses, you can remain profitable over the long run.

In a trading range, there is neither strong buyer control nor strong seller control. Bull attempts fail, and bear attempts also fail. Price is confined between certain highs and lows, moving back and forth.

In this situation, many beginners lose money. They see a big bull bar and rush to go long, only to be immediately pushed back; they see a big bear bar and rush to go short, only to be pulled back again. So they get stopped out repeatedly within the trading range.

The correct approach is: once you identify a trading range, you must change your mindset. No longer assume the trend will continue, but instead assume price will oscillate back and forth within the range. Look for shorting opportunities near the highs and buying opportunities near the lows.

Trading within a trading range is very difficult. The range is usually narrow, your potential profit is limited, and your stop loss can't be too small. Many times, the choppy action causes traders to lose patience and make repeated mistakes.

This is also why Al often emphasizes: trading ranges are the most difficult market to trade. Most traders should reduce their trading frequency in trading ranges, or even choose to just watch. You can perfectly well wait until price breaks out of the trading range, then trade with the trend. Because once the trading range ends, the market often produces a very good trend.

Learn to identify trading ranges. Once confirmed, adjust your strategy—don't chase entries as you would in a trend. Instead, trade reversals at support and resistance, or patiently wait for a breakout and then follow the trend.
