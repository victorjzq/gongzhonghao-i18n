# Price Action: The Trader's Equation and Probability (Part 1)

The Trader's Equation and Probability
=========



===

### 1\. No Perfect Trade, Only "Mathematical Edge"

The so-called perfect trade -- high win rate, huge profit, and minimal risk all at once -- simply does not exist in real markets.
What is truly feasible is a "good trade": striking a balance among **win rate, risk, and reward**, so that the **long-term expected value is positive**.


### 2\. Low Risk and High Win Rate Are a Seesaw

*   If you short immediately after an extreme bullish move with a tight stop loss, the risk is small and the reward looks attractive -- but the win rate will inevitably be low.

*   If you wait until two large bearish bars appear, or a double top bear flag forms before shorting, the direction is confirmed and the win rate increases -- the cost is a wider stop loss and greater risk.


No matter which end you choose, the essence is always a tradeoff between **"high win rate - high risk" and "low risk - low win rate"**.

### 3\. The Tradeoff Between Trend and Trading Range

*   **Trending market**: Entry points often come with wider stop losses, yet the win rate is relatively high.

*   **Trading range**: Stop losses can be placed very tight and the risk-reward ratio looks attractive, yet the win rate drops significantly.


Traders must first identify the current environment, then decide whether to prioritize "win rate" or "reward."

### 4\. "Breakout" -- The Closest Thing to a Perfect Moment

Across any instrument and any timeframe, truly strong breakouts account for only **5-10%** of all bars.
When price plunges sharply or surges, producing a huge trend bar:

*   Getting in early at the bar's launch point: smallest risk, highest win rate, greatest potential profit.

*   As the bar grows larger and follow-through weakens, the stop loss moves farther away, and the window of opportunity closes rapidly.

*   Even with an extremely strong breakout, there is only about a **60%** chance it will reach its measured move target; the remaining 40% will fail and reverse.


### 5\. The Trader's Equation

> **Win rate x Expected reward  >  Loss probability x Risk**

Only when the left side exceeds the right side does a trade possess mathematical merit.
When applied in practice, you predetermine your stop loss and target, leaving only the **win rate** to be estimated from experience:

*   In a strong breakout environment, the win rate can be roughly estimated at around **60%**.

*   Within a trading range, the raw win rate may be only **40%**; widening the stop loss and scaling in can moderately raise the win rate.


As long as you plug into the equation and the result is positive, you are making a trade that "will be profitable over the long run."

### 6\. The Edge Is Always Small, Yet Compounds Over Time

In the short term, the market is nearly zero-sum; over a year, a persistent small edge accumulates into substantial profit.
Top traders' margins are equally slim -- the difference is simply that they **never stop executing according to the rules**.

### 7\. Key Takeaways

1.  **You cannot have all three**: high win rate, high reward, and low risk -- at most, pick two.

2.  **Breakout opportunities are fleeting**: the window is tiny; once missed, the risk-reward ratio deteriorates rapidly.

3.  **Math comes first**: make every trade conform to the Trader's Equation, rather than chasing one-time perfection.

4.  **Tolerate imperfection rather than demand perfection**: consistently producing "good trades" is the true long-term competitive advantage.
