# Price Action: Trading Requires Considering Multiple Factors

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 3 years. Today is my 273rd article. You should also learn Price Action.

A trader's discretionary judgment comes from assessing the market: if the trend is strong, the expectation is that there is still room to go higher; even if there is a pullback, price should subsequently test higher levels. If buying during the later stages of a move, a reasonable strategy is to enter with a small position size, leaving room to scale in at lower levels. This is because during later stages of a swing, the market usually consolidates sideways first rather than immediately continuing the trend.

Typically, buying in the later stages of a parabolic wedge pattern (after two pauses) carries high risk even if the breakout continues. At this point, buying often coincides with the market preparing to pull back. Therefore, the best strategy is to buy with a small position, or wait for a limit order entry; you can also wait for a High 1 or High 2 setup to appear, then enter using a stop order or market order, rather than entering directly at the close of that bar.

The order of analysis should be: first observe the higher time frame, then examine the overall structure, and finally examine the local structure.

Counting legs within a tight channel usually points toward a pullback rather than a reversal, unless it's a counter-trend trade. For example, in a bear market, when the higher time frame price is below the moving average (MA), a parabolic wedge rally often completely reverses and drops after a pause.

In a bull market environment, after a bear breakout, the market typically bounces at support. Context and environment take priority. As long as there is a tight channel whose direction aligns with the higher time frame trend, the market will most likely continue to rise after consolidating sideways.

If the leg count shows three legs, you need to observe the overall structure to distinguish the type of wedge: is it a hidden parabolic wedge with only pauses and no real pullbacks, or is it a wedge where the trendline has been clearly broken, forming a broader channel (such as a rally followed by a two-leg correction)? These two situations are entirely different.

No single factor can guarantee a trade will succeed. Every trade requires consideration of at least three factors. If you can only find two factors, you should force yourself to step away from the mouse, because you cannot trade with incomplete information.
