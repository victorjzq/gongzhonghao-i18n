# Price Action: Concept Notes (3)

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 229th article. You should also learn Price Action.

The two most critical elements for distinguishing a breakout pullback from a trap reversal are spacing and gaps. The basis for identifying a pullback is a prominent lower tail. Reversal signals are certainly important, but equally critical is capturing all strong breakout opportunities.

An ideal bar should not be a long-bodied bar that surges sharply and closes at the high. The bars we need should be somewhat larger than average—the bar range should exceed the average bar, yet the bar should not become one of those extreme climax formations. Since climax bars typically retrace before continuing the move, using them as entry signals means enduring an extended pullback.

During the formation of a pattern, many swing lows will appear. Five consecutive bars, all in an uptrend, with each swing low above the previous down trendline—therefore, going short at the moving average (MA) has a low success rate. The best trading strategy is: avoid going short directly; instead, wait for a strong bearish bar to appear, or go short on a pullback after a breakout. There is a 15%-20% probability of missing the trade entirely.

If today is a bear trend day, the success rate of all bull signals is automatically reduced, with greatly diminished odds. You can build positions in batches at the moving average (MA) level. Every signal that appears to be a buy actually conceals a shorting opportunity. This is the typical characteristic of a strong market. The specific technique matters less—what's key is trading with the trend.

Therefore, determining the overall market bias is critically important—anticipating the day's bullish or bearish direction. The higher time frame structure shapes the directional expectation for the day. After all, the core of our trading is capturing direction, which allows us to trade with the trend and follow the market's movement for the day. It's like sailing with the wind—you don't need to expend too much effort. As long as trade management is basically sound, you can profit without exceptionally skilled execution. This is why grasping market direction is so important, because counter-trend trading fails more than 80% of the time.

When bulls spot a strong micro channel, they clearly identify two things: first, there may be a second leg up; second, they establish long positions by buying below strong bull bars. This is the basic trading logic.

The difference between beginners and professional traders is significant: beginners need to see a clear signal bar before they dare to enter (relying on limit orders or market orders), while professionals can see the market structure across multiple time frames and position ahead like market makers. This requires accumulated experience—you need to learn both chart reading and complete trade management. But time alone isn't enough. Using the two-legged pattern trading method as an example, beginners must strictly follow trading rules: initially trade very rarely, signals must be clear, and you must clearly understand the entry pattern, conditions, and when to exit.
