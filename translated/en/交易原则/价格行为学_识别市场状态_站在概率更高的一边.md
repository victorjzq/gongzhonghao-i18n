# Price Action: Identify Market State, Stand on the Higher Probability Side

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 204th article. You should also learn Price Action.

Uptrend, downtrend, and trading range. These three formations encompass virtually all possible market states. Price is always switching between these three states.

Your task is: identify which state the market is currently in as quickly as possible, then select the appropriate trading strategy.

If it's an uptrend, you should stand on the bull side. Look for pullbacks to go long, or wait for breakouts to go long. Don't try to pick the top. Because in a strong trend, trying to pick the top almost always loses money.

If it's a downtrend, you should stand on the bear side. Look for bounces to go short, or wait for breakouts to go short. Don't try to pick the bottom. Because in a strong decline, trying to pick the bottom almost always loses money.

If it's a trading range, you must be especially careful. Trading ranges are the easiest market state for traders to lose money in. If you do want to trade, look for shorting opportunities near the highs and buying opportunities near the lows. Otherwise, it's best to patiently wait for a breakout, then trade with the trend.

I know this might sound simple. But in live trading, the difficulty is: you must make judgments in real time, in the moment, rather than knowing the answer after the fact. The market is always changing, and the transitions between trend and trading range aren't always obvious.

So you must constantly train yourself. Every day you need to look at charts, make annotations, and ask yourself: "Is this a trend or a trading range? If it's a trend, will it continue? If it's a trading range, when might it break out?"

There's also one more extremely important point: don't expect yourself to be 100% correct. Nobody can achieve that. Even Al is often wrong. Trading is not prediction—it's probability. Your goal isn't to be right every single time, but to stand on the higher probability side most of the time.

At the same time, you must control risk. No matter how confident you are, you must set a stop loss. If you're wrong, you must accept a small loss. If you're right, you'll gain a profit larger than your risk. Over time, your trading will be profitable.

The core of trading is three things—identify market state, stand on the higher probability side, and strictly control risk. As long as you can persist in these three points, you can become a consistently profitable trader.

So find your best time of day, and only trade during that window—that's enough.

Many people are afraid that a tight stop loss will get triggered, so they set it far away. But the problem with this is that if the market really moves against you, your loss will far exceed expectations. The correct approach is: the stop loss must be placed at a reasonable location, not placed far away arbitrarily. A reasonable location is a structurally clear spot on the chart, such as a previous high or low.

If you feel that stop loss is too far, making the risk too large, you should reduce your position size rather than endlessly widening the stop loss. With a smaller position, your loss remains within a controllable range. So a distant stop loss doesn't equal safety. True safety comes from a reasonable stop loss and an appropriate position size.

Remember: having the right direction is only the first step; patience in execution is the key. Don't rush—you don't need to always be the first one in.

Remember, the market is always there—there's no need to rush to make back losses. Controlling yourself is the most important thing.
