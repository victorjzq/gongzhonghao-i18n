# Price Action: Be As Mechanical As Possible, Reduce Variables

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 214th article. You should also learn Price Action.

For any breakout of a resistance level, the minimum requirement is one bull bar with a body at least average in size, closing near its high, with the close above the high of the previous bar. A big bull bar closing at its high and above the high of the previous bar—this is a breakout. What did it break out of? At least the high of the previous bar. If you look to the left, you might also see that it broke out above an earlier high.

If a second bar then appears, also a bull bar closing at its high with a relatively large body, you now have two closes above that resistance level. If the entire body of the second bar is above the resistance level, there is a 60% to 70% probability that the market will continue upward in some form of measured move. The larger the bars, the greater the probability of continued upward movement.

Simplify what you do as much as possible. The fewer "actions" the better. In trading, you must be as mechanical as possible and reduce variables.

If a strong bull trend forms a very tight trading range in its later stages, it is likely a "final bull flag." In this situation, the upward breakout often fails and reverses downward.

Always ask yourself: could this be a final bull flag? If there is an upward breakout, look for an opportunity to reverse and go short.

In a bull trend, you go long, and then suddenly a large bear bar appears closing at its low—exit below it. If two more consecutive bear bars then appear, closing at their lows, the market probably has about a 50% probability of having entered a bear trend.

If a trend is the kind that is strong enough for you to "chase and buy at the close," and it suddenly reverses without giving you a chance to take profit, some bulls will wait for the market to strengthen again, forming a nice bull bar, and then scale in above that bar. Doing this gives an 80% probability that price will return at least one tick above your average entry; and a 60% probability that it will return to your first entry level.
