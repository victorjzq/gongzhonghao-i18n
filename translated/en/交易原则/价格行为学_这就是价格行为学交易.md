# Price Action: This Is Price Action Trading

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 150th article. You should also learn Price Action.

About 5% of the time, the market is in a strong breakout—whether up or down—the direction is very clear, and at that point no decision-making is needed; just trade with the trend.

Set your profit target based on "actual risk." So what is actual risk? It's "the minimum stop loss distance needed to avoid being stopped out." Regardless of where you initially placed it, when the market starts moving in your favor, look back and ask: "How large a stop loss would I have needed to not be stopped out?" That distance is your actual risk.

You'll find that many profit targets occur at one or two times the actual risk. This indicates that many algorithmic trades are based on "actual risk" rather than "initial risk," because everyone's initial stop loss is different.

Generally, you should always take partial profit when reaching "the minimum target required by a positive Trader's Equation." If you've done the math and found that "taking profit at this level is reasonable," then you should take profit.

For any trade that moves in your favor, at some point the "mathematical calculation" shows you have a positive Trader's Equation, and you should take partial profit. Since most traders cannot determine the exact probability of success, as long as a profit target of two times "actual risk" is reached, the Trader's Equation becomes positive.

So you should always close at least half your position at "two times actual risk."

In a trading range, there are always people going long and people going short. Both bulls and bears have ways to make money.

You might say: "Then how do I know whether to go long or go short?"

You don't need to know. If you're not sure, as long as you know this is a reasonable setup, you can consider trying.

Once you enter, if the market moves in the direction you don't want, you stop out; if it moves in the direction you expect, you gradually take profit.

You can set partial profit targets, trailing stops, or close the entire position when price reaches your target level.

So how do you set your target?

The simplest method is to use a measured move target. For example, add the height of the signal bar to the breakout point to get a preliminary target. You can also simply calculate one or two times the "actual risk."

You might ask: "Who is right?"

Nobody can be certain which side is right. You can only, before entering, assess as clearly as possible: if the market moves in the direction you don't want, what will you do; if it moves in the right direction, how will you take profit.

Your goal is: only enter when you believe the probability of success is high, then manage the trade with rigorous logic.

That's the entire method. No magic, no secret.

It's just evaluating probability, placing orders where you think it's worth the risk, and gradually taking profit as price moves in your favor.

This is Price Action trading.
