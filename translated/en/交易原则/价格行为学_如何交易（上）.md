# Price Action: How to Trade (Part 1)

## 1. Understand the Phase the Market Is In

Breakout phase: only go long.
Channel phase, especially broad channels: you can go long and also scalp short.
Trading range: if the range is wide enough, you can buy low and sell high, scalping; if the range is too tight, don't trade.

## 2. Common Market Misconceptions

Many people mistakenly believe that only "a few key points" are good for entry.

But when you observe the chart, you'll find that every bar has significant volume.

This means institutional traders consider any moment a good time, as long as you know how to structure your trade.

## 3. The Standard for Measuring Whether a Trade Is Reasonable

We use the Trader's Equation to determine whether a trade is valid:

The Trader's Equation is: probability of success multiplied by potential profit must be greater than the probability of failure multiplied by the potential loss.

As long as this equation is satisfied, it is a mathematically reasonable trade.

## 4. Binary Assessment: Determining Current Market Structure

Common assessment dimensions include:

Is it a bull trend or a bear trend?
Is this a rally within a trading range, or a rally within a trend?
If it's a trend, is it in the breakout phase or the channel phase?

Different phases require different approaches.

## 5. Trading Strategy in a Bullish Structure

If it's a bull breakout, you must go long.
No need for a perfect reason—even a market order entry is fine.

If it's a bull channel, it's suitable to go long on pullbacks. Common pullback long entries include:

The midpoint of the previous rally
A reversal bar after a pullback
A bull flag breakout
Following the trend and going long

## 6. Special Strategy in a Tight Channel Structure

Characteristics of a tight channel:

Each pullback lasts only one to three bars
Pullbacks are small and the pace is fast—suitable for going long with the trend, not suitable for going short

## 7. Limitations and Risks of Going Short in a Tight Channel

When can you go short?
Only when the channel is broad enough to allow for scalping.

Common failure scenarios include:

Going short at the top of the channel, but the market doesn't pull back, resulting in being trapped
Going short at the previous high, but the market continues higher and your stop loss is triggered

Expert strategy:

Enter short with a limit order, placing the order at the high of the previous bar
If stopped out, continue adding short positions
When the market pulls back to between the two entry points, exit at breakeven

Note: this type of operation is only suitable for experienced limit order traders.

## 8. Initial Characteristics of a Trading Range

Many trading ranges initially evolve from a bull channel.
At the beginning they are not obvious, making them difficult to identify.

A common evolution path is:

Bear trend ends, bull breakout occurs
Bull pullback, attempt to go long at the reversal point
Channel is still tight, don't go short yet
Wait for structural signals before considering going short

Common structural signals include:

Wedge structure
Second entry short

Shorting methods:

Place a stop order below a bear bar
Only trade short-term or scalp
Stop trading when the market is quiet and volatility is low

## 9. Assessment Methods in a Bear Trend

Continue using the binary assessment method to determine the current structure:

Is it a bear trend, or a bearish leg within a trading range?

If the channel is broad enough, you can trade both directions
If pullbacks are too small, don't go long

Strong bearish signals:

Bear breakout, with the bar closing below the low of the previous twenty bars
High probability of a second leg down
May evolve into a "spike and channel" structure

Trading strategy:

Regardless of the reason, you must go short
You can use market orders or stop orders
At minimum, establish a small short position

Continued tracking:

If trend continuation bars appear, you can add to shorts
If reversal attempts fail and the structure remains tight, you can continue adding

If a double top structure, small wedge, or choppy back-and-forth appears:

You can go short at reversal points
Even if the stop loss is wide, you can participate with a small position; otherwise, don't participate
