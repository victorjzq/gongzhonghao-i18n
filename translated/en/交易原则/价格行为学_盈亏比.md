# Price Action: Risk/Reward Ratio

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 3 years. Today is my 266th article. You should also learn Price Action.

A large risk/reward ratio does not equal a real trading edge—it is not the same as expected value.

Looking for the point with the greatest risk/reward ratio is essentially guessing—it's a low-probability endeavor.

You cannot know in advance which point will become the "farthest starting point of a one-directional move."

So building a system around "catching the perfect starting point" is fundamentally staking the outcome on an unobservable variable.

Don't pursue getting in at the cheapest price in one shot. Start with a small position to test; when the market proves you're right, scale in; once the structure deteriorates, exit quickly.

This replaces "guessing the point" with "the market providing proof."

Use confirmed scaling in to replace a single all-in bet.

If the market moves in your direction, you scale in—specifically following Price Action rules for scaling in.

It may seem like your position is getting larger. From a single-trade perspective, it does feel like the risk/reward ratio per entry is declining.

But from a system perspective, what you sacrifice is the theoretical maximum multiple of a single entry; what you gain is that in a genuine one-directional move, your overall average cost moves with price, and your position is maximized in the right tail.

So the more accurate way to put it is: it's not that the risk/reward ratio gets worse, but rather you've changed "high multiples from entry point" to "high multiples from position distribution."

At the same time, because you exit quickly, and the volatility of a single bar is limited, this greatly minimizes losses.

In other words, the actual risk is not that large.

System expectancy doesn't come from a single entry, but from "leveraging returns when you're right."

Many systems have negative skew:

They have high win rates most of the time, with a smooth equity curve, but they rely on holding through drawdowns or mean reversion to grind through. Then one extreme move wipes out months of profits.

Single-trade risk/reward ratio is not expected value, nor is it an edge. Scaling in concentrates your position in the right moves, making the overall return distribution more favorable.

Al Brooks' methodology also involves multiple scaling-in entries and quick exits.
