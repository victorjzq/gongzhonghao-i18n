# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Probability

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 148th article. You should also learn Price Action.

Regarding market direction, 95% of the time it is actually unclear. It is never as clear as we hope.

When bulls look at a trading range, they always want to buy when price goes a little lower; when bears look at a trading range or channel, they always want to sell when price goes a little higher. When price dips slightly, buyers become more aggressive and sellers become more passive, which pushes the market slightly higher; when price rises slightly, sellers become more aggressive and buyers become more passive, which pushes the market back down.

The result is: the market spends most of its time in a sideways trading range.

Within a trading range, most of the time price is in the middle zone. In this middle area, bulls believe the current price level offers buying value, while bears believe it offers selling value.

Sometimes bulls and bears reach a consensus—both agree that price is too low—and the market quickly rises in the form of a bull breakout. Sometimes they agree that price is too high, and the market experiences a bear breakout, dropping rapidly.

Once the market makes a fast move, the 60/40 rule no longer applies.

The Trader's Equation involves three variables: probability, risk, and reward. Among these, "probability" is the most important. It is also the most difficult part of trading to deal with. Probability is the root of all trading emotions; it prevents traders from making completely rational decisions.

Beginners often miss potentially excellent trading opportunities because they feel "the probability of success isn't high enough." They also frequently exit too early or too late due to probability uncertainty, missing out on profits.

Generally, you should give yourself only two choices for the "probability" variable:

If you feel a trade "will likely succeed," then assume the probability of success is 60%. In this case, you should trade with a "target equals risk" approach—for example, if you're risking 50 cents on a stock, aim to make 50 cents.

But most of the time, you're not that confident. In that case, the probability might only be 40% or 50%. When you're uncertain, you should set a "target equals two times risk."

Let me say it again:

If you feel this trade "should succeed," then assume the probability of success is 60% or higher, because only when you think it has at least a 60% chance of success would you call it a "good buy point."

We trade every day in an environment full of uncertainty. Only during strong breakouts does the market briefly reveal a clear direction.

The vast majority of the time, trading is ambiguous and unclear, and the win rate for any trade falls between 40% and 60%.

So if your win rate on a trade is 40% or 50%, the opposite side—the traders positioned against you—also have a 40% to 50% chance of success.

Most of the time, both bulls and bears have a reasonable path to profit.

The Trader's Equation works like this: if your probability of success is low, only 40%, but you're aiming for a reward of two times your risk, then your Trader's Equation is still positive.

In most cases, if bulls or bears simply trade with a "reward equals two times risk" approach, even with a win rate below 50%, they can still make money.

We must wait for a buy point to appear—wait for that moment on the chart that makes us feel this trade "might succeed." Only then do we enter.

This is the definition of a "buy point": a chart pattern that makes you feel "this trade will likely succeed."

We are not looking for trades that are "certain to succeed," because such certainty simply doesn't exist. We are only looking for opportunities that "feel like they will succeed."
