# Price Action: Good Trading Rules

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 3 years. Today is my 279th article. You should also learn Price Action.

Whether a method is good or not mainly depends on whether it can be executed repeatedly and consistently over a long period by an ordinary person, even during emotional ups and downs.

A system that allows itself even "one instance of non-execution" will almost certainly collapse over the long term.

Many Price Action concepts are individually correct, but the problem is that they weren't designed for execution. Each point makes perfect sense when examined in isolation, but once placed in live trading, it becomes like this: you don't know where to start your assessment, you don't know which point takes priority over another, and you don't know when to apply it versus when not to.

As long as a rule requires you to first think "what should I look at now?" during live trading, that rule is essentially unsuitable for real combat.

I later became increasingly clear about one thing: the sequence itself is the most important element in a trading system.

The human brain doesn't think sequentially—it simultaneously generates many voices and many judgments. If rules haven't pre-established the sequence, then under high pressure you can only make ad-hoc judgments and trade-offs. The result is usually hesitation when you should execute, delay when you should be decisive, and eventually emotions take over and rules become distorted.

Truly good rules lay out the sequence for everything that needs thinking, all in advance. Once you're in the market, you only do two things: did you see it or not, and if you saw it, execute. You only need to know what the next step is.

For example, first look at the signal bar. If the signal bar is bullish, then you only think about going long. If the signal bar is bearish, then you only think about going short. This single step already cuts half the disagreements for you. This is respecting human execution capability.

Trading has never been about who can explain things more elaborately or understand more deeply. What truly creates the gap is whether, when the market moves fast, you can do the same action again and again.

When the sequence is clear, you don't need to think on the spot. When you don't need to think on the spot, rules can be executed over the long term.
