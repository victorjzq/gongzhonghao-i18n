# Price Action: Binary Decisions

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 195th article. You should also learn Price Action.

Formulate your process in a binary decision tree so that each node has only two choices.

Each node in a binary decision tree has only two possible choices.

In complex environments, people are often held back by hesitation. The best way to solve this problem is binary decision-making: each step can only have two outcomes.

If a problem cannot be reduced to a binary choice, it means you haven't thought it through clearly. Binary thinking forces you to make a judgment: either yes or no. Wherever there is ambiguity, that is where mistakes happen.

Many problems are complex and cannot be resolved in a single step. In that case, you need to layer the problem—break complex issues apart and reduce each layer to a binary choice. Working backwards is a form of verification: if any layer cannot be reduced to a binary choice, it means there are gaps in your thinking.

True simplicity is not about thinking less, but about making every node binary. When the system forms a closed loop, you won't panic, because you know that no matter what happens, it comes down to "yes" or "no."

The world of computers has only 0 and 1, yet it supports an entire civilization. If it were not binary, the error rate would be amplified infinitely.

Complex systems become chaotic because they exceed the binary framework. Successful paths are all similar, but there are countless ways to fail. When you can't execute, it's often not because the market is too complex, but because some conditions in your system haven't been reduced to binary choices.

Don't overcomplicate things—this doesn't mean rejecting new elements, but rather that new elements must be classifiable in binary terms. For example, when using a moving average (MA), you must define it clearly. Vague descriptions only lead to confusion.

A real system is a method that can be implemented through binary decisions.

The difficulty in building a binary framework lies in: grinding vague knowledge into something actionable.

The ancients said: "One gives birth to two, two gives birth to three, and three gives birth to all things." Two represents order; three represents chaos. This is the same as the "three-body problem": two bodies are predictable, but three enter chaos.

Therefore, two is the boundary, and three is chaos. To maintain stability amid complexity, you must compress the boundary back to two.
