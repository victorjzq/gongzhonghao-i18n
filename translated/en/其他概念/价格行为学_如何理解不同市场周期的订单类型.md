# Price Action: How to Understand Order Types for Different Market Cycles

First, look at the big picture and current market state — this always comes first. Figure out which phase of the market cycle we're currently in.

The market cycle can be roughly divided into several phases:
It generally starts with a powerful surge — a new breakout officially launching. During this phase, stop orders and market orders are the most appropriate order types. After the spike, it enters the channel phase — the second half of the entire trend. Channels can be tight or loose. A tight channel, viewed on a higher timeframe, still qualifies as a breakout; if the channel widens progressively, it's telling you this move is entering its final stage. Wide trading range with bulls and bears battling intensely — the channel phase eventually evolves into a trading range.

Once a trading range forms, first assess its width and adjust your approach accordingly. If the range is very narrow, limit orders are best; if it's extremely narrow, don't trade at all. If the range is wide enough, the same logic as wide channels applies, and all order types can be used. Entry points should be determined by combining the pattern itself, the signal pattern, the current market context, and the conditions that formed the signal. Trends often resume their direction after a trading range. But when a trend enters its late stage, it's best to watch and be ready to catch a reversal at any time.

In small-pullback rallies, there are more pullbacks, and the optimal strategy is to fade false breakouts in the counter direction — you can place orders below the previous bar's low. Another approach is to buy directly at the close of a bear bar, since pullbacks typically last only one or two bars. But by the time you see a signal bar, the market has often already moved a significant distance, and risk expands accordingly.

Wide channels are hard to trade because the market pulls back deep enough to momentarily switch to "Always In Short." But once it becomes "Always In Short," it happens to hit a trendline and immediately reverses — the market re-enters "Always In Long" mode. But once it enters "Always In Long," it's often already at the top of the channel, ready to reverse again. Because of this constant back-and-forth, trading wide channels requires a higher skill level.

The first step is always to ask: What's the current context? This is your ace that beats all quantitative machines. Because fully digesting the visual language in front of you simply cannot be packed into code. The information you absorb from a single glance at a chart is far more than what can be written into an algorithm, and it's more accurate. Investing your effort in context analysis — understanding what the market is actually doing — gives you a decisive advantage over all computers and algorithms.
