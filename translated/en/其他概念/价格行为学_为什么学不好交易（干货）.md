# Price Action: Why Most People Can't Learn Trading Well (Practical Insights)

Why do so many people fail to learn Price Action well? Many people study it, but the result is often that the more they learn, the more they lose. Various explanations emerge: some say Al Brooks is too verbose and unclear, others say the text volume is too large and the videos too long.

Ali mentioned the "concept spiral" — you keep learning, repeating, and reviewing, and as the number of study cycles increases and understanding deepens, you suddenly reach a moment of entirely new insight. Your cognitive dimension is elevated, and things you couldn't understand before suddenly "click." This description is superficially correct, but it only explains the phenomenon, not the cause. The real problem isn't that "you haven't studied enough," but whether you've followed the inherent learning rules of abstract concepts.

Price Action is a highly abstract conceptual system, and the vast majority of people have no methodology for "how to learn abstract concepts."

Wan Weigang mentioned in "Elite Daily Course" that an important characteristic of elites is that they enjoy and excel at discussing abstract concepts. Ordinary people are more accustomed to talking about concrete things: what they ate today, whether you've eaten, how someone is doing lately. Abstract thinking ability determines whether a person can understand patterns, structures, and systems, rather than staying at the level of specific events and surface experiences.

Elon Musk gave an excellent example in an interview: If a Kryptonian came to Earth, they would naturally be as powerful as "Superman." But this "Superman" isn't powerful because of innate talent — it's because of capability transfer. In the auto industry, mass production is a very ordinary, mature capability. But when this capability is transferred to rocket manufacturing, it immediately becomes a decisive, enormous advantage. What enables this cross-domain transfer isn't a specific technique, but the ability to understand abstract structures.

Abstract concepts are a formidable skill — not everyone naturally possesses it. If you try to discuss these issues with someone whose abstract thinking ability is extremely weak, it often becomes like talking past each other. From the perspective of the person who doesn't understand, you're easily labeled as "all talk, impractical, can't be applied." But the problem isn't that you're being vague — it's that the other person lacks the decoding ability to understand what you're saying.

This leads to a very typical challenge: What's the use of studying all this? You talk about what love is, what essence is, what structure is — what use are these abstract things?

The value of abstract concepts is never immediately visible. Their returns manifest in the long term, through transfer and reuse.

YJango is the person I've seen who truly explains "how to learn abstract concepts" clearly.

In "Gradual Construction: World Model," he provided a very systematic exposition of this issue.

Learning happens through lexical network constraints and old meaning recombination.

**Lexical network constraint** refers to when a word to be learned appears in diverse sentences, forming a relationship network with many known words. This network, through **grammatical rules** and **collocational conventions**, imposes multiple constraints on the word, thereby assigning specific attributes to the "abstract concept" it represents.

This leads to three prerequisites that cannot be skipped.

First, you must precisely master foundational concepts. Any abstract concept is explained through existing vocabulary. If your understanding of these foundational words is already off, subsequent reasoning will continuously amplify errors, creating entropy-increasing mismatch. For example, understanding "spontaneous" in physics as "voluntary" in everyday language — the entire logical chain is already derailed from the start.

Second, you must be able to correctly parse grammatical structure. Many people severely underestimate the importance of grammar. The same sentence, with different grammatical interpretations, can lead to completely opposite conclusions. Al Brooks is a native English speaker. People with strong English ability can directly parse the modification relationships and logical structures in the original text, resulting in a more stable, systematic understanding of Price Action. However, those who heavily rely on translated texts and secondhand interpretations have their grammatical structures broken and abstract relationships flattened, multiplying the learning difficulty.

Third, you must clearly define the behavioral goal of learning. The goal of learning is never "memorizing definitions" but gradually constructing generalizable models — forming the ability to predict unseen situations. If the goal isn't clear, even if you understand the text, you'll fall into inefficient wholesale memorization, unable to truly grasp the discriminating conditions of concepts.

Precisely because learning abstract concepts must rely on precise foundational definitions and be gradually understood through lexical network constraints, certain learning approaches are doomed to fail. There are many teachers on the market who compress various trading philosophies into short "essence versions," summarizing them into a few rules or sentences for teaching. The greatest harm of this approach is that it gives learners the illusion of "having already learned it," while actually cutting off the formation of lexical network constraints. Once you encounter abstract concepts through this highly compressed, decontextualized, unconstrained method, it's nearly impossible to truly learn them. In terms of outcomes, it's essentially predetermined: virtually no one who follows this learning approach long-term will truly learn to trade.

Price Action is precisely the disaster zone for abstract learning failure. It is itself an abstract concept — no concrete "Price Action entity" exists in reality — so it cannot be learned through imitation or just by looking at more charts. Learning Price Action can only rely on lexical network constraints. But in reality, most learners neglect foundational definitions — for example, never seriously figuring out under what conditions Always In Long holds and how it constrains subsequent judgments; neglect grammatical rules by learning through translated materials, leading to structural misunderstanding; and have wrong learning goals, only pursuing "understanding the text" without ever clarifying what they need to judge — entry conditions, exit conditions, or which situations require doing nothing. The result is that they appear to understand but cannot generalize; they can explain but cannot apply.

In the AI era, this gap will only widen further. Abstract ability is essentially cross-domain capability transfer, and AI will dramatically increase the efficiency of this transfer and verification. This is also why I've repeatedly said in the group: if trading has been mastered to graduation level, the truly valuable discussions are certainly outside of trading — in literature, history, philosophy, and thinking.
