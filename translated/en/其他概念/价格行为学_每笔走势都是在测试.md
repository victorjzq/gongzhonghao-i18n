# Price Action: Every Move Is a Test

Every move is a test.
A "test" means the market returns to a support or resistance area. Traders typically place orders based on how the market performs during the test.
If the breakout test fails, the trend reverses and forms a new breakout.
A trend reversal is essentially a failed test, and a successful test necessarily leads to a failed reversal.

A successful test promotes trend continuation — this is essentially a failed reversal. A failed reversal is a pullback pattern.

So whenever a reversal pattern appears, it necessarily involves a breakout. Similarly, when a test pattern appears, it means a reversal is forming. This is what creates tests. Test results can either succeed or fail.

These concepts are closely interconnected. They are linked together because they are all part of the overall market mechanism.

Every reversal is a breakout in the opposite direction of the original trend. Price action is never random fluctuation. Every move is testing some key level.

Second entry traps are essentially a form of exhaustion pattern. They don't belong to the trend continuation, breakout, or complete trend categories. Of course, in terms of pattern they're all breakouts — after all, they all break through some price area. But structurally, second entry traps are essentially a form of exhaustion pattern. Exhaustion patterns don't have the continuity that trends do. This is the most fundamental difference. Second entry traps usually feature consolidation or other characteristics in between.

If price pulls back, observe the specific pattern of this pullback. Could it initiate a bear trend? So scaling in cannot be done blindly. You must also observe the signals the market gives. If there's sufficient reason to be extremely disappointed with the move, then don't scale in or change your plan.

The point where you exit may be exactly when the market is forming a support layer or testing a key level. But regardless, you always have to choose somewhere to exit. So at least exit first, then reassess the market. Yes, this is indeed a very important psychological issue you've raised — almost every trader encounters it. "But if I sell and exit now, the next bar will definitely reverse." But you don't know yet what will happen. You haven't seen what the next bar will do. So you must establish trading rules for handling every situation. This requires deep thinking. If you don't want to trade systematically but want to trade discretionarily, you must deeply analyze and develop a plan for every scenario.
But you must set stop loss levels — they can't be perfect. So don't pursue perfection. Exit first, then reassess. If exiting was the wrong decision, correct it promptly. This is the so-called principle of immediate trading.
