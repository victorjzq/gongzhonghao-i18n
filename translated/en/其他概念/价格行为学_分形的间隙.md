# Price Action: Gaps in Fractal Structure

Recently I took a break from writing because I was studying criminal law and working on a criminal complaint for a fraud case. Fundamentally, it's also about studying the boundaries of rules, which is no different from studying trading.

Back to trading — the market is a **fractal structure**, and within fractal structures, **gaps exist**.

There are certain **positions that should not be reached**.

It is the shared stop loss level across both larger and smaller timeframes.

As long as price hasn't touched this level, the analysis assumptions on the larger timeframe remain intact.

But once price actually reaches this level, the assumptions no longer hold.

**Within the current analytical framework, if price reaches this point, you are no longer trading within the same logic.**

The closer price gets to this level, the less risk you bear, yet the logic hasn't deteriorated.

**The closer the smaller timeframe enters near this level, the better the risk/reward ratio of the trade actually becomes.**

Precisely because of this, this level should not be moved arbitrarily.

It is not a tool for "dynamic position management" — it is the boundary that defines whether your analysis is still valid.

Once you frequently adjust it, you are actually constantly modifying the premises of your analysis,

and the originally clear risk/reward ratio advantage will disappear along with it.

Re-entering and adjusting the stop loss level will lead to repeated losses.

Of course, this doesn't mean you must hold your position at all times.

You can choose to actively exit, then re-enter based on Price Action to achieve a better cost basis.

But the stop loss boundary that both larger and smaller timeframes point to has not itself changed.

From this perspective, there are indeed some "better positions" in trading.

These positions are not derived from predicting targets,

but appear near the gaps in fractal structure —

those places that, under the current logic, price should not have reached.

Understanding this, you'll realize:

The risk/reward ratio is not something artificially designed,

but rather a result inherently provided by the fractal structure itself.
