# Price Action: Always In

### 1. What Is Always In?

If you must always be in a trade, you should look back to the left side of the chart, find the last point where the market direction was clear, and hold a position in that direction.

Trading in the "Always In" direction tends to make it easier to profit.

* * *

#### 2. How Traders Enter Long Positions

There are many ways to enter:

*   Buy on a pullback;

*   Buy on a pullback breakout;

*   Buy after a bear bar closes, betting that the bearish reversal will fail;

*   Place a buy limit order below a bar;

*   Buy at market price -- as long as the direction is right and position size is reasonable, there is an opportunity to profit.

* * *

#### 3. How to Determine If This Is a Swing Trade?

When a trader believes a move has the potential to achieve **a profit target of at least twice the risk**, it is a swing trade.

Methods to determine this include:

*   Measuring based on the height of the trading range above;

*   Estimating based on the height of the current breakout bar itself;

*   Judging whether the market is more likely to move up or down first -- for example, whether it will rise 10 points or fall 10 points first.

As long as you can determine which side is more likely to be reached first, you can establish a swing position accordingly.

* * *

#### 4. The Principle Behind Always In Trading

If the market is in a trend, a trader can enter in the direction of the trend for any reason.

"Always In trading" is a simple and effective entry method:

*   Any chart, any time, any bar can be an entry point;

*   As long as the direction is clear, combined with an appropriate stop loss and reasonable position size, it is an acceptable trade;

*   Even if there is a loss, it should be a loss that your overall trading system can absorb.

* * *

#### 5. Determining the Always In Direction Is Not Easy

Most of the time, traders are in a state of uncertainty:

*   It is difficult to determine whether a reversal has truly changed the direction;

*   Sometimes you only realize the "Always In direction" has changed after the market has moved a long way.

A more prudent approach is to:

*   Wait for a **breakout + follow-through**;

*   A series of strong bars confirming the trend;

*   Or wait for a more obvious reversal structure to appear before considering a change in direction.

* * *

#### 6. When Should You Exit?

A trader can enter for any reason, but should hold the position until a clear counter-trend signal appears.

For example:

*   A bear goes short at a certain point;

*   He can hold the position until he sees a reversal structure: such as a micro double bottom + a three-push decline forming a wedge;

*   Or after a double bottom structure, the first breakout fails, followed by a successful second breakout.

This type of structure is a signal of trend reversal and an opportunity to take profits or trade in the opposite direction.
