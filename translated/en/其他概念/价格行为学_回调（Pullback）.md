# Price Action: Pullback

## I. What Is a "Pullback"?

In an uptrend:

- When a bar's **low is lower than the previous bar's** low, it constitutes a pullback;

---

## II. What Is an "Implied Pullback"?

Some pullbacks aren't visible on the current timeframe, but **switching to a smaller timeframe** (e.g., from 5-minute to 1-minute) reveals them.

How to identify:

- The current bar **hasn't broken the prior low**;
- But it has a **long lower tail** or is a counter-trend bar;
- In essence: a pullback occurred within the smaller timeframe → this is an "implied pullback."

**Significance:**

- The trend is pausing and may resume at any time;
- It's a noteworthy entry signal.

---

## III. Why Are Pullbacks High-Probability Trading Opportunities?

Many traders will:

- Wait for a pullback to appear;
- **Enter as the pullback ends**, catching a ride on the trend resumption.

Even just an "implied pullback," like a bar with a lower tail, can be **a valid entry point**.

---

## IV. When Does a "Pullback" Stop Being a Pullback?

When a pullback **lasts more than 20 bars**, it's no longer a pullback — it's a trading range.

This means:

- The market is no longer clearly biased toward bulls or bears;
- Bulls see double bottoms, High 2 patterns;
- Bears see double tops, lower highs;
- Both sides' win rates **approach 50%**, and signals become neutral.

---

## V. Where Should the Stop Loss Go?

When going long in a trend (e.g., breakout buy):

- **The stop loss should not be placed only below the signal bar**;
- The correct position is: **the start of the most recent up leg** or **the bottom of the previous strong breakout**.

In other words:

Regardless of which bar you enter on, **the stop loss position is the same** — it's related to the trend structure, not the entry point's high or low.

---

## VI. How Do Institutions Manage Trend Risk?

When institutions trade trends:

- They uniformly place their stop loss at the trend's starting point;
- The stronger the trend, **the farther the stop loss**, but **the greater the risk**.

How do they control it?

- They **take partial profits** in batches, reducing overall risk;
- Bulls taking profits → the market pulls back;
- Bears lack strength → another long entry.

This is the common reason for "pullback + resumed rally" patterns in trends.

---

## VII. The Critical Significance of the 50% Pullback

When the market retraces from the trend high to half (50%) of the move:

- Bulls and bears have **symmetric risk/profit**;
- But since the context is a bull trend, the bulls' win rate is **far higher than the bears'**.

Therefore:

The 50% pullback area is usually **the highest "value" buy point for bulls**.

---

## VIII. What If the Pullback Breaks Below the Previous Leg's Starting Point?

Sometimes the market will:

- Pull back **below the starting point of the previous up leg**;
- But then reverse strongly and continue rising.

This can still be viewed as a "large-scale pullback," especially when:

- Structures like **wedge bottoms**, **micro double bottoms**, or **long lower tails** appear; as long as the trend context isn't destroyed, it's still considered a pullback.

---

## IX. When Can It "No Longer Be Called a Pullback"?

When three conditions occur:

1. The pullback creates a new low;
2. It breaks below the entire trend's starting point;
3. Consecutive strong bear bars appear with **no bounce signal**;

Then:

- The trend may have ended;
- The market may be entering a trading range or even transitioning to a bear trend.

---

## X. Bar Counting: A Supplementary Entry Tool

Many people ask: "When is the best time to enter?"

One answer is: **count bars.**

Definition:

- You observe the number of "resumption attempts" within a trend;
- Each time the market attempts to continue the trend, it gives you a signal;
- The more definitive the trend and the more attempts, the more confidence you have to enter.

---

## XI. High 1 / High 2 in Practice

During a pullback in an uptrend:

- **High 1**: The first bar whose high breaks above the previous bar's high;
- **High 2**: After High 1 fails and the pullback continues, the second time the high is broken.

Strategy:

- Attempt to go long at High 1;
- If it fails → wait for High 2 as a higher-probability entry point.

---

## XII. When to Stop Counting Highs?

If you've already seen:

- **High 4, High 5, or even High 6**...

→ The market has very likely:

- Entered a trading range;
- Or reversed into a **bear trend**.

At this point:

- It's no longer advisable to continue going long;
- You should switch to watching, or even consider going short.

---

## XIII. High 4 Bull Flag & Consecutive Bottom Structures

A typical structure:

- First, High 1 and High 2 appear;
- Then High 1 and High 2 appear again;
- Forming a **High 4 Bull Flag**, also called **consecutive bottoms**.

This represents the **second attempt at reversal** within a pullback, usually with a **higher win rate**.

---

## XIV. High 5, High 6: You Should Be Going Short

When you continue counting to High 5, High 6...

The market has often already entered:

- A bear leg within a trading range;
- Or a genuine bear trend.

At this point:

- Stop looking for bull pullback buy points;
- Start looking for **sell rallies (bear entry points after bounces)**.

---

## Summary

Pullbacks are **the highest-value opportunities** in trend trading, but you must clearly see whether they still qualify as "pullbacks":

- Strong trend + implied or explicit pullback = enter with the trend;
- Pullback too long or too deep = may be entering a trading range or reversal.

The real money isn't in "guessing direction" — understanding structure is the key to escaping the low-win-rate trap.
