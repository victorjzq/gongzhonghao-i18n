# Price Action: Trading Is an Extremely Difficult Skill

Trading is an extremely difficult skill that requires not only learning technical analysis but also training your psychology and discipline. Most people need three to five years before they can truly become consistent.

So if you've only been studying for a year and feel you're not good enough, you're being too hard on yourself. Think about it — how many years does it take to become a doctor? How many years to become a lawyer? Why would you think that something as complex as trading can be learned in just a few months?
As long as you're learning and practicing every day, improving a little bit each day compared to yesterday, you are definitely on the path to success. The key is not to give up.

One more important point: you need to find a style that suits you. Some people prefer short-term trading, others prefer medium to long-term; some people prefer breakouts, others prefer reversals. You must find the approach that best fits your personality and schedule through continuous experimentation. Only then can you persevere.

In live trading, you're always being controlled by emotions.
There are two solutions. First, you must keep a trading journal. Every time you make a mistake, write down the reason. For example, you chased because you were afraid of missing out; you tried to pick a bottom because you felt price had already fallen a lot. Write it down, review it repeatedly, until you are completely clear about your own mistakes.
Second, you must establish clear rules. For example, "If price has already produced three consecutive large trend bars, I absolutely will not chase." Or, "If the market is still in a trading range, I absolutely will not try to pick a bottom." Write these down and post them next to your computer.

Every time you make a mistake, force yourself to look back at these rules. Over time, you will gradually reduce these errors.
Trading isn't about being perfect — it's about continuous improvement. As long as you can reduce mistakes, even by just 10%, your profitability will improve noticeably.

Scaling in is an advanced technique. Many professional traders use it, but the prerequisite is that you must be very experienced and able to control risk well.
If you're a beginner, I don't recommend scaling in frequently. Because most people, once they add to their position, end up with a larger overall position, and when a pullback comes, it creates enormous psychological pressure.
The most common approach is: enter with half your position first, and if the market moves in your direction and the breakout is confirmed, add the remaining half. This way, you can both control risk and amplify gains within a trend.

The key is: your total risk cannot exceed the plan. For example, if you plan to risk a maximum of 1% of your account per trade, then even after scaling in, the total risk must still be controlled within 1%. Otherwise, scaling in becomes gambling.
