# Price Action: When Support and Resistance Fail

From the single-bar level, whenever these buy zones or support zones fail, they become resistance zones for the side that has captured the area. Once an area is clearly broken below with a close beneath it, it means the buyers who were defending that zone have failed, and that price zone transforms from "support" to "resistance." When the market pulls back to test these areas, it will stop falling near these "former resistance levels."

The role of momentum is that the side controlling momentum will invalidate those price zones. In high-momentum situations, you expect the trend to resume immediately — at this point, the closing price can also be considered a buy zone, because you expect price to rise immediately.

High momentum destroys the prerequisite for "mean reversion trading": price must oscillate within the range for pending orders to capture the swings.

In high-momentum conditions, you should expect sell zones to fail and the market to break through them. Essentially, high momentum invalidates market-maker trading.

If a bar's sell zone fails, then there should now be buying at that zone, and when price pulls back to test that area, a bounce should occur. So when you see momentum, expect more buying, especially above strong bull bars; in a bear trend, expect more selling below strong bear bars.

Traders bet that market momentum and directionality will persist for at least a few more bars, so they're willing to trade in originally low-probability zones — for example, selling at the buy zone of a bear bar.

People holding positions in the wrong direction with highly concentrated stop-trigger levels are trapped traders. Momentum breaks through a zone, causing traders to get trapped, and the other side — knowing they're trapped — enters aggressively. Some traders are desperate to exit, and traders who know their predicament seize the opportunity to enter. Most of the time, two different scenarios work in combination.

For example, when price touches a resistance zone and the daily bar has a larger operating logic, this affects the lower timeframe's price action, which in turn affects individual bars' buy and sell zones.

If you monitor higher timeframes while wanting to trade lower timeframes, you typically need to scale in flexibly with very small positions, because lower timeframe chart rhythms are slower. So lower timeframe charts require more bars to align with the higher timeframe.

When this alignment is supposed to break, enormous traps often emerge. Observe the lower timeframe chart to verify whether the current price action is consistent with higher timeframe expectations.

But if you want to control your trades personally — not just place orders and walk away, but fine-tune your entry points, build positions through multiple buys to achieve a better average cost, and then hold for several days.

Pay attention to the comparison between breakout magnitude and counter-trend move magnitude. Once you notice counter-trend moves becoming deeper and larger, even exceeding the breakout direction's magnitude, the market has definitely entered a trading range. At that point you can start building positions: buying below strong bars and scaling in lower.

The small discounts gained through intraday support and resistance levels will, over the long term, become a significant component of your profit and loss.

If you trade higher timeframes, you can use intraday price action to obtain more favorable entry prices rather than buying or selling directly on the higher timeframe. A more advanced application is combining options to amplify returns: you can build option positions in intraday batches.
