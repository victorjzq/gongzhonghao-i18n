# Learning Price Action Using Gabriel's Method

First, exhaustively list all the questions you must answer, then learn backward from real results, filling in only the understanding necessary to complete your judgment.

Price Action, by its very nature, is not suited for the "start from basics, learn step by step" approach.

This problem is amplified infinitely when learning a system like **Al Brooks'**.

The traditional learning path is typically:

First learn concepts, then rules, then patterns, and finally try to "apply them comprehensively."

But the real nature of Price Action is not like this.

It's not a "collection of rules" but an **integrated behavioral system**.

What you truly need to understand is not:

What is H2, how is an inside bar defined.

But rather:

What phase is the market currently in,

which side has the advantage,

which behaviors are inevitable.

If you learn these things the "basics first, then synthesis" way, it's almost guaranteed to be painful.

---

Starting from the ultimate goal,

first exhaustively list the questions you must answer,

and only learn the knowledge necessary to achieve the goal.

The most critical point of this method is:

it doesn't pursue "comprehensive mastery" but rather **"sufficient to complete the judgment."**

---

Step one is to change your learning goal.

Stop using these goals:

"I want to systematically finish studying Price Action,"

"I want to finish reading the book."

Instead, switch to more specific, verifiable goals, such as:

Can I look at any chart and determine whether it's a trend, channel, or trading range?

Can I explain: why is continuation more likely here, and why is failure more likely there?

If I go long here, what behavior am I actually assuming?

Price Action must target **judgment ability**, not **volume of knowledge**.

---

Step two is to exhaustively list "the questions you must answer."

Before actually starting to learn, list out the questions first.

For example:

From the structural level:

Is the current move a spike, channel, or trading range?

Is the market expanding or contracting?

From the behavioral level:

Is this signal bar trying to break out, or is it a failed test?

Is this pullback a healthy retracement, or momentum exhaustion?

From the probability level:

In this context, why is the with-trend win rate higher?

Why do most breakouts fail, but some failures are actually opportunities?

From the boundary level:

Under what conditions would this judgment fail?

If the market doesn't follow expectations, what's the most reasonable explanation?

You'll discover:

**These questions are more important than any specific pattern.**

---

Next, learn backward from real charts, not forward from concepts.

Open a real chart,

and for every point you can't understand or judge reliably, ask backward:

What role does this bar play in the current structure?

If I ignore it, what judgment error would result?

What core concept does it depend on?

Without using jargon, how would you explain it in one sentence?

What you learn is no longer "patterns"

but: **why is the market doing this here.**

---

The test of understanding is not "I get it" but **"I can explain it."**

The most dangerous state in Price Action learning is:

"I understand it, but I can't articulate it."

A very effective method is:

After watching a segment of price action, narrate the entire thing in your own words:

Who is controlling the market now,

what is the counterparty waiting for,

what is the most reasonable next move.

If you can't explain it clearly, it means you don't truly understand.

Price Action, in essence, is a **narrative discipline**, not a memorization discipline.

---

When questions are exhausted, understanding naturally connects.

Many people think Price Action requires "talent" or "inspiration."

But the reality is:

When you've asked and thought through more than 90% of the key questions,

that remaining bit of "inspiration" will naturally appear.

At that moment, you'll discover:

Patterns are no longer isolated,

concepts begin to explain each other,

and you can anticipate what the market is more likely to do next.

Learning Price Action is not about stacking concepts from basics upward,

but first exhaustively listing all "the questions you must answer,"

then working backward from real charts, filling in only the understanding necessary to complete your judgment.
