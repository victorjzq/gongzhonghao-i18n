# Price Action: Understanding Market Cycles

The purpose of the market's existence —
what's the difference between a gallon of milk and an apple at a supermarket?
The only difference is the price people are willing to pay for them.

Essentially, the market is always a union of buyers and sellers.
In any market,
there will always be a price that's too expensive and a price that's too cheap,
and the market will explore up and down to find these prices.
Eventually, a news event changes market consensus,
fundamentals shift,
and the market breaks out.

So what happens at the "fair price"?
As I just said,
the market will keep probing up and down
until a major news event makes everyone agree the current price is wrong.
But since the market's goal is always to find the "fair price,"
when price discovery occurs,
the market repeatedly tests that price
and eventually forms a "tight trading range,"
before continuing to develop.

The market forces traders to keep trading.
This is the reality of trading.
If you want to buy a house, buy anything,
you need both buyers and sellers participating.
Without buyers and sellers, without market transparency (meaning high volume),
you can't know what the "fair price" is.

Now we understand that the foundation of the market is "price discovery,"
it revolves around the "fair price,"
and it brings buyers and sellers together.

Market cycles have two components: trends and trading ranges.
I know you'll say: what about channels?
Don't market cycles include "breakouts, channels, and trading ranges"?
That's correct.
But I view market cycles as two things:
either a trend or a trading range.

A channel is just a transitional phase.
Because when you see a channel,
it actually exhibits "trend behavior,"
but when the channel ends,
it always enters a trading range.

So the key point about market cycles is:
it's a never-ending loop that never stops.
It consolidates,
then comes a strong breakout,
maybe two legs down,
then enters a trading range again.

Another point is:
the market is always either in a trend
or in a trading range.
You'll see uptrends and downtrends,
and most of the time it's a mixture of both,
which is exactly why the market is confusing.
This is why you often hear Al say:
"90% of the time you can make money trading both directions, 10% of the time it's this type of move."

The key to understanding market cycles is:
higher timeframe influence always exceeds lower timeframe influence.

The first key to understanding market cycles is: breakout.
This is the fundamental element of trading.
A breakout can be one bar or multiple bars.
A breakout is the moment that resets the market cycle.

We can understand it this way:
a breakout is the first phase of the market cycle.
But strictly speaking,
a breakout "breaks out" from the previous market cycle,
and every market cycle's endpoint is a "trading range."

So after you see a breakout,
there will always be a pullback next.
What does this pullback form?
The channel phase.

There are several more important principles about market cycles, such as:
**The Law of Inertia**
**The 80% Rule**

80% of the time, the market will continue doing what it's doing.
If the market is in a strong trend,
say this is a bear trend,
you could call it a broad bear channel,
then 80% of the time it will remain in this broad bear channel.
You'll see many upward reversal bars fail.

Why 80%?
Because in a small pullback trend,
or in a channel that's beginning to overlap,
approximately 5 major reversal attempts will typically fail,
and then the market will enter a trading range.

The logic for trading ranges is the same:
within these counter-trend moves,
80% will fail.
