# Price Action: What Are Trading Charts?

The market reflects rational human behavior,
and it exhibits fractal patterns.

Because this is what emerges when countless intelligent traders
make countless trades based on different motivations.

These behaviors are all built on rational logic.

Charts reflect the behavior of rational humans,
and this is what they look like.

This pattern is A-B-C-D,
first leg, second leg,
call it whatever you want.

For example, look at a tree.

A tree is a perfect example of fractal structure.

You have a trunk,
which branches into thicker limbs,
those thicker limbs themselves act like mini-trunks,
branching out into smaller branches,
and finally becoming twigs, tips, and leaves.

This also explains a common question:
Why does Al say a trend bar can be
a "trend bar," a "breakout,"
a "gap," and even a "climax" all at the same time?

This is indeed confusing.

But you can understand it this way.

Take another example — water.

The water molecule is H₂O.

Whether H₂O is ice, liquid water, or steam,
its molecular structure is still H₂O.

Ice is water in a cold state,
liquid is water at room temperature,
heat it up and it becomes steam or even vapor,
but the essence is still H₂O.

So it's like how a trend bar can also be
a breakout, a gap, or a climax —
different states, same essence.

In meditation, focus on the pause between breaths.
What is the pause between breaths?

You inhale, exhale,
rising, falling,
expansion of the lungs, contraction,
cycling continuously.

When you observe deeply,
you begin to see these same patterns
in everything in the universe.

Even we humans ourselves
are fractal structures.

What about the pause between a breakout and momentum?

Have you ever wondered:
Why did we stop going up here?
Why did we stop going down here?
Why did it reverse right here?

This structure keeps repeating.

This is what Ali calls the "secret handshake" —
there's an "unspoken handshake" between traders.

Bulls buy, hoping for a surprise rally.

But actually, the bears have a second leg down waiting.
The bulls try but fail,
so they start taking profits, becoming sellers.

The bears were already selling,
and everyone agrees price has to go lower.

Then we finally find support at some point,
the bears cover their shorts, starting to take profits;
the bulls see value and step in,
so the market rallies again.

Every trade requires a counterparty —
a trader on the other side of your trade.
You must respect that trader on the other side.

And that trader may be an institutional trader.

Every time you enter,
there is an institution on the opposite side.

Although an institution is on the other side going short,
it doesn't mean they're looking at the same 5-minute chart you are.

At the end of the day, the market is made up of institutions.

Their volume is so large
that we are "invisible" in the market.

And their volume is so large
that many of them don't even day trade.

They're managing billions of dollars,
and some don't even look at charts.

Even when they do look at charts,
it's mostly daily, weekly, or even monthly.

So when I buy, yes, someone is selling,
but what that person is doing has nothing to do with me.

Their perspective is different.

What used to confuse me was:
How could a smart institutional bear
sell short at a swing low
after such a strong rally and a two-legged pullback?

So I started doubting myself
and was afraid to buy.

These institutions don't care much about precise entry points,
not like us day traders who focus so much on Price Action.

They might buy at the very top,
use a very wide stop loss,
and scale in as price drops.

And they still end up making money.
