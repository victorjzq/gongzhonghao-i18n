# Price Action: How to Understand Technical Structure

Technical structure specifically refers to more complex combinations of multiple patterns. Therefore, the pattern contains more subdivided technical structures, including various sub-patterns and secondary structures. Specifically, the main structure refers to a relatively complex, fully presented technical pattern on the chart, such as the final test point — specifically referring to a technical pattern confirmed as the ultimate key node. All information on the chart represents the latest price action; sometimes a single bar can form a complete trading signal.

When you observe the latest bar, you might conclude: this is the latest development on the current chart. But the underlying cause may carry deeper meaning than the surface price action. For example, when one day's price action affects the next day's bar formation, the market operates according to the laws of cause and effect.

Sometimes you can ignore the overall pattern and focus on the latest signal. Beginners often find it easier to start by focusing on a single signal. But excellent traders consider both aspects — they can simultaneously weigh multiple factors, grasping both the macro trend and micro changes. The latest price action may be one of the contributing factors.

The market has only two responses: breakout failure or success. Higher timeframe charts require at least two pushes. The market reacts to every micro change, and these reactions are reflected on smaller timeframes. For large-scale moves, you need to observe higher timeframe charts, which present more complete pattern structures and technical characteristics — the market will inevitably respond accordingly.

When two opposing forces exist in the market — like two gangs fighting for territorial control — this depends on the trader's mastery of details. When you can identify all chart details without thinking, you can capture more trading opportunities at any time. You don't need to wait for textbook trading signals.

Any trade requires at least two sufficient reasons for support. A single basis is often unreliable — there must be corroborating conditions. When two reliable signals appear simultaneously, the win rate of that trade increases significantly. When trading experience and knowledge haven't been established yet, it's best to stick to simple strategies. Don't get caught up in other complex factors. For example, wait for a clear signal bar. Never enter randomly — only trade high-quality signal bars, and patiently wait for first or second entry opportunities. Choose patterns that are immediately obvious, with clear definitions and intuitive identification methods, allowing rapid judgment in live markets. However, as trading experience grows — and a very important part of that experience is learning the correct way to think — your reactions will become fast enough to observe all the detailed changes within each bar as it forms. At that point, trade execution will never be a problem again.
