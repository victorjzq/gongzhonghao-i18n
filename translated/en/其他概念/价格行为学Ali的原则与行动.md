# Price Action: Ali's Principles and Actions

As Price Action students, we cannot overlook Ali's learning advice. The 10,000 Trades channel has two long-form interviews with Ali that comprehensively build the psychological framework and practice methods beyond trading techniques. We summarized the principles and actions Ali discussed from 4 hours of interviews.

## Part One: Core Philosophy and Mental Models

1. **Engineer's Mindset**
   Core idea: The market is precise; there is no such thing as "noise." Ali proved through programming and quantification that even a one-tick difference affects algorithmic decisions. If you can fully understand Price Action, your win rate can exceed 75%.
   Action: Reject ambiguity; everything must be quantified. If it doesn't meet the definition, don't execute.

2. **Factory Owner's Mindset**
   Core idea: Trading should be like running a factory, with the goal of producing one high-quality "product" (trading setup). You don't need to master every model — just perfect one model (such as micro channel breakouts).
   Action: Focus on only one setup, like producing one perfect widget. As long as this product is profitable, keep producing it repeatedly.

3. **Aviation Safety Mindset**
   Core idea: Citing the pilot's principle: "Don't let anything touch the airplane." In trading, this means you must have absolute defensive mechanisms.
   Action:
   - Pay extreme attention to detail and contingency plans. Like a telecom engineer maintaining systems at dawn, you must have Plan A, Plan B, and Plan C before operating.
   - Like anticipating road conditions while driving, before entering a trade you must think: "What if the breakout fails?" "If there's a large profit, should I scale in or take profit?"

4. **Process Over Results**
   Core idea: A beginner's goal should not be "how much money to make," but "perfect execution of the process."
   Action: If the process is correct, money is an automatic byproduct. Like a doctor treating patients or an engineer doing a project, focus on the work itself, not the compensation.

## Part Two: Learning Path and Methodology

1. **Spiral Learning Model**
   Principle: The core knowledge in trading is small; the difficulty lies in converting knowledge into subconscious intuition.
   Action: Continuously return to core knowledge points through practical cycles (spiral ascent). Each cycle brings new experience to re-examine old knowledge, until you can see through the logic behind charts at a glance.

2. **"1,000 Charts" Principle**
   Principle: Becoming an expert requires analyzing at least 1,000 full-day charts (approximately 4 years of data).
   Reason: You must cover one complete "U.S. presidential election cycle." Macro political events change market volatility; looking at only short-term data creates bias. You must have seen market reactions under different volatility environments.
   Action: Persist in reviewing and analyzing until you've covered a sufficient time span.

3. **Deliberate Practice**
   Tool: Use market replay function at 3x speed.
   Action:
   - Single focus: Each practice session focuses on only one concept (such as "breakout" or "micro channel").
   - Quantitative scoring: Score yourself based on preset criteria (such as 10 characteristics of a micro channel). Deduct probability points for each missing characteristic.
   - Variant practice: Repeat the same concept across different market days.

4. **Build a "Flashcard" Database**
   Action: Ali created over 831 PowerPoint slides. Whenever he found an interesting phenomenon or made a mistake, he would take a screenshot and create a slide.
   Review: Use weekends to randomly draw cards to test yourself, or use the search function to find specific concepts (retrieval practice).

## Part Three: Systematic Decision-Making Process

1. **Binary Decision Tree**
   To make decisions within seconds, you must simplify the market into a "yes/no" selection process:
   - Layer 1: Market state (bullish/bearish?) Criteria: Look at the moving average and slope.
   - Layer 2: Market behavior (trend/trading range?)
   - Layer 3: Current phase (breakout/channel?)
   - Layer 4: Follow-through strength (strong/weak? Acceptable?)
   Action: Only act when each step has a clear answer. For example: strong breakout in a trend with strong follow-through = market order to buy.

2. **From Systematic to Discretionary**
   Principle: You must first systematize all rules before you can eventually earn discretionary authority.
   Action: Beginners should not try to be "flexible" but strictly execute rules. Through building a system, force yourself to define every detail (e.g., exactly how many overlapping bars constitute a trading range?).

3. **Calibrate Expectations**
   Action: Before entry, set your "expected value" for the trade based on the signal bar's strength and contextual structure.
   Application:
   - Strong trend breakout → Set high expectations → Hold for longer.
   - Counter-trend trade in a trading range → Set low expectations → Only scalp.

## Part Four: Tactical Execution Details

1. **Entries Are More Important Than Exits**
   View: For day traders, entries are more important than exits and position management.
   Reason: Intraday trading has limited time; there's no time to salvage a bad entry through position adjustments. A good entry directly provides a safety cushion.

2. **Time Segmentation Strategy**
   - Open: Determines the tone for the entire day.
   - Mid-session: Usually a trading range; use more limit orders for counter-trend trades.
   - Close: Response to or correction of the open.
   Action: The system should include a "time filter," using different strategies for different time segments.

3. **Handling the Opening Gap**
   Action: Use quantitative indicators to assess gap size.
   - Large gap: Such as 2.6 times the average daily range. This usually indicates the entire day will be dominated by the gap direction; don't take counter-trend trades.
   - Small gap: Usually fills quickly.

4. **Specific Micro Channel Tactics**
   Identification: A series of bars with no pullback (highs/lows continuously rising/falling).
   Criteria: If 70% or more characteristics are met (such as no overlap, large bodies, close at extreme), enter at market price directly; don't wait for a pullback.

## Part Five: Capital Management and Growth Expectations

1. **Parabolic Growth**
   Expectation: A trader's growth is not linear but parabolic. The early stage includes a long "flat period" — 2-3 times longer than expected — spent building neural connections.
   Breakout: Once you pass the critical point, skills and wealth grow exponentially.

2. **Specific Financial Goals**
   - Initial goal: Earn 0.5% of your account daily or a fixed amount (such as 250-500). This only requires catching 3-5 clear setups per day.
   - Professional goal: Only when annual trading income reaches 3 times your living expenses (1/3 for living, 1/3 for taxes, 1/3 for reinvestment) is it safe to trade full-time.

3. **Overcoming Psychological Barriers**
   Action: When account size grows large, "hide the dollar amount" on the trading interface and only display "points."
   Purpose: Focus on execution process scores rather than monetary gains/losses — this is the only way to overcome large-account psychological pressure.
