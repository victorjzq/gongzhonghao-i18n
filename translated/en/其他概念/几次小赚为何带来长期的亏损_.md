# Why Do Several Small Wins Lead to Long-Term Losses?

One of the difficulties in learning to trade is that even when the entire process is wrong, you can still end up making money.

Some people might say, so what? Making money is all that matters.

If someone's goal in learning to trade is to make just one profit in the market and never enter again, then I'd say there's nothing wrong with a completely flawed process that happens to make money.

But as long as our goal is to profit consistently in the market over the long term, we cannot ignore whether the replicable process we build within uncertainty is reasonable.

The feedback mechanism in learning to trade is quite different from doing homework in school.

With a math problem, if the steps are wrong, the result is most likely wrong too (unless the numbers happen to coincide), and every problem's solution method is fundamentally similar.

But in trading, someone who randomly constructs their process — like flipping a coin to go long or short with eyes closed — can still make money, and it's entirely possible to be profitable for several consecutive days.

Is this a good thing?

This person will then establish a pathway, or through external feedback, believe they've discovered a "mechanism" — that randomly going long or short based on a coin flip can make money.

They won't think they have too much money. They'll repeat this action until they encounter their first loss.

Will they stop at that point? Most likely not.

Because they still depend on this pathway to earn back what they lost.

They keep executing. Until they've lost so much they question their existence, only then might they abandon this approach and decide to relearn trading.

But then they go through the same process again — randomly constructing pathways, small wins, repeating, big losses. Spending enormous amounts of time and energy each day, going round and round, returning still unable to profit consistently. This is actually the path most of us take when learning to trade — it's just that our "techniques" look a bit "wiser" than flipping a coin, since they include a whole bunch of so-called analysis.

How do you break this cycle?

The solution is to change an anchor point in your mind: short-term profits don't mean "correct" trading. So what is correct trading?

This isn't easy — the floating profits and losses jumping on your screen will inevitably affect your mood to some degree.

Because making money naturally creates a psychologically irresistible positive feedback loop. When account gains are substantial, it's hard not to feel restless, overconfident, and unable to calmly review and reflect.

Don't be discouraged — this is human nature. As long as we can recognize it in time and correct our behavior, we've gained a workout. Do this repeatedly — like lifting dumbbells — and you get thoroughly trained.
