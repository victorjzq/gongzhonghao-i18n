# Price Action: If You Go Long

If you enter long, you need to place your stop loss below the breakout starting point. As long as price is still running within the channel, the trend remains valid. Only when price truly breaks below the breakout starting point can you consider that the trend might be ending.

Don't look at a single bar in isolation — understand it within the entire context. The market is always asking two questions: Will the trend continue or reverse? You must answer based on context, not rely on a single isolated signal.

The big picture is key. If the context is a strong uptrend with a breakout and a channel, then you must assume the market is more likely to continue rising than to reverse. A true reversal requires much stronger evidence.

Al often says the market likes to move in two legs. After the first leg up, there's usually a pullback or consolidation, followed by a second leg. Your job is to identify this second leg and participate with the trend. When the market finally breaks below the channel, or even below the breakout starting point, that's the signal — the uptrend may be over and the market is entering a trading range or even reversing. You should take profits when price breaks below the breakout starting point. Because once it breaks, the market enters a new phase, and you can no longer assume the previous trend still holds.

Entry points can be diverse. You can enter on a pullback within the channel, or enter on a range breakout. The key isn't the specific entry point, but whether the direction is correct and whether risk management is sound. For example, you enter on a small pullback in the middle of the channel with a stop loss below the channel bottom — as long as the trend continues, you'll profit. Even entering on a range breakout, though slightly late, you can still make money with the trend.

So don't obsess over finding the perfect entry point. What's more important is understanding the entire context and making sure you're on the side with the highest probability.
