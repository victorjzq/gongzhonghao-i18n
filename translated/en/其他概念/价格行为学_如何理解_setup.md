# Price Action: How to Understand Setups

A setup is a pattern on the chart — it can be a single bar or a combination of multiple bars. A setup is something that can lead to a profitable trade.
If the setup's direction follows the current or recent trend, it's a with-trend setup; if the setup's direction opposes the recent trend, it's a counter-trend setup.
Within a setup, the bar that may actually lead to a profitable trade is called the setup bar.

As traders, our goal is to find imbalances: when it's clearly apparent that buying pressure above will exceed selling pressure, that's a buy point; when it's clearly apparent that selling pressure below will exceed buying pressure, that's a sell point. We want to find this kind of imbalance where one side overwhelms the other.

Once the setup bar is triggered — meaning the next bar breaks above the high of this bar — the setup bar becomes the signal bar. The bar that triggers the buy entry is called the entry bar. Importantly: after entry, there should be a with-trend follow-through bar. Al mentioned that sometimes the follow-through bar doesn't appear immediately — there may be a few bars of sideways movement first — but as long as you're in the trade, you want to see with-trend follow-through as soon as possible, showing urgency.

In a strong trend, when you buy above a bull reversal bar, you want to see follow-through immediately. If the entry bar is a small doji, it shows no urgency — the market is pausing. Al suggests that if the context isn't particularly strong and the entry bar is a small doji, it's probably best to exit at breakeven immediately rather than waiting.
So the entry bar, along with all these bars' patterns and how they interact, determines trade management.

A final point is recognizing mistakes. For example, frequently buying below the moving average, but the entry bar and follow-through bar never break above the moving average, remaining below it. If you can identify this as your biggest mistake, here's the insight: when the follow-through bar doesn't break above the moving average, the pattern becomes a bear flag. If the entry bar is a doji closing below the moving average, and the follow-through bar is also a doji closing below the moving average, then this is a potential double-top bear flag, and the market may continue falling. This way, you can review your trades and discover where you most frequently make mistakes — such as misreading the entry bar or follow-through bar — and make adjustments.

As trend strength increases, the importance of with-trend signal bars decreases. In a very strong bull trend, bull signal bars are less important — you don't need a perfect signal bar to trade with the trend. But in a strong trend, counter-trend signal bars become more important. Because reversing a strong trend requires a very good signal bar. In other words, when trading with the trend you can be less picky, but when trading counter-trend you must be extremely selective.

The strongest trends often have "poor" signal bars. You can find almost any reason to enter, as long as you use an appropriate stop loss. If the trend is truly strong and signal bars are all perfect, the market would quickly run one-directionally and everyone would see it immediately — only scalps would be possible, not big swings. But because the signal bars are "poor," market participants keep guessing and hesitating, only able to buy incrementally with small positions — which is how a trend sustains itself all day. So the strongest trends often have "unclear" signal bars. But in a strong trend, clarity doesn't matter — any reason works for entry as long as the stop loss is reasonable.

Conversely, the strongest reversals tend to have very strong signal bars. Because reversal probability is lower, you need a near-perfect signal bar plus the right context to justify a counter-trend trade. Al's definition is: break the bear trendline, get above the moving average, or make a strong breakout through the trendline and moving average, then test the prior extreme.

So Al's standard for counter-trend trading is: you must break the trendline, test the extreme, and then see a very good signal bar. But on counter-trend trades, he is actually very strict. Even with over thirty years of experience, he remains extremely selective about signal bars. His advice is: beginners should only enter on with-trend setups. In a bear trend, only go short, and only below a bear bar that closes near its low.

Similarly, in a bull trend, only go long, and only above a bull bar that closes near its high. On a moving average pullback, a bull bar closing near its high is also a buy point.

These setups are not necessarily all optimal, but the general principle is: if the market is rising, only look for bull signal bars, preferably closing at or near the high. This will greatly increase the probability of success.

When I first started learning, I frequently made mistakes too. Actually, it's not just beginners — if your trading isn't going well, you should also return to these foundational rules. Al's advice applies to beginners, and it's also the way to help any trader who's lost direction or confidence return to core principles.
