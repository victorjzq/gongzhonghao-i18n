# Price Action: How to Understand the Importance of the Close

The close provides critical information about a bar's strength or weakness. Strength depends primarily on four elements: open, high, low, and close. Ignoring the close means giving up most of the information. The close tells us the final result after the market's battle during that time period. Closing near the high indicates strong buying; closing in the middle shows indecision; closing near the low shows strong selling. Whether the current bar's close is above the previous bar's high, whether it's above the previous close — these judgments all depend entirely on the closing price. If you don't wait for the close, you'll miss these important signals.

Patterns often change in the final seconds. The larger the timeframe, the greater the impact of these changes. The last day of a weekly bar, the last week of a monthly bar — these are critical moments that determine the closing pattern. This is also why Al emphasizes the importance of Friday's close, because it determines the entire week's closing level. For example, whether the bulls can close above the prior week's high, whether they can close above key resistance — this usually isn't finalized until the last day. The same applies to monthly bars — the month-end close often determines the entire move's significance at the monthly level.

Another reason is that institutions use line charts. Line charts only display closing prices. If institutions use closing prices as their primary basis for analysis and trading, then as retail traders, we must also pay attention to closing prices. Follow the institutions, understand their logic.

The cost of waiting for the close is usually less than the benefit. This can be understood from three aspects: entry, exit, and profit-taking.

For entries: If the market is in a strong trend — for example, three consecutive large bull bars in a bull move — Al often says in these situations to "just buy anything," market order, because the probability is already high enough and there will be a second leg up. In this context, not waiting for the close isn't a big problem. But if your entry depends entirely on a specific bar's pattern, you must wait for the close, otherwise the signal is incomplete.

For exits: The closing price also provides important guidance. For example, in a long trade, if the bar closes strongly at its high, it often means there will be follow-through; but if it closes in the middle or lower half, you should be alert for a possible pullback or reversal.

For profit-taking: The closing price helps determine whether targets have been reached and whether profits should be locked in. For example, when the market tests a key level, whether the close holds above it is more meaningful than a brief intraday breakout.

Therefore, the closing price is extremely important in all three aspects: entry, exit, and profit-taking. Waiting for the close, although it may mean giving up a small portion of profit, significantly improves signal reliability. Al's core advice is: if the trade depends on bar patterns, you must wait for the close.
