# Price Action: Goals and Path

## 1. Ultimate Goal (Portrait of a Professional Trader)

- "Full cycle" understanding of the market and charts: able to explain the origin and context of every bar.
- Probability sense reliable enough: can distinguish between high-probability and low-probability moves, and act accordingly.
- Trading action skills automated: can trade reflexively upon seeing certain setups.
- Rapid trade construction: can frame entry, profit target, and stop loss within seconds.
- Intuition for the "normal behavior range" — can react immediately when something exceeds it.
- No psychological barriers to stop losses, treating losses as costs.
- Skilled trade management: can judge when to cut losses early and when to scale in to repair.
- Knows the limits of trends or pullbacks.
- Maintains objectivity, not projecting personal expectations onto the market.
- Relaxed and eager while in a trading state.

---

## 2. Learning Method (Spiral Model)

- Learning is layered, like a spiral that keeps returning to the starting point, but higher and wider each time.
- Reviewing and practice are core — gradually linking knowledge and experience.
- From depending on rules to intuition: the ultimate goal is to "feel the market" — hard to articulate, but correct.

---

## 3. Learning Path (Specific Steps)

- Repeatedly watch Brooks' course videos and books until you can recite them.
- Find abundant examples on charts; the breakthrough threshold is analyzing 2,000-3,000 charts.
- Learn concepts → explain why → connect small patterns to form larger structures.
- Be able to explain intraday structure, end-of-day results, and how different timeframes interact.
- Practice probability analysis: what is a high-probability setup? How to dynamically update probability?
- Trade management: decide RRR, entry method, and whether to scale in within seconds.
- Establish note-taking and review habits (intraday, end-of-day, periodic).
- Practice skills: print charts to find all possible trades, use market replay, until automatic reactions form.
- Decisions must be completed within 10-15 seconds — practice with binary decision trees.
- Making mistakes is part of learning; the key is "making mistakes responsibly."
- Establish trading business processes and plans, maintain health, build consistency.
- Once stable, gradually increase position size, expand processes, even develop into a team.

---

## 4. Comfort Zone

- Formed by the intersection of three parts: personality + skills/strategies + market.
- Traders need to find the market and market phase best suited to their personality and strategy.
- Different market rhythms suit different people: some suit slow markets, others suit fast markets.

---

## 5. Intuitive Traders

- The essence of "feeling the market" is massive experience.
- Static charts lack 20-30% of "energy information" — only long-term screen time can accumulate it.
- Intuition comes from the brain's neural network synthesizing experience with "speed and magnitude of volatility."

---

## 6. Deliberate Practice

- Be clear about what setup, what market, and what type of day to practice.
- Each practice session has goals and discipline: trade when you see a good setup, execute cleanly.
- Practice rapid setup identification and rapid order placement.
- Design contingency plans for unexpected outcomes (very good, very bad, neutral).
- Continuous evaluation (CEP): dynamically adjust with new information after entry.
- Mental state must match the task: stay centered, open, and confident.
- Review and score: build Excel spreadsheets, track the progress curve.
- Once skills mature, start with small live positions, then gradually scale up.

---

## 7. Signal vs. Setup

- Setup: A combination of conditions requiring multiple bars to build.
- Signal Bar: The final bar that triggers entry, determining the specific order price.
- Professional traders are precise down to "one tick" — this is the difference between consistent execution and casual trading.

---

## 8. Perspective on Challenges

- Trading is a luxury profession — the cost is high, but it's worth it.
- Learning to trade is like getting a PhD — there's no fixed endpoint; it could take 2 years or over 10.
- The "staircase" of trading has no final step — every day of practice is another step upward.
