# Price Action: Common Trading Questions (Part 2)

Sometimes the market will first make a deeper pullback, hitting your stop loss, and then continue in the original direction.

There are two solutions. The first is to set a wider stop loss. For example, instead of placing it at the low of the previous bar, place it below the previous significant range. This way, it's much harder to get stopped out. The downside is that the risk is larger, so you must reduce your position size accordingly. The second solution is to accept getting stopped out, then re-enter. That is, when the market shakes you out first and then reconfirms the direction, you chase back in. This requires you to stay calm, accept the small loss, and then decisively get back on board.

In reality, both methods work — the key depends on your personality and trading style. Some people prefer wide stop losses with small positions; others prefer tight stop losses with multiple attempts. As long as you can control risk well, the results are similar over the long term.

The market will suddenly produce a large trend bar, break out in one shot, and pull away immediately. Don't chase too eagerly. If the market has already moved far away, rushing in will often result in buying at the high or selling at the low, followed by a pullback. The correct approach is to wait for the pullback. Almost all breakouts have pullbacks. If you're patient, you can definitely wait for price to return to a more reasonable level.

Of course, sometimes the pullback is very small and you might miss the opportunity. But that's okay. The market always has new opportunities. Don't chase highs or sell lows because you're afraid of missing out. Remember, avoiding one bad trade is more important than making one good trade.

In a sense, every trend bar could be a new beginning. But you can't interpret this as meaning you can enter at any time. You need to consider context. If the market has been running for a long time and then produces a trend bar, it might just be the last burst of energy, not a new beginning. Conversely, if the market just broke out of a range and immediately produces a trend bar, this is more likely a new breakout that will be followed by a channel.

So you must look at the context. A trend bar itself is very important, but it must be understood within the entire market environment.
