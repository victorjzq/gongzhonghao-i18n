# Price Action: How Price Approaches Resistance

"Working very hard" in trading means:

During execution, treat yourself as a "machine running only the frozen version";

During non-execution time, treat yourself as a "researcher who only produces rule hypotheses";

"Effort" is not a quantity but an order.

Most definitions mix "learning, execution, and correction" into one pot.

Feeling like you're working hard, proving it with output.

The key point is that the resistance level itself is important, but how price approaches the resistance level is equally important.
If the approach to resistance has very strong directional momentum, then selling at resistance or taking the first trade in the direction of the prior trend are both low-probability moves, because this type of approach requires another test.

If you want to buy at support, a moving average, or another level, you want to see the move approaching that price as a push within a trading range — meaning that by the time price gets there, the bears are exhausted, with two, three, or four downward attempts all having failed, and the bulls buying, buying, buying.

Once you see a very strong, directional move, it probably won't actually stop there.
It may break through the resistance level, pull back to test its backside, and then continue rising.

If the move is strong and it's the first pullback, the pullback is usually very brief — triggering and immediately reversing.
So in the early stage of a new cycle, pullbacks are typically very brief.

Traders see the gap starting to close and bet that the breakout to new highs/lows will lack follow-through and that the pullback will deepen.

When the bulls can't keep the gap open, it means there's selling above.

So there's selling above the new swing point, and the market will likely return to near the moving average.

Moving average gap bars usually lead to a trend restart,
but in most cases, this trend restart is also the last push of that trend.

There are only two emotions in trading: urgency or uncertainty.

If you've already bought but see urgency to sell, then exit — perhaps reverse to short, or stay flat.

But uncertainty and "not knowing what to do" are two different things.
When traders feel uncertainty, they start shifting toward counter-trend trading:
Fading breakouts, fading strong bars, buying at support, selling at resistance.
If you feel uncertainty, start trading counter-trend;
If you feel urgency, trade in the direction creating that pressure, then evaluate based on this logic.

When a very prolonged consolidation range appears, it acts like a magnet pulling the market back inside, so reversals occur.
