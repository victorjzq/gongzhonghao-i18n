# Price Action: Actual Risk

I. When you can't determine whether the win rate is above 60%, the safest approach is to set the profit target at twice the initial risk. This way, even if the success rate is only 40%, the Trader's Equation remains positive.

II. Only after a move begins can you see the "ideal stop" — usually a key high or low left by the most recent deep pullback. The distance from your entry price to that level is the trade's true "actual risk."

III. Many quantitative models use actual risk rather than initial risk to set profit targets. For example, in a High 2 bull flag, the algorithm buys at the signal bar with a wide initial stop loss. After the pullback doesn't hit the stop, the model treats the blue box height as the actual risk and takes partial profits at 1x that level. The first time price touches that level, a small dip often occurs — this is the result of such algorithms taking profits.

IV. For entries with sufficiently high probability (strong trend pullbacks, buying at bull flag lows, etc.), taking profits at just 1x actual risk is reasonable. But remember: this is the "minimum requirement" — it satisfies positive expectancy but isn't necessarily optimal.

V. If a very large breakout occurs, the market has about a 60% probability of reaching the measured move. In this case, using "2x actual risk" as the target is too narrow. It's better to use the measured move, prior highs, or trendlines as more distant resistance targets.

VI. Approximately 90% of bars on a chart are within channel or trading range structures. This means that most of the time, the win rate isn't particularly high. In these cases, setting profit targets at 2x actual risk is the safest baseline; even when risk is very small, don't shrink the target too much, otherwise it can't compensate for the low win rate.

VII. The tighter the stop, the lower the win rate typically becomes; the wider the stop, the smaller the position must be. The right balance is: risk matches the volatility you can tolerate, the profit target is at least twice the risk, rather than blindly pursuing the smallest stop loss.

VIII. In actual trading, guard against "sampling error." Don't conclude a strategy works just because of one or two successes. You need a sufficient sample — for example, completing 100 trades: high win-rate trades using 1x risk profit targets, low win-rate trades using 2x risk profit targets. Only long-term statistics are meaningful.
