# Price Action: Triangles

## [I] Triangles: The "Confirmation Channel" Within a Trading Range

## [II] The Nature of Triangles

- Any oscillating price action consisting of **three pushes** (three up-pushes or three down-pushes) can be considered a triangle; at minimum, there must be **5 swings** (alternating up↑ down↓).
- It can be either a **reversal pattern** or simply a **flag** within a trend.
- Markets rarely jump directly from bullish to bearish or vice versa; most reversals first transition into a trading range, and a tight triangle is the classic format of this transition.

## [III] Pattern Types

1. **Converging Triangle** — Swings gradually diminish.
2. **Ascending Triangle** — Flat top, rising bottom.
3. **Descending Triangle** — Flat bottom, declining top.
4. **Expanding Triangle** (megaphone) — Swings become progressively larger.
5. Whenever the top/bottom shows a clear slope, it can be called a **wedge** (upward slope = bear flag wedge; downward slope = reversal wedge).

## [IV] Probability Framework

- Triangle = a tight trading range bounded by two lines → **Breakout model:**
  - Probability of upward breakout ≈ probability of downward breakout = 50%.
  - Probability that the first breakout fails and reverses ≈ 50%.
- **Sloping wedge:** Approximately **75%** will break out in the *opposite direction*.
- The prior trend before entering the triangle still gives a slight edge to the breakout direction (continuation has a slight advantage).

## [V] The "Magnetic Field" Property of Triangles

- Price within the range is viewed by both bulls and bears as **fair value**; once it escapes, it is often pulled back.
- When sideways oscillation persists for **≥ 20 bars**, the market enters a neutral state, with equal chances of going up or down.

## [VI] Expanding Triangles

- Legs diverge rather than converge; if the scale reaches 15-30 bars, it often qualifies as a **major trend reversal**.
- Three consecutive up-pushes or three down-probes constitute an expanding triangle; price often reverses quickly after making a new high/low.

## [VII] Nested Patterns: Probability Stacking

- A small triangle/wedge nested within a larger same-direction triangle → Improved success rate.

## [VIII] Trading Key Points

1. **Confirm the Swings**
   - Count the waves first: Are there already ↑↓↑↓↑ five segments? If fewer than five, the pattern is immature.

2. **Second Entry**
   - For going short: Wait for the bear bar below after "two up-pushes";
   - For going long: Wait for the bull bar above after "two down-probes."

3. **Slope Assessment**
   - Upward slope = bear flag wedge → Expect a downward breakout;
   - Downward slope = reversal wedge → Expect an upward breakout.

4. **Targets and Management**
   - After a breakout, first look for a measured move target equal to the triangle's "mouth width";
   - If the first breakout fails, immediately seek an equal measured move in the opposite direction.

## [IX] Pattern Quality = Reliability

- Clean lines, symmetrical swings → Algorithmic funds identify them more easily → Price action behaves more "obediently."
- Distorted structure, chaotic proportions → Low-probability pattern, indistinguishable from "random" → Not worth trading.

## [X] Summary

Triangles are the "confirmation channel" between one trend and the next:

- **Five complete segments**, **20 bars**, and **slope direction** — these three rulers determine whether it's a continuation, reversal, or pure trading range.
- Only well-formed triangles are worth trading; treat ugly patterns as if they don't exist.
