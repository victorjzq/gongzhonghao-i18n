# Price Action: Common Trading Questions (Part 3)

No matter how good the opportunity looks, always use the same risk percentage. For example, you always risk only 1% of your account. No matter how good the opportunity seems, don't exceed this percentage.

Because even the best opportunity can fail. There are no certainties in the market. You must always use small positions. Only this way can you survive in the long run. Remember, trading isn't about getting rich on one trade — it's a lifelong endeavor.

Many people know they should follow the trend, but they often only recognize it after the trend has already run for a long time, leaving them in a dilemma.

First, don't chase a trend that's near its end. If the market has already produced many consecutive trend bars and is far from the moving average, the risk/reward ratio is poor at this point. If you enter recklessly, you'll most likely buy at the high or sell at the low.
Second, you can wait for a pullback. Almost all trends have pullbacks. If you missed the first half, patiently wait for a pullback, then enter with the trend where the pullback ends. This way, the risk is smaller and the profit potential is greater.
Third, you can reduce your position size. If you really don't want to miss out, use a very small position to participate. This way, even if you're wrong, it doesn't matter. It's like buying a "ticket to participate" rather than making a big bet.

Trends don't appear every day, but opportunities are endless. Missing one isn't scary — what's truly scary is entering at the wrong place because you're afraid of missing out.

Trading ranges are the hardest market conditions. Because in a trading range, signals are often false — you go short at the high and it breaks out; you go long at the low and it breaks down. The end result is that you get stopped out repeatedly, creating enormous psychological pressure.
If you're a beginner, simply don't trade trading ranges. Wait for a trend to emerge, then trade with the trend. Trends are easier and clearer.
If you're experienced, you can operate with small positions in a trading range — for example, going short with a small position at the range high and going long with a small position at the low. But you must accept that getting stopped out is normal. The purpose of doing this is to practice patience in the trading range, not to make big money.

Overall, trends are the main opportunity for making money. Trade less in trading ranges, trade more in trends — this is the simplest approach.

The market is not a game of logical deduction but a game of probability. No matter how good your analysis is, it's only a probability. For example, if you judge there's a 60% chance of going up, that means there's still a 40% chance of going down. You can't expect to be right every time — that's impossible.

So the key isn't whether your analysis is perfect, but whether you've accepted uncertainty. True experts aren't right every time — they know that even when they're wrong, they won't lose big.

In other words, your task isn't to predict the market but to manage risk. Prediction only gives you a direction; risk management is what determines whether you can be profitable in the long run.
