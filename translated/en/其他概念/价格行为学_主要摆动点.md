# Price Action: Major Swing Points

The characteristic of major swing points is that they are "clearly visible without any effort to find them." The more bars involved and the clearer the structure, the greater the significance of price breaking through that swing point.

If the higher timeframe is still in a tight channel, the first reversal should be a minor reversal.

Markets have fractal characteristics — they require a trendline to be broken, retested, and broken again. Even when a reversal is strong, there will be hesitation or pullbacks.

Only when "all the elements are present and in the correct order" can a major trend reversal pattern be reliable. If too many compromises are made, the reversal will fail to materialize until all elements are in place. Even when all elements are present, if a particular leg is too strong, the reversal will still encounter resistance, hesitation, or pullbacks.

You must patiently wait until all elements are in place and the higher timeframe context is correct before executing a high-probability bear swing trade.

In most cases, the market will completely retrace to near the starting point, testing the weak launch level.

Major reversals have strong contextual relevance — this relevance comes from higher timeframes, not just the chart in front of you.

When the higher timeframe supports the bears, it doesn't take much force to push price downward. The opposite is true in a bull market. If higher timeframes (such as the daily or weekly chart) are in an Always In Long state — that is, a bull market structure — then even if a decline occurs and the major trend reversal pattern isn't ideal, a bull move can still begin because the higher timeframe is dominated by the bulls. Multi-timeframe thinking is the key to making good trading decisions; understanding this helps you become a better day trader.
