# Price Action: Two-Legged Moves

BO, BO+FT, 3BMC, 4BMC, BO+2nd leg — these are essentially different entry points for the same trade. In other words, they are just different ways to enter the same move structure. Whether you chase the breakout, trade the follow-through, or wait for second-leg confirmation, you are ultimately participating in the same two-legged cycle.

The two-legged pattern is the most universal structure in Price Action, forming the fundamental unit of most chart patterns. Every market movement can be broken down into two pushes.
After the first push (AB), there is always a pullback (BC), followed by a second push (CD). In most cases, the AB leg and CD leg are roughly equal in magnitude, though this is not absolute. Almost every chart you see is a variation of this two-push structure.

From a practical standpoint, you should at least capture the major trend swings, because these dominant trend legs are what truly determine trading success. Minor moves can be participated in with limit orders, but core profits always come from the major swings. Analyzing and applying the two-push pattern is the best method for determining trend continuation versus reversal.

When the market is in an uptrend and begins forming a second push, evaluate the strength of the first push. If the first push consists of one or more strong bull bars with full bodies and strong momentum, followed by a weak bear bar, this is typically an ideal limit order opportunity. Price is probabilistically more likely to continue higher, so place a limit order as the second push begins.
If the limit order at the bottom doesn't get filled, this creates psychological urgency. At this point, you can be more aggressive — instead of insisting on buying at the bar's low, you can enter at market price within the bar's range to capture the continuation of the second push.

The core theme is always: When analyzing, first consider the market context. This usually requires multi-level analysis. Ask yourself: What is the higher timeframe doing? Where is the current timeframe in the trend cycle?

Although we can see the full picture when reviewing, in live trading we only see bars forming one by one. You must establish strict trading discipline to avoid entering prematurely.
For example, a breakout above the moving average may look good, but if the body is still below the moving average, bears may still enter short at the close. You need to wait for the next bar to confirm.

Reliable swing identification usually requires confirmation through a trendline break. After a series of bars form a trendline, observe whether price breaks that line in the opposite direction. If price breaks upward, it forms a second swing. A trendline break is the key to determining a change in the move.

Some pullbacks never touch the trendline — these small pullbacks are usually insignificant. For example, in a continuous bull move, although smaller timeframe charts may show a line break, the pullback lows on the current timeframe still hold the trendline. As long as the trendline remains intact, the main trend is still continuing.

In this case, all short-term counter-trend signals belong to secondary structure, and the real trading opportunity lies in confirming the continuation of the main trend. The integrity of the trendline is the core basis for determining whether a two-legged move is still in play.
