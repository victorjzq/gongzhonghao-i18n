# Price Action: The First Pullback

Breakouts, and the ability to distinguish successful breakouts from failed ones, are among the most important skills for a trader.

Traders must learn to discern: does a trend bar trigger a trend, or is it just a trap? Does it lead to a successful breakout, or does it eventually become a failed breakout and reversal? Because all trend bars can be viewed as breakouts. So breakouts within a trading range are essentially the same. The core skill is judging: when will a breakout lead to a new trend, and when is it just a failed attempt?

Once the first pullback occurs, bears realize that the subsequent rally will carry more two-sided trading, so they start selling above and below key bars. They know these price levels have value for bears. Once price breaks below the channel, they add to their short positions and take profits at the channel bottom. Why? Because they understand this is a "spike and channel" pattern. And the current move is just one bull leg within a trading range — the market will likely retest lower, perhaps even back to the channel bottom. So bears dare to add above and take profits at the bottom. Meanwhile, bulls buy at the channel bottom, and price bounces. After the bounce, Al says the "spike and channel" pattern is complete, and traders look for the next pattern. This is the market's rule: after the breakout phase, the market eventually devolves into two-sided trading, and the breakout gradually fades.

Remember a key principle: treat all bear channels as bull flags, and all bull channels as bear flags.

When a channel breaks upward, there's a 75% probability it will fail within 5 to 10 bars. So most bull channels, even when breaking upward, will quickly fail rather than producing stronger trend continuation.
Traders must accurately judge: when is a channel just one bull leg within a trading range, and when is it truly tight enough to be a breakout on a higher timeframe?

Next is the test. A test means the market returns to a support or resistance level. Traders decide whether to enter based on how the market behaves during the test.

The essence of reversal is transitioning from one type of behavior to the opposite behavior. Trading range behavior is the opposite of trend behavior. So when we say "major trend reversal," it doesn't necessarily mean price will completely change direction — it may just transition from trend to trading range.
This is also why the major trend reversal cases Al mentions don't always mean a true trend reversal — they often mean the market may enter a trading range phase.
