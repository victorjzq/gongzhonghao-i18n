# Price Action: Holding Long Equals Initiating Long

Holding long equals initiating long.

As a trader, sometimes you look at the chart and think: "I should be long right now." Or perhaps you prematurely exited a position you should have kept holding, but then hesitate: "I can't enter now because I'd be chasing."

Al's point is: if this was originally a valid long trade and you're currently flat, that is exactly the same as having continued holding the long — so you should just buy.

Any time you believe this is a reasonable swing trade, you should go long directly, with appropriate stop loss and position sizing.

A thought experiment can help solidify this concept.
Suppose you're a swing trader who bought at bar 5's close, intending to hold. As the market breaks out strongly and price rises above the prior swing high, you also move your stop up. Now suppose at bar 17, you accidentally exit due to a misclick, leaving you flat. But you know you should still be holding because this is a reasonable swing. What would you do? You wouldn't hesitate saying "the stop is too far" — you'd immediately buy back in. Because this is the same as having continued to hold — you just accidentally got out.

If the same accident happens on the next bar and you're flat again, you'd still buy back immediately.
If you imagine this scenario of "exiting, re-entering, exiting, re-entering" throughout the entire swing, it's actually no different from holding continuously. The key is: you always knew you wanted to maintain a long position.

Therefore, when you're a swing trader and you see an ongoing swing move that you believe is a reasonable long opportunity, you should just enter at market price with appropriate position sizing and stop loss.

If you're good at trading bull flags but not breakouts, that's fine — just focus on bull flags. Most people see the pattern one or two bars too late, missing the entry. But if it's a truly good trade, even entering slightly late with a light position is still reasonable.

Scalpers will exit more quickly but must re-enter more quickly; swing traders will take partial profits when risk increases, then add back when new patterns appear.

Computer programs in the market similarly wait for new buying opportunities after exiting. When they see a bull bar closing near its high, those traders who previously exited will add back to full position.

As long as the context is reasonable and the swing is valid, "going long now" when flat is equivalent to "continuing to hold" from the previous position.
