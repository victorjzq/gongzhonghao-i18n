# Price Action: Understanding Support and Resistance

The remarkable thing about support and resistance is that
you can always find clues that were previously hinted at.

At a support level, bears desperately need follow-through.
Actually, after a trendline break within the overall trading range,
buying on two legs down is better.

Two legs up touching resistance.
Bulls need follow-through.
If a disappointing bar appears, it may trigger bulls to give up.
Some bulls may start exiting by selling,
and the market subsequently declines.

Two legs up and at the top of the trading range.
No matter how strong the breakout looks,
within a trading range,
when price surges to the range top pulled by a vacuum,
even the strongest-looking breakout is useless — context determines everything.

Where's the bulls' problem? The risk/reward ratio.
Huge breakout, strong bars,
but the 1:1 reward level is already far away.

Traders going long are chasing momentum.
We're at resistance within a trading range.
The give-up bar indicates that momentum isn't there when it should be.
So bulls may abandon the trade here and start selling their positions.

If there are big up moves and big down moves,
but the bull breakouts are larger and stronger than the bear breakouts,
it's attracting buying.

A pattern structure more favorable for buying, with support.
Strong bull breakout, deep pullback,
but the gap still hasn't closed.
Meaning there are trapped traders here.

When the market tests this zone,
you expect a bounce or upward reversal.

When you see cyclical behavior in the market,
the market will cooperate with that behavior.

A nested wedge structure,
double bottom appearing at trading range support.
All factors favor the bulls.

Support and resistance levels are important, but even more important is the market's reaction when testing those levels.

For example, at a support level, we want to see a bullish structure — three pushes, double bottom — ideally three large-bodied bull bars closing at their highs.
