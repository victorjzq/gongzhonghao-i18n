# Price Action: Common Trading Questions (Part 4)

Trading is a marathon, not a gamble. You must build your account through a series of small, steady wins, not through one big success.
No successful trader in the world got there by "one trade that turned everything around." They all reached where they are through long-term discipline, strict risk control, and a stable system.
So please don't fantasize about changing your fate with one trade. That's a casino mentality, not a trader's mentality.

Sometimes after a few consecutive losses, you desperately want to win it back.
This is called revenge trading. Almost everyone has experienced it, and it's one of the main causes of large losses.
The best way to overcome it is: when you lose two trades in a row, stop immediately — regardless of how much time is left in the day. Turn off the computer, leave the market.
Because when your emotions are running high, it's very hard to stay calm. You'll rush to win back your losses, but the market won't give you opportunities just because you're anxious. On the contrary, the more anxious you are, the more mistakes you'll make.
So develop a habit: stop trading after two consecutive losses. Come back the next day. The market will always be there — one day won't make a difference.

The market is full of uncertainty. Every pattern you see can have two or more outcomes. For example, a triangle can break upward, break downward, or sometimes fake out and return to the range.
So trading isn't about predicting correctly — it's about accepting uncertainty. You must learn to think this way: whether it goes up or down, as long as it happens, I can handle it.
This is why we use probabilistic thinking. If you judge there's a 60% chance of going up, that means there's a 40% chance of going down. Your task is not to pursue 100% accuracy, but to ensure that when you're right, you earn more than when you're wrong.
In other words, your job is to manage risk well, ensure probability is on your side, rather than trying to predict the future.

Many people enter prematurely because they're afraid of missing out, only to get stopped out before the market truly starts moving.
One solution is: wait for a confirmation signal. For example, wait for a clear trend bar to close, or wait for follow-through buying after a breakout before entering. Although this means entering at a less favorable price, the success rate is higher.
Another approach is: you can enter in batches. Enter with a small portion of your position first; if the market truly goes in your direction, add more. This way, even if the early position gets stopped out, your loss is very small.
The key is: don't enter recklessly because you're afraid of missing out. The market has opportunities every day — you don't need to take unnecessary risks.

Every trading system has market conditions it's suited for. For example, a trend system will fail in a trading range; a trading range system will fail in a trend.
So it's not that your system doesn't work — the market environment has changed. You need to learn to identify whether the current market is trending or in a trading range, then use the corresponding system.
Additionally, you need a long enough sample to validate a system. Many people only trade for a few days, decide it doesn't work, and switch to another system. They end up constantly switching and never mastering anything.
My advice is: use at least several hundred trades to validate a system. If the overall result is profitable, then the system is effective. Don't give up because of short-term difficulties.
