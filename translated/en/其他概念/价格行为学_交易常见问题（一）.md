# Price Action: Common Trading Questions (Part 1)

Don't try to predict reversals — wait for the market to give sufficiently clear signals.
In a strong uptrend, if you see the market suddenly produce several consecutive large bear bars that break below previous key support levels, this is a signal that the trend may be reversing.

You cannot judge a reversal simply because "price has gone up too much" or "price has gone down too much." You must wait for structural signals — such as a trendline being broken, key levels being violated, or price patterns changing. Only when these conditions appear do you have sufficient reason to attempt a reversal trade.

Even then, you must remain cautious, because many so-called reversal signals ultimately turn out to be just a pullback within a larger trend. So once you attempt a reversal trade, you must strictly control risk. The stop loss must be placed at a clear level, and losses must be manageable.

The most common problem in trading range markets: You go short at the range high, and the market may break out directly; you go long at the range low, and the market may break down directly. The result is that your position gets stopped out. This is why trading ranges are the hardest market to trade — they constantly deceive you.

There are two solutions. The first is to reduce trading, or even stop trading entirely, and wait until the trading range ends before entering with the trend. Although this means fewer opportunities, the risk is much smaller. The second is, if you decide to trade within the trading range, you must accept that getting stopped out is normal. You may fail several times in a row, but as long as your stop losses are small enough, once the market truly breaks into a trend, you can earn it back. The key is still risk control — if you trade within a trading range, use very small position sizes and place your stop loss outside the range. This way, even if you're wrong several times, it won't hurt your account.

Almost every trader has experienced the fear of missing out. They see price move and can't resist the urge to enter immediately. The way to overcome this is actually simple: establish a clear set of rules. For example, "I will only enter when price breaks above the prior high AND two consecutive large bull bars appear." This way, when market conditions aren't met, no matter how impulsive you feel, you won't place a trade.

Another method is to deliberately pass on some opportunities during the learning phase. You see a decent signal, but it doesn't fully meet your criteria, so you don't enter. Even if it ultimately succeeds, you must resist. Because you need to train yourself to only act in the clearest situations.

Finally, constantly remind yourself: the market will always have opportunities. You don't need to make money on every move — just capturing the most obvious, highest-confidence opportunities is enough to be profitable. Patience is actually a habit; the more you practice it, the more natural it becomes. Eventually, when you see unclear price action, your instinctive reaction won't be "opportunity" but "danger" — that is the mindset of a truly mature trader.
