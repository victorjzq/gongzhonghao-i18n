# Price Action: Overview of Market Structure Trading Methods

## I. The Real Probability Logic in Trading

When you look at charts, you often feel the market is "very likely to do this or that."

But whenever you have this feeling, you need to pay special attention — the market may do the exact opposite of what you expect.

Because when something looks highly probable, the actual probability is often not as high as you imagine.

Price is truth, not your intuition.

If the market is falling, you should go short.

Even if you don't understand why it's falling, as long as it's falling, you should sell.

As long as you deny that the market has started doing something, you'll continue to suffer — this is the "Pain Trade."

So when a low-probability event begins happening, you can't keep deceiving yourself.

You must tell yourself: "I know it should be going up, but it's not — it's going down."

At this point, no matter how impossible something looks, you must believe the chart.

Trade what you see, not what you expect.

However the market moves, that's how you trade.

Price is truth, not the probability you imagined.

## II. Four Categories of Market Structure

Every chart's price action falls into four categories:

- Breakout
- Tight Channel
- Broad Channel
- Trading Range

Different structures have different trading methods:

### (1) Breakout

If the market is rising strongly with no significant pullbacks, large bar bodies closing near highs:

Even if there's only one large bull bar, but it's followed by 10-15 rising micro channel bars, the overall momentum is still strong.

Specific methods for going long:

- Market order to go long;
- Buy at the close of bull bars;
- Place stop orders to buy above the prior bar's high;
- Buy at the close of bear bars, betting the first reversal attempt will fail and the market will continue up.

### (2) Tight Channel

Once the breakout begins showing pullbacks, it enters the channel phase.

If the channel is very narrow, it's essentially a breakout on a higher timeframe.

In this case, just go long with the trend — the specific reason doesn't matter much.

You can:

- Buy below bars, betting bear reversal attempts will fail;
- Buy above bars, betting the trend continues;
- Buy at bear bar closes, buy at bull bar closes — all are acceptable.

### (3) Broad Channel

Even in a tight channel, over time, deeper pullbacks will occur.

When pullbacks deepen, the market enters an "Always In Short" state, even though there are still higher highs and higher lows — the overall trend is still bullish.

But because pullbacks are deep enough, bears can also make money:

- Selling near prior highs can be profitable;
- Going short at reversal points can also be profitable.

This type of price action is called a slanted trading range, tilting upward.

Although the overall trend is bullish and you should go long, you can also go short on bounces.

If you go short in a broad channel, it's usually scalping, unless a very strong reversal appears.

Bulls can go long at 50% pullbacks, or buy at the midpoint of the previous up leg.

You can place your stop below the prior low.

You can also choose to go short at the prior high, or even go short at reversals, using limit orders and scaling in.

### (4) Trading Range

A trading range is similar to a broad channel but more horizontal.

The operating strategy is:

- Buy low, sell high, primarily scalping;
- Both market orders and limit orders to buy are acceptable;
- Sell in the top 1/3 of the range;
- You can scale in with a wider stop loss;
- Bear second entries and micro double tops are classic shorting tactics.
