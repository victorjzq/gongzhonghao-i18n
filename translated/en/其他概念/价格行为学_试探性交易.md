# Price Action: Probing Trades

Doji bars appearing early in a trend convey a very important message: **a pullback is very likely imminent**. Moreover, it's more likely to be a trend resumption after a pullback.

If the market chooses the trend continuation path — for example, price breaks below the doji's low — that breakout level is often tested. In many cases, this test occurs almost immediately. This means you can take profits here while simultaneously preparing a limit order for the next move.

Think of doji bars as traders' "regulators" — they constantly remind you: **at this stage, you should adjust your behavior**.

Early in a trend, traders should look for buyable pullbacks to participate in the bull trend; late in a trend, doji bars often mean **you should consider taking profits**.

Late-trend doji bars are especially important because they typically signal the market is transitioning toward a trading range. As the market continues upward searching for a top, reversal risk is gradually accumulating. Therefore, if you're already holding a long position, the more reasonable choice at this stage is to look for profit-taking opportunities, or even start watching for counter-trend trades. Because as the trend enters its late phase, counter-trend trading becomes practically viable — even if price is still rising, the purpose is often to complete a reversal, not to continue the trend. For experienced traders, those seemingly insignificant bars are precisely telling them **what to do next**.

As a trader, when I miss a trend move, I easily develop the urge to chase, and the result is often getting trapped in the subsequent pullback. If I know in advance where to place limit orders and where to expect pullbacks, I can significantly improve this situation. Especially late in a trend, if emotions start getting impatient, trading errors are even more likely.

We're often taught to "sell below bars, buy above bars," but in actual trading, the more critical reference is often not the body but the tail. Truly high-quality limit trades usually occur at the top or bottom of tails.

When you do closing analysis each day, you'll discover that a large number of trading opportunities actually come from limit orders placed at tail positions. As long as you understand what tails represent and know where to place orders, you'll find there are far more tradable opportunities than you imagined. This point alone is well worth repeated study. The midpoint of the tail is equally important because it usually corresponds to key support or resistance within a trading range, especially when two bars in opposite directions appear.

If a tail is very prominent, the areas approximately 30% from the top and bottom of the tail also carry significant importance. Take extremely long tails as an example — these bars often come with strong momentum. In this case, rather than trading at the tail's midpoint, consider selling at approximately the top 30% area of the tail. This typically means a larger trading range is forming on a lower timeframe, and the selling zone of that range is often concentrated in the upper third. Therefore, selling between the midpoint and the top of the tail is reasonable, and the upper third is often a better estimate. Numerous cases show that when the market tests these long tails, it frequently reaches this level.

For example, some bars' retests exceed 50% depth but ultimately stop near the upper third. If you plan to place limit orders on these large bars with long tails, the areas 30% from the top and bottom of the tail are typically very ideal prices. However, accept this fact: approximately 30% of the time, the market won't retrace to the midpoint, and your limit order won't get filled. This is part of the cost. But when it does fill, it usually means you've obtained an extremely advantageous entry price.

All methods of placing limit orders based on estimated support or resistance are essentially probing trades. The meaning of probing is: the market may attempt to reach a certain price, but once it touches that price, a reversal occurs. Tails are important tools for estimating these prices. You're not chasing price at lower levels — you're waiting at higher levels for the market to briefly touch and reverse. Sometimes the market doesn't reach your estimated price before pulling back — this happens frequently in daily trading, but it's not a problem. Because when the estimate is correct and the order fills, you get an optimized, high-quality entry that carries long-term value in itself.

In Al's live trading course, he frequently uses this approach. He explicitly states that many limit orders won't get filled, and this is the norm for probing trades.

Another typical application is when you identify the market forming a channel structure that has already produced two and a half or nearly three pushes, and you also see doji-type formations like those mentioned above. At this point, you can consider selling near the trend channel line.

The specific approach is: observe the magnitude of the previous breakout that formed the channel line, then project the same magnitude above the last bar and place a limit sell order, expecting price to reverse at that level in preparation for the next leg.

As for why not buy instead — that depends on the overall context. If the buying opportunity itself isn't ideal and the market is already late in the trend, continuing to go long likely faces severe reversal risk. In this case, a probing sell is actually more reasonable. The value of tails lies precisely in their ability to help you estimate where buyers and sellers are likely to congregate, keeping your trading behavior synchronized with market structure.

If these estimates fail — for example, a strong upward closing breakout occurs — then the price zone previously used for selling often transforms into a new buying zone. At that point, you can consider placing a limit buy near the tail's midpoint, or making a short-term trade.

These structural opportunities are often overlooked because most traders focus only on strong bars and momentum. But in reality, the market spends most of its time in trading ranges — whether viewed from an intraday, weekly, or single-bar perspective. The trading range within a single bar also has trading value, and how to exploit this is a very important skill.

If you miss a trade, don't worry. Usually within 15 to 20 minutes, the market will form a new structure, providing you with a new entry opportunity.
