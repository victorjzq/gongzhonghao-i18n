# Price Action: Common Trading Questions (Part 5)

You clearly judged the direction correctly, yet you were scared out by short-term fluctuations.
The reason is: you haven't truly accepted that "volatility is normal." Every trend has pullbacks — no move goes straight up or straight down. If you want to capture the entire trend, you must endure the fluctuations along the way.

There are two solutions:
First, reduce your position size. With a smaller position, you won't be frightened by minor fluctuations because the loss is within your tolerable range.
Second, set your stop loss and stick to it firmly. Tell yourself: "As long as the stop loss hasn't been hit, I don't move." This is how you can sit through the trade.

In other words, you need to learn to tolerate imperfection. Otherwise, you'll only ever capture small profits while missing the big moves.

Prediction is one of the biggest traps for traders. Because once you make a prediction, you psychologically "hold yourself hostage." You'll desperately try to prove you're right instead of following the market.
The correct trading attitude is: don't predict — follow. If the market tells you it's going up, go long with it; if the market tells you it's going down, go short with it.
You don't need to predict — you only need to react. However the market moves, that's what you do. This way, you won't stubbornly cling to your views.

Results are indeed important, but in the short term, they're random. You can do everything right and still lose; you can also make mistakes and get lucky making money.
So if you only look at results, you'll constantly be pulled around by emotions and never learn anything real.
The correct approach is: focus on the process, not the result. Ask yourself: "Did I follow the rules? Did I stop out when I should have? Did I exit when I should have?"
As long as the process is correct, the results will eventually be correct. Because over the long run, your edge will manifest.

When you can consistently execute your system, no longer be swayed by emotions, and no longer experience wild elation or despair over a single win or loss, that's when you're a real trader.
It's not about how much money you make, but whether your process is stable and rational. Even if you're not yet profitable, as long as you can strictly execute your plan, you're already closer to success than most people.
A real trader is not defined by results, but by discipline and execution.

If you had to summarize the core of trading in one sentence:
Bet when you have an edge, control risk, and let probability do its work.
That's all there is to trading. You don't need complex theories, you don't need to predict the future — you only need three things:
First, find a setup that is statistically in your favor.
Second, always set a stop loss to ensure risk is manageable.
Third, persist over the long term, giving probability enough occurrences to pay off.

Achieve these three things, and you can survive in the market and ultimately profit.
