# Price Action: How to Understand Market Inertia

Market inertia, also known as the 80% rule. This inertia manifests primarily in two aspects: first, momentum and trends — momentum tends to form trends; second, mean reversion — which typically forms trading ranges.

Both types of moves exhibit inertia characteristics. Therefore, when the market is trending, you can usually observe the trend establishment process, and you should realize: at least the first two or three reversal attempts will not succeed.

When looking for reversal opportunities within a trending market — this is what's called reversal trading — we need to capture tradable reversals. In the reverse direction, the reversal direction, how do we use the 80% rule to capture these reversal opportunities? After five consecutive reversal signals appear, the success rate for reversal trades becomes very high. 80% corresponds to one out of five. You could say that from the fourth reversal signal bar onward, the probability of a successful reversal increases and the win rate starts rising — this is the classic expression of the wedge pattern.

In a wedge reversal, the market usually doesn't immediately reverse downward at the third signal — it typically retests resistance one more time, ultimately forming a fourth reversal bar. This is the type of low-probability, high-reward opportunity that experienced traders know well.

When using the 80% rule to capture reversals, you typically need to watch for: first, the market triggering a sell signal, and second, a bar closing below the signal bar.

In trading range conditions, if previously triggered signals appear along with selling pressure, then a reversal on the third or fourth bar may occur. But in a small-pullback trend, it may take five, six, or even more attempts. Sometimes the pullback extends to the sixth push, and bears lack effective follow-through bar confirmation — the market will very likely continue the uptrend. However, when price climbs to a higher level and a sell signal bar appears, but the market's close doesn't break below that bar's low, if another sell signal bar appears, this will be a higher-probability trading opportunity.

Most pullbacks are just minor reversals. When identifying pullback patterns, they must meet the typical characteristics of pullback patterns. Specific characteristics include: weak opening, weak follow-through, or although the move is strong, it forms too early. When a spike is very powerful and a strong signal bar appears, but the position isn't ideal.

When traders discover five pushes within a trading range, they typically stop counter-trend trading, which accelerates the formation of a breakout. Because if no one is trading counter-trend, the side holding their positions can dominate the market's direction.

When you're holding a position and encounter a market reversal, this is the market's normal behavior. Whenever a strong one-sided move occurs, the market always returns to a state of chaos to shake out weak hands. But if you deeply understand this principle, you won't easily get shaken out.
