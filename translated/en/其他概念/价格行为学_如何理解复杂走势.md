# Price Action: How to Understand Complex Price Action

Counter-trend micro channel patterns are critical — even if the pattern fails this time and the market makes a new low, it will usually at least retest that price level.

In a bull market, when a strong bear bar appears and price tests the moving average, bulls will buy the dip, scaling in gradually and adding to their positions while waiting for the trend to continue. When a bear market is established, if a strong bull bar appears near the moving average, traders will go short, betting on trend continuation.

A so-called complex price push is composed of smaller fractal structures, containing one or more pullbacks within it. Each small-scale push's pullback breaks a micro channel trendline, so the pullback must break at least one small trendline. Once a trendline is broken, it means the previous push has been disrupted, and at this point the probability of that move completing increases significantly. You need to watch whether the trendline has been broken. Before the trendline is broken, the probability of a two-push pattern occurring is low, because as long as the market maintains this move, the trendline won't be broken. Only after the break can a second push occur.

All complex pushes are essentially trading range pushes — they are variants of channels. In a trading channel, multiple price pushes can form, and as the price action develops, the number of pushes continues to increase. What happens to the trading probability in this situation? First, a basic price push forms, then a spike occurs, and at this point you can observe a clear channel pattern. At the bottom of the channel, we need to evaluate the probability of trade success.

How many reversals have already occurred? During the first spike phase — the early stage of the channel — the probability is roughly 60% to 40%. As the channel extends and multiple pushes develop, the probability of continuation drops below 25%, while the reversal probability exceeds 75%. Therefore, when price pushes become complex, the probabilities shift — during the push process, the probability of a pullback increases.

The more complex the move, the greater the likelihood of a pullback. When a pullback move occurs and the trendline is broken, the move becomes complex because when the trend restarts, a new push forms. This complex structure is more common than simple pushes. This complexity increases pullback probability because the market is now showing not a single push, but multiple pushes within a trading range or channel variant. Even if one-directional momentum appears, it cannot sustain indefinitely — channels will eventually correct. This is a matter of probability.

Of course, there are counter-trend traders who specialize in the 25% probability trades.

For the 75% probability traders, you need to clearly define the risk range, which usually appears as a signal bar. Traders enter based on this signal bar, allowing at most one additional signal bar's worth of movement as the reasonable range for price fluctuation. Trading strategies vary by individual and need to be adjusted based on current market conditions, but through a probability distribution chart, you can clearly understand market expectations. Thoroughly grasping this principle is crucial, because when analyzing charts, you must constantly think from the counterparty's perspective and clearly identify your trading opponent.

For example, if you see the daily chart is in a bullish trend, with the day's low, then a spike bar appears followed by a channel move — this forms a spike-and-channel bull trend — and the market should start pulling back. At this point, you could say it's a 75%-25% probability trading opportunity. But if the higher timeframe chart shows a tight bull channel, when you review you'll discover what happened to those seemingly certain-to-fail patterns. This is the effect of the higher timeframe suppressing the lower timeframe chart, because all forces are pushing in the same direction.

Therefore, even with high-probability opportunities like spike-and-wedge channels, if the trend is currently strong and in the early stages of a move, the pullback probability decreases and the failure probability increases.

This is just the standard probability, not the absolute probability — the true probability depends on the current market environment. When the higher timeframe is in a strong uptrend, this probability model no longer applies to the current chart. Suppose the daily timeframe is sideways or even in a downtrend, but there's an unfilled gap below the current small pullback uptrend — would this still maintain approximately 55% upside probability?

Regarding trading ranges and fixed timeframe situations, the key is to look at where price is within the range. If price is in the middle, bulls and bears each have 50% probability. When price approaches the bottom of the range, bottom support will dominate the price action. Therefore, the key is the relative position between the 5-minute chart and the higher timeframe framework. Price typically oscillates within the range, making you think it's about to break out and continue the trend. But when the higher timeframe is in a trading range, the market is actually governed by the rules of the trading range.
