# Price Action: Support and Resistance

What controls price is support and resistance, not news.

Support is a price zone below the market — a place where buyers may be stronger than sellers; resistance is the opposite. Price action at these "inflection points" either breaks through or reverses, so they always harbor the potential for a significant move. Stop losses and position sizes must be set in accordance with the timeframe you're trading.

## I. Trend Phase: The 80% Rule

**Strong bear trend**
80% of support levels will be broken, and 80% of reversal attempts will fail. Price won't generate heavy buying at prior lows but instead keeps declining; multiple bull bars and small double bottom reversals often fail as well.

**Strong bull trend**
80% of resistance levels will be broken through. The market has inertia and tends to continue in its original direction.

## II. Trading Range: The Same 80% Rule

When the market enters a trading range, 80% of trend breakout attempts will fail.
The range top = obvious resistance, the range bottom = obvious support. Traders expect bear breakouts to fail and tend toward buying low, selling high, and quick scalping, since both downside and upside movement are limited.

## III. The Attraction and Testing of Support and Resistance

The market constantly probes support and resistance, searching for the fair price zone. This is the core subject most worth studying for traders — not news.
Observe whether the chart's left side has already tested a particular structure: if not yet tested, price typically reaches the level first before deciding to stay or leave; if tested multiple times, a breakout or reversal may happen at any time.

## IV. Breakout Test

If a bear trend breaks below the range bottom, observe whether price pulls back to test the breakout point before continuing down; if a reversal appears, go short.
Some bulls who bought at the range bottom scale in 1-2x after the breakdown, hoping for a bounce to exit; the scaling-in itself pushes price up for the pullback, forming the breakout test.

## V. Common Sources of Support / Resistance

- Trendlines, channel tops
- Prior highs, prior lows
- Measured move targets
- Moving averages at various levels; daily 50/100/150/200-day MAs
- Round numbers and options strike prices
- 50% pullback: support buy point in bull trends, resistance sell point in bear trends
- Entry / scaling-in / exit prices, profit target levels
- Daily and prior day's high, low, open, close; corresponding levels on daily, weekly, monthly charts
- Highest close and lowest close within swing zones
- Reversal test points before trend endings

Any of these can become the most critical support or resistance on a chart, depending on the context and strength at the time.

## Conclusion

Support and resistance are the dominant forces behind market movement and the source of trading opportunities. You need to know where they are, learn to observe patterns, understand the 80% rule, watch for breakout tests — rather than being led around by news.
