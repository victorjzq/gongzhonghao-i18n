# Price Action: How to Understand the Relationship Between Higher and Lower Timeframes (Part 1)

Sometimes the daily chart will be in a "close-to-buy" bull trend or a "close-to-sell" bear trend. If so, it typically creates trading opportunities for intraday traders on lower timeframe charts. For example, if the daily chart is in a "close-to-buy" bull trend, then the morning session usually features a small pullback, followed by a bull trend day for the rest of the session. Therefore, intraday traders should look for upward reversal opportunities after the morning pullback to buy.

If the daily chart is in a strong trend — for instance, a "micro channel" with no pullbacks — then traders will look for the first pullback as an entry opportunity. For example, if there's a bear micro channel with four, five, or six bars and no pullback, traders will go short when the market first breaks above the previous day's high. If you're an intraday trader, you would wait on a lower timeframe (such as the 5-minute or 60-minute chart) for the market to break above yesterday's high and then form a downward reversal. Once you see the reversal, you should go short because the bear trend is very likely to continue.

When the market is in a trading range, large bull bars often appear at the top of the range. At this point, you should not buy on the close — you should assume the rally will fail. This means you should go short when you see a downward reversal. If you're an intraday trader and see a downward reversal in the morning on the 5-minute or 60-minute chart, that's a shorting opportunity. The same logic applies: if a large bear bar appears at the bottom of the trading range, and if you're an intraday trader who sees an upward reversal in the morning, that's a good buying opportunity for a swing trade. Finally, if you see support or resistance on a higher timeframe chart (such as the daily or 60-minute chart) and you're an intraday trader, when the market reverses at these levels, it's often a good swing trading opportunity.

Even in a strong bull trend, even with consecutive strong bull bars, the bars usually have lower tails. In 90% of cases, this is due to the morning pullback; in 10% of cases, the pullback occurs later in the day. If you're trading the 5-minute chart and you know the market is in a "close-to-buy" bull trend on the higher timeframe, you should look for a morning decline followed by an upward reversal for a potential swing trade. You must assume the closing price will be higher each day, so every day will be a bull trend day. You look for a morning low and wait for an upward reversal.

When the market is in a strong "close-to-buy" trend, you should look for reversal opportunities after morning pullbacks every day. Each time a pullback approaches the previous day's close, the day's open, or the moving average and forms a reversal structure like a double bottom, wedge, or triangle, you should actively look for buying opportunities. These are all excellent swing entry points.

The higher timeframe's pattern structure determines support and resistance levels and their corresponding breakout probabilities. We should follow the side with higher probability and wait for entry conditions on the lower timeframe — this is the essence of "follow the higher timeframe, enter on the lower timeframe."
