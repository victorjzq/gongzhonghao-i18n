# Price Action: How to Understand Reversal Bars

A reversal bar is a signal bar, but it must have context support. The context conditions are: there must be a trend line breakout, a test of an extreme, and then the reversal bar appears.

Looking for perfect signal bars is especially important when trading counter-trend. When trading with the trend — such as buying pullbacks in a trend or selling pullbacks — you can be more lenient.

However, meeting the minimum criteria does not equal meeting the ideal criteria. For example, a bull reversal bar should at least close above its open, or close above its midpoint. But this is the minimum requirement, and only when the context is particularly good can you accept the minimum standard. If you are attempting a major trend reversal, the standard must be higher.

Characteristics of a good reversal bar:
The open is near or below the prior bar's close. For example, some bars even open one tick lower, showing that bears were overly aggressive and were quickly bought up.

The close is above the open and preferably well above the prior bar's close, showing strong bulls.

The lower tail is one-third to one-half of the bar, and the upper tail is very small or nonexistent. The lower tail indicates that intrabar price was rejected. Don't just focus on patterns — understand the price action. Reversals may look different on different timeframes. For example, on a 30-minute chart you might see a classic large lower tail with a bull body, while on the 5-minute chart it is not obvious, but the result is the same.

The close breaks through the close or high of the prior few bars. Even without classic tails, as long as the close reverses the preceding price, it counts as a reversal bar. The key is to understand what the market is expressing.

There is not much overlap with the preceding few bars. If the reversal bar overlaps too much with prior bars, it is likely just a bounce within a range rather than a trend reversal. This situation often appears at the high of a tight bear channel or after a bear breakout — buying here is actually chasing.

Next are some cautionary notes:

Be wary of bull reversal bars with tails at the top, or bear reversal bars with tails at the bottom, because this means the opposing side is still present.

Avoid reversal bars that are much smaller than the preceding bars. For example, after a large bear bar, if the reversal bar is just a small bull bar, the success rate is not high.

Avoid situations where the entire reversal bar accounts for all the movement within the range. This is common in tight channels or trading ranges. For example, you buy above a so-called reversal bar, but you are actually at the top of a range with no room left.

Finally, the highest probability reversals are with-trend reversals. For example, a reversal bar at a small bear flag in a large bull trend, or a reversal bar at a small bull flag in a large bear trend. These are the highest probability reversals because they are essentially with-trend.
