# Price Action: How to Understand Reversals (Part 4)

A trading range is the result.
What should happen before a trading range forms?
Two-sided trading.
This is actually caused by another reason.
A trading channel formed.

What role do trading ranges play?
They continuously break trend lines, breaking up and down repeatedly.
So the formation of a trading range requires both a reversal and that reversal breaking through a trend line.

If price breaks through a major high, the count restarts.
When the close breaks through a major high, it constitutes a reversal signal for bears, and the count must restart.
When price continues to rise and forms a very clear close above that swing point, this force dominates the market until it fails.
The sign of its failure is the market closing below its bottom.

The trend line is the line that has been tested the most times.
But sometimes the situation is unclear — there may be tails slightly breaking higher, or some tests that don't quite reach the trend line. All these situations are possible.
If this line connects the most test points, it is very likely the main control line of the trend.

When a trend experiences a pullback — regardless of what kind of pullback — it usually leads to more sideways consolidation.
Experienced traders use this to judge when to take profits and when to stop trading with the trend.
Being flat is itself an advantage — you can buy at discount prices and position for trend resumption.
As the trend progresses, pullback magnitudes gradually increase, triggering larger-scale corrections.

When you see small pullbacks and can identify some minor trend legs, understand that these are just brief pauses, not true pullbacks.
These all break trend lines on smaller timeframes, but no significant pullback has occurred on the current main timeframe.

All these moves constitute the first pullback on a higher timeframe.
At this point, trend continuation is more likely.
The first pullback on this timeframe provides you with another high-probability entry opportunity in that direction.

Knowing full well that the next two weeks will be sideways to down.
This fear of missing out — worrying about missing the rally while holding a position — is precisely the fundamental reason why those trend-following authors write books spanning hundreds of thousands of pages.
If you have read their books, you will find that the fear of missing out is a theme that runs throughout.

Since you know the market will go sideways or decline, why endure the pain of drawdowns and waste already-earned profits?
Those traders who choose to hold through pullbacks typically size their positions smaller, because no one knows when the pullback will end.
If you choose not to hold through the pullback and simply exit, you may actually reduce risk.

When the pullback ends and the move resumes, getting back on the trend becomes very difficult.
Traders miss out on profits while also avoiding the anxiety and stress of pullbacks.
Test moves are usually very strong and directional, looking like complete reversals, but they are actually just the testing phase — eventually stopping somewhere and reversing.
