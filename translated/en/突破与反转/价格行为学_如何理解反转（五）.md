# Price Action: How to Understand Reversals (Part 5)

From an operational strategy perspective, to achieve stable and excellent trading performance, the best approach is to make multiple repeatable trades rather than betting on a single long-term position.

For traders with much smaller capital, as long as they put a little more thought into entry timing, the success rate will noticeably improve — this is actually not difficult. Every time you see three to four legs, at least take profits on half the position. If the market unexpectedly surges, at least the other half can continue to profit, but the probability of this is very low — extremely low.

A pullback will not completely reverse the trend, but it will push the market into a trading range until the opposing force tries two or three times before giving up. During their attempts, as a bull you are not making money, because you are stuck in a trading range. Sometimes this situation can last for years.

When a pause appears in a downtrend, the probability of the market continuing to plunge is not high. But sometimes the market declines contrary to expectations — the price level that should have formed support and triggered a reversal or sideways movement fails to stop the decline, creating a price vacuum with a rapid drop followed by an immediate stop and reversal. This indicates that from a probability perspective, after multiple reversals, the market should have bottomed here and entered sideways consolidation. Many traders, trading systems, and algorithms trade based on these common market patterns, but when market behavior deviates from the norm, these traders are often trapped at highs.

Many traders adopt a scaling-in strategy because they know price will likely be tested, and once the test is complete they choose to exit. This phenomenon attracts buying. Bears also understand this. Facing this situation, would you continue to bet on 100% of the move? Most people would not — they would choose partial profit-taking. This gives bears the opportunity to cover and close. Especially when a strong reversal appears followed by a second reversal after the open, bulls enter aggressively. Market participants all understand that price will inevitably test the level where it should have reversed and entered sideways, so selling willingness is clearly insufficient. This wait-and-see attitude continues until that price level is tested. Once the test is complete, the objective is achieved.

What truly needs to be deeply understood is that even minor pullbacks play an important role in limiting directional price movement.

The most critical thing is to analyze the nature of this decline. If this move shows strong directionality — for example, forming a breakout pattern — then the credibility of a higher low decreases, the credibility of a double bottom also decreases, and the market often still needs one final test.

If this is part of a trading range, when bulls hold the low and take control of the market, the probability of an upward reversal significantly increases. In this case, the reversal is characterized by a higher low and qualifies as a major trend reversal. This major reversal stems from a breakout of the bear trend line, and the breakout itself and how it occurs are equally important. The more control and momentum the market shows during the breakout, the higher the probability of forming a major trend reversal. Most reversals do not develop into major reversals, while a major reversal means a strong breakout signal above the bear trend line appears, followed by trend continuation and the start of a new market cycle.

If it is a major reversal, you will see an opposite market cycle, along with a breakout and channel-type bull trend. This is what a major trend means. But in most cases, the market does not produce this — the actual probability is about 40%, with the remaining 60% becoming trading ranges. The more factors on the chart that match major reversal characteristics, the more likely that 40% probability will be realized. At this point, when the market reverses, it does not pull back, does not return to the original area, but breaks key levels and begins to surge significantly. A lower low is simultaneously a bear breakout that breaks below the major key level. In this situation, the success probability is high. Before a major trend reversal initiates, there will be at least a higher low or a double bottom pattern.
