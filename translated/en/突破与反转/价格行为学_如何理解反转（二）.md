# Price Action: How to Understand Reversals (Part 2)

Trend reversals.
Usually the first step requires a breakout of the trend line.
There are many trend lines, but the more important the trend line, the more reliable the reversal signal.

And the result of most trend line breakouts, even major breakouts, does not immediately trigger a reversal.
Instead, there will be another test.
After a trend line breakout, the prior high will be tested.
The market will pull back and test the other side of that trend line.
At some point, when bears begin to build momentum, the pullback test may not actually reach the trend line.
But for those initial early trend line breakouts, there is usually a pullback test that just reaches that line, sometimes overshooting slightly.

This is actually one way to build a reversal trading system — one that chooses later entry timing.
They wait for a trend line breakout and then bet that the pullback test will not return to that level.
So they choose to sell at the trend line test.

When a reversal begins, the most critical question is choosing when to enter.
Reversals usually begin with seemingly insignificant signals.
Therefore, precisely defining the reversal signal is crucial.
This is the role of definitions.
Every trader may have a different definition of reversal signals.
What matters is having a clear understanding of your trading instrument.
With clear definitions, we can promptly identify reversals when they occur.

If a flag pattern fails to function and continue the existing trend, then the pattern may have already failed.
So what does a failed flag lead to?

Part of a new trend.
This first leg usually belongs to two structures simultaneously:
It is part of the bear trend, because it formed a bear flag and a swing point;
At the same time, it is a component of the bull trend, which begins from a leg within the trading range and then breaks out.

When you encounter a bear trend, you will see some form of flag pattern — the most reliable being a triangle.
Then there will be one to two continuation legs, followed by a reversal.

A triangle as a flag can actually be any pattern.
It could be a wedge, a completely horizontal tight trading range, or a wider trading range — it doesn't matter. It could even be an expanding triangle.
In short, whatever the flag structure, it can occur before or after the final leg.

At this point, trend continuation is still more likely.
So the probability of trend continuation is higher.
But if trend continuation does not happen and instead there is an upside breakout that begins to rise, then this is still a final flag.
This is typically an important component of the flag pattern, establishing a key pullback level for the bear trend.

All selling operations in this area are low probability trades.
