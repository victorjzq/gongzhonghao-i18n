# Price Action: Failed Breakouts

The most reliable reset patterns often appear in the form of traps: the market reverses and provides an entry reason, such as a good bear signal bar or a second short opportunity. After triggering the signal, the market suddenly surges sharply upward.

A narrow trading range must simultaneously have both a double bottom and a double top. If either of these elements is missing, it cannot be called a narrow trading range — it is likely just a pullback.

When in doubt, avoid buying at the bar's close, especially when most of the body is below the moving average. It's better to wait for a signal away from the moving average.

In a failed breakout, the market tests a price level. For example, there was previously a bear channel, and this price level is the top of that channel. At this point, the market shows strong momentum moving toward that price level. The market slightly breaks above it and then fails. Subsequently, the market moves in the opposite direction, and the bottom of the breakout — which should have been a support area or where stop losses were placed — gets broken strongly with good follow-through. Usually at this point, there will be a 1 to 2-leg pullback, followed by at least one more leg down; if conditions are more extreme, an entirely new channel may form. This is a failed bull breakout, and the result is the formation of an opposite pattern.
