# Price Action: Pattern Failures Create High Probability

Pattern failures often breed new high-probability trading opportunities. Any trading structure has two sides: one is the successful path where things run as expected, and the other is the failure path where things deviate from expectations.

Failure itself must be studied systematically. The key is not the failure outcome, but the specific form the failure takes: What kind of bar structure appears? How fast is the push or reversal? What contextual factors are simultaneously at play?

If you incur a loss while betting on the high-probability direction and the market is currently unfolding a low-probability path, the real question is: after your stop loss exit, is there a new, immediately identifiable high-probability opportunity? In most cases, the answer is yes. Failures often transform into new high-probability trades, but the prerequisite is that you have studied and familiarized yourself with these failure structures in advance. Otherwise, you will only fall into anxiety and confusion in the moment, missing the window to recover losses.

Market behavior is usually influenced by two levels of factors: one is the local rhythm, and the other is the higher-level overall structural pattern. Rhythm is dynamically changing. If you cannot clearly judge the current rhythm, you can assume it is between 3 and 4. This means the market will frequently attempt reversals, even if these attempts do not necessarily succeed — on average, one attempt occurs every 3 to 4 bars. Historically, a 3-bar rhythm was more common, but recently 4-bar rhythms have noticeably increased.

Any double top structure includes a neckline. If price breaks through the neckline with at least minimal follow-through behavior, conditions are typically in place to initiate a bear trend.

Factors that make double tops or double bottoms unreliable include: insufficient distance between the second test and the first test; insufficient strength in the second reversal bar; and a still-strong market context where the trend has conditions for resumption. This description uses bulls as the example, but the same applies in the bear direction. When these factors overlap, the reversal effectiveness of double tops or double bottoms is significantly reduced.

When discussing market context, we can also introduce an independent judgment dimension: whether the reversal structure is complete. If what you are looking for is an intraday high or intraday low type reversal, and the structure has not yet fully formed, that price level will very likely still be tested again.

A so-called "incomplete reversal structure" usually means one of the following situations: insufficient reversal bars to constitute an effective push; the reversal signal itself was not truly triggered; or the key trend line has not yet been broken. Among these factors, the trend line breakout is particularly critical.

Comparing the channel regression line with the moving average is a very effective observation method. The moving average represents the average price, while the channel regression line represents the average momentum. When the average momentum and average price remain roughly parallel, it indicates that market control is still solid, and the more reasonable approach is to continue looking for opportunities in the trend direction.
