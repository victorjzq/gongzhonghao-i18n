# Price Action: Final Flag (Part 2)

## [1] Take Partial Profits at Twice the Initial Risk

When price reaches 2R (twice the initial risk), it is a reasonable first reduction point.
This level is often near the prior high (or prior low), where partial position closing is common.
Traders realize: the market may not necessarily enter a new trend, and is more likely to chop first, so they switch to "buy low, sell high."

## [2] The Many Faces of the Final Bear Flag

Triangle zone: can be viewed as a final bear flag. If the bear breakout fails and a bull breakout occurs, it becomes part of a higher low MTR second leg up.
Head and shoulders bottom: left shoulder - head - right shoulder; this is also another appearance of the final bear flag.
Small flag: even just one or four bars can be the final flag after a selling climax.

## [3] Tight Consolidation at the End of a Bull Trend = Final Bull Flag

After 20+ bars of bull movement followed by sideways consolidation, it is often the final flag.
If the upside breakout is accompanied by a large bull gap without good follow-through, it is very likely an exhaustion gap rather than a measured gap.
The structure is often "two legs up + bear bar closing near its low," meaning the second leg is a bull trap; traders go short at the bear signal bar's low, targeting a return to the rally origin and measuring an equal decline based on height.

## [4] Selling Climax and the Final Flag

After three or four consecutive selling climaxes, bears tend to take profits and stand aside; strong bulls use wide stops to scale in at lows.
The ultimate selling climax is often paired with a moving average gap bar (the bar's high is below the moving average, with a gap between the bar and the moving average).
Low-risk long: buy above the gap bar's high, stop loss below the low; target at least a return to the top of the previous selling climax — the height-to-risk ratio is extremely high.

## [Conclusion]

The final flag can be a wedge, triangle, head and shoulders, or even a single bar; the key is that it is located at the end of a trend and is accompanied by momentum exhaustion.
Take partial profits at twice the risk first, then determine the bearish-to-bullish or bullish-to-bearish transition based on structure.
Exhaustion gaps and moving average gap bars are important supporting evidence for identifying reversals.
