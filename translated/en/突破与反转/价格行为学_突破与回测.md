# Price Action: Breakouts and Retests

Breakouts and retests are the most common and most error-prone structures in the market. Understanding the momentum logic and trading rhythm behind them is the key to distinguishing real from false breakouts and improving win rates.

---

## 1. Basic Logic of Breakouts and Retests

1. The breakout point is often the retest target

   * After the market breaks through an important level, **it usually retests the original breakout point**;
   * This is the convergence zone for bull and bear traders, where new momentum release often occurs.

2. Identifying breakout traps

   * If there is no follow-through after a breakout, it is often a **failed breakout structure**;
   * Counter-trend traders easily profit here, and price often produces a "breakout -> reversal."

> If the structure does not continue, the retest is the entry;
> If momentum does not follow, most breakouts are false.

---

## 2. Methods for Judging Whether a Breakout Is Valid

1. Think from the opponent's perspective

   * If you were the **opposing side**, would you easily be trapped at this level?
   * If the opposing side is already in trouble, the market tends to **run one-directionally**.

2. Trading strategy

   * **Sustained breakout -> trade with the trend**;
   * **No follow-through -> watch for counter-directional entry**;
   * If momentum clearly weakens, consider taking early profits or testing a counter-directional position.

---

## 3. Stop Loss Strategy: Choosing Between Wide and Narrow Stops

1. Wide stop loss

   * Suitable for trending markets, providing more error tolerance;
   * But must **exit decisively** when the structure becomes invalid.

2. Narrow stop loss

   * Precise positioning, suitable for small-scale trades after breakout confirmation;
   * Easily hit by noise, but the small stop means large potential returns.

> Different strategies require different stop structures. Do not mix them.

---

## 4. Trading Psychology and Loss Management

1. Accept losses

   * Losses are part of the trading process — they are **feedback that a structure has been invalidated**, not failure.

2. System refinement

   * Every failed trade is worth reviewing;
   * Analyze false breakouts and incorrect entries to improve **structural recognition ability**;
   * Reduce ineffective trades and improve overall system win rate.

---

## 5. "Always-In" Logic for Direction Judgment

1. Bull-bear judgment

   * There is always one side in the market that **can make money more easily** — trade that side;
   * If the structure clearly favors bulls, then "**Always-In Long**" — do not go short;
   * When the structure points down, "**Always-In Short**" — do not go long.

2. Opposing side judgment

   * Determine whether the opposing side can still profit;
   * Once the opposing side is invalidated, trading with the trend has the highest success rate.

---

## 6. Three-Wave Theory and Reversal Opportunities

1. Three-wave structure

   * First wave: breakout;
   * Second wave: retest correction;
   * Third wave: extreme momentum + bull/bear trap, **triggering a reversal**.

2. Reversal hints

   * **Multiple reversal attempts increase success rate**;
   * Typical pattern: **wedge structure**, often the top/bottom of a three-wave structure.
