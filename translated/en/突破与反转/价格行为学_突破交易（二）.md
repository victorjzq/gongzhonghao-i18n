# Price Action: Breakout Trading (Part 2)

Whenever a breakout is weak, or the subsequent follow-through is poor,
it will most likely evolve into a trading range.

If the pullback is deep,
this is not a breakout into a bull trend
but more likely a breakout leading to a trading range.

If the pullback is shallow,
you may see some kind of measured move up
based on the breakout height
or based on the height of the prior trading range.

A big rally followed by a big decline creates confusion,
and confusion usually leads to sideways consolidation.

Once you see the context is:
a failed bull breakout,
followed by a successful bear breakout,
plus two consecutive extremely large bear bars both closing at their lows,
you should judge the market as "Always In Short" —
meaning you believe the market will most likely continue to decline.

All trades require good stop loss management.
For most swing traders,
the best stop is at the obvious point at the top of the entire decline leg.

Another option
is to place the stop above the top of the bear breakout.

If this is the beginning of a bear trend,
regardless of what stop you use,
the tighter the stop, the smaller each loss,
but the more frequently the stop will be triggered;
the wider the stop, the less chance of being swept,
but once it is triggered, the loss will be very large.

The key is to use a "reasonable stop."
If you use an unreasonably tight stop,
you will be stopped out frequently;
if you use an extremely loose stop,
that is also unreasonable because you will occasionally lose a lot of money.

Where you place the stop has nothing to do with where you entered.
The stop depends on the market's structure before your entry.

Usually it is best to place the stop at the top of the most recent "major decline leg,"
because if you are betting on a swing decline,
the market must keep making "lower lows."
Once it breaks above a major "lower high,"
your short logic is negated, and you should exit immediately.

The simplest approach is:
see a strong bear bar close,
go short at market price, and use an appropriate stop.

"Sell-the-close" traders
go short whenever they see a strong bear bar,
selling all the way down.
They don't sell on every bar,
but they will choose at least one of them.

Some aggressive traders
will also add to their position once the trade is profitable.
For example, they sell at this bar's close,
see the decline continuing,
and may add on the next bar
or add again at the next breakout.

This is adding to a winning position,
not adding to a losing position.

The key point is: you must find opportunities to go short.
All of these bear bar closes
are reasonable entry points.
