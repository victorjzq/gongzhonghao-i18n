# Price Action: The Number of Breakout Attempts

The inertia principle: in a trend, most reversal attempts fail. A rough empirical ratio is: approximately 80% fail, 20% succeed.

How to capture that 20%?
Number the reversal attempts in the same direction and same structure sequentially (1st, 2nd...).
If you roughly understand the success rate as "one in five," then in a series of consecutive reversal attempts, the later attempt (such as the 5th) is more likely to be the one that finally succeeds.

Relatively, if there are multiple consecutive breakout attempts in the same direction, the 5th breakout has the lowest success probability — it may take the 6th or more before a truly effective breakout is more likely to occur.

A filtering rule:
Do not trade when a third consecutive signal in the same direction appears.
This rule applies both to reversal-plus-follow-through confirmation signals and to breakout signals.

Multiple consecutive same-direction attempts often mean you are already in the later stage of the move:
near the edge in a range, or near the tail in a trend.
Continuing to chase usually becomes "selling at lows, buying at highs," with both win rate and risk-reward deteriorating noticeably.

A bear micro channel is usually one of the best signals that the "channel phase is nearing its end."
In the late channel, there is usually obvious selling pressure above;
for the late channel segment, this at least provides a decent short-term trading opportunity.

"Breaking below the trend line" is often a common prerequisite for forming a relatively reliable reversal.

When the market undergoes a state switch, the first attempt to return to the old state usually fails.
Therefore, traders often choose to go short near the moving average, expecting the new state to continue at least for a while — perhaps one more push.

The role of the moving average is more about speeding up decisions and simplifying recognition.
It is a tool we overlay on the chart, not a language inherent to the chart itself.
The chart itself only contains bar sequences; the moving average is not information naturally contained in price action.

If you do not over-rely on the moving average, you are actually less likely to be blindsided by sudden market reversals.
Once you pay too much attention to the moving average, you unconsciously attribute "mystical properties" to it,
as if "since this is the moving average, it should do such and such."

But this does not mean the moving average has no value.
The moving average still has value because it is essentially a statistical calculation of price action.

When you set the lookback period for the moving average,
you are actually choosing your sample size.

Using a 20-period moving average as an example:
20 can be viewed as an empirical lower bound for "when the sample begins to have minimum statistical significance."
Therefore, a 20-bar moving average can usually give a reasonably good location estimate for recent prices.

But it is ultimately just an estimate.
That means the distribution of price relative to the moving average can be very diverse:
sometimes above, sometimes below, sometimes crossing back and forth,
and it will not produce some inevitable event just because "it reached the moving average."

Sometimes the sample distribution of price around the moving average is very compact,
like here: price mostly runs close to the moving average with minimal deviation above or below.

Sometimes this distribution is very noisy with large variation,
meaning higher standard deviation.

Higher standard deviation markets mean:
price deviates more from the moving average, is more unstable, and volatility is more violent.

In such conditions,
the moving average's location estimate for price becomes rougher and less reliable.
Traders need to expect larger back-and-forth swings
and less stable follow-through moves.
