# Price Action: Selling Climax

## 1. Concept Positioning

A trend bar is simultaneously a breakout and also a climax.
If it occurs early in a trend = breakout; if it occurs after the trend has already run for 20+ bars = a potential selling (or buying) climax.
The vast majority of climaxes consist of only one bar.

## 2. Common Rhythm After the Trigger

Typically, the market goes sideways for 3-5 bars, up to 10 bars, before deciding to continue or reverse.
All the gaps left during this sideways phase are often filled all at once by a large counter-directional bar — all these gaps are exhaustion gaps.

## 3. How to Confirm a "Selling/Buying Climax"

If a large counter-directional bar fills the entire string of gaps in one move, then the preceding rally/selloff is classified as an exhaustion-type buying/selling climax.

Two possibilities follow:
a. A quick continuation of 3-4 large counter-directional bars closing near their extremes -> the measured move target will very likely be reached;
b. Weak follow-through in the opposite direction -> the market enters a longer consolidation phase.

## 4. Two-Level Measured Move

First target: project the vertical distance from the most recent "lower high / higher low -> gap" horizontally.
Second target: if price passes through the first target, project the distance from the entire trend top (or bottom) -> gap midpoint horizontally.

After 3-4 consecutive large bars closing at their lows/highs appear, the probability of reaching the measured target increases significantly.

## 5. Continuation or Reversal? A Judgment Framework

The following combination suggests exhaustion rather than momentum initiation:

* The trend has already run for 20+ bars;
* The channel is steep or is a micro channel;
* The breakout is extremely strong but immediately lacks follow-through;
* A large counter-directional bar then appears and fills the gap.

Conversely, if a breakout occurs very early and has sustained follow-through, it mostly represents a genuine trend initiation.

## 6. Chaos = Signal of a Trading Range

Long strings of alternating large bull and bear bars (e.g., bulls produce 7 consecutive bars, then bears immediately produce 7 consecutive bars) indicate major chaos — the market will most likely enter a trading range.
During this phase, scalping by buying low and selling high is appropriate; do not rush to bet on a major reversal.

## 7. Options Hedging and "False Climaxes"

A series of selling climaxes is often not genuine market bears, but rather
institutions who sold puts being forced to sell spot/futures as a hedge.
If you see a strong bounce, suspect that "things are not that bearish" — wait until the climax fully concludes before judging direction.

## 8. Practical Tips

Conservative approach: wait for two or three consecutive large counter-directional bars all closing near their extremes, then enter with the trend at the third bar's close or the next bar's breakout.
The first reversal wave is usually small — mostly bear/bull flags or narrow consolidation; for a major reversal, a second wave with a measured leg is typically needed.
Price returning to the breakout point or gap edge is a critical level for adding/reducing positions and reassessing.
Remember the 20-bar rule: the later the breakout occurs, the more it looks like exhaustion; the earlier it occurs, the more it looks like the start of momentum.
