# Price Action: Reversal Trading (Part 1)

I. The Commonality Between Reversals and Breakouts

Nearly every reversal is accompanied by a breakout, however small.

Minor reversals tend to produce trading ranges; only major reversals can lead to a new trend in the opposite direction.

II. Bull Reversals in a Bear Trend

An initial upward reversal is usually just a bear flag or a trading range.

Criteria for a "Major Reversal":

It requires a structure of 10 or more bars (major trend reversal).

The success rate is about 40%; the remaining 60% of the time the market continues to oscillate or extends the bear trend.

For example:

Tight bear channel → minor reversal breakout → bear resumes → probability of the next reversal increases.

A slightly higher double bottom + bull signal bar → swing traders buy above the high, betting on a 40% major reversal, expecting at least two legs up.

III. Bear Reversals in a Bull Trend

In a bull environment, the default assumption for every decline is a "minor reversal": a bull flag or trading range, followed by a continuation higher.

If a sufficiently strong minor reversal occurs:

Price breaks below the bull channel, especially below the trend line, and persists for 10 or more bars.

Traders begin to doubt that the bull trend has ended and expect a trading range or a bear trend.

For bears to establish a new trend:

Price must break below the channel bottom and below the trend line.

A lower high + lower low must appear.

Wait for a short opportunity at the lower high.

IV. The Trade-Off of Reversal Entries

A reversal offers the earliest entry into a new trend: the risk is small (buying at the bottom in a bull reversal, selling at the top in a bear reversal), but the success rate is low.

After a trend unfolds, there are still multiple entry opportunities: H2 bull flags, tests of buying climax lows, three-push wedges, and more.

When you see the market has entered an "always-in long" state, it is never too late to enter.

V. The 80% Rule and Trading Range Breakouts

Attempts to break out of a trading range fail 80% of the time. Expect failure first, then look for confirmation signals.

VI. Cup and Handle Pattern

A reversal is called the "cup," and then a pullback from the reversal is called the "handle." Whenever a reversal occurs, there will eventually be a pullback, forming a "cup and handle pattern." The market drops sharply, then reverses strongly upward, followed by a pullback that tests the breakout level at the top of the trading range. This is what traders call the "cup and handle pattern": this area is the cup, the pullback is the handle — the reversal is the cup, and the pullback test is the handle. You can see it resembles the shape of a teacup: a cup with a small handle.

Two long strategies:

Enter directly at the cup reversal.

Enter at the handle pullback after the breakout (buy the handle).

VII. Overview of Swing Opportunities

Bear-to-bull, bull-to-bear, or trading range breakouts can all capture swings.

The key is judging whether the reversal is "minor" or "major," combined with breakout location and bar count for decision-making.

Minor reversal = trading range; major reversal = structure of 10 or more bars.

The success rate of a major reversal is 40%; 60% of the time, the market remains in a trading range or the original trend.

Cup and handle: first the reversal forms the cup, then the pullback forms the handle — both locations offer long entries.
