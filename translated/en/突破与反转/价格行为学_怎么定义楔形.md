# Price Action: How to Define a Wedge

A strong decline is actually just price being pulled down by a vacuum to test a support level.
After a very strong decline, many traders will wait for a micro double bottom to appear before buying.
A micro double bottom represents a test of the bear trend bottom, and bears who sold at this close will buy back their short positions here.

The success probability of most patterns is between 40% and 60%, which means their failure probability is also 40% to 60%.
So no matter how perfect a pattern looks, always be prepared for it to fail, and constantly think about whether you can trade in the opposite direction if the original pattern fails.

How do you define a wedge?

The definition of a wedge is very broad.
Usually in a market with some reversal tendency, the market will try to do something twice.
If the second attempt is still strong, there will often be a third.
That third attempt then forms a wedge.

It can be a double bottom followed by another break below forming a reversal — in my view this is a wedge: three attempts to decline followed by a reversal.
The traditional definition says a wedge is a converging triangle where the three waves decrease in strength, with a downward-sloping wedge bottom and an upward-sloping wedge top.
Some people call all triangles wedges, and some people call all wedges triangles.

Usually if the pattern is sideways, we call it a triangle; if it is sloped, we call it a wedge.
Structurally, triangles and wedges are actually the same: five legs, with three in one direction and two in the other.
But a wedge can be divergent — it does not have to converge.

If you limit yourself to perfect patterns, you will never trade — because there are no perfect patterns.
You must be flexible and understand what the market is trying to do.
As long as the market tries to do something three times and fails — for example, three failed declines forming a reversal — I treat it as a wedge, even if it does not look like the traditional wedge shape.

I am not overly attached to every pattern matching the textbook definition.
What I care more about is: has the market tried the same direction three consecutive times without success?
If so, then I consider whether it is forming a bottom or a top, and whether a reversal may follow.
