# Price Action: Wedge

Today let's discuss a single pattern: the Wedge.

A wedge is a classic end-of-trend structure.
It is not for prediction, but for confirming momentum exhaustion.

Used well, it can lock in reversal positions in advance with low risk and high reward.
Used poorly, it is just another "failed breakout trap."

---

## The Wedge Is a Precursor to Reversal, Not the Reversal Itself

Wedges generally appear at the end of a trend as a structure of exhausted momentum.

Typical characteristics:

* The trend is still moving, but **each push is weaker than the previous one**
* Highs and lows are increasingly narrowing
* Most critically — **the signal truly lights up when the third push fails**

Many people see two legs up and rush to guess the top and go short,
but reversals are confirmed by "the third failure," not by guessing.

---

## What Makes a Reliable Wedge?

A **standard wedge** looks like a slanted, converging triangle:

* Both upper and lower boundaries have an angle of inclination
* Pushes gradually weaken
* Bars within the pattern become increasingly tangled
* Only when the third push up fails to break through does it potentially become a reversal point

Sometimes it does not converge but moves in a back-and-forth fashion — that works too.
As long as one condition is met: **momentum is exhausting, and the market starts to wait and see.**

---

## True Reversal Requires Two Signals

**First, momentum exhaustion:**

* The third push up fails
* Long upper tails, small bodies, doji bars appear
* Several consecutive attempts fail to break the prior high
* The market is no longer willing to chase longs

**Second, a valid breakout:**

* A strong bear bar appears
* A high-volume break, or an engulfing bar
* Breaking below the lower boundary of the wedge confirms the reversal

Haven't seen these two signals? **Don't move.**

---

## How to Execute the Trade?

Using a **rising wedge for shorts** as an example:

* **First wait for the third push to fail**
* **Then wait for a clear reversal bar** (Pin Bar, large bear bar)
* **Place a sell stop below the signal bar**
* **Stop loss above the wedge upper rail + 1-2 ticks**
* **Set two targets:**
  * First target at the wedge's starting point
  * Second target at the structural support below

---

## Key Rules for Trading Wedges:

* **Wedge does not equal reversal** — until confirmed, everything is a false move
* **Don't enter early** — orders that rush in mostly contribute liquidity as cannon fodder
* **Give your stop loss a bit more room** — don't get "stop-hunted" by the last wave
* **The larger the timeframe, the more reliable the pattern** — recommend observing on daily/hourly charts

---

Wedge structures are useful, but cannot be forced.

Don't fantasize about catching the top on the first try. The key is:

* Wait for the three-leg structure to complete
* Wait for momentum to break
* Wait for a valid breakout

When that moment arrives, the market naturally gives you the signal.

What you need to do is not "predict it will fall,"
but "confirm it has fallen, then follow the move."

**Understanding wedges means you truly understand the rhythm of reversals.**
