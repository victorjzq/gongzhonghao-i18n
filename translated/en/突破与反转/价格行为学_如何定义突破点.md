# Price Action: How to Define Breakout Points

Any location where the market is breaking out can be a breakout point. Any of these bars has support or resistance levels. The upper half of a bar is resistance — that is the bar's sell zone — while the lower half is support. So if the market produces a new bar that crosses one of these two boundaries — in this case, it moves up — then the breakout point can be the close, the high, the open, or a more micro-level price you observe. Different perspectives will change your definition of what the true breakout point is. The key is to be clear about what you are using it for.

So it really depends on what you are looking for. The breakout point can also be the bar's open or close; essentially it is any support or resistance level. Every time you see a breakout, it has a breakout point. The significance of this breakout point depends on how large the breakout is, the number of bars you are observing, and the bar structure to the left.

Many times, when you get a signal bar, you may not successfully enter the trade — I think every trader has experienced this. One approach is to switch to a lower timeframe, use the lower timeframe breakout point to enter, and then manage the trade based on the higher timeframe.

The best approach is to scale in at lower prices. A common situation is that once you enter, the market pulls back. So keep an open mind. You can buy on a strong bar while reserving the option to buy once or twice more at lower prices. If the market has already produced five or six consecutive bars, then don't chase — wait for a pullback, because that is usually the limit of most micro channels.

A micro gap can also be part of a breakout gap. A breakout gap is the distance between the bar's close and the price we are breaking through — that is the breakout gap. A micro gap is a gap within the bar itself. Breakout gaps are usually structural, so a breakout can carry different gap characteristics. Within the structure of a breakout, micro gaps may or may not appear, but the breakout gap still exists.

A very strong AB leg usually produces a tradable BC leg. But if you want a larger-scale move symmetrical to AB, you usually need to wait for bears to try two times, sometimes even three. This forms a double bottom flag or wedge flag.

There is a two-test principle in the market. The market usually tries the same thing at least twice, sometimes three or even four times. If both tests fail, then the market will turn to test the other side of the range.

If the market is in a trading range, especially when you get a second strong reversal signal, it is usually better to bet that the market will move at least one more leg rather than rashly trading against the trend. Wedge patterns that run counter to the trend direction usually have a lower probability of success.
