# Price Action: Wedge (Part 1)

## 1. Concept
A wedge refers to any structure with three pushes, regardless of whether the lines converge. It often incorporates small trading ranges and is essentially a pullback within a trend. Most bull channels on higher timeframes are actually bear flags, with approximately 75% probability of a bear breakout leading into a trading range.

## 2. Nesting Characteristics of Wedges
In actual price action, it is common to see small wedges nested within larger wedges. When multiple timeframes exert pressure at the same area, the reversal probability of a nested wedge is higher than that of a single wedge.

## 3. Characteristics of Wedge Tops
1. Three successive pushes higher all fail to continue
2. Internally it is a small pullback bull trend, accompanied by buy-the-close action
3. Buying is FOMO chasing; once two or three bear bars appear, all recent chasers become trapped, triggering panic selling

## 4. Key Points for Identifying Wedge Bottoms
1. The magnitude of the three downward waves decreases progressively
2. Obvious overlap appears between the decline legs
3. The bounce after the second decline is higher than the low of the first decline
4. After appearing, the minimum reversal target is ten bars with two legs up

## 5. Market "Fair Price" and Testing Logic
The market constantly tests "how far is too far." A wedge bottom is the point of rapid reversal after downside probing to a level deemed too low. When bears covering and bulls bottom-fishing overlap, a two-leg upward structure typically forms.

## 6. Selling Climax and Pain Trades
Consecutive sell-the-close bars constitute a selling climax. The low-probability continued decline repeatedly frustrates bottom-fishing bulls, creating crowded positions. When momentum is exhausted, bears cover en masse and bulls counterattack, easily producing a V-shaped reversal.

## 7. Trading Approach
1. When seeing a wedge top, first treat it as a bear flag and prepare for a bear breakout. If the breakout fails and quickly reverses, immediately watch for trend termination and look for a two-leg reversal.
2. When seeing a wedge bottom, after confirming overlap and momentum decay, you can go long above the strong bull bar at the end of the third push, with stop loss at that bar's low, targeting at least TBTL (Ten Bars, Two Legs).
3. If a moving average gap or selling climax coincides with a wedge bottom, the reversal probability increases further.

## Summary
A wedge is a three-push structure with slope. It often appears at the end of a trend accompanied by convergence, overlap, and momentum exhaustion. When these signals appear simultaneously, be alert for a final flag reversal. Correctly identifying and trading wedges is a core step in building a with-trend reversal system.
