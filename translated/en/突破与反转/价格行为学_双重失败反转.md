# Price Action: Double Failed Reversal

A double failed reversal is a pattern stronger than a single failure, because it combines two consecutive failed breakouts, which can significantly undermine the confidence of traders in the original direction and attract more counter-directional participation.

At key levels (the top or bottom of a trading range, or the end of a trend), the first breakout attempt fails, the price returns to the original range or trend interior, and then attempts another breakout in the same direction. The second breakout also fails and quickly moves in the opposite direction.

Why is the momentum of double failure stronger? The first failure causes some traders to doubt the trend's continuation, but some still choose to wait for a second opportunity. When the second attempt also fails, these traders completely lose confidence and exit with stop losses. At the same time, counter-trend traders enter aggressively, creating significant opposing momentum.

Common double failed reversal scenarios include: At the top of a range, when price breaks above the range high twice and is quickly pulled back both times, after the second failure the price quickly drops toward the range bottom; at the bottom of a range, when price breaks below the range low twice and is quickly pulled back both times, after the second failure the price quickly bounces toward the range top; at the end of a trend, in a bull trend when two consecutive new highs fail, or in a bear trend when two consecutive new lows fail.

Regarding trading strategy, first identify the first failure. When a failed breakout pattern appears and price quickly returns to the range or trend interior, it indicates the first attempt has failed. Then wait for the second breakout. If the second breakout also fails, that is the entry signal. The entry position is at the trigger point of the reversal after the second failure, with the stop loss placed beyond the high or low of the second failed breakout.

For target and position sizing, the first target of a double failed reversal is typically the other end of the range, or the first major support or resistance level in the opposite direction of the trend. When momentum is particularly strong, it may develop into a complete trend reversal.

However, note that although the double failure pattern is strong, it must have clear context support, such as the end of a trend or at an important range edge. If the context does not support a reversal, the continuation of the double failure will be greatly reduced. It is best to combine with higher timeframe resistance or support levels for confirmation.

The double failed reversal is an opportunity combining pattern analysis and market psychology, capable of producing significant volatility in a short time. But its success rate depends on context — if the context is wrong, even if the pattern appears, you should not enter rashly.
