# Price Action: Buy and Sell Zones in Breakouts

A reversal is essentially caused by price effectively breaking through the key structure of the original trend; without a structural breakout, there is only a pullback, which does not constitute a reversal.

When a reversal first occurs, the market usually transitions from "one-directional trend" back to a "bull-bear repricing" phase:

Old trend traders are stopping out / exiting

New direction traders are entering / adding positions

An effective reversal often pushes the market from a trending state back to a rebalancing state; during the rebalancing phase, price is more likely to produce second tests and counter-directional swings, so the first reversal is often followed by a "failed reversal / re-reversal" attempt. Such reversals are usually low probability because the market already expects a second push.

You can observe many different charts with this perspective and will find that simply based on "the first reversal attempt after a breakout is a low-probability event," you can find many trading opportunities.

After a breakout occurs, the market often contains a structural expectation of "at least two legs of continuation." Therefore, the first reversal attempt after a breakout is mostly just a pullback or failed signal. Unless extremely strong counter-directional follow-through appears, its success probability is usually lower than with-trend continuation or second test patterns.

Breakout: price crosses the boundary of an existing equilibrium / range / trend structure (prior high / prior low, trend line, trading range upper / lower boundary, key moving average zone, etc.), and is usually accompanied by follow-through.

The core concept of breakout trading is "breaking through what?"

The essence of "breaking through what" is locating the stop-loss concentration zone / defensive boundary of bulls and bears: once crossed, which side will be forced to stop out, which side will gain with-trend follow-through. This is equivalent to asking "can the breakout receive follow-through and continue."

When we divide a bar into zones, for a bull bar, we define the upper half as the sell zone and the lower half as the buy zone.
But when this bar receives subsequent follow-through, we then consider there to be more buying below it.

For example, first there is a bull bar, and the next bar breaks above its high.
We know the upper half of this bar is the sell zone and the lower half is the buy zone.
When the market breaks above this bar's high, it enters its sell zone and exceeds it by a certain distance.
So when this bar's high breaks above the previous bar's high, what is essentially happening is a move that "should not have occurred."
In this case, when the second bar closes, there will be more buying below its low.

Whether the previous bar is a bull bar or a bear bar, if the next bar breaks below its low,
it means the market has entered the previous bar's buy zone, but buying failed to hold that area and price continued to decline.
At this point, there will be more selling above the second bar's high.

Theoretically, since the second bar broke above the first bar's high,
there should be buying throughout the entire bar range,
because the second bar's price action shows that buyers were buying even in the sell zone.
Buyers bought the sell zone, so now there is buying throughout the entire bar range.

The breakout's follow-through indicates this area is now entirely in bull territory.
The follow-through bar has its own buy zone,
and what was previously considered its sell zone (the upper half)
now also becomes a buy zone based on the next bar's price action.

In other words, the buy zone range has been further extended downward.

This is also the principle behind buying within a breakout gap.

Similarly,

price broke below the previous bar's buy zone,
and now the entire area has become bear territory.

Place your stop loss beyond the "critical boundary where the breakout is established": once price reverses back through that boundary, it means the core premise of the breakout (follow-through / structural switch) has been disproven, and you should exit. Conversely, as long as price remains outside the boundary, with-trend capital is usually defending at that point, thereby reducing the probability of your stop being triggered.
