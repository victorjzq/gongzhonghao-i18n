# Price Action: How to Understand Reversals (Part 1)

Reversals are actually a form of breakout. Without a breakout, a reversal cannot occur. This is because when market behavior changes, the force causing that change is a breakout.

Every change in market behavior is driven by a breakout.
Likewise, reversals must be accompanied by breakouts.

When the market is in an uptrend, price continues to rise.
If the trend reverses and begins to decline, that decline is a bear breakout.
Therefore, a bear reversal is actually the manifestation of a bear breakout.

Most counter-trend breakouts actually fail.
But the mechanism is the same.
The mechanism is that a counter-trend breakout receives sustained follow-through.
Once it gets follow-through, it forms its own trend and the direction changes.

But it also starts with a breakout.
If a trend transitions into a trading range, a breakout is also needed to change direction.
From trend to trading range and back to trend, all require breakouts.
So these are all transitions in behavioral patterns, all requiring breakouts to initiate.

How does a trend end?

The conditions are either a complete price structure undergoing correction, or a test of a certain level — such as a resistance test — leading to a reversal.
Or a pullback that fills the prior breakout gap.
This situation often leads to the market transitioning into a trading range or reversing.

Sometimes these types appear simultaneously. For example, when the market reverses, the prior breakout gap has not been filled, and it is testing a certain price — a test of resistance — and the reversal triggered by this resistance does not form a trading range.
The price simply continues to decline, accompanied by all the characteristics mentioned above.

So these market characteristics cluster together during the same period.
But this process usually follows a fixed sequence.
Usually the first step is for the gap to be filled.
After the gap is filled, the market will bounce.
Then it will test upward again.

The so-called failed breakout — unable to close effectively.

The most direct result is at least the formation of a trading range.
Sometimes there will be a strong breakout that surges forward without looking back.
But at minimum, a trading range will form.

Reversal breakouts are relatively rare.
For a reversal pattern to truly take effect, it needs to dominate the chart.
For a reversal to succeed, there must be no resumption of the original trend.
Therefore, one of the most critical factors in all reversals is the transfer of market dominance from the original direction to the new direction.

This transfer of dominance marks the beginning of a new trend.
This transfer usually does not succeed on the first attempt.
This is why I say a trading range will form at minimum.

Because when the market reverses within a trend, the original trend often resumes.
Therefore, trend resumption usually causes the market to test the prior high again.

During this period, the market exhibits two-sided trading characteristics, forming a trading range.
For example, bulls might try two to three times, but if they cannot produce a new breakout, they cannot push the market higher.
Or if the breakout immediately fails, then the probability of a market reversal is even higher.

This is the core concept of major trend reversal patterns.
