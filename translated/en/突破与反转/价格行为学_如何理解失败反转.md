# Price Action: How to Understand Failed Reversals

In trading, almost every failed with-trend trade implies an opportunity for the other side. Professional traders deliberately seek these situations because they often lead to fast, clear moves.

For example, in a bull trend, if a bull signal bar is triggered and price quickly reverses and breaks below the signal bar's low, it means buyers are forced to stop out. These stop-loss orders convert into sell orders, driving price further down — this is the bears' entry opportunity. Similarly, in a bear trend, if a bear signal is triggered and its high is quickly breached, bears are forced to cover, creating buying pressure that drives price further up, benefiting the bulls.

The key is to quickly identify failed signals and enter at the moment the failure is confirmed. The advantages of this type of entry are:
Clear momentum, because the other side's stop losses are driving the move;
Higher win rate, because the context supports a failed reversal;
Often able to capture considerable profits in a short time.

To judge whether a signal has failed, you must combine context and price action. Simply breaking below a bull signal bar's low does not necessarily mean a trend reversal. If the context remains strongly bullish, this may just be a deeper pullback, after which price will resume its advance. Therefore, when considering a counter-directional trade, you need to confirm:
Whether the context supports a reversal — for example, end of trend, strong resistance area, second failed breakout, etc.;
Whether the force of the failure is clear — such as a fast, large break through the signal bar in the opposite direction;
Whether market sentiment has shifted — for example, several consecutive strong bars closing in the opposite direction.

A common high-probability scenario is the double failed reversal. This typically occurs at the top or bottom of a range:
Price's first breakout fails and returns to the range;
Price's second breakout in the same direction also fails;
The double failure causes a large number of traders to lose confidence, driving price rapidly in the opposite direction.

At the end of a trend, this double failed reversal is especially powerful because it combines both trend exhaustion and market sentiment collapse.

There is another key to utilizing failed reversals — timing. If you enter too early, you may be stopped out by brief fluctuations; if too late, you may miss the best momentum phase. Professional traders enter on the bar where the failure is confirmed or the next bar, placing their stop loss beyond a reasonable structural level — such as the other side of the breakout point. This protects against being stopped out by false moves while allowing first entry when momentum kicks in.

In failed reversal operations, position management is equally important. Because the trigger for this type of trade is often accompanied by strong momentum, short-term volatility can be significant. Therefore, appropriately reducing initial position size allows you to maintain controlled risk while having room to absorb necessary fluctuations. Some traders use a scaling-in approach for failed reversals: enter half the position at the moment the failure is confirmed; if the next few bars continue the reversal momentum, add the remaining position. This increases exposure after momentum is truly confirmed while avoiding being fully trapped in a false reversal.

Regarding profit-taking strategy, the first target of a failed reversal is usually a return to the other end of the range, or a test of the previous major structure (such as support or resistance). Once price reaches these key levels, the market may show new hesitation or bounce, so taking partial or full profits at these levels is an effective way to protect gains.

Another point to note is that failed reversals are not always trending opportunities. Sometimes they are just back-and-forth swings within a range, especially in high-volatility choppy markets where the persistence of these reversals is very poor. In such cases, it is best to simply close when the target is quickly reached rather than hoping it evolves into a new trend.

Professional traders typically ask themselves three questions when facing failed reversals:
Is the context of the failure sufficient to support a trend reversal?
Is the momentum clear enough to drive price to the next major target?
Does the structure present a larger trading opportunity — for example, is this reversal the starting point of a higher timeframe trend?

If two or more of these three questions have negative answers, then this failed reversal is not worth a large position.

Overall, failed reversals are excellent short-term opportunities provided by the market, but they require quick identification, decisive execution, and strict risk management. When your judgment is correct, they can produce considerable profits in a very short time; but when your judgment is wrong, you must stop out quickly and never hold on with a hopeful mentality.
