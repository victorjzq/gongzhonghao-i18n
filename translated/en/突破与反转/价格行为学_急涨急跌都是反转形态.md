# Price Action: Sharp Rallies and Sharp Declines Are Both Reversal Patterns

When multiple bars line up in the same direction, a micro channel forms. A micro channel is actually a simple price push. We know that these simple pushes compose more complex price pattern structures, and all charts are made up of these basic units. The most basic pattern is a three-leg move.

One-directional sharp rallies and sharp declines are both reversal patterns. A common mistake beginners make is seeing strong surge bars and assuming the market will keep going up (or down) non-stop, but in reality every surge is a reversal signal. After a one-directional move, a reversal is highly probable. The reversal will usually fail and evolve into a pullback, because a larger-scale structure is being built, but all one-directional moves are potential reversal signals.

Whenever a trading range appears, there are only two possibilities: either it is a trend reversal or it is a trend continuation pattern. A trading range cannot last forever — there is no other option. Everything has cycles and life spans. If the current move fails to form an equal-sized bear second push — using the two-leg ABCD pattern to calculate — if bear forces launched from a certain position, they should form an equal-magnitude second decline. So bears hope to replicate a similar move, but after two or three failed attempts, they will give up trying at this price level. Instead, they will look for shorting opportunities at higher prices. Meanwhile, both bulls and bears re-enter, take control, and push the market to continue rising.

Pay attention to five-push patterns rather than three-push patterns. Frequently ask yourself: will this move have three pushes or five? When a narrow channel appears, it usually produces at least five pushes, and sometimes exactly five. In trending markets, successful reversals often appear around the fifth or sixth reversal bar.
