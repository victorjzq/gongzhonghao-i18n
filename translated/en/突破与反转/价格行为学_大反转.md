# Price Action: Major Reversals

A major reversal must simultaneously satisfy three conditions: the right context, a strong minor reversal (which must meet several requirements including time), and that strong minor reversal must "break a key level." Using a bull scenario as an example, this means breaking above the "major swing point in the bear trend."

In a bear trend, even when a strong reversal appears, it is initially still a minor reversal. Suppose after a pullback, the price breaks above the previous "major swing point." This swing point is defined as "major" because it corresponds to a move that made a new low. Any swing point that creates a new low is a major swing point.

Additionally, it must align with the higher timeframe price action; the test of the decline must be "part of a trading range move." If it is a decline within a trend, price may not hold at a higher low.

When the market must traverse a significant distance to test a price level and produces a strong trend along the way, it is very likely that the market has transitioned into a trading range rather than maintaining the prior bear trend.

Regarding major reversals, the classic terminology describes it very accurately: in a bear trend, price stops making new lows, transitions to sideways, and eventually breaks out to the upside. Edwards and Magee called this an "accumulation bottom," which reveals its essence:

Bears stop selling or selling pressure is insufficient to suppress buying, while bulls continue to buy. This stems from the operational logic of fund managers: accumulating positions through gradual buying at lows or buying pullbacks, in preparation for long-term holding.

The construction of an "accumulation bottom" requires a large number of bars to complete the gradual position building. After a high-momentum decline, there must be a sufficient time span with enough bars to prove that bears have lost control and bulls have taken charge.
