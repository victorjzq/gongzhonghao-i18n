# Price Action: How to Understand Breakouts

If the follow-through bar moves in the same direction as the breakout bar, the breakout is still in progress. If the follow-through bar is a pullback bar, the breakout has failed.

If the breakout is still in progress, it will form a breakout gap. A breakout gap is the distance from the breakout point to the current price — that is, the most recent price during or after the breakout. As the breakout continues to advance, a reversal bar will eventually appear, triggering a pullback and causing the market to turn. At this point, the market retests the breakout point — this process is called a breakout test.

The test shrinks the breakout gap, sometimes even filling it completely. Whether the gap is filled or preserved, it is still a breakout test. If the breakout test does not close the gap, traders immediately recognize the gap as a measuring tool and use it to project a target for the second leg. The second leg is typically a channel move with diminishing momentum, and it usually reaches exactly the target calculated from the gap. At this point, the gap is officially named a measured gap.

If the gap is completely filled, traders consider the breakout failed and the gap has become an exhaustion gap. An exhaustion gap is often the prelude to a reversal, and traders treat this pullback as a new breakout in the opposite direction. They begin watching the gap left by the new breakout. This retest has already filled the old gap and may evolve into a breakout gap in the reverse direction. Then the market continues to test this new gap, and traders use it to evaluate whether the reversal is more likely to produce a second leg. Therefore, regardless of the situation, whenever a breakout occurs, watch the breakout gap and its retest — the next step is a second push in the breakout direction.

After the breakout gap is filled, the market may be reversing — not only filling the last breakout gap but often filling body gaps as well, and this is the first signal for reversal traders.

When the market pulls back and overlaps with the breakout point, the breakout gap is quickly filled and the market enters a trading range. Because within a trading range, when the breakout gap is filled, the energy that drove the move needs to be digested over a period, usually over several bars or an entire trading range. This is the process by which trading ranges form. The market re-enters breakout mode or disorderly chop within the trading range until it eventually breaks down with sustained follow-through.

Another type is called a micro gap. A micro gap refers to a gap between the highest point and the lowest point of two non-adjacent bars. In plain terms, it is the gap between the first and third bars of a three-bar sequence. For example, in a bull breakout, the gap between the first and third bars forms a bull micro gap. Micro gaps are divided into strong and weak categories. A strong micro gap requires that both bars forming the gap be strong trend bars. If the gap is weak, it means one of the bars is not strong — it may even be a counter-directional bar — which reduces the gap's reliability.

Any trend, especially a strong trend, often produces micro gaps. When the market is preparing to enter a trading range phase, micro gaps are filled first, then bar bodies begin to overlap, followed by a reversal and the start of a new opposing push. This phenomenon repeats in virtually every reversal. Before every reversal, the market first goes through the formation of a trading range and then completes the reversal. Therefore, by continuously watching micro gaps, you can identify areas where the market is prone to reversing in advance.

Sometimes a breakout pullback coincides with the breakout point, but from the breakout point to the breakout test, the bar bodies do not overlap — the gap between bodies is called a body gap. For a bullish body gap, you must ensure that the highest bear bar's open in the breakout pattern does not touch the pullback's bear bar close. Once the bodies of these two bars are separated, a valid bullish body gap is formed. The definition for a bearish body gap is exactly the opposite — you must ensure that the lowest bull bar's open in the breakout does not touch the highest bull bar's close during the gap retest.

In a strong breakout, price may retest the gap, but as long as the bodies do not overlap, the move can still continue in the breakout direction. For example, after a certain breakout, the retest bar just touches the bottom of the gap without penetrating deeper — only barely touching it — this still counts as a valid gap, and the move may still push further.
