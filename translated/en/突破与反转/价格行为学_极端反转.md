# Price Action: Extreme Reversals

## Extreme Reversals

A so-called "extreme reversal" is essentially a failed breakout.
Whenever you see a breakout that does not look particularly strong,
this usually means the market will quickly enter a trading range.
Every breakout has the possibility of succeeding or failing.
If it succeeds, it typically leads to a measured move up,
and the breakout bar becomes a measuring gap.
If it fails, the market usually reverses and drops below the starting point of the breakout bar,
at which point that bar constitutes an exhaustion gap.

One thing to observe:
In a bear trend, can the bulls still make money?
If so, this usually means the bear trend is weakening
and the market will very likely transition to a trading range soon.

For example, a "stairs pattern,"
where each pullback overlaps the previous breakout point,
usually means this channel is about to evolve into a trading range.

The key to extreme move reversals is:
We have a strong trend,
then the market tries to push price again
but fails and quickly reverses.
This reversal usually occurs after the trend has gone very far,
when the market becomes overextended.
Traders see price continuing to rise or fall,
become overly excited, and enter in large numbers.
These traders who buy at tops or sell at bottoms
quickly find themselves in trouble
because the market produces violent counter-directional movement,
forcing them to stop out or trade in the opposite direction,
further propelling the new counter-trend direction.
You can see that in extreme move reversals,
sometimes the trend completely reverses,
while sometimes it only transitions into a trading range.
You need to carefully analyze the price action, bar patterns, pullback structure,
and whether both bulls and bears can profit in the current move,
to determine whether it is an extreme move reversal.

When you see a big breakout late in the trend,
such as a strong bear breakout in a bear trend
or a strong bull breakout in a bull trend,
you should consider:
whether to close your with-trend positions
and even consider attempting a counter-trend trade.

Sometimes you will hear people mention so-called V-bottoms or V-tops,
but these are extremely rare.
They occasionally appear during highly unexpected news events,
but these are extreme exceptions.
Almost every time you think a V-top or V-bottom has appeared,
it is actually some other pattern,
and the market had already given clues before the reversal occurred.
True V-tops or V-bottoms reverse suddenly without any warning.

As long as you see any form of extreme move —
meaning an "unsustainable" move —
there is a 70% chance you will see
"10 bars of sideways + two upward legs" as an adjustment.
This is the minimum target after every extreme decline.
The next question is:
After experiencing a major bear breakout,
what is the probability that the market continues to plunge further into a stronger bear trend?
Only 30%, or possibly even lower.

I think one thing to remember is that
even if there are no consecutive enormous bear trend bars in this decline,
and it does not look particularly strong on this timeframe,
on the higher timeframe chart it is still very strong.
So bulls should not buy at this point —
buying will not be profitable.
But bears will buy — to take profits.
And once the move evolves into a trading range,
bulls will start considering entering.

## The Trading Range

When you see the market displaying obvious sideways characteristics, you cannot treat it as a trend anymore.
In a trading range, there will not be many large bull or bear bars.
What you see is oscillation up and down, repeated fluctuations,
with most bars having both upper and lower tails,
indicating that both buyers and sellers are entering and exiting the market
but neither side can consistently dominate.
In other words,
every time price approaches the high, bears enter and sell;
every time price approaches the low, bulls enter and buy.

In this structure,
what you need to watch is: do not chase momentum.
Instead, sell at highs and buy at lows.
The traders most likely to lose money in this environment
are those who chase longs when they see a rally and chase shorts when they see a decline.
If you see a large bull bar, buy, and then the market reverses down;
or you see a large bear bar, sell, and then the market bounces.
This is the classic trading range trap.

The correct approach is to look for shorting opportunities at the top of the trading range
and longing opportunities at the bottom of the trading range.
This is "sell high, buy low."
Of course, if you are good at breakout trading,
you can also wait until the market exits the trading range,
breaking in one direction before following through.
At that point, make sure it is a strong breakout
accompanied by good follow-through.

The edges of the trading range are very important structures.
Many traders place entry and stop orders at the edges of the trading range.
Therefore, when price touches the edge of the trading range,
the market often experiences violent fluctuations.
The trading range is an area the market uses to balance supply and demand.
Price consolidates here, waiting for new driving forces to appear.
It can also be understood as: the market is building strength for the next trend.
So you cannot ignore the existence of trading ranges —
they often foreshadow that the next major trend is about to begin.

## Small Extreme Reversals

So-called small reversals typically have small magnitude, insufficient to cause a major trend reversal,
such as from bull trend to bear trend, or from bear trend to bull trend.
More likely, they trigger a trading range or a pullback.

Remember that market cycles are important.
A strong trend directly reversing into an opposite strong trend is very rare.
Usually it must first transition through an intermediate state, such as a trading range.
Therefore, if you see a strong trend attempting to reverse,
that reversal is very likely minor,
resulting either in a bull flag within a bull trend
or a trading range within a bear trend.
