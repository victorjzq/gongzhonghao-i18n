# Price Action: Limit Orders on Breakouts

First see the breakout and hope to see a reason that "the bears will fail."
This reason usually manifests as a weak sell signal bar.

You know there is support below: those bars below constitute a support area.
This trade will be psychologically easier to execute.

But this confirmation-based entry also has drawbacks:
sometimes the market rallies directly, and by the time you react it is too late;
the market simply does not pull back below that bar.
The more "greedy" your limit order — the further away and the more discount you want — the more likely you miss it.

Don't blame yourself if you don't get filled.
This naturally happens: about 20% of the time, your limit order simply will not get filled.

So you must have more than one method prepared for following the trend.

What if the order doesn't get filled?
Two paths:
Either give up on this trade and find the next one;
or accept that you need to chase more actively.

Wait for a 50% pullback.

In micro channels, the market often does not give obvious pullbacks,
so you can define "discount entry" as:
entering near the midpoint of a momentum bar.

The reason is:
the market frequently retests and attempts to fill the gap created by the breakout.

Put another way:
if you see a strong move
where the lows of momentum bars have never been tested with a downside probe,
this is a good thing.

You want to follow along, but you don't want to chase at the close —
you want a little discount.

Frankly, in strong momentum conditions, this small discount usually does not matter much for the final outcome,
because as long as momentum exists, price will most likely continue higher.

But it matters a lot for the trader's psychology:
it makes you feel you are "buying systematically"
rather than chasing emotionally;
and it makes it easier to get into profit more quickly.

After all, nobody wants to get filled and have price immediately move against them.

One approach is to look back at key levels from two or three bars ago.
Its high was later retested by the market,
so the reasonable expectation is:
the market first needs to break above this high,
then will come back to test these highs.

You can place limit orders at these "post-breakout retest" high areas.

But there is a timing question here:
do you place the order now,
or wait until the next bar develops before placing it?

After seeing the breakout bar appear, plan to place the order.
Wait for the next bar to open,
place the limit order at the level where you expect the market to "retest,"
and wait for the pullback to fill you.

But whether you can do this
depends on the bar size.

If it is a very large bar
that clearly broke above the previous bar's high by a lot,
then usually you can place the limit order directly near the previous bar's close.

Conversely,
if bars are very tight with a small range,
placing the order too early won't gain you much advantage.

In this case, the more reasonable reference point is often the close,
or simply don't rush to place the order.
