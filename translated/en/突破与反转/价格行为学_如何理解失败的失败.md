# Price Action: How to Understand Failed Failures

A simple method to identify the failed failure pattern is when a test fails, and the counter-test also fails. Suppose a trading range has already formed. When price breaks above the upper boundary of the range, this should become support — theoretically this is where support lies. When price pulls back to test this support, instead of bouncing to form a second push, it breaks directly below. This means more counter-directional positions are trapped, and it is actually stronger than the original move. At this point, we can conclude the support has failed because there was a bearish breakout below the support. Correct. But then the bear attack also fails, and now price surges back up strongly, breaking above that line again. The support had already failed and the line had turned into resistance, so now resistance has also failed — meaning one failure, immediately followed by another failure. This situation is very likely a failed failure pattern, and if there is a downside retest, it will trigger an even stronger bounce. Its effect is similar to a low-probability event, because more traders are on the wrong side. When a low-probability event occurs, the directional probability surges, the market moves unexpectedly, and a failed failure is even rarer, so the market volatility it produces is even more intense.

If you make a wrong one-sided judgment, it is best to stop and observe first. The market is in a sideways consolidation phase, so be prepared for continued choppiness — every seemingly strong signal often implies a counter-directional opportunity. In trending markets, failure patterns actually rarely appear. When the market forms a strong one-directional trend confirmed by multiple consecutive trend bars, the probability of a failure pattern occurring is extremely low.

Start with breakout trading systems because this is the foundation of all trading concepts. Beginners only need to focus on identifying the second push — this is the highest win-rate trading opportunity and appears very frequently. Reversals are essentially a form of breakout pattern. When the first push is strong, you can indeed consider reversal trading, and most reversal moves will also produce a second push.

The context of the chart is virtually endless. When you fall into the trap of over-analysis, tell yourself: "I'm over-analyzing because I can't see the big picture," and then immediately stop and look at the chart from a macro perspective. The overall picture is clear in most cases. Install a circuit breaker in your brain to prevent overthinking. Look at the overall trend — there is no need to overthink, because the market will either achieve a breakout with effective follow-through, or it won't. If there is no breakout, the market will either reverse or chop within a range — it is simply one of these two outcomes.

With strong breakout moves, reversals usually first form a trading range. For a strong trend reversal to occur, you will see a clear breakout when the breakout signal appears, and then you can look for a second entry opportunity. The market context determines the next trading opportunity. It usually gives you a reasonable expectation of what the market will do next.
