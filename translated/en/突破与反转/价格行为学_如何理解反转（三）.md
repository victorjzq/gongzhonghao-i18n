# Price Action: How to Understand Reversals (Part 3)

Usually the decline into a flag is very strong, and you think: this level of strength is not enough to reverse the trend, so there should be trend continuation.
But the expected continuation never materializes, and the market reverses directly, running in the opposite direction.

In strong trends, trading flag breakouts succeeds most of the time.
The win rate of this trading approach is indeed quite high.
But no method is foolproof.

One type of failure is when you identify a flag pattern.
But this flag actually formed after the trend had already begun to turn.
Although on the surface it does not look like a reversal, the trend eventually turns.
This flag later proves to be the last flag of the trend.
You should know that a failed flag breakout is a form of trend reversal.
This failed flag subsequently forms a trading range, a structure that is typically a necessary process when the market transitions from bearish to bullish.
Most reversals need to go through a trading range.

A trend line breakout also means the trend is weakening.

A trading range containing five legs:
This pattern typically consists of three legs in one direction plus two legs in the opposite direction.
At this point, the market has entered breakout mode.
These attempts form multiple legs within the trading range.
So the market has entered breakout mode.

Pay attention to how this leg formed.
The first leg during the formation of the trading range is an important signal.

That is, a leg in the opposite direction of the original trend.
There are two key factors to watch. First is the first directional reversal move with significant strength.
This means it is more likely to form a trading range, or the range is more likely to trigger a reversal.

The second key point is testing the area above the last swing point, depending on the trend direction.
That is, a downside breakout corresponds to bears, an upside breakout corresponds to bulls — this is the location of the final swing point.

To improve the accuracy of this judgment, additional conditions are needed: Did price close above the last swing point? Were there two consecutive closes?
This is just one of the conditions.

In fact, breaking through the last swing point and testing the swing point are essentially the same thing.
Major swing point.
This is because within a trend, there are major and minor swing points.
If the first leg of the reversal breaks through the major swing point, the market has very likely entered a trading range.
However, this is just one of many probability factors.

The stronger the directionality of the initial reversal, the better, but judging this directionality often depends on the trader's subjective interpretation.
However, to establish a more rigorous and clear standard: when the first reversal forms a micro channel, the probability of the market forming a trading range is higher.

As for the second condition, a more rigorous definition is: you must ensure that the test is of the area above the major swing point.

So what happens when both conditions are met simultaneously?
The probability of buying appearing within the range and below the swing point will greatly increase.
And the decline of the bear trend should not be too large.

The determination of major swing points is also quite ambiguous — it largely depends on the trader's personal judgment.
Unless we define a major swing point as: if price reaches the intraday low — the extreme price of the day — then that constitutes a major swing point.

In the case of a micro channel, there is absolutely no ambiguity.
This does not require reliance on the trader's subjective judgment.
