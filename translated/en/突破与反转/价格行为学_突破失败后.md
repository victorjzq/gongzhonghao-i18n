# Price Action: After a Failed Breakout

A bull breakout should open a channel; but once it fails, it becomes part of a bear trend. This simultaneously produces a measured target — a reverse equal-distance projection. This equal-distance target often becomes a price level the market is very likely to reach next.

When the market is in a breakout phase, the probability of continued advancement in the trend direction is usually higher — price is more easily pushed along by "momentum."

The strength of trend momentum depends on which phase of the market you are in and the current contextual structure. Weaker momentum often appears inside channels, especially when the channel gradually becomes "old"; when a channel has just started and the market phase appears to have reset, the market may not even provide pullbacks — this is also why breakout gaps are more likely to appear. The conclusion is: momentum must be continuously incorporated into decisions.

When a strong breakout occurs within a trading range, if price has just crossed the range midpoint, following the common path, the midpoint often transforms from a former "pass-through point" into potential resistance, and the market tends to pull back to test it. But if bears are strong enough, they may keep the breakout gap open and not give the opportunity for a "midpoint retest."

This requires you to prepare a second plan: when your original "smarter first plan" (such as waiting for a pullback, waiting for confirmation) fails, you need another equally reasonable, equally executable way to participate.

When two decent entry candidates appear on the chart simultaneously and momentum is strong, the market will usually test the first level but may not continue to test the higher one. So the key is not betting "it must reach a certain level," but rather: you need multiple entry paths. Momentum and context determine "what is more worth trading," while the entry itself can be diversified.

When long upper tails begin appearing on the chart, followed by outside bars, inside bars, or multiple bars that clearly overlap each other, this usually means: the trend is most likely exhausting, and the room for further new highs will noticeably shrink. This is essentially an "exhaustion-type pattern."

Conversely, if you see a signal bar with almost **no overlap** with the previous bar, then placing a stop below it (or at the theoretical stop level) will be more easily triggered. In this case, the more reasonable approach is often not to "tough it out" with a stop order, but to directly **buy the signal bar**, building the trade logic on a simpler premise: price should **break above its high** — this is the logic behind this type of entry.
