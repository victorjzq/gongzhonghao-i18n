# Price Action: Wedge (Part 2)

## 1. Wedges Are Rarely Perfect
As long as there are three pushes, it can be considered a wedge; most channels are essentially wedge variants. The closer the structure is to the "ideal wedge," the more programs and traders will act on it, and the higher the reversal probability.

## 2. Three Pushes + Double Top (or Double Bottom) = Wedge Variant
After seeing a double top followed by another push up, this is essentially the third push. If the third push fails and quickly reverses, the market views it as a wedge top: bears go short, bulls stop out, forming long upper tails and bear bars. More conservative traders will wait for three or four consecutive bear bars before going short.

## 3. Three Pushes Alone Are Not Enough — Context Support Is Needed
The three legs must be clearly stronger than surrounding swings, and there must be context on the left side that supports the "reversal logic." Otherwise, price will most likely continue to chop rather than reverse.

## 4. Poor Wedges Do Not Produce Reversals
If there is no overlap between the three pushes, gaps appear, or the channel is too tight, these are "poor wedges." In a bear trend, such wedge bottoms often fail and continue to decline; in a bull trend, the same logic applies as price continues to rise.

## 5. Reversals Usually Precede the End of the Old Trend
From bull to bear, or bear to bull, there is a transitional phase where old and new trends coexist. Switching to a smaller timeframe can often find entry points earlier.

## 6. Parabolic Wedge = Buying Climax or Selling Climax
A three-push structure with progressively steepening slope is a parabolic wedge.
A bull parabolic wedge is a buying climax; a bear parabolic wedge is a selling climax. There is no overlap between the three pushes, accompanied by gaps. Aggressive traders will short a bull parabolic wedge. If extreme conditions are absent, the original trend can continue.

## 7. Wedges in Strong Trends Often Fail First
Most strong trends end with a wedge, but the first batch of wedges often fail — price makes a new high or new low before truly reversing.

## 8. Failed Wedges and Measured Moves
If a wedge breaks out in the "wrong direction" with strong force, it usually produces a two-leg acceleration (pullback then extension). When a horizontal wedge breaks downward, there is approximately a 60% probability of completing the measured decline. If the breakout immediately fails, a second reversal upward may follow.

## 9. Consecutive Bottoms and Second Entry
After a wedge bottom breakout fails and forms a double bottom or slightly lower new low, a reversal constitutes "consecutive bottoms." First bottom -> failed breakout -> second bottom -> higher low MTR, followed by two legs up. Bulls can enter at the failed downside breakout or wait for the HL MTR entry.

## 10. Selling Climax and "Pain Trades"
Consecutive bear bars closing at their lows constitute a selling climax. The low-probability continuation creates a pain trade, repeatedly frustrating bottom-fishing bulls. When momentum is exhausted, bears cover and bulls rush in, easily triggering a V-shaped reversal.

## 11. Stop Loss and Measurement
Bulls going long at wedge bottoms often use wider stops, such as below the measured move of the wedge's height. After bears trigger the obvious stop loss on a breakout, there is a 50% probability of completing the entire measured target and a 50% probability of the breakout failing with a second reversal.

## Conclusion
A wedge is a three-push structure with slope. To judge its reversal effectiveness, you must simultaneously check the strength of the three legs, overlap conditions, contextual logic, and whether a parabolic climax or selling climax has occurred. Both ideal wedges and failed breakouts contain measured targets and two-leg moves, making wedges one of the most critical components in a reversal system.
