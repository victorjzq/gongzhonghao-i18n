# Price Action: Microchannels (Part 2)

A tight channel is essentially a microchannel on a higher time frame, just containing more bars, so expectations should differ. For a microchannel, we often expect only that the first break below the prior low might fail and resume the original direction; in a tight channel, pullbacks usually last more bars before resuming the original direction, so we cannot apply the same expectations.

After a microchannel is broken in the opposite direction, there are four main possible outcomes:

The first is a successful breakout. That is, the move continues after the breakout. For example, after a bull microchannel is broken downward, price continues to fall. For scalpers, a successful breakout may just mean a tradable second leg appears; for swing traders, a successful breakout may mean a trend reversal with a new low. Therefore, before betting on a successful breakout, you must be clear about whether you are scalping or swing trading, and what your expected target range is.

The second is a failed breakout that resumes the original trend. The breakout does not go far and instead becomes a pullback, after which the market resumes the original direction. For example, after a bull microchannel is broken downward, it quickly resumes upward.

The third is a failure of the failure — that is, a breakout pullback continuation. The logic is: the first breakout fails and price resumes the original direction — this is the first failure; if this resumption also fails, that is the second failure, and then a "breakout — pullback — continuation" pattern forms. For example, after a bull microchannel breaks down and fails, price resumes upward, but the rally also fails and turns back down, creating a breakout pullback sell opportunity.

The fourth is a failed breakout that enters consolidation. After the breakout fails, the counter-direction momentum is strong enough, but it does not directly resume the original trend. Instead, it enters sideways consolidation, and the market enters breakout mode. At this point, the probability of the original trend continuing is still slightly higher than a reversal, but as the sideways period extends, the probability gradually approaches 50/50.

Understanding the quality and location of microchannels helps determine whether to trade in the breakout direction or counter-trade. If you expect the breakout to succeed, trade in the breakout direction; if you expect the breakout to fail, take the opposite side. Quality and context can also help predict the path after the breakout. For example, in a trading range, if you want to counter-trade a breakout but see that the microchannel quality is very strong, it probably will not reverse on the first attempt. The more likely path is: the first reversal fails, price resumes the original direction, then the resumption fails, creating a second reversal opportunity. In this case, it is better to wait for the second reversal rather than entering rashly on the first.
