# Price Action: Scaling In

1. Why Beginners Often Get Dragged Down by Scaling In
Many beginners do not know when to cut their losses and exit, or simply refuse to exit, allowing losses to keep expanding. The more they lose, the more emotional they become, the weaker their objective judgment gets, and the trade gets dragged into an abyss by poor management.

2. "A Size I Truly Don't Care About"
The first priority when scaling in is controlling position size. The size must be small enough that you can sincerely say "I don't care." Only when your position is small enough that you no longer focus on profit or loss can you calmly read the chart instead of making mistakes by staring at account fluctuations.

3. The Price of Trading Probability
Scaling in during a loss increases the win rate because the price only needs to bounce back to the original level to reduce the loss or even break even. But the higher win rate comes from greater exposure: the risk/reward ratio deteriorates, and the maximum potential loss doubles. Many people who scale in have actually given up on profiting and just want to erase the paper loss.

4. Stay Objective Rather Than "Pray"
The market will not change because of your hopes. Whatever is happening on the chart, operate according to it. Once you find yourself "praying," it means you have lost objectivity.

5. When to Consider Scaling In

1.  The logic still holds: your original thesis (for example, price is at the bottom of a trading range) has not been invalidated by the chart.

2.  The scaling-in level has structural support: back at the range low, at the extreme of a trend pullback, etc.

3.  Total risk has been calculated: the combined stop loss after merging both entries is still within an acceptable range.

4.  The exit is planned: at the latest, reduce or exit the entire position at the breakeven point of the first entry, without hoping the market will "come back on its own."


6. Typical Operational Approach

*   **Enter in two parts**: Build half the position first; if price pulls back to the expected area, add the other half.

*   **Set the target at the other end of the range**: If you judge the market has entered a trading range, the upper boundary is the ideal exit point.

*   **Exit when disappointed**: As soon as you are disappointed with the price action, reduce the position at the breakeven point; if the second entry has a profit, lock it in along the way.


Scaling in rarely offers a good risk/reward ratio, and total risk can easily spiral out of control with the slightest carelessness. Unless you are extremely confident in the structure and position management, it is better to simply take the stop loss and wait for a new, clear entry opportunity.
