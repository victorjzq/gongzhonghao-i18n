# Price Action: The Smaller the Stop Loss, the Lower the Win Rate

## 1. The Real Win Rate of Small Stop Losses

Many people like to set "extremely small stop losses" when trading, such as only 1-2 ticks.
In theory, the smaller the stop loss, the better the risk/reward ratio -- seemingly minimal risk with enormous reward.

But the reality is:

*   **The smaller the stop loss, the higher the probability of getting stopped out.**

*   The market often reverses briefly after a signal bar breakout to "shake out" traders before continuing in the true trend direction.


In other words, **there is no place in the market that simultaneously offers a "small stop loss" and a "high win rate."**

* * *

## 2. The Relationship Between Stop Loss and Win Rate

In practice, stop loss size is directly correlated with win rate:

*   The smaller the stop loss, the less room you allow for price fluctuation, and any market movement will take out your order, so the win rate will naturally be very low;

*   The wider the stop loss, the more room you give the market to fluctuate, the lower the probability of getting stopped out, and the win rate naturally increases.



In simple terms:

*   **Small stop loss = Low win rate, high risk/reward ratio (ideal state, difficult to achieve)**

*   **Wide stop loss = High win rate, stable risk/reward ratio (more realistic, more sustainable)**



The most effective way to improve your overall win rate is to use a **wide stop loss**.

* * *

## 3. How Exactly Should a Wide Stop Loss Be Set?

Different market structures require different wide stop loss placement methods:

*   **Wide stop loss for with-trend trades**
    When trading with the trend, a wide stop loss is generally placed at **the beginning of the current leg**.
    For example, in a bull trend, after a pullback you buy with the trend -- your stop loss is typically placed at the low of the current upward leg's starting point.

*   **Wide stop loss for counter-trend trades**
    When going short or long against the trend, a wide stop loss should consider **the measured move (MM) target of the current leg**.
    For instance, if you go short against the trend at a wedge top, the wide stop loss should not simply be placed above the signal bar, but rather at the MM level of the last upward leg.
    This gives the market enough room to fluctuate so your position is not easily taken out by brief market noise.

## 4. The Market Does Not Offer a "Perfect Spot" with Small Stop Loss and High Win Rate

Market structure dictates:

*   **If you want a small stop loss, you must pay the price of a low win rate**;

*   **If you want a high win rate, you must accept the reality of a wide stop loss**.


There is no ideal state in trading that simultaneously satisfies "small stop loss + high win rate."

Therefore, there is only one effective way to improve win rate --
**Appropriately widen the stop loss: use the leg's starting point as the stop for with-trend trades, and the leg's MM level as the stop for counter-trend trades.**

This is the truly sustainable trading approach.

When price forms a clear **wedge top structure** and completes three waves up (a Low 4 pattern), Bar 68 can be considered a classic **second entry short**, and it is also a pullback entry point after breaking out of the "final flag." However, note that Bar 68's signal bar is only 3 ticks tall. If you place the stop loss just 1 tick above the signal bar, the risk seems extremely small, but it is very susceptible to the market bouncing back and stopping you out. This "stop hunting" pattern is very common: first trigger the short entry, then quickly stop it out, and then the real bear trend actually begins. Therefore, at this type of setup, the stop loss must not be too tight -- it should be placed 4-5 ticks or even 2-3 points away, leaving enough room for market fluctuation. In other words, the smaller the risk on the stop loss, the more likely it becomes a trap. **A wide stop loss is what truly improves the probability of trading success.**
