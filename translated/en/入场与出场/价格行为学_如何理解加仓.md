# Price Action: How to Understand Scaling In

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 209th article. You should also study Price Action.

Achieving a 90% win rate is extremely difficult, unless you are willing to use a large stop loss and scale in. Even then, it is still very hard to accomplish. Most skilled scalpers are indeed willing to use wide stops and scale in, and they use this approach to boost their win rate.

Win rate is the counterpart of risk and reward. You cannot have both a high win rate and an excellent risk/reward ratio in the same trade. Because there must be someone on the opposite side of your trade. What you earn, they must lose. So every trade requires a trade-off among win rate, risk, and reward.

If you want a high win rate, you must sacrifice reward in exchange. Institutions are willing to be your counterparty, on the condition that they are compensated. So when a trader scales in, they are increasing risk while also increasing the probability of success. This is why skilled traders frequently scale in -- they use wide stops and scale in to increase the probability of success.

Generally speaking, about ninety percent of price action on any chart is in a trading range or a channel. In this situation, as long as the trader manages properly, both buying and selling can be profitable.

Some traders like to look for patterns on a larger timeframe and then look for earlier entry opportunities on a smaller timeframe to enter as early as possible and reduce risk. For example, they find an opportunity on a 60-minute chart and then switch to a 5-minute chart to execute; or they find a signal on a 5-minute chart and then switch to a 3-minute or even 2-minute chart to look for a finer entry.

One of the common problems beginners have is incorrect stop placement. They often set their stops too tight. Additionally, scaling in is especially dangerous for beginners, as it can lead to extremely large losses. Beginners do not realize that scaling in requires using a very small position size. They often hold too large a position, and once the market moves against them, the psychological pressure becomes too great, causing them to exit at a loss prematurely instead of holding and waiting for the market to eventually turn in their favor.

When you are trading, the appropriate stop loss level is usually obvious. If you know where the stop should be placed but that level is too far away, then you either must trade with a very small position size or simply not enter at all. Not taking a trade is a perfectly reasonable choice. Whenever there is any reason that prevents you from executing a trade smoothly -- such as the stop being too far, or your mental state not being right -- you can pass on the opportunity. The market always has opportunities; there is no need to force it.

If you are swing trading, you should hold your position until the market produces a clear reversal signal, or there is a strong reason requiring you to exit. You rely on your stop loss to protect yourself. As the market develops, you can trail your stop to follow the move. Taking partial profits at support or resistance levels is a good way to protect profits. If the market produces a reasonable reversal signal, you should also exit promptly.

Swing trading requires a swing-sized stop. If the trade does not look good, you either exit immediately or rely on your stop. Beginners are often afraid of stops because they are too focused on losses and view being stopped out as a label of "failure." But in reality, being stopped out is simply part of trading.

Develop this mindset: "Keep your position size small enough that you do not care about being stopped out." This way, you can focus on executing correctly rather than fixating on risk. The trader's equation includes three elements -- probability, risk, and reward -- but beginners always fixate on risk alone, ignoring the other two.

If this is a potentially large swing opportunity, keeping the position small means you may not get rich, but you can practice doing the right thing. Once you have accumulated enough experience and consistently make correct decisions, you can gradually increase your position size, and eventually turn correct execution into wealth. Many successful traders actually do not trade large positions, but their execution is very consistent, and they still make a lot of money.

Scaling in is for experienced traders. If beginners scale in incorrectly, they can easily lose far more than expected. The essence of scaling in is to increase the win rate. But every time you increase the win rate, you weaken the risk/reward ratio. Usually this means increasing risk.

The biggest problem for beginners is that they do not significantly reduce their position size when scaling in, resulting in losses much larger than expected. Moreover, they are often unwilling to admit that the trade has failed and just keep scaling in, hoping the market will reverse, ultimately falling into enormous losses. Hope combined with scaling in is extremely dangerous.

Experienced traders know that when the trade logic has failed, they must stop out immediately rather than continue scaling in. Beginners, however, often fall into the trap of "refusing to stop out." A single large loss can easily wipe out the gains from a dozen or even twenty profitable trades, and then you need the same number of successful trades just to recover.

Therefore, scaling in must be based on objective judgment, not emotional hope.
