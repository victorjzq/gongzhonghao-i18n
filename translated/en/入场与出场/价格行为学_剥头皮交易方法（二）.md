# Price Action: Scalping Methods (Part 2)

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 140th article. You should also study Price Action.

Beginners should avoid scalping.
It is simply too easy to lose money.
To profit from scalping, you must maintain an extremely high win rate,
and that is impossible for a beginner to achieve.

Some scalpers use two timeframes:
they look for trading opportunities on a larger timeframe,
and then look for earlier entry points on a smaller timeframe.

So why do beginners prefer scalping?
It is because the risk per trade is the smallest when scalping.
They are especially focused on risk
because they scraped together just enough money to open an account,
hoping to make consistent money from trading, or even change careers.
They do not have much capital and still have family pressures --
a spouse and children to take care of --
so they must be extremely protective of their account
and are unwilling to easily lose a large sum of money.

Therefore, beginners naturally focus their attention on "risk."
But this is precisely the root of the problem.

Because when designing a trade, you must consider three variables:
**Risk, Reward, and Probability.**
Every time a trader prepares to enter,
they must ensure the combination of these three variables is reasonable.
In other words, the trader's equation must be positive:

**Win rate x Expected reward > Failure probability x Expected loss.**

Did you hear that? There are three components involved:
probability, risk, and reward.
The biggest problem for beginners is that they only focus on risk.
What they should focus on is: **the relationship between risk/reward ratio and win rate.**

Many times, the market will produce consecutive bear bars
attempting to break out below a trading range.
Some traders will buy at the close at that point
because they know:
the bears still need one or two more bars to truly take control.
At that point, there has been no clear break below the trading range,
and no strong follow-through bar has appeared.
Therefore, the bulls will buy at the close,
betting this breakout will fail,
because most breakouts of trading ranges ultimately fail.

Beginners spend too much time worrying about risk.
If you only focus on one variable,
you will neglect the other two variables (reward and probability),
and the result will inevitably be losing money.
Every trade must be a reasonable match of all three variables,
rather than fixating on just one aspect -- risk.

Beginners are afraid of blowing up their account,
afraid of losing their "trading dream."
Once the account is blown up, or shrinks to the point where they cannot even maintain the minimum margin,
they will completely lose hope.
This is what is known as "blowing up the account."
So they fixate on risk,
completely ignoring reward,
and especially ignoring win rate.

An experienced trader
can see potential entry opportunities on every bar.
This does not mean they will take every trade,
but it shows they can see a large number of possibilities.
They only have a few seconds or minutes to process:
the calculation of risk, reward, and probability.
If they feel it is not worthwhile, they will not enter.

There are 81 bars each day,
and at least 40 reasonable 1-point scalp opportunities.
Most experienced scalpers
make 10 to 20 trades per day.
