# Price Action: Some Common Swing Trades

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 261st article. You should also learn Price Action.

Many people lament:

I've watched the videos, studied the materials, even reviewed them several times, but the more I trade, the more I lose.

So they end up asking this question:

How many times do I need to watch this video? They even ask others how many times they've watched it.

But this question itself is wrong.

How many times you watch it does not matter.

What truly matters is — whether you have completely watched it through the first time.

The purpose of the first viewing is not to "learn it."

In fact, it is impossible to learn it on the first viewing.

The only value of the first viewing is to help you find your own problems.

Only after you have completely watched it once do you, for the first time, gain a big-picture perspective:

You know what this system is teaching, what the structure is, which concepts exist, and how the concepts point to one another.

Before that, you do not even have the problems themselves.

Price Action is fundamentally not a collection of techniques, but an independent language system.

It is a language used to interpret what the market "is currently saying."

Only when you first clarify:

- What each concept means by definition

- How different concepts connect, constrain, and transform into one another

Can you then discover:

Which sentence you did not understand

Which piece of logic you could not follow

Which market state you simply cannot recognize

Then, you can go back with a clear sense of what questions to ask and look for answers.

Here we must introduce a piece of basic common sense that every college-educated person knows, but almost entirely forgets when it comes to trading.

Anyone who has written a thesis knows:

The most important part of a thesis is not "your opinion," but "citations."

Strictly cite the research results of others, rather than rushing to express your own understanding.

The reason is very simple.

Any reliable conclusion is the result of enormous time, repeated verification, and long-term trial and error.

Do not reinvent the wheel.

The old man (Al Brooks) researched for decades,

Lost money for ten consecutive years in between,

Before finally summarizing a complete set of experience.

And you, after watching the videos twice, want to "understand it yourself," "optimize it yourself," "add some personal style,"

And then walk the same path he already walked — a path he paid an enormous price for — all over again from the beginning.

This is not bravery.

This is foolishness.

So I often tell some students:

You must strictly cite. Do not have your own interpretation.

Your interpretation simply does not matter.

Truly smart people do not say "I think."

Instead:

Do exactly as you are told to do.

Strictly cite.

Strictly imitate.

Only imitate, do not invent.

Until you have reached the stage of "replicating without any deviation,"

All of your so-called "understanding," "improvements," and "upgrades"

Are merely finding a dignified excuse for failure.

The principle is actually very simple,

But most people have already gone off track from the very beginning.

It is not that you cannot learn it,

But that you started being clever too early.

Take one of the most specific concepts as an example: swing trading.

The old man says it very directly in the original text:

If you want to trade for a living, you should be doing swing trades.

What exactly is swing trading?

How specifically should I do swing trades?

What categories of patterns do swing trades break down into?

This step is where you start asking questions while comparing with the original text.

You will discover that swing trading is not a vague, feeling-based word,

But a set of structures that can be named, classified, and repeatedly referenced.

You can list out a batch of swing trade categories.

If you are not clear about these categories,

Then there is only one thing you need to do:

Go back to the book, go back to the original text, and find the answer.

It is precisely in this process that you truly begin to build your own learning system.

Because if you do not even know what a trade is doing,

Or do not even know what it is called in the original text,

Then you simply cannot execute the next step.

You cannot review,

You cannot cross-check,

You cannot determine whether you had an "execution failure"

Or whether you were "not trading according to the original text at all."

Underneath each swing trade name, there is not a single fixed pattern.

There are a large number of variations, variants, and differences in contextual conditions.

These variations

Are the true direction of research.

They are also the research direction I myself have been pursuing long-term.

But this "research"

Is not invention starting from a blank slate,

But refinement and extension built upon clear naming, strict citation, and stable replication.

If you cannot even articulate "which category of swing trade you are currently making,"

Yet you are eager to talk about understanding, optimization, and style,

Then this path was already crooked from the start.

When you have not even figured out the names, and you are already improvising on your own, of course it is easy to lose money.

For example, some common swing trade names:

consecutive bull bars with at least one being big and closing near its high

wedge bear flag and DT at EMA

bear trap, test of triangle

20-gap bar buy

parabolic wedge, buy climax

LH MTR

.....
