# Price Action: Scaling In Is Equivalent to a New Trade

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 3 years. Today is my 269th article. You should also study Price Action.

**By increasing the frequency of high-probability trades and shortening the holding time of each individual trade, you can achieve a smoother and more stable equity curve. At the same time, treat scaling in as a new independent trade, and only use it when there is clear structural support and it has been planned in advance.**

**Scaling in is essentially equivalent to a new trade.** In other words, if you had no position right now, would you still be willing to enter at the price of the "second scale-in"?

Many people, when scaling in, actually decide "I want to scale in" first, and then look for reasons to justify the decision: "I will scale in here." Their real motivation is often to find a way to recover losses from an already losing trade -- this is very dangerous.

Unless you are a trading expert, scaling in must be explicitly planned before the initial entry. For example: pre-determine three specific levels to scale in three times. If it has not been planned in advance, you should not scale in on the fly. Furthermore, unless you are already consistently profitable, it is best not to attempt scaling in lightly.

If you are an intraday scalper, an important principle is: **Do not build your trade on a bet that a breakout will follow through.**

You must be clear that you are not betting the market will definitely break out and continue running. A more reasonable approach is to exit your position before price reaches a support or resistance level.

The core idea is: **Use trading ranges and reversal bars to dynamically calibrate your analysis and trading decisions.**

At the same time, failure scenarios are equally critical. Because once the market generates new momentum with good follow-through and a clear price target, price tends to rush toward that target more quickly, exhibiting a characteristic of being "pulled toward the target."

Low-probability events, once they occur, tend to move faster and farther. Therefore, if you have not understood in advance how a failure will unfold, once you encounter such price action, you can easily become paralyzed and even suffer devastating damage to your account.

But if you can recognize in advance: this is a low-probability trade, and you understand what it will look like if it fails, then you can quickly adjust your position, participate in the new direction, not only recovering losses but even gaining additional profit.

Excellent traders view every trade as one of two outcomes: either it succeeds, or it fails. And once it fails, they profit again in the subsequent test or follow-through. Because a failure often triggers more than just one subsequent trade, this significantly reduces the psychological pressure of trading.

When a gap appears opposite to the trend direction, even if the gap is filled, it may segment this move into a parabolic wedge, thereby limiting the extension of the move before a reversal.

In the late stages of a trend, these counter-trend gaps often suggest the move may be nearing exhaustion. The market typically needs to pull back and test these gaps.

A parabolic wedge can also be viewed as a breakout pattern on higher timeframes. Usually there will first be a correction, and then another test of the high. The first pullback is often bought, after which the trend resumes and continues upward.
