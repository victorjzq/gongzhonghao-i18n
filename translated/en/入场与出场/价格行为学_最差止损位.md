# Price Action: The Worst Stop Loss Placement

Most people fail at trading not because they read the direction wrong, but because their **stop loss placement** lands right where both bulls and bears are actively trading. This kind of stop loss is called a **bad stop**.
============================================================

* * *

## 1. What Is a Bad Stop?

A bad stop refers to a **neither here nor there** stop loss approach:

*   **You neither exit early to save yourself, nor use a wide stop loss to hold until the end;**

*   Instead, you choose a seemingly reasonable "middle point."

*   Bulls and bears fiercely clash at this level;

*   And you get **precisely stopped out right here**.

Put simply, this is not because you "couldn't decide," but because you were **both greedy and afraid of losses**.
You did not want to miss the move, yet feared losing too much, and ended up being precisely punished by the market.

* * *

## 2. The Choices You Should Actually Make:

###

Taking buying at the close of bar 26 as an example:

*   **First choice: Exit early** (e.g., stop loss below bar 27)

*   **Second choice: Wide stop loss and hold to the end** (e.g., place it below bar 14)

But if you place your stop loss in the "middle," such as below bar 24 —
this is the most typical **bad stop**:

*   Strong bulls enter here, bears cover their positions here;

*   Your stop order becomes the easiest target for the market to sweep out.

### Buying at bar 46, stop loss at bar 45 — what happened?

###

*   The market had a buying climax at bar 46;

*   You placed your stop loss at bar 45, which seemed reasonable, but it was right at the key shakeout point where both bulls and bears were focused.

What happened next?

You got shaken out at bars 48, 49, or waited until the large bull bar at 54 to chase and re-enter at the high,
resulting in being repeatedly shaken out and your trading completely falling apart.

You did not lose to the trend — you lost because your **stop loss was set "just right"** in the worst place.

## 3. What Is the Correct Way to Place a Stop Loss?

Al Brooks gives a very clear answer:

### (1) Early stop loss, re-enter when the situation becomes clear

*   For example, if the price breaks below bars 27 or 29, stop out immediately;

*   Wait for confirmation that the move has improved, then re-enter.

### (2) Wide stop loss, small position size to control risk

*   Place the stop loss far away, such as below bar 14;

*   Wait for a clear bounce confirmation signal (e.g., the large bull bar at 54) before scaling in.

Other than these two approaches, any choice of the "middle road" — such as placing the stop loss at bars 24, 45, or 48 where bulls and bears clash — is a high-risk, high-hit-rate way to get "slapped in the face."

* * *

**A stop loss is not placed randomly**:
It must be far from the "magnet zone" where bulls and bears trade frequently. For example, below a buying climax, or below a second leg trap.

**The most common wrong mindset**:
Wanting to hold on, yet fearing losses, resulting in placing the stop loss exactly at the position where the market is most likely to take it out.
