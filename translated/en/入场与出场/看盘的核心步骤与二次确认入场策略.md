Core Steps for Reading the Market and Second Confirmation Entry Strategy

In the trading market, the most important aspect of reading the market is often "first identify key levels, then observe the breakout dynamics at those key levels." However, **the outcome of the battle at key levels** often becomes clearer only on the **second attempt**, and entering at that point typically yields a higher success rate.

* * *

**1. Bull/Bear Leg Origins: The "Starting Points" of Key Levels**
--------------------------

### **1.1 What Are Bull/Bear Leg Starting Points?**

*   **Leg (Swing / Push)**

    : Refers to a relatively complete one-directional move. It can be a bullish push upward or a bearish push downward.

*   Every leg has its "starting point" — the key level where price begins to "accelerate" or "reverse" in a certain direction.

### **1.2 Why Do Starting Points Become Key Levels?**

*   **Battle Focus**

    : Bulls and bears form a disagreement at a certain area, triggering a powerful one-directional move. When price pulls back (or bounces) to the vicinity of this "starting point," an intense bull/bear confrontation often occurs again.

*   **Foundation Points for Trendlines/Channel Lines**

    : When you connect the starting points of multiple legs, you can form the market's primary trendlines, channel lines, or range boundaries, helping us quickly determine the market's current phase (e.g., trending, consolidating).

> **Tip**: In practice, look back at recent upward or downward legs, identify which price levels were obvious "turning point launch" positions, and mark them. These areas are often the key zones where bulls and bears converge during subsequent pullbacks or bounces.

* * *

**2. Connecting Starting Points: From Key Points to Trendlines**
---------------------

### **2.1 Connecting Lines to Form Trendlines or Channel Lines**

*   When we connect the multiple starting points of bull/bear legs, we may obtain an ascending or descending diagonal line. By symmetrically connecting the corresponding highs or lows, we can derive the channel range of price movement.

*   This connecting line helps us understand: Is the market in a **strong trend**, a **tight channel**, or a **wide channel / trading range**?

> **Significance**:
>
> *   Identifying potential support / resistance: When price repeatedly finds support or encounters resistance at the same connecting line, it confirms the validity of that trendline.
>
> *   Observing breakouts or false breakouts: When price forcefully breaks through this connecting line and subsequent follow-through bars maintain momentum, it suggests a new leg or trend may be starting.

* * *

**3. Constructing Key Levels: Integrating Three Core Elements**
--------------------

When we combine "bull/bear leg starting points," "connecting lines between starting points," and "the 20 EMA," we can form a more three-dimensional and precise key level assessment. The general operating steps are as follows:

### **3.1 First, Find the "Leg Starting Points"**

*   Take a macro view and look back at recent upward or downward moves, marking the obvious "rally starting points" / "decline starting points";

*   These points are often the areas where price initially gained momentum. Pullbacks to these areas are highly likely to trigger a new round of bull/bear confrontation.

### **3.2 Connect Starting Points to Form Trendlines / Channel Lines**

*   Connect adjacent leg starting points to see if you can derive a sloping trendline;

*   Observe whether connecting the corresponding highs or lows forms a channel;

*   If the connecting line is clear and price repeatedly finds support or resistance at this level, it can be considered a reliable key structure.

### **3.3 Add the 20 EMA Moving Average**

*   Observe the position of price relative to the 20 EMA (above or below) to help determine whether bulls or bears currently dominate;

*   If the channel line or leg starting point overlaps with the 20 EMA in the same area, it may become an even stronger key level.

* * *

**4. Practical Approach: Enter After the Second Test**
--------------------

In actual trading, many traders like to place trades when price **first** touches a key level, but this is often a probing breakout or a stop-hunting trap with a low success rate. The **"second test"** or **"second confirmation"** approach is more reliable.

### **4.1 Observe the Market's First Reaction to the Key Level**

*   If price is immediately engulfed in the opposite direction, it indicates the key level has initially held;

*   You can wait for price to return to this area and observe its behavior before making a decision.

### **4.2 Wait for the Second Test or Breakout**

*   If the second attempt successfully holds above / breaks below the key level, and the follow-through bar shows sustained momentum, the entry has a higher win rate;

*   If the second attempt also fails, it may mean the market will move toward a reversal swing or continue consolidating within the range.

> **Core Concept**: The first test is merely a probe. The second test is more likely to distinguish a "true breakout" from a "false breakout," thereby improving the accuracy of trading decisions.
