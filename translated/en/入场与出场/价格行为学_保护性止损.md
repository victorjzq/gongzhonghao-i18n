# Price Action: Protective Stop Loss

1. The primary purpose of a protective stop is to guard against being wrong: once your judgment proves incorrect, the loss won't spiral out of control. It also has an equally important function -- quantifying risk. The distance between the stop loss point and the entry price is the size of risk for that trade; only with this data can you calculate the appropriate position size.

2. The most common mistake beginners make is keeping the "stop loss" in their head rather than placing it in the market. Once price moves through their mental stop level, they cling to hope and wait a little longer, so the virtual stop keeps getting moved further away, and the loss snowballs. Remember, the meaning of a stop loss is "stop doing what you are doing."

3. Losses are part of trading. Setting a stop loss won't prevent you from losing money, but it ensures you won't lose too much. The key in trade management is not where you entered, but what the market is doing right now -- new information determines whether to continue holding the position.

4. The market is not a forgiving "person." Many beginners anthropomorphize the market, believing it "made a mistake" and will come back, so they give it chance after chance. The result is usually an even larger loss.

5. When encountering an extraordinarily large breakout (for example, a bull bar five times the average bar length), you cannot still use your usual small stop loss. The larger the bar, the wider the buffer zone needed.

6. For a bull trend to continue, it must hold the low formed by the previous major pullback; the same applies in reverse for a bear trend. If price breaks below that low, the trend often ends or transitions into a trading range. Many bulls place their stop loss below the most recent major low, and bears place theirs above the most recent major high.

7. Some traders prefer a wide stop loss because it raises the win rate; others are willing to extend their target to more than twice the risk. Both approaches are valid, but position size must be adjusted according to stop loss width -- the wider the stop, the smaller the position; the tighter the stop, the larger the position can be. Otherwise, a single mistake could devastate the account.

8. There are many methods for choosing stop loss placement. One approach is using a measured move (MM): measure the height of the pattern, project an equal distance, and place the stop at that line. Alternatively, you can place the stop beyond the signal bar; but note that if the signal bar is extremely small, this level is usually easily breached, so it's best to leave some extra room.

9. In practice, approximately 40% of trades will hit the stop loss, and this remains true until you have accumulated sufficient experience. Therefore, any single loss should only wipe out the profit from the previous winning trade, and should never erase the gains from multiple past winners.

10. Scaling in can improve the overall win rate, but it also amplifies risk. Only when you are certain that the stop loss, position size, and target are all within a controllable range is scaling in a reasonable approach. Otherwise, enlarging the position will only turn a bearable loss into a disaster.

Finally, do not treat a tiny stop loss as a magic bullet. The tighter the stop, the lower the win rate; frequent small losses can also erode capital rapidly. Sound trading is about achieving a balance among stop loss width, win rate, and position size, rather than simply pursuing any single one of these factors.
