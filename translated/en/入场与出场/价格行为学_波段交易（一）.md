# Price Action: Swing Trading (Part 1)

## 1. Core Criteria for Swing Trading
The core criteria for swing trading are simple: the planned profit in points must be at least twice the stop loss distance. Most people who are profitable over the long term use this approach, because it has relatively low demands on personality -- you just need to be able to tolerate the back-and-forth movement of price. In contrast, scalping requires capturing a small piece of movement in an extremely short time, the win rate needs to be astonishingly high, and the demands on focus and emotional control are extremely harsh -- beginners should stay away from it.

## 2. Swing Holding Logic
After entering a trade, swing traders continue holding until the original bullish or bearish rationale disappears. For example, in a bull trend, if a possible final flag appears, or after a rally of twenty-some bars the largest bull bar is printed, immediately followed by a bear bar closing at its low, this suggests that buying momentum may have peaked and bears have reason to step in. At this point, it is perfectly reasonable for a swing long to exit, because the bullish logic has weakened.

## 3. The Difference Between Swing Trading and Scalping
To distinguish whether a trade is a swing trade or a scalp, the key is not the outcome, but the management plan at the time of entry. If you are willing to let the market wobble in a small adverse move, and willing to hold the position for ten to twenty bars, not closing as long as conditions remain intact -- then you are trading in a swing manner; even if you end up making only a little, or nothing at all, it is still a swing approach. Conversely, if you plan to exit within a few bars from the moment you enter and absolutely cannot tolerate a pullback, then that trade is a scalp.

## 4. Trend Targets and Stop Losses
A true trend usually covers at least half the screen in distance. Swing traders expect at minimum "two legs plus ten bars," with more common targets being twenty bars or more, while profits are at least twice the risk and often much more. Many traders are not sure at entry how long they will ultimately hold -- the best approach is to think in terms of swing trading from the start: if the market doesn't cooperate, exit quickly; if the market delivers, hold until the target is reached.

## 5. Stop Loss and Target Placement
After entry, first measure the distance from the stop loss to the entry, then set the minimum target at twice that distance. If you only aim for one times the distance, that is a high win rate, small target approach -- which is not wrong in certain situations, but strictly speaking it is not a swing trade. If you want to scalp, you typically finish within one to five bars and cannot tolerate any significant pullback.

## 6. Experienced Traders' Perspective
Many experienced traders actually do not enjoy scalping either, because the process is too tense, easily leads to fatigue, and any scalping opportunity, when viewed on a larger timeframe, is often also a reasonable swing entry point. In other words, every scalp is simultaneously a potential swing trade initiation -- as long as you dare to give a wider stop loss, or even add to the position during a pullback, the win rate actually becomes higher.

## 7. Conclusion
The conclusion is straightforward: beginners should practice swing trading first -- twice the reward, consistent rules, less hassle. Once you can survive consistently, then decide whether you want to pursue the scalping game that only a very few people are suited for.
