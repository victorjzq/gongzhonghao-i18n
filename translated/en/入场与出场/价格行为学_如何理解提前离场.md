# Price Action: How to Understand Early Exit

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 219th article. You should also learn Price Action.

Many traders will proactively close their short positions as soon as they see three consecutive bull bars, with one or more having large bodies and closing near their highs. The same logic applies to longs — if you see three, four, or five small bars, especially when two or three of them close near their lows, you should also exit at the close of one of those bars.

One large bar against your position is a strong exit signal. Two medium-strength adverse bars are also a reasonable reason to exit. Three to five small adverse bars are also a valid basis for exiting. Do not wait for your stop loss to be hit. Once you see three, four, or five bars going against you, if you are long, your stop loss will eventually be hit, so it is better to exit proactively before the stop loss is triggered. For example, you exit at the close of the third or fourth bar, especially when the fourth bar's close comes as the bars are getting larger and the market starts accelerating downward, producing a series of large bear bars. This is bulls beginning to give up, and the market is reversing this upward move. A big rally followed immediately by a big decline, with bar bodies getting larger, indicates that bulls have given up and bears are taking control of the market. The market is likely to continue falling. The market has entered an "Always In Short" state. Every trend eventually ends, and every trend eventually disappoints some people who hoped the trend would continue.

In a "sell the close" bear trend, if a bull bar appears that closes near its high, you must exit with a stop above the high of that bull bar.

When you discover during a trade that the premise of your trade has changed, you must adjust your trading style.

The core of trading is not "always being right," but "controlling losses." You cannot be correct every time, but you can ensure that when you are wrong, your losses are small, and when you are right, your profits are sufficient.

Strong pullbacks are important because they mark the beginning of two-sided trading after a breakout. The market starts to enter a channel phase. A pullback is always the starting point of a channel. Breakout — pullback — channel start, and then the market enters a bear channel. After that, we begin to pull back. The channel will eventually evolve into a trading range, and this is the market cycle: breakout — channel — trading range.

Typically, with about 75% probability, a bull channel will break downward, and the channel will evolve into a trading range. The bottom of the trading range is usually the low of that pullback.

But you must trade what the market gives you. A sideways area of nearly 20 bars is a market consensus zone. Regardless of which direction it breaks out, it usually tests back to this trading range.

Trading is based on rational and repeatable human behavior, so there should be certain identifiable patterns in the market. If you can learn enough patterns, you can learn to trade.

For new traders, confidence is a big issue. You will doubt whether you are worth trusting. You will doubt whether those patterns can really be profitable. It takes two years to build trust in charts. One difficulty of trading is that everyone wants 100% certainty. They want to know beyond any doubt that a trade will make money. But trading does not work that way. You are walking in dense fog, and it is never as clear as you would like. In every trade, you must assume that an institution is trading against you. Of course, there are also institutions trading in the same direction as you, but you must realize that there are always two forces in the market. This means that at any given moment, the probability edge between buyers and sellers is very close. This applies to all markets. You must assume that at any moment, as long as you know how to design a trade and manage it, you can always find a way to go long and go short.
