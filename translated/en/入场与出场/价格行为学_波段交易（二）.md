# Price Action: Swing Trading (Part 2)

## 1. Definition of Swing Trading
Target profit >= Risk x 2.
Expect the move to last at least 10 bars + two legs as a starting point, ideally extending to 20 bars or more.

## 2. Why Most Profitable Traders Do Swing Trading
Scalping requires an extremely high win rate to survive; that demands rare talent and psychological fortitude.
Beginners can hardly survive in a high-frequency, small-profit, low-margin-for-error environment; swing trading allows time and pullbacks, making success more realistic.

## 3. When to Exit a Swing Position
As long as the entry rationale still holds, continue holding the position.
Typical **exit signals for longs:**
A suspected final flag appears.
After 20 bars of rally, the largest bull bar of the day appears, immediately followed by a bear bar closing at its low -- suggesting a climax signal with a possible transition to a trading range or reversal.

## 4. Distinguish "Swing" from "Scalp" at the Time of Entry
Swing mindset: tolerate 10-30 bars of minor adverse movement, hold until the target is reached or the logic fails. Even if the trade doesn't end up producing 2R, the management approach is still a swing trade.
Scalping mindset: plan to close within 1-5 bars, do not accept any significant pullback, profit target is less than twice the risk.

## 5. Large Bars in a Trend Are the Minority
True strong breakouts account for only about 10% of all bars. Within this 10%, short-term scalping with the trend is mathematically viable.
The remaining 90% of the time, the market is either in a channel or a trading range, with success probability between 40-60%. During these periods, it is best to swing trade and set the target at 2R or above.

## 6. The Difficulty of Scalping
For 1R versus 1R small trades, if the win rate is below 60%, the expected value immediately turns negative.
To make consistent money from scalping requires a long-term win rate of 70-90% -- which the vast majority of people cannot achieve.

## 7. Avoid "Mismatched" Operations
Do not drag a trade that was originally planned as a scalp into a swing trade -- the direction is already wrong, and waiting longer will only amplify losses.
Conversely, if a swing trade doesn't get the trend it needs, you can take profits near 1R first, treating it as a small scalp to end the trade.

## 8. Counter-Trend Trades Require Waiting for a "Staircase" Pullback
If you want to go short in a strong bull trend, you must see a pullback that breaks below the previous breakout point to form a "staircase" pattern; otherwise, counter-trend scalping will most likely result in losses.
