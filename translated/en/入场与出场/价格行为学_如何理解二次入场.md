# Price Action: How to Understand Second Entry

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 196th article. You should also learn Price Action.

A second entry is more reliable than a first entry. The reason is that a so-called second entry means one side's second attempt, which implies the other side has already failed twice. So you are betting that the other side has already failed twice.

Second entries are especially important when momentum is strong. When you wait for a second entry, you are actually waiting for more information. When you wait for one side to fail twice, you gain more information. But Al says information is not free — you must pay a price for it. So he says second entries usually come at a premium. This usually means a worse price. In this case, your buy price is higher, your stop loss is farther away, and your profit potential is smaller. This is the cost: you pay a worse risk/reward ratio for a higher probability.

The concept of "paying a premium for a second entry" leads to what is called a second entry trap. If you actually get a better price on a second entry, it is very likely a trap that will catch you. Al says, "A good fill = a bad trade." In other words, if you have gained more information (seeing one side fail twice), and the market simultaneously gives you a better price, then you should be cautious. This suggests something may be wrong.

Do not think of "second entry" as a fixed pattern. Sometimes, it is simply an interpretation of context: when you see that one side has already failed twice, the other side's second attempt usually has a greater edge. This is not a specific pattern, but rather a "logic": you are betting that the losing opponent has run out of strength.

The concept of second entry is most commonly seen in wedges. For example, in a falling wedge, bears push once, twice, three times, and each decline is bought back by bulls. Eventually, bulls often buy on the second entry after the third push. Similarly, in a rising wedge, after three pushes by bulls, bears will wait for a second entry to go short. So wedges and second entries are often closely connected.

A second short after a double top, or a second long after a double bottom, is usually more confident than the first. In a double top, bulls have tried twice and failed, so bears' second attempt has a higher success rate. In a double bottom, bears have tried twice and failed, so bulls' second attempt has a higher success rate. This is the common application of second entries in patterns.

A second entry is not a perfect signal. It does not mean "any second entry will succeed." It simply means: compared to the first entry, the second entry tends to have a higher probability. At the same time, a second entry often comes with a "worse price," because while the market gives you more confirmation, it also makes you pay a premium. Therefore, traders must weigh probability against risk/reward ratio: if you want a higher success rate, you must accept a worse price and a farther stop loss.
