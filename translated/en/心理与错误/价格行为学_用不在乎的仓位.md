# Price Action: Trade with a Position Size You Don't Care About

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is the 215th article I have written, and you should also learn Price Action.

Charlie Munger once said something that directly answers your question. He said: **"I'd rather hire someone with an IQ of 130 who thinks it's 110 than someone with an IQ of 150 who thinks it's 170."**

What he wanted was someone who can accept their own mistakes. In trading, being able to accept mistakes is critically important.

By following some simple rules and recurring patterns, **anyone** can make money on some timeframe.

The human brain works in an interesting way. When you experience something **extremely painful**, over time the **feeling of pain fades**, but the **memory of the event remains**. Each of us probably had some very painful experiences as a child — for example, losing a soccer game that made us very sad. Ten years later, you still remember that the event made you sad, but you no longer feel the original pain.

Most traders will try every possible mistake. When you first start learning to trade, you try all kinds of methods, and during this process, you discover that you keep repeating "stupid things" you **shouldn't** have done, until you finally make up your mind: "Enough, this approach doesn't work, I need to stop doing this."

Some people keep repeating the same thing, hoping it will eventually work. Sometimes this approach works in other areas. For example, if you want to knock down an old fence to replace it, pushing a few more times will eventually knock it down. But **trading doesn't work that way**. If you keep repeating something wrong, it won't become right just because you've done it many times.

"Not caring" is extremely important. Because you are competing with many very smart people, as well as some logically rigorous computers. These machines operate entirely on logic — they only analyze Price Action and then make trading decisions based on their analysis.

If you introduce **illogical variables** like "fear" or "I need to make money," you become distracted. What you're watching is no longer what the market itself is showing, but rather your emotions.

So how do you control your emotions? The answer is to **reduce your position size** until you **truly don't care about winning or losing**. Only then can you focus on doing the right thing, following your rules, and properly managing your trades.

This is where my concept of "I don't care position size" comes from.

The benefit of this approach is that you won't lose much money. The downside is that you also won't make much money. But the trick is that you use this "I don't care" position size to accumulate experience, continuously improve, and **gradually scale in**, until you can trade larger sizes while still remaining calm and profitable.

This is **one of the best pieces of advice** in trading.

It is indeed very simple, and any trader can do it, but many people simply **refuse to do it** — especially wealthy newcomers who have been successful in other industries. They always think: "I don't need to trade just one contract. I can trade ten or twenty contracts at once. I have the capital and can afford to be wrong many times."

This is a **huge mistake**.

Although you won't get rich this way, you can gain a tremendous amount of practice and build confidence in your own trading style. This confidence will **ultimately allow you to increase your position size**.
