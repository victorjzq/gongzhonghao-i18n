# Price Action: The Fear of Trading

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 172nd article. You should also learn Price Action.

The so-called fear of missing out is mostly caused by incorrect training habits. You have been waiting for a signal, the signal finally appears, but by the time you react, the opportunity is already gone. So you want to catch the next trade, afraid of missing out again.

If you can realize that there is no need to be so tense or so focused on catching a specific bar or signal, you will calm down. Because on any chart, there are always more opportunities than you need.

For professional traders, the real driving force is first determining on the chart whether the market is in a trend or a trading range.
If it is a trend, there is urgency for with-trend trading. Most bars will not successfully reverse. One characteristic of professional traders is that their entry methods are not limited to just one. During a rally, they can trade using market orders, limit orders, or stop orders, and they can quickly determine which is most appropriate. Even with this urgency, they execute in a calm, pressure-free state.

If the market is in a trading range, there will be two types of uncertainty. First, uncertainty about which bar will actually reverse the market. Second, uncertainty about whether there will be one more leg. Professional traders care about uncertainty and urgency, not fear and greed. Fear comes from losses or missing out, but professional traders are not afraid of losses because they have long accepted that losses are part of trading.

Whether through years of experience or systematic trading, they understand that losses are part of daily life. There may be one or two losing trades each day, but it is no big deal. After experiencing five hundred such losses and still being in the market, it proves that losses are not enough to destroy you.

Professional traders handle losses like professionals. No dwelling on it, no lingering — just move on to the next trade. Focus on the future, think about what the next trade will be, rather than being immersed in what happened fifteen minutes ago. They do not get so emotionally distraught from one loss that their hands shake and their heart races.

A loss to them is like a doctor whose patient cancels an appointment at the last minute. That half hour has no patient, which means lost income, but the doctor does not dwell on it — they might even go grab a cup of coffee. By the end of the day, they cannot even remember who cancelled. Traders are the same way — a loss is just a deal that did not work out, and new trading opportunities will soon come along to make up for it. The key is that losses must be small. This means you must have a clear definition of failure — once a trade fails and does not develop as expected, you must decisively close the position and move on to the next obvious opportunity.

The market constantly switches between trends and trading ranges. Most of the time, price does not move in a straight-line trend but pushes forward in legs. During trending phases, the number of trend bars is limited, and there are more overlapping bars. Limit orders have great value in trading ranges, helping you enter at low cost in the direction of momentum. Fear of missing out is a common problem among beginners, but professional traders focus on context, probability, and urgency — not fear and greed. Losses are part of trading. The key is to quickly identify failure and exit decisively while looking for opportunities in the opposite direction.

Context is a crucial factor in determining whether a signal succeeds or fails. The same price pattern has completely different probabilities in different contexts. A seemingly perfect bull signal bar, if it appears at a high resistance area after the third push up followed by a pause, will have a noticeably lower win rate. Conversely, even an imperfect bull signal bar, if it appears during the first pullback in a strong trend, may still have a very high success rate.

When evaluating signals, you cannot just look at that one bar — you must also consider the trend direction, location, market rhythm, and volatility state. In a strong trend, the entry opportunity after the first pullback is usually the best, because trend inertia is still present and the opposing forces are not strong enough to reverse the trend. At the end of a trend, price often enters a choppy phase, patterns become more complex, and the reliability of a single signal bar drops significantly.

Volatility state is also important. During volatility expansion phases, follow-through on signals is usually straightforward. During volatility contraction phases, signals are more easily negated by opposite-direction breakouts. Traders must judge whether the current state is volatility expansion or contraction and adjust their strategy accordingly. If you enter during the first leg of volatility expansion, you can be relatively aggressive — even if there is a brief adverse move, you can continue to hold. If you enter during a volatility contraction phase, you should be more cautious — once a signal does not get immediate follow-through, consider reducing your position size or exiting altogether.
