# Price Action: Losing Money Due to Mistakes (Part 6)

Another common mistake beginners make is:
they see the market is in a trend,
they know the price is falling, and they want to go short.
But they often wait until they see a very large bear bar --
a very strong trend bar -- before deciding to enter.
They think this strong momentum will bring more follow-through,
pushing the price further down and making them a profit.

In other words,
they always try to chase the short
after the trend has become extreme and has already gone on for a long time.

These traders think
they are seeing a new, stronger down leg.
But what they fail to realize is
this is very likely the last bear flag,
the accelerating tail end of the trend,
a classic trend climax ending phase.

They waited through the entire down leg without acting,
missing all the real shorting opportunities.
Then when the market accelerates into its final phase, they finally decide to go short.
Next, bull bars start appearing,
even multiple consecutive bull bars.
They feel very disappointed, even regretful.

But an experienced trader would not think this way.
After seeing the price action go against him, he would immediately assess that he is probably wrong,
and then place a limit order at his original entry point,
attempting to exit at breakeven or with a small loss.

Many beginners want to go short,
but they actually short when the market is already extremely exhausted, near a selling climax phase.
Or they want to go long,
but they chase the long right when the market is forming a buying climax.

The problem is,
once the market enters a climactic breakout or final flag phase,
the win rate actually becomes very low.
From a mathematical standpoint,
trading in the opposite direction at this point
may actually offer better odds.

Because if you go short at this moment,
it is a low-probability, high-risk, low-reward trade.
Rather than doing this, consider trading in the opposite direction.
But beginners typically cannot do this,
while experienced traders
may actually enter in the opposite direction at this point.

The problem with beginners is
they want low risk
while also hoping for extremely high win rates.
They often mistake "momentum" for "probability."

They think:
strong bull bars mean high probability;
large bear bars also mean high probability.

Sometimes this is indeed the case,
but this only holds true in the early or middle stages of a trend.
Once the trend reaches its tail end, things change.

For example, when a new trend just begins,
going long or short at early positions is indeed a high-probability trade.
But if you have already seen 20 or more bars
all moving in one direction,
and the momentum is very strong,
that actually suggests the trend may be about to end.

At this point, the market is more likely to enter a trading range phase
rather than continuing the previous trend.

But beginners often cannot see this.
They only look at the most recent bar,
or even just the previous two big bars.
They say: "Look how sharply it dropped, it must keep dropping!"

But they fail to see
that this may be the last leg of the trend.
They ignore the background of the entire structure,
focusing only on the immediate price momentum.

They also mistakenly think:
"Institutions see this momentum too, so they should let me make some money."
But this is actually wrong.

The market will not take care of you.
No institution is specifically looking to be your counterparty.

In fact, the market does not care who you are
and does not know what you are doing.

Your account is too small.
Nobody cares about you.

Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Bank of America,
or some Japanese bank --
they only want to make money from each other.
They will not waste their time targeting your small account.

So you must accept a reality:
in trading, you are invisible.
No one will "take care of you" or "make things difficult for you."
No one will specifically help you make money or cause you to lose money.

The only thing you can do
is rationally and objectively evaluate market structure,
make correct judgments,
and avoid making elementary mistakes at the tail end of trends.
