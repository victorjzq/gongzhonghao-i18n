# Price Action: Losing Money Due to Mistakes (Part 5)

Even experts lose money from making bad trades.
But experts rarely make bad trades,
and once they realize it is a bad trade,
they usually exit very quickly.

Some trades inherently make it difficult to establish a reasonable Traders' Equation.
For example, going short in a strong bull breakout is a mistake;
or going long with a stop order in a tight trading range is also a mistake.

If you take a trade and then within one minute,
or at any point realize it is a mistake,
the best course of action is usually to exit immediately and take the loss.
This is part of trade management,
and it is what expert traders do.
Experts manage their trades well,
so even when losses occur, they do not lose much.

For example, we see a micro double top:
the market goes up, down, then up again,
and now it breaks below the micro double top.
You could also call it a double bottom --
we are breaking below the double bottom,
and a very large bear bar has formed.
Suppose you are an experienced trader
and you go short at the close of this bar.
Most traders would not do this;
they would wait for another bear close for confirmation,
but it does not come because the next bar is a bull close bar.
You did not get the bear bar you wanted.

After going short, you see a bull close bar.
You realize you made a mistake.
You can choose to exit at the close of this bar or above its high.
Look at this bar's open and its close:
the open is above the previous bar's close,
which means many bears who shorted here
did not like this bull close bar.

They bought at market immediately after this bar closed to exit their positions.
When this bar closed, bulls rushed in to buy,
causing the next bar to open with a gap up,
opening above the previous bar.

This shows that many bears are giving up --
those who shorted here or below the double bottom are disappointed
because they wanted to see a strong follow-through bar.

Look at those bulls who went long here,
they got a follow-through bar,
which increases the probability of further price increases.

This was originally a bear breakout,
but the follow-through bar is a bull bar,
and it is a reversal bar.
Poor follow-through means the move is unlikely to continue downward.

If you went short here,
and then felt disappointed seeing this bull bar,
you might place a limit order to exit at your original entry point,
to break even.

But look at this bar's low --
it did not reach below the close of the bar where you shorted.
So if you set a limit order at that bar's close price,
you would not have been filled.

Some traders are more practical.
They set their exit limit order slightly above that bar's close price,
because they know many people also want to exit at that level,
and the price may never reach it.

Traders who set their orders this way might exit successfully,
while those who insist on breaking even get trapped
and can only cover their short positions at higher prices.

An experienced trader,
if he fails to exit here,
might choose to exit at the close of this bar,
or set a stop loss exit above its high.

This way he only loses a small amount.
Then he can start doing the right thing -- going long,
and earn back far more than what he just lost.
He cut his losses quickly after making a mistake,
which paved the way for subsequent profits.

For example, he can wait for two big bull bars closing at their highs.
At this point, the market is very likely to continue upward,
at least making a measured move based on the size of that rally.

Beginners often do this --
they see a High 2 and double bottom setup
and excitedly enter long.
They make a large number of bad trades,
meaning trades where the Traders' Equation does not work.
