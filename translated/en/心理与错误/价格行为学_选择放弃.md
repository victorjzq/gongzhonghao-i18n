# Price Action: Choosing to Pass

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 3 years. Today is the 275th article I have written, and you should also learn Price Action.

In a bear environment, a parabolic wedge rally has a higher probability of triggering a reversal than continuing the upward move.

When analyzing, in addition to considering the previous day's price action, the current day's continuation of the previous day, and the current chart's pattern type, you must also factor in the trend of higher timeframes. One research conclusion is critically important: if bulls cannot complete scalping trades using limit orders within the first two hours after the open, the probability of the market continuing to rise is extremely low.

When the market produces a minor reversal, the stronger the reversal, the more likely it is to force the market into a trading range. Therefore, a strong minor reversal is the first signal to watch for before concluding that the trend may reverse. You need to distinguish between being in a trend and being in a trading range that you mistakenly believe is a trend. In a bull environment, a bear breakout testing the bottom of a trading range is very likely to fail. However, if a strong reversal that resembles a minor reversal occurs within a trend, the market will most likely be forced into a temporary trading range before bulls resume the rally. This is an important concept: strong minor reversals usually lead to at least a short-term trading range.

Typically, when a trend begins, it provides two, three, or even more limit order buying opportunities. If these don't appear, bears will expect another leg of sideways-to-down movement.

Generally, if traders can participate using both market stop orders and limit orders, the probability of the move continuing is higher. Conversely, if limit orders cannot be used, many traders will choose to stay out. For example, if you would need to buy with a market order below the moving average, you would rather miss the opportunity than endure the stress. Since you know that 85% of breakouts eventually pull back, why gamble on the 15% exception? Better to stay within your comfort zone.

Many traders choose not to participate precisely because of their understanding of probability: if a particular move does not provide reasonable limit order entry opportunities, its probability of continuation is actually quite low.

This contradicts textbook theory, which often says "strong trends don't look back." But on real charts, very few strong trends can sustain themselves without a pullback or reversal. Textbook examples tend to select critical junctures — higher timeframe reversals or the beginning of new market cycles. Except in cases where a higher timeframe breakout occurs, most moves where "you can't enter with limit orders" are difficult to sustain; conversely, moves that allow limit order entries tend to have greater continuation.
