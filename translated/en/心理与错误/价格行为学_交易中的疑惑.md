# Price Action: Doubts in Trading

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 252nd article. You should also learn Price Action.

There should be no uncertainty when you are trading.

If you look at a chart and wonder "what should I do now," and cannot immediately come up with an answer, it means there is a gap in your knowledge that you must fill. However, it is easy to forget these doubts after the fact.

For example, after multiple consecutive bars below the moving average, on the first test of the moving average, bears will sell that bar.

The lower the time frame, the more exceptions and failures you will see — this is the nature of Price Action.

The problem with beginners is that

they lack the concepts of minimum targets and pattern symmetry,

so when they are in profit, they immediately take profits,

and when they are at a loss, they hold on and refuse to exit.

More experienced traders understand minimum targets,

so if the market has not yet reached the minimum target, they will be more patient,

at least waiting for that minimum target to be reached.

The breakout point is the price corresponding to the support or resistance level that the market is breaking through.

Any price level on the chart that the market is breaking through can become a breakout point.

Each bar within these bars has its own support and resistance levels.

The upper half of a bar is resistance,

which is the selling zone of that bar.

The lower half is support.

There are usually multiple breakout points,

depending on what you consider a breakout and how closely you observe.

These factors all affect your judgment of breakout points.

Different breakout points are used for different purposes.

Because a bar's open or close can also serve as a breakout point.

In essence, any support and resistance level can become a breakout point.

When the market is in a tight channel, the entry reason does not really matter —

getting in early is more important than getting in precisely.

The best approach is to scale in on the way down.

Because very often, as soon as you enter, the market will pull back — this is extremely common.

So you can first buy a strong bar,

and reserve one or two opportunities to add to the position at lower prices.

But if five or six bars have passed without entering, do not buy anymore.

Wait for a pullback, because this is usually the limit for most micro channels.
