# Price Action: Trading Is a Journey in Pursuit of Happiness

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 224th article. You should also learn Price Action.

Think of trading as a journey in pursuit of happiness — this is the best guide for keeping you on the right track. If trading does not give you this feeling, then there is definitely something wrong with your approach. If you feel fear, anxiety, or other negative emotions while trading, it means your trading system is not yet complete. Generally speaking, to reach this state, you must develop a trading strategy that has been thoroughly researched and validated — this is what is called a trading system.

You need to fully trust and follow that trading algorithm, regardless of how it is designed. The essence of trading is actually the pursuit of happiness. For trading to truly serve this purpose, you must develop a thorough trading plan so that you have a clear understanding of your own operations. Then execute trades strictly according to the plan, letting the trading plan guide every step of your actions.

Regardless of how your plan is designed, when you truly trust it, you no longer need to feel anxious. Even with thorough research into your trading strategy, it does not mean it will work every single time. But over the long term, after hundreds of iterations, it will show positive returns. It is like using a fixed strategy to place bets at a casino — when you keep repeating the same operation, after a hundred times, your final returns will inevitably exceed your initial capital.

In this situation, what would you choose? Of course, you would maximize your trading frequency. Because the accumulation of certainty gradually dissolves your anxiety. The strategy either works or it does not. If it does not work this time, it will very likely work next time. As long as the method itself is correct, the anxiety of trading will naturally dissipate, replaced by steady confidence.

In actual practice, you also need to consider the actions of your trading counterparts. Although this way of thinking may seem counterintuitive, you must establish this understanding: you are continuously transferring money from the other side's account into your own. Pay close attention to the behavior and reactions of the other side, because if you ignore these, you cannot observe the market from a holistic perspective. Otherwise, you will be caught off guard in trades and miss a large number of potential opportunities.

Understand the true intentions of the other side, especially those traders who are taking the opposite direction from you. When you can see through their behavioral logic, also learn to be gracious about it. After all, winning and losing are both part of the game. If they win, then accept the stop loss gracefully — honor the bet. Do not stubbornly hold on to a losing trade, and do not let one bad trade pull you into an emotional spiral. Worrying about gains and losses, doubting yourself — these are a trader's greatest enemies.

A truly mature trader is not someone who has never failed, but someone who can remain clear-headed in the midst of failure. They understand that trading is a game of probability, an art of discipline and repetition. Through the process of executing again and again, they experience the tranquility that comes from recognizing patterns — and that tranquility is the highest state of trading happiness.
