# Price Action: Losing Money Due to Mistakes (Part 3)

Another common mistake beginners make is:
they enter trades as swing trades,
but manage them as scalps.

The problem is,
if you are taking a swing trade,
the probability of success is typically only about 40%.

A beginner buys above a bull bar.
At first, he may have set the correct stop loss,
but later, because the follow-through bars are not good, he becomes uneasy.
So he decides he does not want to risk that much,
and moves his stop loss to just below the entry bar.
As a result, he gets stopped out.

He ended up losing money on a good trade,
simply because he did not use the right stop loss.
He temporarily converted a swing trade into a scalp,
and scalping typically requires a 70% to 80% win rate.
But the win rate at that location was nowhere near that high,
so it is perfectly normal that he lost money.

Sometimes the situation is that
a beginner enters on the 5-minute chart,
and then has five to ten minutes to think about the trade.
Then he starts worrying:
what if the market reverses and falls all the way back to the starting point?
He does not want to take that risk.

But if you are not willing to take that risk,
then you should not have taken this trade,
because that level is the reasonable stop loss location.

If you enter as a swing trade,
you must use a swing-appropriate stop loss,
and you should only exit when the stop loss is hit
or the market has clearly turned bearish.

Another common but opposite mistake is:
beginners enter as scalps,
but manage them as swing trades --
meaning they take on risk far greater than their expected profit.

Furthermore, beginners should not be scalping at all,
because scalping requires a 70% to 80% win rate,
and they simply cannot achieve that.

Suppose the market has a breakout, price continues to rise,
then another breakout, followed by a pullback.
A beginner might buy at the close,
hoping this is the beginning of the next leg up.

He may know it is a double top or a wedge top (legs 1, 2, 3),
but he buys anyway.
Then the next bar is a bear reversal bar,
and now he very likely feels regret.

He is scared by that bear bar.
Now he is frozen, numb,
and starts thinking: "If I take my stop loss, I will lose money."

He knows that scalping must be profitable most of the time,
so he refuses to take his stop loss,
and instead moves his stop further away,
trying to convert the scalp into a swing trade.

But then he does not manage it correctly as a swing trade either,
and ends up losing much more than expected.

When he bought at the close of that bull bar,
he actually knew the win rate was not high,
but he "hoped" the upward momentum would make him money.

After seeing that bear bar,
he looks back to the left and might say:
"Oh, maybe it is a double top, maybe it is a wedge top,
maybe I made a bad trade."

Then he thinks, swing traders can make money
as long as they use a wide stop loss and scale in,
so he decides to use a larger stop loss,
perhaps placing it at the bottom of the recent bull leg.

But that bottom is actually just a minor swing low.
The rally did not have consecutive strong bars,
and it was likely just a bull leg within a tight trading range.
The market could fall below it
and still maintain an Always In long status.

In the end, he might get stopped out at the bottom of the bull leg,
and the loss on this trade
could be four times his original expected scalp profit.

If every loss is four times your profit,
you need an 80% win rate just to break even.
To actually be profitable, you need over 90% win rate,
which is nearly impossible.

Even a very skilled trader
would have great difficulty maintaining a 90% win rate every day for an entire year.

So if a beginner has this kind of expectation,
it is unrealistic.
His strategy itself is flawed
and is destined to lose money.

If a beginner enters as a scalp,
he must manage the trade as a scalp.
This is a bad trade,
and the problem was already obvious when that bear bar appeared.
He should have exited immediately.

Either take the stop loss below the bear bar,
or take the stop loss below the buy signal bar --
he must take the stop loss.

In fact,
as soon as a trader determines he made a bad trade,
he should exit immediately.

If the beginner bought at the close of that bull bar,
he could have exited at the close of that bear bar,
rather than waiting for the market to break below the bear bar's low.

He must realize that
the moment he saw the close of that bear bar,
this trade was no longer a high-probability opportunity to make money.
