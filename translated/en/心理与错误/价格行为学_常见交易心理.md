# Price Action: Common Trading Psychology

90% of traders fail not because they lack technical knowledge, but because they cannot properly handle losses.

When facing losses, a normal person develops "hope" — hoping the market will reverse to avoid the psychological pain of realizing a loss.

This leads to holding onto losing positions until they become unbearable.

When facing profits, a normal person develops "fear" — afraid that profits will disappear, and therefore tends to take profits too early to lock in the "happiness."

You must reverse this instinct: when in a losing position, you should feel "fear" (cut losses quickly); when in a winning position, you should feel "hope" (let profits run and scale in).

When experiencing consecutive losses: acknowledge that you were wrong and break the current psychological inertia.

Switch from short-term charts (1-minute or 5-minute) to longer timeframes (15-minute or hourly charts).

Get away from market noise and see the big picture structure clearly.

Significantly reduce your trading size and refocus on the execution process rather than the dollar amount.

Wait for bar closes to confirm before entering — do not try to guess bottoms or tops.

Overcome the "supermarket mentality." In a supermarket, people see steak on sale (price dropping) and think it's a "bargain," leading them to believe that falling prices are "cheap" buying opportunities and rising prices are "expensive" selling opportunities.

This leads to trying to "catch a falling knife" (bottom fishing) in downtrends, and shorting or exiting too early in strong uptrends.

Trends tend to continue. Chasing momentum is actually the correct way to trade. Train your brain to accept the logic of "buy high, sell higher; sell low, sell lower."

Simply accumulating "screen time" does not make you an expert; you must engage in deliberate practice.

Don't aimlessly watch charts. Set specific tasks, such as: "Study what happens to the index between 8:00 and 9:00 AM if the first bar is a bear bar."

Collect statistical data and develop your own "scenario analysis."

Replay bar by bar — cover the right side of the chart and go through it bar by bar, making trading decisions without knowing what comes next.

Record specific patterns. For example, document the performance of the 4th 10-minute bar after every FOMC meeting in the past year to derive statistical patterns.

Maintain objectivity by not looking at your P&L. Focus your attention entirely on chart patterns rather than monetary values.

Do not reward yourself after a trade ends (for example, going out for a fancy dinner after making money). This trains your brain to chase outcomes (money) rather than process.

You must learn to derive dopamine from "executing the process" itself, not from "making money."

If you broke your rules but still made money, this should be considered a failure, because this random reward reinforces bad behavior.

Keep your blood sugar stable. Avoid trading when fatigued or when your blood sugar fluctuates significantly (such as after consuming a large amount of carbohydrates), as this reduces willpower and decision-making ability.
