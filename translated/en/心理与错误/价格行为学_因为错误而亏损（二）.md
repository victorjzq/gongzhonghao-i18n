# Price Action: Losing Money Due to Mistakes (Part 2)

Beginners often focus only on risk,
while ignoring probability and reward.
This causes them to frequently choose the wrong entry points.

Beginners typically have small accounts,
and because their accounts are small, they do not want to lose money.
They equate the losses in their accounts with the destruction of their dream of becoming a successful trader.
So they are desperate not to lose money.
Moreover, their spouses may also be angry about them losing money.
Therefore, they are under enormous pressure to focus on risk.

Unfortunately, the result is that they only see risk.
But to be profitable in trading,
you must also consider **probability** and **reward** at the same time,
not just risk.
If you only focus on one variable,
you will very likely lose money,
because you still need the other two variables.

For example,
suppose a beginner sells at a certain point.
He thinks, "There are two big bull bars here, it shouldn't be this strong.
Now the market is going back and forth, we might be about to reverse.
I want to go short here,
betting this is a buying climax that will be followed by a reversal down."

If he goes short at this point,
over the next few bars,
he will likely start doubting whether he made a mistake,
wondering if this might actually be a valid bullish breakout.

But many times, the beginner will hold on to this short position,
hoping this time will be an exception.
He actually already knows
that the market will most likely continue to go up,
but he still refuses to take his stop loss and keeps holding.

Because he knows there is no 100% certainty,
and the market indeed **could** reverse in the direction he hopes.
This probability is very small, but it does exist,
so he chooses to ignore the reality in front of him,
ignoring the fact that he made a bad trade.

He does not take his stop loss,
but instead puts his hope on the market eventually moving in his desired direction.

This is another common problem with beginners.
Humans are naturally hopeful creatures,
and this is perfectly reasonable.

Thousands of years ago,
if you had no food in front of you
and did not believe you could still find game to feed your family,
you would just lie there and eventually go extinct.
But another person, who went out actively searching,
firmly believing he would find something, ultimately survived.

So hope is a survival instinct deeply rooted in human genes.
But in trading, this "hope" is lethal.
You cannot rely on hope to make money.
You must objectively assess the current market conditions.
Once you revert to "hope mode,"
you will lose money.

Here is another example.
Suppose a beginner sees a pullback in a bull trend.
The market broke out of a bull flag,
and then pulled back after the breakout.
At this point, a decent buy signal bar appears,
which looks like a reasonable buying opportunity.

But this beginner only considers risk.
He is unwilling to accept any possibility of loss,
so he uses a very tight stop loss,
placing the stop just below the buy signal bar.

This is actually the wrong approach.
If you want to take this trade,
the only reasonable stop loss placement
is below the low of the initial rally.

You cannot use a very tight stop loss.
This beginner actually made a good entry,
buying above a bull bar,
but used a terrible stop loss,
and ended up getting stopped out.

Although it was only a small loss,
if you make two or three such trades a day,
losing a little each time,
the losses will eventually add up to a very large amount.

This is the consequence of focusing only on risk while ignoring probability and reward.
Every single trade
must consider the reasonable relationship among risk, reward, and win rate.
This is the foundation of long-term profitability.
