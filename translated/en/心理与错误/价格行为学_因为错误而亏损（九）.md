# Price Action: Losing Money Due to Mistakes (Part 9)

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 147th article. You should also learn Price Action.

What beginners focus on most in trading is risk.
They want the risk on every trade to be as small as possible,
because they are afraid of losing all the money in their account.
They view "losses" as extremely serious
and constantly try to control risk and minimize losses.

From a motivational standpoint, this is not wrong.
But if you understand the trader's equation,
you will know:
every time you compress risk to a very small level,
your expectation for reward becomes very high,
because you are using very small risk to chase a very large profit.

As a result, your risk/reward ratio looks extremely favorable.
But at the same time, the probability of success becomes very low.
Because the market never allows you to have all three things simultaneously:
small risk, high reward, and high win rate.

These three can never coexist.
If a trade truly had all three,
everyone would take it,
and then no one would be willing to be your counterparty.

Every trade needs a counterparty.
If your risk/reward ratio is exceptionally good,
then your counterparties — those institutional traders and market makers —
very likely have a very high win rate.

They are the other side of the market.
What you have is a great-looking risk/reward ratio;
what they have is a high probability of making money from you.

In other words,
you will most likely be the one who keeps getting stopped out.

Imagine a typical scenario.
The market is in a bear trend,
then a wedge bottom forms,
followed by a reversal with a rally.
You see a higher low has formed,
so you decide to go long after the breakout.

You tell yourself:
"I don't want to lose money.
I'll just place my stop loss below the buy signal bar,
so the loss will be very small."

From a risk control perspective, this is very reasonable.
But the problem is:
you are using an extremely tight stop loss
hoping to capture a very large reward.
Your risk/reward ratio is excellent.

But what about your win rate?
Very low.

You are very likely to get stopped out within a very short time.
If you keep repeating this pattern,
using an extremely tight stop loss every time,
the market will chip away at you bit by bit,
slowly draining your account as if by "death by a thousand cuts."

Let us look at an extreme mathematical example.
Suppose your win rate per trade is only 10%,
but when you win, you earn 20 times your loss.

This sounds like a mathematically viable strategy.
But the problem is:
you only have a 10% win rate,
meaning only 1 out of 10 trades is profitable,
and you must manage that 1 winning trade almost perfectly
to make up for the losses from the previous 9 trades.

In reality, this is nearly impossible.
Because the market rarely allows you to manage a trade perfectly.
Market volatility, unexpected events, emotional interference, technical issues —
all make it difficult to maximize your profits.

So while this strategy is mathematically valid,
it is virtually impossible to be consistently profitable in practice.

Some people will say:
"Then I'll set my stop loss further away so it doesn't get hit so easily."
That is certainly one approach,
but if your stop loss is set so far away
that you cannot afford the potential loss,
then you should not take the trade at all.

If you cannot afford the loss that the stop loss would bring,
then the trade is too large for you.
You should wait for an opportunity you can handle before acting.

We need to understand a fact:
low-risk trades are essentially low-probability trades.
This is an extremely costly pattern.

You can only rely on those very few winners
to compensate for the majority of losses.
And this requires extremely strong trade management skills —
the ability to maximize the few winning trades you get.

Or you must have a very high win rate —
at least above 70%.

But as a beginner,
you cannot possibly achieve such a win rate.
You have not even mastered basic trading skills.
You do not yet know how to structure a trade, manage a trade, or identify mistakes.

So you simply cannot execute such a strategy.
For you, it is destined to fail.

If your trader's equation is negative,
it means you are executing a losing strategy.
No matter how hard you try, the long-term result will be losing money.

You may see some experienced scalpers
whose stop losses are set very tight,
but whose win rates reach 80% or 90%.

That is because they know where the high-probability setups are.
They understand how to construct high-probability trades,
and they trade very frequently, with extreme focus and lightning-fast reactions.

They do not make money through risk/reward ratio;
they profit through an extremely high success rate.

But this is a game for very few people,
a style that is very difficult to master,
and a strategy that is heavily dependent on personality and mental state.

The vast majority of traders
are not suited for this scalping style.
They are better suited to reasonable stop losses, reasonable risk/reward ratios,
combined with above-average probabilities of success.

And what beginners need most
is to stay away from those opportunities that look like they have "very low risk,"
because those opportunities usually have very low win rates
and are not appropriate for your current skill level.

What you truly need
is to learn how to construct a complete trade,
how to weigh risk, reward, and probability,
how to manage every trade,
and how to accept reasonable stop losses.
