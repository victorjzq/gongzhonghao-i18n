# Price Action: Trends Will Eventually Disappoint Traders

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is the 212th article I have written, and you should also learn Price Action.

Whenever you see a wedge, you should expect it to test the bottom of the third leg. Because the third leg usually signals that a trading range is about to form. A rally, then a reversal attempt — a reversal attempt means the market tries to reverse the trend direction. Imagine a giant cargo ship at sea, sailing forward at a certain speed. If it wants to turn around, it can't reverse instantly. It must first slow down, then gradually turn, and only then can it truly change direction. Trends work the same way: the market must first rally, then go through several reversal attempts, form a trading range, and only then can a real trend reversal occur.

All trends will eventually disappoint traders. When you see some signs of disappointment, you must make a decision. Every trader makes mistakes sometimes, even experts. Most losing trades result from mistakes, not bad luck. Every trade has a probability of success and a probability of failure. Even experienced traders can lose due to bad luck. A huge difference between experts and beginners is: when experts make mistakes, they cut their losses quickly and lose less. They don't develop emotional attachment to their positions, they don't feel the need to prove they're right — for example, holding onto a bad trade hoping the market will eventually prove them correct. If they discover they're wrong, they exit with a stop loss.

Market risk refers to risk that is beyond your personal control. Personal risk is when you make a mistake; market risk is when the market makes a move you considered unlikely. This happens frequently: you take a trade with reasonable entry logic, but the market doesn't behave the way you expected it to. This isn't a bad trade — it's a good trade that went bad.

You need some rules to prevent bad trades from turning into huge losses. When the market is in a swing move, trading with the trend is relatively easy to profit from: when the market is going up, buy; when the market is going down, sell. As long as the trend continues, you can hold your position or scale in at any time in the trend direction.

If you are long, there are three ways to protect yourself. First, you always need a protective stop loss. After every strong breakout to a new high, you can move your stop loss up to just below the most recent major low. For example, if there is a new high breakout, your stop loss can be moved below that swing low. If the stop loss is too far away, you must reduce your position size; or, if you built your position at a level where the stop loss is very far away, you must trade with a small position. Whenever the market produces a reasonable counter-trend signal, even if that signal isn't perfect, it's reasonable to consider exiting.

There is another situation where you should also exit: when there is a strong counter-trend breakout. If you are long, even just one large bear bar, or three or more medium-sized bear bars closing near their lows, you should consider taking profits and exiting.

Every channel will eventually end and usually turn into a trading range. The most important protective rule is: there must be an actual stop loss order in the market. Not a "mental stop," not "a stop in your head" — an actual stop loss placed in the market. If your stop loss is in the market, then the most you will suffer is an "acceptable loss."

Anytime there is a reasonable counter-trend signal, that is a valid reason to exit. If the counter-trend signal does not trigger a reversal, it is just a flag, and the trend will continue — you can always re-enter at any time.
