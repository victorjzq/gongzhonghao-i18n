# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Psychology

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is the 194th article I have written, and you should also learn Price Action.

The issues of psychological factors and discipline are often more important than the technical analysis itself. Many traders lose money not because they don't understand the technicals, but because they make psychological mistakes.

You clearly know that the current market is in a bear trend, and you also know that the probability favors the bears. However, when the market suddenly produces a large bull bar, you panic. You begin to doubt yourself, fear that the market is reversing, and hastily close your position, or even reverse to go long. Then the market quickly resumes its decline, and you lose money. This is a classic psychological problem — you did not follow your discipline and instead let emotions control you.

After you enter a trade, the market doesn't immediately move in your expected direction but instead pulls back slightly against you. You can't tolerate it and immediately close the position. But right after you exit, the market returns to your direction, and you end up missing a good trade. This is also a psychological problem — you lacked patience and didn't trust your own judgment.

In trading, fear and greed are the most common enemies. Fear prevents you from holding positions, and greed prevents you from taking profits. Many people have already earned sufficient profits but still want a little more, only to end up giving back all their gains.

Therefore, you must establish strict discipline. Before every trade, you should set your stop loss and take profit levels and commit to never changing them arbitrarily. If you decide to place your stop loss at a certain price, you must execute it unconditionally — do not move it just because the market fluctuates slightly. Similarly, if you decide to take profit at a certain target, you must stick to it — do not try to hold for more just because the market is moving well. You need to understand that the market will always offer new opportunities; you don't need to capture every last cent from a single trade.

There is another extremely important point: you must control your position size. Many traders lose money not because their direction was wrong, but because their positions were too large. Once losses occur, the resulting enormous psychological pressure makes it impossible for them to think clearly. The correct approach is to always trade with a position size you can afford to lose. Even if you lose several trades in a row, it won't damage your account significantly, and it certainly won't cause a mental breakdown. This way, you can continue to execute your plan consistently.

The biggest difference between trading and gambling is discipline. Gambling relies on luck, while trading relies on probability and discipline. As long as you consistently stay on the side with higher probability and strictly control risk, you can be profitable in the long run.

Technical analysis can help you find trading opportunities, but the key factor that ultimately determines whether you succeed is discipline and psychology.
