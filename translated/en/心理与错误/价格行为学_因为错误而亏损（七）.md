# Price Action: Losing Money Due to Mistakes (Part 7)

One problem with major losses is that they can wipe out the profits from many previous successful trades.
And if you want to recover from that big loss through subsequent winning trades, it often takes many trades to do so.
Therefore, avoiding major losses is critically important.

In any reasonable trade, you have about a 40% to 60% chance of making money.
In other words, opportunities to profit are actually quite frequent.
But if a single major loss blows through your account,
even if you have many reasonable trades afterward, they are just being used to fill that hole,
and you cannot truly become profitable.

Whenever the market presents a reasonable opposing signal,
the wise thing to do is usually to exit your current position first.
For example, if you are short within a bear flag in a double top structure,
and then a reasonable buy signal appears,
you should close your short position first.
If the market later produces another clear sell signal,
you can look for an opportunity to go short again.

But regardless,
as soon as you can clearly see that you made a mistake,
you should exit immediately.
Do not sit there waiting for a miracle.
Do not hope the market will move slightly in your favor
so you can lose a little less.

This kind of thinking is dangerous,
because you are gambling on an extremely low-probability event against the market.
Real traders do not operate based on fantasies.
They control risk through management.

The most important way to avoid major losses
is to set a protective stop loss.
The stop loss cannot exist only in your mind --
it must be physically placed in the market.

The true purpose of a stop loss
is not to protect you from the market,
but to protect you from yourself.

Some traders clearly know where the stop loss should be placed,
yet deliberately choose not to set it,
thinking, "What if the market hits exactly my stop loss level and then reverses?"
They are afraid of being washed out by a "false breakout."

But this is the wrong way to think.
You must define a specific price level --
once the price hits it, it means you were wrong,
and you must exit.
You cannot continue to hesitate.

If you only think of a stop loss level in your mind
but do not place it in the market,
the biggest problem is that this stop loss will forever exist only in your head.
You will never actually execute it.

The result is that you watch helplessly as the price keeps moving against you,
but you still sit there,
hoping the market will come back,
and the eventual loss far exceeds what you originally intended to risk.

And this one big loss
could wipe out all the profits from your previous 20 successful trades.
To recover from this loss,
you might need to spend one or two weeks
making another 20 successful trades.

This is not an exaggeration.
This is something many traders have actually experienced.
So you must understand
that you cannot afford this kind of major loss.

There is one more point
about adding to a losing position -- scaling in when a trade is going against you, trying to average down the cost.

If you are in a reasonable trade,
for example, you are long in a bull trend,
and the price temporarily moves against you,
but your assessment of the trend direction still holds,
then appropriately scaling in may be a reasonable strategy.

But if the market is clearly no longer in a bull trend,
and has even formed a bear trend,
then continuing to add to the position makes absolutely no sense.

At this point, scaling in will only cause you to lose more,
and will not bring the reversal you hope for.
What you need to do is not add to the position and hold on.
Instead, you need to admit the mistake, exit with a stop loss, and protect your capital.
