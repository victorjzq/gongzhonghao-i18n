# Price Action: Losing Money Due to Mistakes (Part 8)

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 146th article. You should also learn Price Action.

Each of us is born with certain personality traits
that often help us overcome difficulties and get through tough times in life.
One very important trait is that we are full of hope for the future.

Thousands of years ago, survival in nature was extremely difficult for humans.
Those who easily gave up in the face of hardship quickly perished,
and their genes disappeared from this world.
Only those who persisted and never gave up survived,
and were able to pass their genes to the next generation.

So today, most of us have inherited this trait of not giving up easily.
We always carry hope, always feeling that things might still turn around.
But in trading, this "hope" can actually hurt you.

For example, a trade has clearly gone bad,
but you still cling to the fantasy that it might reverse,
so you choose to add to the position and keep averaging down.
You want to hold on and wait for the price to come back.
But this is not rational behavior — it is an emotional response driven by your genes.

What beginners fear most is being forced to admit defeat.
In their eyes, cutting a loss and exiting means being a loser,
means giving up, means admitting they are not good enough.
So they would rather hold on a little longer than take the stop loss and exit.
They often tell themselves: "Just wait a bit longer, maybe it will come back."

There is another reason — social influence.
Society does not like people who "give up."
We are taught from a young age to persevere, to work hard, and never to quit easily.
But in trading, experienced traders know that
sometimes the best thing to do is to give up promptly and exit immediately.

Because the market does not care how long you have been trying.
As soon as your premise is wrong, you must exit.
You cannot keep adding to the position, holding the losing trade, or gambling on a reversal.
That only makes the losses grow larger and larger.

The goal of trading is to make money,
not to be a hero with unwavering conviction.
You need to stay alive to continue trading.
So "being a living coward" is far better than "being a dead hero."

Every person who starts trading has a small account.
That money was hard-earned and represents their dreams and hopes.
They are especially afraid of losing money, afraid that once they lose, they will never have another chance.
So they focus all their attention on "not losing."

But the real logic of trading is not just about risk.
The trader's equation has three variables:
risk, reward, and the probability of success.
You must consider all three simultaneously — you cannot just fixate on one.

Risk is how much loss you are willing to bear;
reward is how much profit you hope to make;
probability is the likelihood of achieving your goal.
Only when these three work together in a reasonable balance is it a good trade.

Sometimes you hear the term "margin call."
It does not mean your account goes to zero, but that your capital has fallen below the minimum required by your broker,
and you get forcibly liquidated and can no longer trade.
This shows that looking at risk alone is not enough —
you also need to clearly assess whether the trade has sufficient potential reward
and how likely it is to succeed.

Many beginners only take low-risk trades,
but these trades have very low win rates.
They refuse to take stop losses and just keep holding,
waiting for those few big winning opportunities to make up for previous losses.
But if you cannot capture those opportunities, your account will slowly bleed dry.

To achieve consistent profitability, there is only one way:
commit to serious learning and master how to trade step by step.

There are no magical bar patterns in the market,
and no easy ways to make money.
You are up against the smartest group of people in the world.
If there were a simple method, they would have already exploited it.

Every trade must have someone on the other side.
So what you need to do is find a small edge —
an edge that gives you a slightly higher probability of success than others.

This edge cannot be very large,
but as long as you stick with it over time and manage it properly,
you can gradually accumulate profits.

You must take responsibility for your own trades
and cannot blame others for your mistakes.
But you also should not blame yourself just because you lost money,
because losses are a part of trading.

Excellent traders also lose money,
but they quickly realize they were wrong
and immediately exit, then look for the next opportunity.

What you need to do is exactly this:
when you are wrong, get out — no hesitation, no delay.

Some people, out of fear of losing all the money in their account,
initially look everywhere for a "guru" to rely on.
They want to find a master, a TV show, an analyst
and just follow their instructions to trade.

But this does not work.
The TV says "you can go long now,"
you enter the trade, then the market reverses.
They will not come to help you, nor will they be responsible for the money you lost.

You must rely on yourself to understand the market and make decisions.
And you must accept a fact:
you will make mistakes, and you must learn to admit your errors promptly and exit.

Others can only give you a "fish,"
but you must learn to "fish" in order to survive.

Once you have learned to trade, life can be completely different.
Many people lost money consistently for years,
but once they found the right approach,
the money they made far exceeded their own expectations.
