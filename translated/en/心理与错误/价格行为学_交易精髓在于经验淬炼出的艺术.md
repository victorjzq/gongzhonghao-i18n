# Price Action: The Essence of Trading Lies in the Art Refined Through Experience

The truly critical trading patterns are actually very few. These patterns are already open secrets in the industry, but the real essence of trading lies in the art refined through experience. When you try to build a trading system based on specific concepts, the core challenge is: how many detail parameters are in the initial framework? The more parameters there are, the more complex the system and analysis become. This state continues until the system completely breaks down.

Therefore, if you add too many details to your object of analysis, each new subtle element — along with the criteria for defining upper and lower boundaries and distinguishing minor differences — will generate a large number of subcategories. Each item within these subcategories also serves as a filter.

When you set filter conditions, this causes the number of qualifying trade signals to decrease. Because now you will filter out many typical trade setups that would have originally met your analysis criteria. While this can improve trading accuracy, the number of actionable trading opportunities will correspondingly decrease.

The trade signals that ultimately pass through the screening are clearly defined and naturally more accurate. The trading probability extracted through this method, based on this tiered screening model, will be significantly more reliable.

A market signal that one trader sees might be viewed by another trader as noise that needs to be filtered out, yet it may be completely outside the observation range of yet another trader — they are not even aware of these factors. Therefore, when traders miss key variables, their market perspective actually becomes more macro when assessing probabilities, their screening criteria are relatively loose, and the number of sub-dimensions in their cognitive framework is more simplified. Consequently, their assessment of market direction possibilities — the signals they see on the chart — tends to be more generalized and not as accurate as those traders who can observe more details, who consider more factors and pattern subtypes, and therefore make more precise judgments about market direction.

A trader's understanding of all details, and the subtypes derived from these details, each can serve as a screening condition. Thus a trader can determine that a specific rapid move in a specific context will exhibit a specific probability distribution.

The essence of trading is understanding the correlation between trade setups and outcomes, conducting probability assessments of these market correlations, and ensuring their market performance meets expectations.

But if we look at it from the broadest classification of price movement and all possible market behaviors, the most macro-level categories are simply trend markets and trading ranges. Trend markets necessarily have directional characteristics.

It is really just the process of price moving from point A to point B. Put more directly, it is just breakouts and trading ranges.

Price either breaks out of its existing pattern or consolidates within a trading range with limited movement. Obviously, breakouts necessarily have direction, so they are either up or down. If we consider this as the first level of analysis, the second level requires adding more information — and this key factor is direction.

This way your classification can be breakout patterns, or trends — trends divided into bullish and bearish. The second category is trading ranges. Note that trading ranges also have directionality, so they are likewise divided into bullish and bearish. Simply adding the factor of direction expands into more subtypes, forming a tiered classification system.

Markets have fractal properties, and all price action developments exhibit multi-timeframe linkage. What appears as a trend with multiple breakouts on a lower timeframe may be just an ordinary trading range on a higher timeframe — the key depends on which timeframe you are observing. But the most basic unit of analysis is the single bar — the smallest unit of measurement. If we view a single bar as a micro-level expression of market behavior, a bar can represent either a bullish trend bar or a bearish trend bar. Similarly, doji patterns also come in bullish and bearish varieties.

Beginner traders, when they first learn a trading pattern — say they just learned the wedge pattern — see wedges everywhere, yet overlook important detail characteristics. This typically leads to two problems: first, an overly generalized understanding of trading patterns; second, frequently overlooking critical detail features. Experienced traders do the exact opposite — they do not over-generalize, they know every detail feature thoroughly. Their minds store various pattern subtypes, and when they recognize a pattern forming, they can immediately identify the specific subtype.

Moderate pattern generalization is actually a sound approach, because learning any discipline starts with mastering general principles first. As knowledge accumulates, you gradually refine the detail elements, ultimately achieving continuous improvement in trading precision.
