# Do You Still Remember When You First Started Learning to Trade?

**Do you still remember when you first started learning to trade?**

I almost forgot.

Recently, I've been re-recording explanatory videos for Al Brooks' encyclopedia charts. When I got to the second chapter on Always In Long, as I was explaining and looking at those annotations, I suddenly had a very strange feeling.

This material--I'm extremely familiar with it. So familiar that I once followed along and drew the charts when I first learned, and now I can explain it clearly to others.

But when I sit in front of my screen ready to place an order, facing an Always In Long market, none of this appeared in my mind.

Not even once.

![](https://mmbiz.qpic.cn/sz_mmbiz_jpg/02pQHc9U6qpYeYlIqqx5cnOzm0tYFickBFYJIt49wvzwsI9Z7e2Qm71vtd3EknBslDRDLqSPdGhmpdl0BMlmnRQ/640?wx_fmt=jpeg&from=appmsg)

I realized that when I trade now, my mind is full of thoughts. But when I take those thoughts back and compare them with Al Brooks' content, the overlap is very low. So low that I started to wonder: am I actually trading Price Action, or am I trading my own imagination of Price Action?

And then a very obvious question emerged:

Why was I able to follow the rules before, but gradually drifted away?

This drift wasn't a sudden betrayal. It was like being trapped in a maze. I thought I was walking in a straight line toward my goal, but I found myself getting farther and farther away, and I couldn't even tell which step was the one where I went off course.

If you've seriously studied Price Action trading, you can most likely relate to this feeling.

So where exactly did things go wrong?

I realized the problem wasn't that I forgot certain details, nor that Al Brooks' content was too difficult. The problem lay in something easy to overlook:

Day after day, and ever-increasing, flashes of inspiration.

![](https://mmbiz.qpic.cn/sz_mmbiz_jpg/02pQHc9U6qpYeYlIqqx5cnOzm0tYFickBUmw1RiaKoZQVc512bL159VEGDL6pNUf8meofO6MIXjFIAeFj2wThOPQ/640?wx_fmt=jpeg&from=appmsg)

This sounds a bit absurd, right? Flashes of inspiration are a positive thing in most fields. They represent cleverness, creativity, and the beginning of being able to integrate knowledge. Confucius also said, "If I show a student one corner and he cannot infer the other three, I will not repeat the lesson." If someone watched Al Brooks' videos and had zero thoughts, that would actually be stranger.

But trading is cruel.

Trading is an industry that doesn't care much about feelings. The industry's attitude toward insights is roughly: you can have insights. But please don't treat insights as the basis for your decisions.

Because the market won't pay me for having many insights. In fact, most of the time the market will take my money precisely because I have too many insights. The market only rewards me for making the right trades.

This sounds like a truism, but only those who have been repeatedly ground into the dirt by trading know how real it is.

And what's worse, these flashes of inspiration are not one-time events. They grow into a way of life.

For example, when I first watched Al Brooks' teaching videos, the annotations were clear and the rules were clear. I followed along, and the results were decent--at least I was genuinely trading by the book. Then gradually, I started having insights, unconsciously adding a tiny bit of my own interpretation on top of each execution.

Note: this process is not obvious. It's even quite natural. It's like when we learned to write as children: at first you had to trace the template, but later you wanted to write faster, more like your own handwriting. So you start writing in cursive, start skipping strokes, and invent a "more comfortable way of writing."

The problem is that trading is not calligraphy. In trading, the stroke you skip might be the very one that costs you money.

Over time, we experience a very common misalignment. We forget the specific content from the original videos, yet remember our own insights from back then--insights that weren't actually important.

More precisely, what we remember is that at the time, I felt like I understood.

This feeling of understanding is like a mental asset that we repeatedly take out and compound. Today one insight, tomorrow one insight, the day after two insights collide and become a new idea. This new idea then gets forcibly applied, and we become increasingly convinced: I'm making progress.

And the real cost of this progress is drifting further and further from Al Brooks.

I started trading with flashes of inspiration instead of Al Brooks' content.

I started doing "what I think Price Action is," not "what Al Brooks teaches as Price Action."

I'm confident that in content creation, technological innovation, and similar endeavors, these flashes of inspiration are enormously helpful.

But in trading, they are usually more harmful than beneficial.

Just as I wrote in my previous article, trading isn't won by knowing a lot or having many insights. Trading is won by whether you can execute the same systematic actions across various market conditions.

The greatest temptation of flashes of inspiration is that they make you feel smart. The greatest danger of flashes of inspiration is that they stop you from studying seriously.

Al Brooks' Price Action system is extremely complex, extremely abstract, and enormously vast. It's not meant for me to generate insights from--it's meant for me to form behaviors from.

So the learning process should not be: watch videos, form ideas, then trade based on ideas.

It should be: watch videos, then transfer the content into behavior--or rather, transfer Al Brooks' behavior into our behavior, forming fixed actions to be executed repeatedly in the market.

Put simply, it's imitation.

![](https://mmbiz.qpic.cn/sz_mmbiz_gif/02pQHc9U6qpYeYlIqqx5cnOzm0tYFickBnkKIWxhKpjb0ghdVmpM9Hyplp0xc9K7Zu1JpiciaWdX1efxzxjJKhchQ/640?wx_fmt=gif&from=appmsg)

And it involves two kinds of imitation: one is active imitation--whatever Al Brooks does, I practice. The other is passive imitation--whatever Al Brooks doesn't do, I firmly refuse to do.

Many beginners who can't stick with learning to trade fail not because they can't understand the material, but because they start improvising too early. Once improvisation begins, you walk into the maze I described. You think you're walking in a straight line, but you're actually going in circles and regressing--and doing so very creatively.

So the conclusion I want to share today might be a bit counter-intuitive:

The version of yourself when you first started learning to trade--the state where you knew nothing and had no insights yet--was actually the best state. Because back then, you hadn't yet been bought off by "flashes of inspiration." You were still willing to follow instructions, still willing to clumsily imitate, still willing to set aside your extra thoughts.

![](https://mmbiz.qpic.cn/sz_mmbiz_jpg/02pQHc9U6qpYeYlIqqx5cnOzm0tYFickBlltY3FibYFzr2SKYlbmJYRCrsQBNsC5iahI8zZM2KznGDiao53Sh8eoGQ/640?wx_fmt=jpeg&from=appmsg)

In trading, I no longer want to have any extra thoughts. I just want to be able to execute the old moves correctly and up to standard on new charts, and continue transferring behaviors from Al Brooks.
