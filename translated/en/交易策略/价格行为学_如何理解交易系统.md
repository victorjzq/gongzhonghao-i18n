# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Systems

Trading systems can be broadly divided into two categories: either trend-following systems or counter-trend/reversal systems, also known as mean reversion systems.

Trend-following systems are closer to how most people trade. Most traders need to see a signal bar first, then enter in the direction of the signal. Counter-trend trading systems are usually harder to operate because they often don't require a traditional signal bar. Additionally, counter-trend trading demands more skill and experience, because you need to believe in advance that a reversal is coming.

Some high-probability trades include: the pullback test following a strong reversal, followed closely by a confirmation bar. Remember, all reversals are essentially breakouts, so this is a breakout entry.

The core logic is that once a trend reverses, it doesn't die out immediately. As soon as price turns around, the trend enters an "always in long" mode. The market usually doesn't snap back right away, because a trend reversal first needs to build enough momentum, and that momentum often produces a second leg. Once the trend turns and you catch the first pullback, the probability of a second push is around 70-80%.

A more conservative approach is to wait until bars are firmly above the moving average before going long. This usually happens after bears have tried to sell twice and failed both times.

If the pullback is very deep — where "deep" means the retracement exceeds a reasonable trend range — then you should not try to swing trade. Deep pullbacks often mean the market is entering a trading range. If the market has already entered a trading range, the trend after recovering from the pullback usually doesn't go far, only reaching a new swing high or low, and often stopping at the bottom of the range. In this situation, the closing price and entry price of the entry bar are already very close, and you should exit — preferably closing the entire position. Especially on the third or fourth push, if you still see a pattern setup, anticipating a reversal is usually the better strategy.

The reality is that most breakout sell signals only offer scalp opportunities and rarely develop into a full swing move. Breakout sell signals that truly produce large swings usually need a larger-timeframe chart context to support them.

There are two exit methods. The first is a standard exit, placing the stop loss extremely close to the market price, forming a tight stop. This works well in the vast majority of market conditions. The second is a wide stop, which is often more reliable in strong trends. I'll use long trades as an example; for short trades, reverse the logic. A long stop loss can be placed one tick below the low of a strong bear reversal bar. The logic is: if momentum is truly strong, bulls will aggressively buy and won't let that reversal bar trigger. But if it does get hit, you should exit immediately. Another tight stop method is to place the stop directly one scalp unit below the low of the most recent bull bar. As for exactly how much to adjust, it depends on the market's volatility — widen it when volatility is high, tighten it when volatility is low.

For a wide stop, you can place it below the previous swing point. This method is only suitable for swing trades and requires the market to be in a strong trend. The purpose is to avoid getting shaken out by normal pullbacks during a strong trend. As long as the pullback doesn't reach the swing low, the logic for the trend long position still holds. If price actually reaches the swing low, the market may have entered a trading range, and the rationale for the trend trade no longer exists.

Exits can also be based on the completion of price patterns. After every pattern completes, the market enters breakout mode, because it must choose between repairing the pattern and actually breaking out. In most cases, the probability of a breakout is lower than a pullback test. Once a pattern completes, it's best to close the position first, because when the pattern settles, the probability has often shifted unfavorably. If you have a profitable position, taking profits at this point is more appropriate. For example, in an ascending channel, if the market makes three consecutive pushes upward and completes the pattern on the ninth bar, that is a good exit point. The risk of continuing to hold is that the market corrects the pattern, eroding existing profits.

Two-leg patterns are common in trading ranges. If you use the first push for a scalp trade and catch the second push, then once the second push materializes, you should close the position and exit. The only exception is when the market is in an extremely strong trend — once sideways consolidation appears, that consolidation forms a trading range, but in a strong trend, sideways movement is more often treated as a flag and will most likely break out in the trend's direction and continue running. For example, in a strong bull move, if the market starts consolidating sideways and forms a double top structure, but the trend is very strong, this double top is more likely to evolve into a flag, and the market will continue to break out upward. In this case, the long position can be held, waiting for the breakout to continue. But if the market is just in ordinary sideways action without showing flag characteristics, it's best to exit after the second push.
