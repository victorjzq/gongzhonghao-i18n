# Price Action: Gaps and Moving Average Gap Bars

## 1. What Is a "Gap"?

A gap is simply an empty space.

It is the space between the current price and any support or resistance.

Support or resistance can be:

- The high, low, open, or close of any bar;
- The current bar, or an earlier bar;
- A moving average (MA), a trendline, or a channel boundary.

---

## 2. Exhaustion Gap

A large bull bar appears at the end of a bull trend, with its close above the high of the previous bar, forming a gap. Price then pulls back and falls below the prior high, and the gap is filled.

This is an "exhaustion gap."

It is not a confirmation signal for a trend reversal,

but rather indicates that the bulls are temporarily exhausted,

and will usually rest for a few bars before attempting to enter again.

---

## 3. Strong Gaps and Trend Initiation

If you see a large bull bar

whose close is far above all the highs to the left,

this suggests the market may have just initiated a new trend.

Bulls will add to their positions on the first pullback,

while bears will stop out, covering their short positions,

creating a consensus buy point in the market.

This structure is often called a "gap," "breakout," or "trend origin."

Its essence is: the market's force has unified in one direction.

The larger the bar and the less opposition, the stronger the trend.

---

## 4. "Valid Gap" and Trend Continuation

When a pullback occurs after a large bull bar,

but the pullback's low still has not fallen below the prior high, and the gap remains intact,

this structure is a "valid gap."

It usually means:

- The trend will continue;
- The market is likely to produce a measured move;
- Bulls continue to hold the initiative;
- Bears lack the ability to push price down.

For trading, you can use the pullback's low as your stop loss,

and use the height of the gap bar as the basis to project a two-leg upward target.

If, after reaching the target, a long upper tail, high-level trading range, or large bear bar appears,

it indicates that bulls are beginning to take profits and bears are attempting to regain control.

---

## 5. The Signal Significance of Moving Average Gap Bars

In a bull trend, if price runs above the moving average for twenty or more consecutive bars,

and then one or more bars appear whose **high has a gap below the moving average**,

we call these bars "Moving Average Gap Bars."

This structure indicates:

- Bears are becoming stronger;
- Price is completely below the moving average for the first time;
- But in most cases, the trend will still continue.

Even if the bears' strength increases, the bull trend may still remain valid.

---

## 6. Signals at the Boundary of Trend and Reversal

When these gap bars appear in the late stage of a trend that has lasted more than twenty bars,

it usually means:

- Bear strength is accumulating;
- The market may be preparing for a Major Trend Reversal (MTR) (such as a double top, lower high, or higher high MTR);
- A two-leg decline may follow.

Although these gap bars do not directly equate to the end of a trend, they are important warning signals:

Bulls are weakening, bears are taking over, and the trend may be shifting.

---

## 7. The 20-Gap Bar Buy Signal

If a bull trend has lasted about 20 bars and price has never fallen below the moving average,

when price touches the moving average for the first time and a gap still remains,

this is a classic "20-Gap Bar Buy Signal."

At this point, many short-term bulls will buy when price touches the moving average,

expecting at least a test of the prior high.

---

## 8. Trend Exhaustion and Reversal Potential

When multiple gap bars appear repeatedly and price keeps bouncing to lower highs,

this indicates that bears are strengthening,

and the bull trend may be gradually losing its power.

This structure increases the probability of a Major Trend Reversal (MTR),

and bears may completely turn this rally into a downtrend.

---

## 9. Trend Gap or Exhaustion Gap?

When you see a series of strong bull bars,

you must determine which structure they belong to:

- Is it a trend initiation, or a trend conclusion?
- Is it a measuring gap, or an exhaustion gap?

The key is to observe where they appear:

- Are they at the top of a trading range?
- Are they a second leg up?
- Is there a lack of follow-through?

If the answer is yes, then it is more likely a buy exhaustion trap than a new trend.

---

## 10. Characteristics of the Second Leg Top Trap

- First leg up;
- Pullback;
- A stronger second leg up.

Although it looks like a trend breakout, it is actually still within a trading range,

ultimately trapping those who bought high at the top.

Especially when it appears at the top of a trading range and lacks continuation,

it is very likely an exhaustion gap,

which will often be quickly closed by a market pullback.

Gaps are the visual expression of trend strength.

When they appear at the end of a trend without continuation, they may be a precursor to exhaustion.
