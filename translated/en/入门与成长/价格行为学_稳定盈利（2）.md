# Price Action: Consistent Profitability (2)

**I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 3 years. Today is my 281st article, and you should also start learning Price Action.**

## What Consistently Profitable Traders Must Repeatedly Execute

**Controlling Losses Is the Top Priority**

Remove the occasional large losses from your account, and the equity curve will improve.

**Repeat the Mathematical Expectation**

Through a sample of 100, 200 or more trades, repeatedly execute positive expectation logic (win rate x risk/reward ratio), without worrying about individual results.

**Managing a Trade Is More Important Than the Entry**

Every day, repeatedly check whether the entry premise still holds. The moment it doesn't, exit immediately regardless of profit or loss.

**Select the Patterns Best Suited to Yourself and Practice Them Repeatedly**

Avoid frequently switching strategies, time frames, or markets.

Frequently changing strategies is the primary cause of the beginner's vicious cycle.

Choose a time frame suited to your personality and go deep.

Build a fixed watchlist and look for opportunities in familiar instruments.

**Consistent Profitability Fundamentally Comes from "Avoiding Self-Destruction"**

Making money is very simple; the truly difficult part is how not to lose money.

Just remove the large losses, and the equity curve will definitely improve.

**How to Handle Consecutive Losses**

You must feel disgusted and sickened by repeating the same mistakes before you can generate the motivation to change.

Severe losses can cause psychological trauma.

You need time and stable income to eliminate the sense of "urgency."

Don't rush to recover after a loss.

Wait at least several bars to reassess the market cycle.

Face your weaknesses directly, and promptly admit mistakes to the market and exit.

**Avoid Adding to Positions in Unfavorable Structures**

Before achieving consistent profitability, the word "scaling in" should not exist in your vocabulary.

Don't scale in to average down costs.

Poor position management is the primary cause of large losses.

Only consider scaling in when the trade premise still holds and the initial position has reached a "risk-free" state.

The combined risk of the initial position and add-on should still not exceed 1%–2%.

**Don't Bet at the Worst Possible Locations**

Skunk stops: Exiting in the middle of a trading range out of fear.

Fading the range: Stopping out at the bottom, chasing at the top.

Climax traps: Chasing a very large bull bar after a long trend — the worst position driven by emotion.

Breakout orders that fail to filter — the market can't even produce a single tick of breakout.

**Common Self-Destructive Behaviors**

Subjectively imagining support and resistance, then blindly going heavy on bottom-fishing.

Mental stops — keeping the stop loss in your head without executing it.

Seeking social validation — when losing, finding like-minded people and holding on stubbornly.

Revenge trading — rushing to enter after a loss to try to recover.
