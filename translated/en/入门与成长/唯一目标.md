# The Only Goal

**Al Brooks:**

I don't really care much about those "goals" and "returns" numbers. For me, there is only one goal, and that is to "do well." I don't actually worry too much about "doing well" itself, but it's the direction I strive toward — in every part of life, I want to perform well. I'm 68 years old now, and I just want to be a good person and a good trader. I don't care about being the best trader, nor do I need to be the strongest bar reader; what I want is to "consistently do well every day" while being kind to others.

When I trade, I don't want to make mistakes. If I do something stupid, I fix it immediately: if there's a misunderstanding with someone around me, I apologize; if something goes wrong in a trade, I cut my loss and exit right away — no hesitation. I believe that in life or trading, once you realize you've done something wrong, you should stop immediately.

I don't care about being number one in the world, nor do I care how many times better someone else is than me. I only care about whether I consistently maintain "correct and steady performance," sticking to the plan I had when I entered the trade. If I judge that today is a trending market, I'll stick with swing trades; if I feel the trend isn't strong, I'll scalp in and out quickly. Occasionally I'll take a smaller profit than expected, but as long as it aligns with the original trading plan, that's fine. If the market continues to move in my favor, I might consider adding to my position or switching to a swing hold.

For me, the most important thing is to do what's logical, manage my trades carefully, and avoid "stupid moves." I believe everyone actually has enough ability to make money from trading; if you still can't profit consistently, it's often because you're also taking trades you know you shouldn't. It's like when the market is in a trading range and you keep selling at the lows or chasing longs at the top; deep down, you know these are low-probability gambles. Stop doing these wrong things, and your trading will naturally lose less money.

**Reflections:**

The essence of trading is not about profit or loss, but about being right or wrong. The key to "right or wrong" lies in whether it aligns with your own trading system, not whether a single trade was profitable or not.

The truly fast way to improve trading performance is not searching for the so-called Holy Grail of trading, but reducing bad trades and improving your win rate. Avoiding obviously "stupid" mistakes is far more effective than chasing high-win-rate strategies.

A mature trader never proves themselves right through a single trade. If you see someone constantly showing off how right they are, it means they are still at the beginner stage of trading.
