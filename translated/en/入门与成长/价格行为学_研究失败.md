# Price Action: Studying Failure

I have been studying Price Action with Al Brooks for 2 years. Today is my 250th article, and you should also start learning Price Action.

The most challenging aspect of trading is that while there are many simple, easy-to-understand concepts in the market, and explaining any single one is not difficult — understanding the basic principles is not hard — the real challenge lies in integrating all this knowledge, reaching a level of deep mastery at the subconscious level, so you can instinctively recognize these signals. These signals will naturally surface, giving you the opportunity to pay appropriate attention to each one without over-focusing on any single factor at the expense of others. This is the essential path to becoming an expert.

When a trading range shows three to four distinct moves, it's best to stay on the sidelines. Typically, after completing a five-wave structure, the market tends to transition toward a breakout. For example, when you observe a five-wave pattern of three up and two down, it means the market is about to choose a breakout direction.

There is a more subtle dimension to trading psychology — the sustainability of a trading strategy system. Traders need to invest equal or even greater effort studying the failure conditions of their strategies. Take the breakout pattern or breakout follow-through as an example: when a perfect trading signal appears, the excitement is overwhelming, as if you've discovered the Holy Grail of trading. You want to apply this strategy to every situation. But the human brain's cognitive mechanism dictates that we always seek to validate our trading beliefs. We believe breakout patterns are excellent entry opportunities that will help us become great traders, so we keep watching and exploring various strategies — it's incredible! This strategy has an 80% win rate, or even 90%.

But the brain subconsciously ignores losing trades, simply because these negative experiences fail to reinforce our trading confidence or shape our ideal trader self-image. We all want to be winners in the market. This trade looks quite good, the current move is going as expected. The key is that you must truly master a specific area, and more importantly, deeply study the various factors that cause trades to fail under specific conditions. This is actually a positive trading mindset, because what you're really doing is gaining control over market uncertainty. Then you develop this mental state — painting beautiful pictures in your mind, all scenes of consecutive victories. But when you actually start live trading, having never seriously studied the possibility of losses, reality completely shatters your beautiful illusions. Lost in these fantasies, your entire plan falls apart, followed by all kinds of negative emotions. This can form a vicious cycle. Do these methods really work?

If you can truly understand this process and treat it with equal respect — viewing the analysis of failure cases as a valuable learning process — this will not only build your resilience but also prevent internal conflict between the dreams you've woven for yourself and the true nature of the market and what it reveals to you. The market also teaches you: in breakout scenarios, a failed breakout signals that the market will enter a trading range. This is essentially the market clearly reminding you to pay great attention to trade failures — even more attention than to successful trades. This kind of review actually brings valuable experience.

As has been said, studying failure patterns is just as important as studying successful signals. When a trading pattern has an 80% success rate — which is uncommon but does exist — once it fails, the loss is often devastatingly large. Now mirror this parameter to the opposite direction, and you get an 80% failure probability.

Consider this scenario: when your initial trade — this 80% probability trade — fails, the situation completely changes. Now this failure pattern has an 80% probability of producing at least two legs of movement. This therefore becomes a high-probability trading opportunity. This is the thinking logic of professional traders. They analyze low-probability patterns. You can use the same method for reasoning. So-called low-probability patterns refer to price action within a trading range — low win-rate trading opportunities. When such a low-probability pattern suddenly exhibits one of two characteristics — either an extreme success move or an extreme failure pattern — whether extreme failure or extreme success, it transforms the trade into a high-probability category. The extreme failure move now has a 70%–80% extremely high probability of producing a second leg. The extreme success move is the same, with a 70%–80% probability of a second leg.

Traders need to think about failure patterns across various market conditions and the probability shifts they trigger. These failure patterns affect trading probabilities in different market environments. Studying these failure patterns is a required course for trader growth. When a trade failure is identified, it means the market direction has already reversed. At this point, the counter-directional move will very likely produce at least two legs. The first leg is the move that confirmed the failure, and this move itself is very likely to trigger a second leg. This is at minimum an opportunity you should capture.

The root cause of problems often lies in the higher time frame. What appears to be a failure pattern on the current chart may actually contain hidden clues. After deep review, you realize that the larger time frame structure had already given clear signals all along. Multi-time frame confluence doesn't happen overnight. You should have anticipated that this breakout would eventually fail. But as long as you truly prioritize and diligently study, the root cause of most failures is a misjudgment of the larger time frame context. So reading both charts and their interrelationship is the key to understanding price action, and enables you to anticipate when failures will occur.
