# Price Action: Trading Range Methods (Part 4)

In a trading range,
it often has two-leg rallies and sell-offs.
Sometimes the second leg is especially strong,
and it looks like the trading range is breaking out to form a trend.
However,
80% of trading range breakout attempts fail.
Those strong second legs
trap traders into buying at the highs or selling at the lows —
they are second-leg traps.
Traders should expect breakouts to fail,
they should trade the opposite direction,
betting that the breakout will fail
and looking for a reversal.

Entry methods when reversing upward near the bottom of a trading range:
Traders usually need to use wider stop losses,
and if they can scale in,
it will increase their probability of profiting.

Trading ranges usually have two-leg rallies and sell-offs,
sometimes three-leg moves forming wedges,
and these patterns often nest within larger patterns.

If you are a beginner
and you are looking at the bars,
you tend to look at the most recent bars
and see a bull trend,
while ignoring the bars on the left, which are telling you
it might be in a trading range or a broad bull channel,
in which case buying at the highs is the wrong approach.

Whenever I see a very strong second leg up,
I think it is more likely an exhaustion move rather than
the start of a bull trend.

When the first leg down is very strong,
the probability of buying the first reversal up is not very high.
Waiting for a second entry to buy has a higher probability.

In a trading range,
you can never be 100% certain whether a reversal will work.
The probability is not as high as you would hope,
and therefore, experienced traders use wide stop losses and scale in
to increase their probability of profiting.

Beginners should not scale in,
and they should not use wide stop losses.
If they get stopped out,
they can re-enter at the second signal.
If the second entry gets stopped out,
do not take a third entry —
take a break and see what happens.
If the second entry fails,
then you are very likely wrong.
You very likely thought the market was in a trading range,
but the market is actually in a trend.

As long as you use a reasonable stop loss,
if you take the same trade 10 times with a tight stop loss,
you will earn the same profit as a trader using a wide stop loss.
The wide-stop-loss trader gets stopped out less frequently —
in other words, he has a higher probability of profiting —
but when he loses, the loss is larger.

Beginners should not sell with limit orders and scale in.

Most traders never plan to let their wide stop loss be hit.
Many traders,
after seeing a breakout bar and a follow-through bar,
will exit directly at market.

If a trader uses a wide stop loss and manages his trade properly,
he has an 80% chance of at least breaking even.
In other words, the best way to avoid a loss is to
look for a bear bar
that closes near its low,
and then sell more below that bear bar.
You have an 80% chance that
the market will at least return to the midpoint between your first entry and second entry,
which will allow you to break even on the trade.
It may even return to your first entry price,
and then you can break even on the first entry
and profit on the second entry.

You always need to have a stop loss in the market
because of the existence of black swan events.
If you lived in England centuries ago,
every swan you saw was white,
and you would believe that black swans do not exist.
However, if you were a world traveler, you would discover that
in New Zealand and Australia,
there are many black swans.
So what you think cannot happen
sometimes does happen,
and just because you do not think
the market can break out into a bull trend
is not reason enough to hold a position forever.
You always need to have a stop loss in place
because the market can produce low-probability behavior.

Would a trader actually let the stop loss be hit?
Probably not. Most traders who use wide stop losses
use the wide stop to give themselves time to make decisions
and manage the trade, scaling in to avoid losses.
They almost never let the stop loss be hit.
Sometimes it happens, but they almost always exit before the stop is hit.
That stop loss is just to protect themselves from
very low-probability and unexpected events.
You do not plan on letting it be hit.

So suppose
the market is rallying and you have no stop loss,
and then you get a phone call
and have to rush out the door
because of a medical emergency.
You have no stop loss and did not exit,
and you hurried out.
The market could continue to rally
and you could suffer enormous losses.
Suppose your computer crashes,
or the power goes out, or your phone dies,
or some other unexpected event,
or your broker has technical problems.
You need a stop loss in the market
in case low-probability events occur,
because these low-probability events do happen,
and they can lead to enormous losses.
