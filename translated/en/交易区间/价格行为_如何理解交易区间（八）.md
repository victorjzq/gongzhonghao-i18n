# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Ranges (Part 8)

When price tests the bottom or top of a trading range, a reversal occurs that makes you realize the best time to buy with a limit order has already passed.

The best time to buy was earlier.

Price momentum explodes.

There is usually a retest of the bottom.

Typically, the first buy signal bar appears first — a first reversal to go long — followed by a rally, and then a second bar appears on the retest.

But at the bottom of a trading range, there is often a lot of two-sided trading, so this is not a definitive signal.

In other words, the second bar you see at this position, although it may be a clearer signal bar, the market may not continue higher but instead re-enter a trading range.

Let us talk about the concept of second entries.

A second entry sometimes looks like this: first there is a first leg down, then a small signal bar appears, then a large signal bar at the second reversal, followed by a strong rally.

It looks like a double bottom pattern, or a micro double bottom.

The exact pattern does not matter.

The core concept is the second reversal.

This structure essentially provides traders who missed the first entry with a second low-risk entry opportunity.

In a bear trend, we expect bulls to reach only the minimum target, not the maximum target.

The minimum target is to break that level, or reach that level, and then observe the subsequent price action.

In a bull micro channel, buying below the low of the last strong bull bar is reasonable, especially when the preceding bar is also a strong bar.

Because this structure often indicates that buying pressure is increasing.

During trend resumption, or when reversing from the bottom of a trading range, we have no opportunity to buy with limit orders.

It is all strong upward moves.

Then you are forced to chase and buy at high prices within the day in a trading range, and chasing highs is usually not a good strategy.

You should use limit orders to enter, employing a scale-in approach to average down.

You need to pay attention to how price got there.

Price broke through with a strong rally, so even if it tests the failed level, it is more likely to bounce again.

This is exactly one of the key differences that distinguishes beginners, average traders, and professional traders.

Nothing can truly suppress the buying momentum.

Usually, a lack of orders on one side causes the market to move rapidly.

This is what we often call the vacuum concept in a trading range, because no one is willing to sell at the bottom.

So price is drawn upward by the vacuum to a level where they are willing to sell.

When the market is in such an asymmetric structure, lacking counterparties, price movement becomes very fast and dramatic.

As a trader, you must realize that this process is not random, but rather because there are gaps in the market structure — there is real momentum and direction.

This is precisely the key juncture for understanding the switch between trading ranges and trends.
