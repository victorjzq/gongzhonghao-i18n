# Price Action: Trading Range Methods (Part 5)

Trading ranges are usually very tight,
and most traders should not trade in extremely tight ranges.
However, experienced traders often use limit orders to enter and scalp.
Every trading range eventually ends and transitions into a trend.
In a trading range, the general strategy is to buy low, sell high, and scalp.
Bulls will look for buying opportunities in the lower half of the range,
and bears will look for selling opportunities in the upper half.
A common trading method is to enter using limit orders.
When the market is in the lower half, traders will place limit buy orders at the prior low
and are willing to scale in at lower levels,
then scalp for a profit.

If you plan to use limit orders and scale in,
you must reduce the size of each trade
to control overall risk.
You do not want the risk of any single trade
to be greater than the risk of your other trades.
The reason traders scale in is that
it increases their probability of making a profit.
However, this requires using wide stop losses and managing them properly,
and these two factors can create pressure and anxiety for beginning traders.
This is an approach that only experienced and consistently profitable traders should consider.
Whenever you increase your position size,
you increase your risk.
But you should always keep your trade size small enough
to ensure that the risk on every single trade does not exceed the risk on your other trades.

From a mathematical standpoint, exiting any trade at twice your actual risk is reasonable.

Twice the actual risk is the minimum requirement for achieving profitability through the Trader's Equation.
But the minimum requirement does not equal the optimal choice.

But when the trading range is very tight,
there is almost no room for profit,
and most traders should not trade.

Sometimes the trading range transitions into a downtrend.

Once you discover that the market is transitioning into a trend,
you can no longer trade it the way you would a trading range —
you must trade it as a trend.
Sometimes the breakout appears as one huge bar
or two medium-sized bars;
and sometimes it consists of many very small bull bars
with almost no pullbacks, forming a very tight bull channel.
On higher timeframe charts,
this type of move constitutes a very strong breakout.
