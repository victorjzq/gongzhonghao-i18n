# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Ranges (Part 6)

Usually within a trading range, the best trading strategy is to wait at the top for a good signal bar to produce a second reversal.

Especially when the rally that formed the signal bar lacked momentum.

For example, a move like rally — doji — rally.

This structure means that the bulls attempted to push higher but lacked sufficient momentum, making it prone to a false breakout followed by a reversal.

When a strong bar first touches the moving average, it usually provides bears with a shorting opportunity.

The brief breakout caused by a strong bar, when it fails, often constitutes a key reversal signal.

When a tight trading range is well below the moving average and the breakout direction is opposite to the market's major trend, a mean reversion toward the moving average usually occurs.

From the bears' perspective, they are shorting at a level well below the average price, their stop losses are far away, so they need a trending market. They want to see trend bars, they want bar closes below the low of the prior strong bar, they want follow-through to continue the decline.

They want the move to develop in the same direction, they want bear bodies to dominate the bars, forming trend bars.

But the reality is the opposite — instead, a tight trading range forms well below the moving average.

The moving average above acts like a magnet that pulls price upward. Therefore, any pullback is likely to be absorbed by buyers, and the outlook is bullish.

This mean reversion behavior is a common characteristic of price action within trading ranges.
