# Price Action: Trading Range Methods (Part 2)

In a trend, 80% of the time, when the market attempts to reverse, the reversal fails;
while in a trading range, 80% of the time, when the market attempts to break out, the breakout fails and reverses.
There are certain characteristics that distinguish oscillations within a trend from oscillations within a trading range.

In a trading range, 80% of the time,
the market will have strong breakout attempts,
rallying or selling off, but these breakouts ultimately fail and lead to reversals
rather than successful breakouts forming trends.
We will see a strong downward reversal,
but it will stop within one or two bars.
A strong upward reversal
will stop within two or three bars.
After three consecutive bear bars, the market reverses upward.
This lack of consecutive strong trend bars
typically appears in the bear legs or bull legs of a trading range.

If you see oscillations constantly rising and falling,
breaking above highs and below lows but then reversing,
this usually means the market is very likely in a trading range leg rather than a trend.
When the market rallies,
I pay particular attention to whether the bears can profit.
If bears can sell at the prior high and profit,
or sell at the prior high and scale in,
then the market
is very likely in a bull leg
that will eventually become a trading range
rather than becoming a bull trend.
If bears sell above a high, forming a High 1 bull flag,
and if bears can profit by selling the breakout of the bull flag —
that is, selling the High 1 and High 2 bull flags —
then there is a problem.
This indicates that the bulls' strength is not very strong.

The market falls back below the breakout point.
This is a staircase-type pattern,
and this is more common in bull trends —
the kind of trend that quickly transitions into a trading range.

When low-probability outcomes occur more than you expected,
you must assume
the market is likely in a trading range.
