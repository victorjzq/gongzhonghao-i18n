# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Ranges (Part 7)

The market has fractal properties, so the small patterns it produces are structurally similar to the large patterns.

This is what forms those complex legs.

But trading ranges have an interesting phenomenon — sometimes the trading range you are in looks like a bear trend, but it is not actually a real bear trend.

It is still a trading range.

This is often because the market temporarily lacks direction, with price oscillating at low levels, but structurally there are no continuation signals for a trend.

Once you have mastered a particular trading concept, it is easy to fall into the trap of using the same concept to explain all market phenomena.

So you must constantly remind yourself that the market is not a fixed textbook structure — every pattern must be evaluated in the context of the current environment.

The appearance of a micro channel within a bull trend usually means we are in a trading range.

At the end of a move, a series of weak dojis appear, forming a tight trading range, and it looks like the bear trend should continue.

But if this pattern appears near the bottom of a trading range, and there was sufficient support strength beforehand, it could indicate that this support level will hold.

So what do these patterns actually represent?

They are usually counter-trend trading signals.

So within a trading range, breakouts fail, which means you can use limit buy or limit long orders to bet against the breakout of these dojis.

These positions often have decent risk/reward ratios and are an important tactic in trading range strategies.
