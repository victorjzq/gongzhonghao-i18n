# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Ranges (Part 1)

The vast majority of bars you see on a chart are running within trading ranges.
Every single bar is part of a trading range.
Therefore they are all trading range bars.
And these bars are formed within trading ranges.
This is the most common phenomenon in the market.
Observe most charts and you will find that they are either in a trading range or in the process of forming one.

Even if not currently in a range, the market will soon enter one.
Therefore, as a trader, the most reasonable expectation when facing a trend is: the trend will eventually pull back.

How do you judge the strength of the close?
Pay attention to the force at the close, and also look at the size of the bar's body.

If both of these characteristics are present, then it is a trend bar — a strong bar.

Usually reversals begin with a weak signal bar or a counter-trend bar, and then we see some failed long signal bars.

Although these reversal bars failed, they formed support levels in subsequent trading sessions.

The market tested this breakout point, after which the trend was able to continue.

Trading within a trading range is a very important skill that every trader needs to master, especially day traders, because it is truly critical.

In most cases, if you cannot predict what the next few bars will do, from a probability standpoint, the market is more likely to pull back than to continue in the same direction.

So there is no need to rush to enter during a decline, because this is how the market normally behaves. Even if you miss a trading opportunity, you can still place a limit order above a bar, or wait for a retest of the prior bar's range, because the market is constantly testing back and forth like this.

If you judge that downward momentum still exists and there is still room for further decline, you can place limit orders at the sell zones of these bars and wait for entry.

You can sell within the bar's range, or use a limit order to sell at the first pullback breakout above the prior high.

After recognizing that this is the most common behavior pattern in the market, your trading mindset becomes much more relaxed.

After all, the fear of missing out is a psychological issue that every trader faces.

The key is to understand that there is never truly a missed opportunity in the market.

Being patient often allows you to wait for better trading opportunities.

And usually when you wait patiently, the situation becomes much clearer.

So there are only two outcomes: either failure or success.

If it fails, you know how to handle it.

If it succeeds, you also know how to capitalize on the favorable subsequent move.

For professional traders, the key is to identify whether the chart shows a trend or a trading range.

If it is a trending market, you need to trade in the direction of the trend.
