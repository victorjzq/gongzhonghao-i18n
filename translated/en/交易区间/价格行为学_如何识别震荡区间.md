# Price Action: How to Identify Trading Ranges

When you determine the market is in a trading range, you bet that breakouts will fail, so you place limit sell orders above bars and limit buy orders below bars; when you determine the market is about to enter a trend, you bet that breakouts will succeed and enter with stop orders.

Three consecutive bear bars mean the market will decline at least one more time.

What was originally a pullback within a bull trend keeps pulling back, pulling back, pulling back, and eventually you have to start thinking that this might not be a pullback, but a bear trend.

Once a bar closes and the market dips slightly by one tick before immediately moving up, this indicates the market is very strong.

You see obvious upper and lower tails, followed by a doji — these are all characteristics of trading range price action.

In other words, traders are not going long above bars expecting a rally, nor shorting below bars expecting a decline — instead, they are doing the opposite. Limit order traders are the ones profiting.

This tells us that traders do not believe the market will go up much, so they sell above bars; nor do they believe the market will fall much, so they buy below bars.

A decent swing buy opportunity. If your entry bar is a doji. If you are a bull, you feel disappointed; and the next bar is also a doji. So as a bull, do you think "I made a great buy," or "this is really disappointing"?

Whenever you feel disappointed, that is a very good signal. It is like your radar telling you: the market might be in a trading range.

A prominent characteristic of a trading range is: the market makes some seemingly promising moves, then immediately disappoints you. Therefore, if you feel disappointed while trading and the market does not follow your expectations, you should start considering: this might not be a trending market.

Any rally or decline is very likely just one leg within a trading range. So I believe that paying attention to the signals of "disappointment" and "confusion" is very important.

Whenever traders feel confused — they do not know where the high or low is — they will buy when the market drops, betting it will not fall much further; and they will sell when the market rallies, betting it will not rise much further.
Therefore, "confusion" and "disappointment" always mean we should consider that the market is in a trading range.
