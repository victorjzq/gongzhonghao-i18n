# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Ranges

In trading, you must look to the left. If there are many bear bars providing resistance on the left, you will automatically know: bears want a second leg down.

The reason a second-leg trap is called a trap is that it lures traders into buying at the highs, making them mistakenly believe this is the start of a new trend. Unless this breakout can far exceed the tops of all bars on the left, you must consider: this could be a second-leg trap, specifically designed to lure beginners into chasing when momentum has not yet been exhausted. This is very likely just a test of the top of the trading range.

Wedges usually form trading ranges, and within trading ranges, the market often tests the bottom of the third leg. If I were the type of person who buys at a horizontal level above the moving average, at the top of the trading range, I would want the market to quickly surge upward so I can profit. If there is no quick surge, I would exit immediately. Just like when you buy a breakout at any level — you buy because you believe there is momentum and expect the market to continue higher. Already near the top of the trading range, traders do not want the market to stall here.

Most traders should look for swing trading opportunities, waiting for a double top or double bottom, or a wedge top or bottom, to form. When you encounter a decline followed immediately by a doji, this is a warning that the market may not continue to fall much further.

When you see a parabolic move — parabolic means very extreme, very fast, with not many bars — this is a possible short-term reversal signal. Wedges usually produce a reversal lasting two legs and half the duration of the wedge. Triangles are trading ranges — breakout mode patterns — with roughly 50/50 odds.

When a tight trading range forms, you have to consider whether there is the possibility of a final flag bear breakout and reversal. Many bears who shorted the breakout get trapped in the rally; at the same time, some bulls who were not ready also miss the entry. As a result, both bulls and bears will buy on any pullback.

Typically, the trading range is a magnet, which also means that triangles are trading range structures. A triangle is a converging trading range, and its center, also called the apex, is usually a magnet. Every time a trading range appears, there simultaneously exists both a trend resumption pattern and a trend reversal pattern. Bears want the trend to resume downward; bulls want the trend to reverse.

This is also why Major Trend Reversals (MTR) often produce sideways structures. Because an MTR is the reversal component within a trading range. A trading range must contain a bull leg and a bear leg. An MTR should actually be viewed as a continuation pattern. Most of the time, MTRs ultimately turn out to be trend resumptions — they should be viewed as continuation patterns rather than reversal patterns. Because most MTRs ultimately produce sideways structures.
