# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Ranges (Part 4)

When you observe breakouts of trading range bars, the market usually shows profit-taking signals as it approaches a measured move target, because this is the common behavior of most traders. This behavior is highly consistent, because when price reaches a preset measured move target, many people will choose to take profits.

However, in some cases, the market will completely ignore the first target and continue to break higher. If you are flat at this point and looking for an entry opportunity, in most situations, waiting for price to retest the measured move target is a relatively sound choice.

This is because market makers understand this behavior pattern very well. They usually actively sell at these key price levels to establish short positions. Since they know most traders will take profits at this level, market momentum often shows a temporary weakening at these points.

The logic behind this operation is sound. Because the market has usually already completed two upward legs, those traders who understand this pattern — whether they bought at the low or chased longs at the trading range breakout — will consider taking profits at this point. So near this measured move target, a significant resistance area usually forms.

But if price breaks through this resistance level and continues higher, then our next observation focus should be on the first pullback. Typically, the first pullback will retrace to the measured move target calculated from the trading range breakout. This level can serve as a key point for reassessing the balance between bulls and bears.

After that, the market usually forms one or two new bars. If you are watching a strong trend, you should pay attention to the pullback depth. If the pullback is insufficient to break below the low of the last strong bar, it indicates that the bulls still firmly control the initiative, and the bears have not yet built enough strength to launch a counterattack.

But if the pullback is deep enough to break below the last bar's low by approximately a scalp distance, and bears are able to profit easily, then this trending move has likely ended and the market is entering a new trading range phase.

In the later stages of a move, trading against the current trend direction is statistically valid. The win rate for this counter-trend strategy is approximately 63%. Although such opportunities are not common, when you identify one and have strong logical support, it is a relatively good trade.

Regarding the logic of stop loss placement, it can also be understood in the context of this structure. Within a leg of a trading range, if the market breaks a key level but bears are still in control, then price should not reach the measured move target above.

In this situation, the market often produces a second sideways consolidation or even an upward move. But if the bulls cannot control the rhythm, this level will not be effectively reached.

When you observe a bull trend in which a trading range forms, and then a second upward breakout occurs, this type of move usually reaches the measured move target calculated from the width of that trading range. This pattern is precisely the flag consolidation within a bull trend.

If the current move is indeed a bull trend and the consolidation pattern here can be defined as a flag, then that measured move target should be reachable. But you should also note that if this is the main characteristic of the day's price action and the market has not yet produced a clear second upward leg, then you should not over-expect the bulls to reach that target.

At this point, you should consider where to set the stop loss. If you choose to take a counter-trend trade here, you can also set the stop loss based on the structure of that bar itself.

Both of these reference points are very important. Together they provide information about the market's rhythm and structure.

Especially when a breakout of a trading range bar occurs and this breakout happens in the late stage of a trend, price itself is unlikely to achieve one or two times the measured move target.

In a large bear trend channel, the first pullback that appears — especially when the trend resumption signal appears on the opposite side of the moving average — typically has a very low win rate. You need to be extremely cautious.

When price is near the moving average and the market still has residual momentum, these counter-trend trades have a very high failure rate, and it is recommended to simply abandon them. Do not challenge this type of low-probability trade.

This should become one of the basic principles for every mature trader. Whenever you see a signal bar appearing on the opposite side of the moving average, you should immediately be alert, slow down your trading pace, re-examine your trading logic, and then decide whether it is worth entering.

Stay vigilant and wait for confirmation — this is the best strategy at this position.
