# Price Action: Trading Range (Part 1)

**1. What Counts as a Trading Range**
- Any two-sided trading area within a trend is a trading range, even if it is just one bar.
- A pause of 1-2 bars is called a "pullback"; only when a pullback exceeds 20 bars is it referred to as a "trading range."

2. Why Price Stalls and the Emotional Markers
- The market stays in a trading range because traders cannot determine whether bulls or bears have the edge.
- Two key emotional signals: confusion and disappointment. If you feel either, you are most likely in a trading range.

3. Price Action That Identifies a Trading Range
- A strong breakout is followed by poor follow-through (applies to both bears and bulls).
- Second-leg traps: a rally-pullback-rally that should break out instead reverses.
- 80% of breakouts ultimately fail; the market often reverses at its "strongest moment."
- When reasonable arguments exist for both long and short positions simultaneously, it is a trading range.

4. Minimum Specifications of a Trading Range
- At least 3 legs + at least 20 bars.
- The top and bottom are never clear-cut, but can be roughly divided into: the top 1/3 as the sell zone, the bottom 1/3 as the buy zone.

5. Transition from Trend to Channel to Trading Range
- Every channel eventually evolves into a trading range.
- Strong bull breakout -> channel -> trading range: the channel belongs to the bull trend but is also part of the forming trading range.

6. Strategy for Narrow Trading Ranges
- If the range is too narrow, it is best not to trade.
- If you can find both bull and bear arguments on the same chart, you are in a trading range; do not rush to enter.

7. Breakout Mode Probabilities
- When a trading range has lasted long enough and shows buildup behavior, it enters breakout mode.
- The first breakout has a 50% probability of failure; after a successful breakout, the odds of moving up or down are roughly equal.

8. Wide Stop Loss and Trap Prevention Principles
- Traders often use wide stop losses, believing a bear breakout will quickly reverse.
- If you see an unusually strong second-leg rally/selloff, "look left":
  - If it only tests the top/bottom of the trading range -> second-leg trap, 75% probability of reversing back to the bottom/top.
  - If the left side is still a larger trading range or a wide bear channel, the breakout success rate is even lower.
- When a "strong second-leg rally + strong bear reversal bar" appears, close longs immediately; the reverse also applies.

9. Specific Operational Guidelines
- Do not chase longs at the top of the trading range or shorts at the bottom, unless the breakout is confirmed successful.
- If price is still in the top 1/3 zone, consider taking profits and looking for short opportunities; the reverse also applies.
- When confusion and disappointment persist, old signals fail, and new signals keep forming, reduce position size or stay on the sidelines.

10. Two Core Reminders
- Every trading range contains an early bull trend and an early bear trend -- be patient before the direction is clear.
- The second-leg trap is the biggest pitfall for beginners: do not chase at highs/lows; wait for confirmation before acting.
