# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Ranges (Part 9)

The initial momentum of a move is the key factor that drives the market to continue developing, and this momentum promotes the formation of secondary moves.

If the first leg lacks sufficient momentum, then all market participants will choose to buy, and the move ends right there.

But if the first leg has very strong momentum, then it is possible to form complex counter-trend legs within the trading range.

Price action is still controlled by the boundaries of the trading range.

But once the opposing momentum is strong enough, complex patterns or legs can form within the trading range.

Excellent traders never ignore the big picture.

When the market situation demands it, they decisively adjust their trading strategy.

Trading should be done without attachment, free from any emotion.

When a trade is over, decisively turn toward the new direction.

In a strong trend, the first reversal signal failing is an extremely high-probability "first-attempt trading principle."

The only exception is when the market is in a trading range, because in a trading range, the second leg often produces a V-shaped reversal.

Two consecutive V-shaped reversals are uncommon, but they frequently appear within tight trading ranges.

The key point to remember is that all these patterns occur within the trading range.

The first leg down makes a new high, and after the second leg down is tested, a trading opportunity arises.

In a trading range, V-shaped reversals are very common, typically resulting from tests of support and resistance levels.

If you observe that the second leg is stronger than the first, but both stop at the trading range boundary, you should always remain vigilant.

We must see a breakout and follow-through.

If both of these conditions are absent, it is likely just a false test of support and resistance levels, and the market will then reverse.

If you chat with beginner traders, you will often hear this kind of complaint:

Every time I see a decent signal bar and enter, the market moves a little and then stalls.

In the end, I either have to exit at a loss or barely break even — it is always a failed trade.

When a wedge pattern appears, with clear support, buying pressure, and a buy signal breaking above the prior high, you can establish a position there.

When a doji appears, you can buy below it.

These are all signals that allow you to place limit orders with sound logic.
