# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Ranges (Part 2)

In most cases, even in trending markets, a trading range usually forms after the trend.

This trading range will then either trigger a trend continuation or lead to a trend reversal.

However, in most cases, especially when the market cycle supports the trend and the move is strong, a trend continuation pattern usually appears.

Trend reversals are relatively rare.

Reversal moves are highly dependent on the market environment.

When the market is constructing a larger trading range, you need to observe other indicators to confirm that a reversal is forming.
Therefore, when the relationship between the trading range and the trend changes, it often suggests that this trading range could trigger a trend reversal.

First comes the trend, then the trading range.

And finally, a reversal may occur.

This indicates that during the formation of the trading range, some market signals appeared that foreshadowed a reversal.

A Major Trend Reversal (MTR) pattern involves first breaking through the trendline and then retesting, forming a similar pattern.

But even these patterns do not actually have a very high success rate.

From a probability standpoint, trading with the trend has a win rate of at least 60%, while betting on a reversal has a win rate of only about 40%.

But if you can accurately understand the market context, you can identify those key areas where reversals are more likely to occur.

This usually requires analyzing the relationship between the current timeframe and higher timeframe charts.
