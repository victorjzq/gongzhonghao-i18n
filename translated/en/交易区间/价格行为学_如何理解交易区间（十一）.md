# Price Action: How to Understand Trading Ranges (Part 11)

If this is the first leg after a pattern has completed — for example, a wedge bear flag has completed — you should participate in the second leg, because this is the highest-probability trade.

The second leg is often the critical phase where the market confirms a reversal or trend continuation. Many successful traders only trade this type of high-probability second leg, because it has sufficiently clear signals and a reasonable structural foundation.

When you see price breaking out, what you should consider is how to trade counter-trend. That is, facing a breakout move, you should think about whether this is the final push rather than blindly following.

And when you see a clear reversal signal, what you should think about is how to enter with the trend. Because at this point, the market has very likely established a new trend direction, and with-trend trading has a higher success rate.

These are not subjective judgments, but behavioral patterns revealed by statistical probabilities themselves. Taking corresponding strategies during these phases is based on summaries of large numbers of historical trade samples.

Regarding the structural division of trading ranges, you should be clear about the following:

The upper third is the sell zone, the middle third is the no-trade zone, and the lower third is the buy zone.

Within a trading range, most price action occurs in the middle no-trade zone, approximately 65% to 75%. That means only 25% to 35% of price action occurs in the top and bottom buy/sell zones.

Therefore, most of the time you are actually watching, not frequently entering and exiting the market. This is the psychological expectation you should have when facing a trading range.

So we only trade counter-trend breakouts at the top and bottom — that is, in the buy and sell zones. Because these are the areas within the market structure where reversals are more likely to occur, and signals are more reliable.

If you see some kind of breakout in the no-trade zone, especially without touching the top or bottom zones, by all means stay away. Do not rashly follow through, because this is the behavior with the highest failure rate.

In most trading ranges, 80% to 90% of the price structure consists of ABCD patterns. In other words, double-leg moves — one push, one pullback, then another push and another pullback.

The remaining portion can be described as: three-leg moves account for approximately 10% to 20%.

These three-leg moves include P patterns, W patterns, as well as wedge and parabolic wedge structures.

Although in actual trading it is difficult to clearly define a specific pattern within a trading range, you may find that the entire trading range is likely to evolve into a triangle or expanding triangle structure.

Within such a structure, various patterns are interwoven, including double-leg and three-leg moves. They may appear sequentially or be nested within each other simultaneously.

Therefore, if you can identify that most moves within a trading range follow an ABCD structure and place this structure within the framework of the entire trading range, it becomes easier to identify the high-probability trading opportunities that may arise in the market.

In other words, this identification logic helps you see the buy and sell signals at key positions.

We can say that these identified reversal structures are all high-probability trading opportunities. Especially when these structures appear in the top or bottom zones of a trading range, their significance is even stronger.

Considering all these factors together, you will find that the most valuable operating opportunities are actually the reversal signal bars that appear within the buy and sell zones of a trading range.

This is an important operational guide within trading ranges, well worth repeatedly comparing and verifying during live trading.

Let us also discuss the parabolic wedge.

This structure typically leads to trend resumption. This is because a parabolic wedge is a breakout structure that forms on a higher timeframe — it is not merely a local repeated test, but a signal of the gradual weakening of a long-term bear trend.

The appearance of a parabolic wedge means that bears are gradually losing control, and the market is beginning to build momentum in the opposite direction.

The strengthening of buying pressure usually leads to a clear break of the bear trendline and the appearance of strong bull bars on the price chart, pushing rapidly in the opposite direction. This type of move usually comes with a large number of short-covering stops and bull chase orders, creating a rapid trend reversal.

The trading opportunity at this point also belongs to the second-leg move we discussed earlier — it has clear structural support and a statistical win-rate advantage. What you need to do is actively look for entry opportunities once this structure has completed.
