# Learning AI Series: The Expert

This is my 6th article on learning AI



===

Why are you still not an expert after learning so many methodologies?



Most people's cognitive systems are fragmented. You've bookmarked hundreds of "must-read" articles, your note-taking app is filled with thousands of highlights, you've taken countless online courses, and bought various methodologies. But when you truly need to solve a problem, your mind goes blank.



How to make accurate judgments quickly amid massive amounts of information, like an expert, instead of falling into decision paralysis.



Creation is the core of the cognitive system.

Creation is not the result of knowledge management; creation itself is the best knowledge management.



All cognitive activities — memory, understanding, analysis — ultimately serve creation.

Creation has three layers of meaning.



The first layer: creation is not primarily about pleasing the audience, but about solving the creator's own life challenges.

The second layer: creation itself is the best form of knowledge management, attention management, and self-management.

The third layer: creation produces "separation gain" — extracting thoughts from the mind, placing them in a stable external space for manipulation, and gaining cognitive benefits.



The brain is not a storage device but a prediction machine. The purpose of memory is not to store the past but to build models to predict the future. When you start creating, the brain naturally looks for clues, distinguishes priorities, and distills content. This process itself is the most efficient memory reinforcement.



Knowledge management pursues "convenience" and productivity, not beautiful presentation. This is the kitchen theory: a good kitchen isn't one with neatly arranged cabinets, but one where you can reach the tool you need the moment you stretch out your hand while cooking.

Memory is not replicating symbols but retracing the "thought-scape."



You need to retrace.

Retrace back to the "thought-scape" when the author generated the idea.

This is the SCQA structure: Situation, Conflict, Question, Answer.



Identify the answer — find the author's core conclusion.

Reverse-engineer — ask what question the author was trying to answer to arrive at this conclusion.

Build the explanation chain — analyze the definitions and principles that support the answer.

Retrace the thought-scape — use your own words to derive the viewpoint along the causal chain.

This is what Dangnianmingyue meant by "empathizing" when reading history.

Knowledge management is about managing questions, not answers.

Recording and processing must be separated.

When recording, pursue speed and convenience; processing is an independent step.

The standard for recording is not "knowledge points" but "sense of inspiration." The sense of inspiration is essentially the brain's "prediction failure" — no matter what it is, write it down first.



All materials should be concentrated in one convenient container, not scattered across multiple platforms. Centralization is better than organization.



During the processing phase, use a question set for interrogation. Establish a minimal question set — small in number but covering multiple knowledge types. Use the question set to process knowledge, breaking massive materials into small chunks, or expanding scattered concepts into large chunks. Store knowledge chunks that have been "interrogated," not ready-made conclusions.

Deep understanding requires overcoming cognitive load.

The limitation of working memory is the biggest obstacle to deep understanding. When you feel you can't absorb what you're reading, your mind feels foggy, and your eyes keep scanning back and forth — this is a signal of cognitive load overload.



Chunk extraction requires externalizing thinking. Draw concept maps, let thinking elements coexist simultaneously, discover gaps — the places where you get stuck are cognitive fault lines.

Knowing what to do but being unable to do it — you're stuck in three places: insufficient knowledge (don't know how), insufficient motivation (don't want to), insufficient skill (can't do it well).

Pure optimistic fantasy makes people relax; pure pessimistic fantasy makes people feel helpless. Only by placing "beautiful wishes" alongside "obstructing reality" for comparison can you generate the drive to act.

Psychological contrast creates an irreducible "sense of difference." This tension awakens the brain and prompts change.

Insufficient skill requires making implicit knowledge explicit. Concurrent verbal reporting — have experts verbalize their thought process while performing tasks, record and organize them into prompts. Make predictions, create mental images, use analogies, describe confusion, demonstrate repair strategies.

Creation is the highest form of resolving personal dilemmas. But creation requires a unique sense of blockage — what life challenges cause me pain, confusion, or remain unsolvable?



AI provides fluidity; humans retain the sense of blockage. AI handles tedious work, rapidly generates content, and crosses technical barriers. Humans experience failure and pain, inject unique soul, and reach the source of creativity.



Don't just use AI as a search engine — treat it as a knowledge consultant, creative partner, and cognitive extension. Provide prior knowledge, give AI sufficient background information, and the quality will be higher.



Reverse questioning — let AI ask you questions, using the "alternating instances" principle to test understanding. Retain the sense of blockage — don't let AI smoothly glide past the places where you truly feel pain.

Based on your unique sense of blockage, pose questions. Use AI to decompose them into SCQA structure or expand into panoramic understanding. Let AI provide fluidity — generate paragraphs, Socratic questioning, translate, organize, and polish. Judge whether the current version touches your sense of blockage.

The expert's moat is time density



An expert doesn't know more "rules" — they can make rapid, nearly unconscious judgments about subtle differences after massive material accumulation.



Beginners rely on explicit rules (if-then), experienced practitioners rely on pattern recognition, experts rely on situational awareness.



This holds true in firefighter cases, medical diagnosis, Go, trading, criminal investigation, and military command. The brain completes a "high-dimensional similarity search" at the unconscious level.



Without the early, extensive "clumsy, explicit, rule-based" practice, the later intuitive compression cannot emerge.



Intuition only comes to those with a sufficiently rich material library. So-called "intuition of a certain type of person" is merely the result of long-term attention focused on a specific domain.



But materials must come from real feedback environments. If materials are self-imagination, emotional projection, or echo chamber repetition, then no matter how much you accumulate, you'll only form biased intuition. This is also why some people "become more stubborn as they age" rather than more accurate.



A master doesn't calculate accurately through rules — they have the judgment first, then rationalize it with a system. Experts go intuition-first, method-second; beginners go method-first, intuition-second.



The true moat is that others must repeat the pitfalls you've stepped through, and the cost of those pitfalls is time.



In an era of extreme information transparency, information is no longer scarce, processes can be replicated, and results can be imitated. The only thing that cannot be replicated is the sequence of experiences and time density.



The value of difficult problems isn't in their "difficulty" but in: whether they produce unskippable experience layers, whether they cause structural changes in cognition.



Not all suffering creates a moat. Suffering without feedback and without review only creates illusions. Mistaking wasted time for investment is the biggest misconception.

Creation above all — all cognitive activities ultimately serve creation; creation is the highest form of resolving personal dilemmas.



The sequence of judgment is more important than knowledge accumulation — reproducing the author's judgment sequence matters more than memorizing the author's conclusions.



Thinking can and should extend beyond the brain — separation gain, externalizing thought for cognitive benefit; AI is an external brain, not a replacement.



Experts are not selected — they are filtered by time; the moat comes from the sequence of experiences and time density.



Use AI's fluidity to impact your sense of blockage, rather than replacing it — the painful places are the source of originality.
