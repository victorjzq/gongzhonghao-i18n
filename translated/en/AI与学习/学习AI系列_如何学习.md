# Learning AI Series: How to Learn

**This is my 4th article on learning AI**
-----------------



Methodology Manual



I. Problem Definition

1.1 The Core Problem That Learning Must Solve

The core ability of life to survive in an environment is "the ability to predict information" — to seek benefits and avoid harm, to actively maintain one's own state within the environment.

Prediction falls into two categories:

\- Experience-based prediction: Can only predict the known, not the unknown

\- Model-based prediction: Finding patterns from known situations that can predict unknown situations

The problem that learning must solve = acquiring the ability to generalize, in order to handle situations never encountered before.

1.2 The Root Causes of Learning Failure

1. Wrong objective: "A wrong objective will directly lead to wrong results" — treating "content that needs to be generalized" as "content that needs to be reproduced"

2. Substituting memorization for learning: "Mistaking 'the linguistic material of knowledge' for 'knowledge' itself, mistaking 'memorization of linguistic material' for 'learning'"

3. Learning by intuition: "Not being clear about what one's objective is during the learning process, or how to achieve it"



II. Decision Sequence (Core)

2.1 First Decision: Reproduction or Generalization

"When you have content that needs to be mastered, first determine whether it is content that needs to be reproduced, or content that needs to be generalized, and use this to decide whether to adopt a learning method or a memorization method."

Input: Content that needs to be mastered

Decision: Does this content need to handle situations never encountered before?

\-- No -> Reproduction objective -> Memorization method

\-- Yes -> Generalization objective -> Learning method

2.2 Reproduction Path

"If the content needs to be reproduced, then simply memorize the material at hand."

Execution: Directly memorize the material



2.3 Generalization Path

"If the content needs to be generalized, then the material at hand is not meant for memorization, but rather for helping you construct a mapping in your mind to handle infinite new situations."

Step 1: Clarify the input space and output space

"When learning any knowledge, you must first clarify the input space and output space of that knowledge."

\- Input space: The scope of application of the knowledge = the range of values of input variables

\- Output space: The prediction target = what information this knowledge can tell us

Step 2: Collect materials

"Collect 'concrete examples' and 'descriptions from others'."

Materials fall into two categories:

\- Concrete examples -> Serve the object layer

\- Descriptions from others -> Serve the abstraction layer

Step 3: Organize and arrange

"Categorize 'concrete-level descriptions' and 'abstract-level descriptions' separately, providing clear inputs and outputs for building 'discriminative models' and 'associative models'."

Step 4: Construct models

"Build a 'bottom-up structure', try to clarify 'the relationship between objects and concepts' to construct 'discriminative models', then try to clarify 'the relationships between different concepts' to construct 'associative models'."

Execution sequence:

1\. Object layer (concrete examples) -> Compare different examples -> Find shared attributes

2\. Abstraction layer (abstract rules) -> Construct universal discriminative rules

3\. Association layer -> Clarify relationships between different concepts

Step 5: Verify effectiveness

"Use new situations to verify the discriminative ability of the 'universal discriminative rules' you have constructed, and see whether they are sufficiently universal."

Evaluation criteria:

\- Correct judgment -> "Truly learning... truly constructing 'universal discriminative rules'"

\- Incorrect judgment -> Enter backtracking

2.4 Condition Change: Backtracking Trigger

"We will encounter countless new situations and may still make incorrect judgments... This means the 'universal discriminative rules' you have constructed in your mind about 'encoding' are not that universal. At this point, you need to go back to the previous steps, re-collect new learning materials, and re-construct 'universal discriminative rules'."

Verification failure

\-- Backtrack to Step 2 (Collect materials)

\-- Re-execute Steps 3-5

III. Core Concepts



| Term | Operational Definition |
|------|----------------------|
| Reproduction | Reproducing experience exactly as-is, predicting known situations |
| Generalization | Constructing patterns from the known to solve unseen situations |
| Input Space | The scope of application of knowledge (domain) |
| Output Space | The prediction target of knowledge (codomain) |
| Object Layer | Lower layer, concrete observable phenomena |
| Abstraction Layer | Upper layer, abstract patterns |



IV. Prerequisites for Use

1. The first decision must be completed first

"The objective is the first thing we need to examine, because a wrong objective will directly lead to wrong results."

2. Input and output must be clarified first

"When learning any knowledge, you must first clarify the input space and output space of that knowledge, referred to as 'clarifying input and output'."

3. The use of materials depends on the objective type

\- Reproduction objective -> Materials are used for memorization

\- Generalization objective -> Materials are used for constructing mappings



V. Failure Conditions / Misuse Risks

5.1 Substituting Memorization for Learning

\- Manifestation: "Words exist but meaning is empty" — only creating new symbols in the linguistic world, without constructing corresponding concepts in the world of meaning

\- Evaluation criteria:

\- Knowing the name but not recognizing the person: "Only memorized the name of the concept 'entropy'... but did not construct the concept of 'entropy'"

\- Reciting by rote: "Only memorized the description of knowledge... cannot recognize it when described differently"

5.2 Knowledge Collapse

Lower layer loss (Object layer loss)

\- Manifestation: "Produces a kind of 'suspended experience', a special experience that can only be encountered 'in conversations with others' or 'in written or oral exams'"

\- Evaluation criteria: "Can only use it for exams or as conversation material, cannot transform it into the ability to solve practical problems"

Upper layer loss (Abstraction layer loss)

\- Manifestation: "Can only solve 'exactly identical situations', does not know how to solve when the situation changes"

\- Evaluation criteria: "Cannot solve 'unseen situations', cannot extrapolate from one example to many"

Layer mismatch

\- Manifestation: "The learner has a 'discriminative model', but this 'discriminative model' does not match, causing 'lower-layer objects' to be classified into 'inappropriate upper-layer concepts'"

\- Evaluation criteria: "Produces reasoning fallacies when applying knowledge"

5.3 Mechanical Memorization

\- Trigger condition: "If learning materials that should be used for generalization are used for rote memorization"

\- Evaluation criteria: Rote memorizing content that needs to be generalized (laws, theorems)

5.4 Overfitting

\- Definition: "Only memorized the situations seen during learning, but cannot solve unseen situations"

\- Evaluation criteria: "Can only solve situations that others have told about, but cannot solve situations not previously informed about"

5.5 Type-Method Mismatch

"If learning materials that should be used for generalization are used for rote memorization, it leads to the pitfall of mechanical memorization. If memorization materials that should be used for reproduction are used for generalization, it becomes unnecessary and leads to low efficiency."



VI. Systematic Evaluation

6.1 Scope of Application

"Can handle learning of all types of knowledge, even 'conducting research', 'learning to drive', 'learning to cook', 'learning to play games' — all have corresponding standardized steps."

6.2 Non-applicable Situations

"Like your ID number — no matter how much you try to generalize, it is still just an ID number. In this case, you should use a memorization method, not a learning method."

6.3 Success Verification Criteria

"If your judgment is correct, it means you are truly learning... truly constructing 'universal discriminative rules', rather than simply memorizing 'learning materials'."

6.4 Failure Verification Criteria

"Mistakenly treating 'whether you can remember others' descriptions' as the way to verify 'learning effectiveness'."


Simple vs. complex problems: "If only solving simple problems, people do not need theory. The power of theory lies in its ability to help us analyze and solve complex problems."

The linguistic limitations of tacit knowledge: "For learning 'tacit knowledge', the role of language is even more limited — people cannot use language to accurately describe 'tacit knowledge'"
