# Learning AI Series: How to Resolve Contradictions

This is my 5th article on learning AI.

Many years ago, I heard a name: TRIZ. It came from Russia and was said to be a complete methodology about "innovation."

Innovation is not the result of inspiration appearing by chance, but something that can be systematically generated.

For a period of time, this system was treated almost like classified material before it was gradually made public. I didn't look into it closely at the time, but I vaguely remembered an intuition: if innovation can truly be engineered, then could those "stuck states" we repeatedly encounter in life and work also have similar solutions?

Almost all truly difficult problems are essentially problems where two needs conflict with each other.

When facing conflict, people usually have only a few responses:

Either give up one side and satisfy only one of them;

Or compromise, making both sides retreat a step, resulting in neither being satisfied;

Or simultaneously satisfy both seemingly conflicting needs. Only this kind of "simultaneous satisfaction" constitutes true innovation.

Contradictions arise from the inevitable nature of system development. The various parts of any system do not evolve synchronously and smoothly. When you improve one part, it often damages another. Improvement is local and intermittent, not a simultaneous upgrade of the whole. It is precisely because of this uneven development that contradictions keep emerging. And these contradictions, in turn, become the driving force pushing the system to continue evolving.

Take today's AI as an example. Almost everyone can feel that it's developing too fast and is replacing a large number of jobs. So you face a very real conflict: if you choose to learn AI, your future competitiveness will increase, but the side effect is that your time gets diverted, and your main job might suffer; if you choose not to learn AI, your current work is more stable, but in a few years you'll likely be eliminated entirely. Both paths seem to make sense, and both carry risks. Many people don't fail to recognize this — it's precisely because both sides are convincing that they end up doing nothing, stuck in place.

First, state the goal clearly. If your goal is to not be eliminated by AI in the future, then the conclusion is actually simple: you must learn AI. Once this judgment is established, the question is no longer "should I learn or not," but naturally moves to the next level: what side effects will learning AI produce?

The answer is equally clear: not enough time, and your main job might be affected.

What's truly critical is the third question: how do you eliminate this side effect?

TRIZ's "separation" thinking comes into play here. The solution is not to keep pulling back and forth between "learn" and "don't learn," but to redesign the path. You don't need to learn all of AI, but only the AI content that can directly improve your main job. Once you gain even a little efficiency improvement in your work, the original side effect gets offset, or even reversed into an advantage. Learning is no longer a burden that disperses energy, but becomes part of solving the problem.

In TRIZ's complete system, separation itself has a whole set of very industrialized processes and terminology — time separation, space separation, whole-and-part separation, perspective and reference frame separation, and so on. Honestly, these concepts don't offer much direct help for daily life; they're clearly designed for engineering systems. But this doesn't prevent us from grasping what's truly important about it. Separation is not the endpoint of solving a problem once and for all, but merely the starting point for moving to the next step.

When you resolve one core contradiction, you will almost certainly generate new problems. Many people give up precisely at this point — they think the increasing number of problems means the method is wrong. But from TRIZ's perspective, new problems actually mean the system is continuing to evolve. The correct approach is not to stop, but to treat new problems as new contradictions and continue solving them along the same logical path, or even evolve simultaneously along multiple paths in parallel.

If there's one thing TRIZ offers that's most valuable to ordinary people, it's probably not teaching you how to "innovate," but teaching you how to face conflict. It removes hesitation from the decision-making process, letting you act first and then handle side effects; it pushes problems from the emotional level to the structural level, so you no longer dwell on "can I do this or not" but instead look at "where is the contradiction"; it assumes by default that evolution is a continuous process, not a one-time perfect solution.

Much of the time, we think what we lack is courage, execution, or talent. But the more likely situation is that we're simply standing before a contradiction without knowing how to keep moving forward. TRIZ doesn't make contradictions disappear — it simply tells you: don't stop in front of them.
