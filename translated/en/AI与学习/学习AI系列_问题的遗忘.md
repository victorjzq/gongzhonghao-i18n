# Learning AI Series: The Forgetting of Questions

This is my first article in the Learning AI series.

To truly master trading has never been just about "learning to trade."

Wu Jun once mentioned a phenomenon when discussing his daughter's math learning:

**Many people struggle with math not because they lack mathematical ability, but because their language skills are insufficient—it's a** reading comprehension problem.

In this era, **not knowing AI is essentially the modern equivalent of being illiterate.**

**Beyond trading, I have chosen to systematically learn AI.**

As always, my learning path is to first take notes, then make those notes public.

In my experience using GPT, I discovered that **once a problem becomes complex and branches multiply, relying solely on the conversation itself is highly unreliable**.

AI often, after presenting a large number of options, gradually forgets the original list of questions as follow-up inquiries deepen; and I myself, while pursuing a particular branch of questioning, unconsciously lose track of other key issues. When context begins to go missing, AI does not stop to warn that "information is incomplete"—instead, it tends to fill in the gaps with seemingly reasonable content, resulting in both omissions and potentially fabricated outputs. For this very reason, the correct way to use AI for complex problems is not to ask follow-up questions continuously, but to **extract intermediate answers as you go, conduct multiple rounds of verification, and then feed the verified results back in as new context**. Only through this process of external recording and repeated re-injection can you prevent question drift and answer distortion, and keep reasoning grounded on an auditable, traceable foundation.

In complex problems, AI is essentially a divergent generator—responsible for rapidly enumerating possibilities under given conditions, generating large numbers of candidate explanations, paths, and scenarios, and reasoning through local problems; while the human's role is that of a convergent filter—one must judge among these candidate pieces of information which are useful, which are merely noise, hallucinations, or redundancy, determine which results need to be written into the "fact ledger," and on that basis bear the responsibility for final decisions that carry real-world consequences. This division of labor is not a matter of usage preference, but is determined by the respective capability boundaries of humans and AI. What should be adopted is a Human-in-the-Loop decision system: AI first generates a large volume of candidate information, the human filters, solidifies, and annotates it with confidence levels, then lets AI continue reasoning around the selected information, after which the human filters again and makes decisions. In this process, AI can only produce "candidate information"—this information must first be temporarily stored by the human, then verified through real-world conditions, evidence, and constraints, before it can ultimately be transformed into truly usable information; without this step, humans easily get led along by AI's continuous generation. Copy the results to another document to perform "information freezing (Freezing the state)"—preventing AI from inadvertently rewriting established conclusions during subsequent reasoning, avoiding personal memory from being overwritten by new information, and preserving an auditable, traceable basis for subsequent judgment.
