# Learning AI Series: Learning from Dan Koe

**This is my 3rd article on learning AI.**
------------------

***The most important skill to learn in the next 10 years*** **has recently gone viral, and some people even bluntly say that Naval's content can be thrown straight into the trash.**

Everyone should read this article carefully and **treat it as a guiding framework for the foreseeable future, using it to re-plan their own path of action**.

Dan's way of thinking goes like this:

The world is not composed of "careers," but of "systems."

An individual's life breakthroughs are never achieved through single-point effort. What truly matters is whether you have found a system capable of producing long-term compound returns. Once the system is established, effort becomes meaningful; if the system itself is flawed, no amount of hard work will do anything but drain you in place. He opposes: linear effort, linear life, and linear career paths.

He consistently emphasizes one judgment: **the life or work model you are currently using is itself wrong**. The problem is not that you are not working hard enough, but that you are working hard within a flawed system.

In terms of identity positioning, he rejects traditional labels. He does not define himself as a writer, blogger, or coach, but rather sees himself as a person who is building a personal system, and he presents this building process publicly. This is also the true meaning behind several concepts he repeatedly mentions: build in public, development-based path, and teach what you learn. He is not standing in the position of a "teacher" passing on experience, but is simply six to eighteen months ahead of you, showing you the paths he has already validated.

Nearly all of his expressions follow the same internal logic: starting from personal confusion or reflection, abstracting it into ideas, then solidifying those ideas into frameworks, and finally narrating them repeatedly from different angles. So you will feel like he is constantly repeating the same thing, but in reality, each round of expression unfolds at a higher level of abstraction.

Ultimately, this set of ideas naturally extends to his approach to commercialization. Dan Koe's commercialization is not accomplished by selling courses or selling techniques, but by selling "a systematized version of himself." In this process, the product is merely a container for ideas, a downloadable version of the system, not the core itself.
