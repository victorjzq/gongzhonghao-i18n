# Learning AI Series: Panning for Gold

**This is my 2nd article on learning AI.**
------------------

Information itself is no longer scarce, but **value that can be converted into results can still only be refined through extensive, repetitive, and tedious filtering and polishing**.

In the past, the "gold" mainly came from information asymmetry; but now, the mining fields are essentially wide open. **The real differentiator is no longer whether you have the qualification to enter, but who is willing to invest time and manpower over the long term, continuously polishing in the low-value-density segments**.

When Zhuoke discussed Manus, its value does not lie in stacking Agents, tool calls, or browser automation — piecing these capabilities together. Rather, it lies in the meticulous engineering around "whether a task can be stably completed": how to terminate promptly upon execution errors without meaninglessly consuming tokens, how to recover strategy after deviation while maintaining goal anchoring, and how to make long-running task processes debuggable and observable. Open-source solutions generally fail at these critical details — not because of insufficient technical capability, but because they lack sufficiently long-term manpower investment to fix, one by one, the numerous failure paths exposed during real-world execution.

As model capabilities gradually approach a plateau, **what truly holds value is no longer the functionality itself, but the system's "failure handling capability"**; and this capability **can only be obtained through the continuous accumulation, reproduction, and individual repair of a large number of failure samples**.

Against this backdrop, the focus of learning has also shifted: it is no longer about acquiring more knowledge, but about **how to repeatedly compress, validate, and polish existing knowledge into a capability that can be stably reused**.

Taking Price Action as an example, it does not lack definitions, patterns, or case studies. What is truly scarce is the understanding of which patterns possess stable statistical edges across different timeframes and market contexts, and **which ones are merely valid as terminology but repeatedly fail in actual trading**. This kind of judgment cannot be obtained through a single reading or summary — it can only be formed through comparative analysis of large samples, post-failure reviews, and gradual convergence of conditions.

In this process, the role of AI is not to replace human judgment, but to **undertake the repetitive labor that is low in value density yet indispensable**: for example, performing structured annotation of historical charts, decomposing patterns by prerequisites, tabulating failure paths across different environments, and continuously exposing systematic biases in human intuition.

Stop blindly trusting experts — you must get in the game yourself. Only you yourself will be fully committed, panning for gold in the sand.
